Imagine scrolling through your phone and seeing a 15-second video. A fitness star is shown from below in cool shadows, while a heavy bass beat plays in the background. Suddenly, you really want to spend your allowance on the protein shake they're holding. You might think you decided to want it on your own. But actually, that video used a bunch of secret tricks to get you to feel that way!

When we read a regular book, we look at nouns, verbs, and punctuation. But **non-print texts communicate meaning through visual, audio, and spatial elements**. A non-print text is anything you don't just read like a normal book—like a YouTube video, a video game, or a social media post. Learning how to understand these videos and posts is like learning a superpower. It helps you see how people use pictures and sounds to change your mind. Let's learn the secret playbook!

## The Grammar of the Screen: Visual and Audio Mechanics

In a book, an author uses descriptive words to make a scene feel spooky or happy. On a screen, the creator has to do this without words. **Visual media relies on elements like color, framing, and lighting to convey tone.** Every single thing you see on the screen is a choice someone made to make you feel a certain way.

### Framing and Perspective
Think about how a camera looks at a person. The angle can totally change how you feel about them!
*   **A low camera angle often makes a cinematic or photographic subject appear dominant or powerful.** This means the camera is down near the floor looking *up* at the person. It makes them look like a towering superhero or a giant boss in a video game.
*   On the flip side, **a high camera angle often makes a cinematic or photographic subject appear vulnerable or insignificant.** If the camera is up high looking *down* on someone, they look small and weak, like a tiny action figure.

### The Symphony of Sound
Our brains react to sound instantly, like cheering when your favorite sports team scores. In movies and videos, sound is divided into two types based on where it comes from:
*   **Diegetic sound originates from a source within the world of the media presentation.** This means the people inside the movie can hear it too. Because it's part of their world, **characters within a film or video can interact with or hear diegetic sound**. Examples are a dog barking on screen, a character playing a guitar, or a car engine revving.
*   **Non-diegetic sound originates from a source completely outside the world of the media presentation.** The characters on screen have no idea this sound is playing. **Background music in a film is typically a non-diegetic sound element used to evoke an emotional response** from you, the audience. When scary string music plays before a jump scare, the main character doesn't hear it—but it definitely makes *your* heart beat faster!

### Composition and Timing
How things are put together changes how we understand them. 
*   **Juxtaposition in media places two distinct images side by side to emphasize a contrast.** Imagine seeing a picture of a totally empty, sad refrigerator, and then the very next picture is a giant, piping hot pizza with extra cheese. Putting those two opposite things right next to each other makes the pizza look way better and the empty fridge look way sadder.
*   The speed of a video matters, too. **The pacing of video cuts influences the audience's perception of urgency or suspense.** If a scene cuts from one camera shot to another really, really fast, it feels like a fast-paced sports match, making you feel anxious or excited. If the shots last a long time without cutting, it feels calm and slow.

Even things that don't move have a "voice." **Typography in digital media influences the reader's perception of the text's tone.** Typography is just the style of the font. A thick, messy font looks like a horror movie, while a neat, bubbly font looks like a comedy. Also, **color symbolism in visual texts evokes specific cultural or emotional responses from the audience**. If a scene is tinted icy blue, you'll feel cold and lonely. If it has a warm, golden-yellow glow, you'll feel cozy and happy.

## Modern Media Ecosystems: Formats and Algorithms

Today, it's really hard to tell the difference between something made just for fun and something made to sell you a product. 

### Distinct Media Formats
Different types of media do different things to get your attention:
*   **A documentary uses recorded footage of actual events and people to inform or persuade an audience.** It looks like pure facts, but remember, the director chose exactly which real-life clips to show you to make you agree with them.
*   **A podcast communicates persuasive messaging entirely through audio elements like voiceover tone, pacing, and background sound effects.** Since there are no pictures to look at, the speaker’s voice going straight into your headphones is what convinces you!
*   **Infographics combine numerical data with visual representations to quickly convey complex information.** Think of an infographic like a colorful chart—it turns boring math into a cool picture. Let's look at a quick math calculation of how that works. If an infographic wants to show that 3 out of 4 kids love a certain game, it might use simple math to find the percentage:
    1. Write the fraction of kids who love the game: 3/4.
    2. Divide the top number by the bottom number: 3 divided by 4 = 0.75.
    3. Multiply by 100 to get the final percentage: 0.75 * 100 = 75%.
    By making the number "75%" giant and colorful, the infographic convinces you instantly!
*   **Interactive media requires active participation from the user to navigate the presented information.** Video games are the best example of this! You actually have to push buttons and make choices to keep the story going.
*   **Hyperlinks in digital texts allow readers to immediately access referenced external sources.** These are the blue, clickable words you see in articles. They let you jump straight to another website to learn more.

### The Invisible Economy of the Internet
The internet wants to keep your attention so it can make money. Here are some tricks it uses:
*   **Clickbait uses sensationalized headlines to encourage users to click a link to an article or video.** A clickbait title is something crazy like, "You won't BELIEVE what this kid found in his pizza box!" You click it because you are super curious, even if the video ends up being boring.
*   **Product placement is a persuasive technique seamlessly integrating branded goods into a narrative media text.** If your favorite movie character is suddenly wearing bright red Nike sneakers and making sure the logo faces the camera, that's not an accident. Nike paid for those shoes to be there!
*   **Native advertising is paid digital media designed to perfectly match the visual format and tone of the platform hosting the ad.** Imagine scrolling through your favorite sports blog. You see an article that looks exactly like a regular sports story, but it’s actually a paid ad written by a company trying to sell sports drinks. It's wearing a disguise!

### The Algorithmic Confinement
The apps you use aren't just giving you random videos. **Algorithms on social media platforms curate content based on a user's past engagement and viewing history.** The app is basically a robot that takes notes on what you like. If you watch a lot of skateboarding videos, the algorithm will show you tons more skateboarding videos.

This creates something tricky called **the filter bubble effect, which occurs when algorithms isolate a user from encountering diverse viewpoints.** If you only see videos that agree with your favorite sports team, you might forget that other teams' fans even exist! Your feed becomes a bubble. This makes a big problem worse: **confirmation bias, the tendency of individuals to favor information confirming their preexisting beliefs.** We all love being told we are right!

## The Architecture of Persuasion: Rhetoric and Propaganda

Tricking people into agreeing with you is an old game. Way back in the ancient world, **Aristotle first defined the rhetorical appeals of ethos, pathos, and logos in the fourth century BCE.** That's over two thousand years ago! Even today, **persuasive media often employs rhetorical appeals to logic, emotion, and authority**. They try to make sense (logic), make you feel happy or sad (emotion), or use an expert (authority) to convince you.

When someone uses these tricks unfairly to manipulate you, it’s called propaganda. In fact, **the Institute for Propaganda Analysis formally identified seven basic propaganda devices in 1937.** These are the sneaky tricks advertisers still use today!

| Technique | Definition | Real-World Translation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Bandwagon** | **The bandwagon technique attempts to persuade a target audience to adopt a behavior because everyone else is participating in that behavior.** | "Every kid at school is playing this new video game! You have to download it so you aren't left out!" |
| **Plain Folks** | **The plain folks technique attempts to persuade an audience by presenting a speaker as an ordinary citizen rather than an elite figure.** | A billionaire wearing a cheap t-shirt and eating a slice of pizza to make you think, "Hey, he's just a regular guy like me!" |
| **Testimonial** | **The testimonial technique uses endorsements from famous individuals to promote a product or idea.** | Your favorite famous basketball player looks into the camera and says you should buy a certain brand of cereal. |
| **Glittering Generalities** | **Glittering generalities are emotionally appealing words linked to highly valued concepts without providing supporting evidence.** | A commercial that just yells words like "Awesome!" "Epic!" or "Freedom!" without actually explaining what the product does. |
| **Name-Calling** | **The name-calling technique links a person or idea to a negative symbol to foster rejection from the audience.** | Calling a rival sports team a bunch of "cheaters" or "crybabies" instead of talking about how they actually play. |
| **Transfer** | **The transfer technique projects the positive or negative qualities of one entity onto another entity.** | Filming a car commercial in front of an American flag so you take the proud feelings you have about the flag and slap them onto the car. |
| **Card Stacking** | **Card stacking involves emphasizing information supporting one side of an argument while entirely omitting contrary information.** | A snack commercial showing how yummy their candy is, but completely hiding the fact that it costs \$50 and tastes like old socks. |

There are two more tricks you should watch out for:
*   **Fear appeals attempt to persuade an audience by describing terrible outcomes resulting from a specific course of action.** (Example: A commercial for toothpaste saying your teeth will all rot and fall out if you don't buy their specific brand).
*   **Snob appeal is a persuasive technique suggesting that adopting a particular belief or purchasing a product elevates the consumer's social status.** This makes you think you'll be the coolest, most elite kid in school if you buy a super expensive jacket.

## Navigating the Noise: Source Evaluation and Media Literacy

To survive the internet, you can't believe everything you see. **Media literacy requires evaluating the credibility of multiple digital and print sources.** This just means you have to be a detective and check if your information comes from honest, trustworthy places. 

> **Triangulation involves cross-checking a factual claim across at least three independent sources.** Think of this like adding up clues to see if a fact is real. If a YouTuber says the sky is turning purple, you shouldn't just believe it right away. 
> 1. Check your first source (the YouTube video). 
> 2. Find a second totally different source (like a science website) to see if they say the same thing. 
> 3. Find a third source (like a news channel) to check again. 
> If all three say the sky is purple, it might be true!

### The CRAAP Test
To help you be a great internet detective, some smart librarians came up with a funny-sounding tool. **The CRAAP test is a source evaluation acronym developed by librarians at California State University, Chico.** It helps you quickly score whether a website is good or garbage.

**The CRAAP test stands for Currency, Relevance, Authority, Accuracy, and Purpose:**

*   **Currency:** **Currency in source evaluation refers to the timeliness of the information presented.** A video game guide from 1995 won't help you beat a game that came out yesterday!
*   **Relevance:** Does this information actually help you with your project or question? Is it easy enough for you to understand?
*   **Authority:** **Authority in source evaluation refers to the credentials and expertise of the author or publisher.** Is the person who wrote the article a real scientist, or just a random guy in his basement making things up? 
*   **Accuracy:** Does the site have facts that you can prove? Can you use triangulation to check them? Are there lots of spelling mistakes?
*   **Purpose:** Why did someone make this video or website? Did they make it to teach you something, to entertain you, or to trick you into spending your allowance? 

When you learn how to spot these tricks, you are taking control back! You can see when a low camera angle is trying to make someone look like a powerful superhero, or when non-diegetic background music is trying to make you cry. You can spot the sneaky algorithms trying to trap you in a filter bubble. You aren't just surfing the web anymore; you are a smart media detective!
