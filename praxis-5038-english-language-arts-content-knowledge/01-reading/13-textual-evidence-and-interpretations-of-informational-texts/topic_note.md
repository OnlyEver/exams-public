A [structural engineer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Structural_engineering) evaluating a building's [blueprint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blueprint) does not guess where the [load-bearing walls](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Load-bearing_wall) are; they trace the lines, calculate the [stress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stress_%28mechanics%29), and point to the exact [steel beam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beam_%28structure%29) supporting the structure's weight. Reading and teaching informational texts demands this exact [forensic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forensic_science) precision. When readers evaluate [non-fiction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-fiction)—whether it is a historical [manifesto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifesto), a [scientific journal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_journal), or a piece of [long-form journalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Long-form_journalism)—they cannot rely on vague impressions. They must identify how an author constructs meaning, extract the unstated implications beneath the surface, and tether every single analytical claim to the precise textual evidence that supports it. 

![Just as a structural engineer identifies specific load-bearing beams to understand a building's physical integrity, rigorous readers must isolate the exact textual evidence that supports an author's analytical claims.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Parallam_support_beam.jpg)

As an English [educator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teacher), your objective is to train students to stop treating reading as a [passive absorption](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Passive_learning) of facts and start treating it as an [active investigation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Active_learning). To do this, you must master the mechanics of meaning, [inference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inference), and evidence. 

## The Architecture of Meaning: Literal and Figurative Foundations

Before a reader can evaluate the complex arguments embedded within an informational text, they must first decode the foundational language. 

**[Literal meaning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Literal_and_figurative_language)** refers to the exact, [dictionary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dictionary)-definition meaning of words and phrases in an informational text. When a [historian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Historian) writes that a battle was "fought in a torrential downpour," the literal meaning is a severe [rainstorm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rain). Often, texts deploy specialized or unfamiliar vocabulary. When this happens, **[context clues](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Context_clue) surrounding an unfamiliar word help a reader deduce the word's literal meaning in an informational text.** 

However, authors of non-fiction do not restrict themselves to literal declarations. **[Figurative meaning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Figure_of_speech) involves words or phrases that depart from everyday literal language to create comparison, emphasis, or clarity.** 

Why do authors use [literary devices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Literary_device) in highly logical, factual documents? Because human beings understand the unknown by comparing it to the known. **Informational texts frequently use figurative language such as [analogies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analogy) to explain complex or [abstract concepts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abstraction) to the reader.** For example, an [astrophysicist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrophysics) explaining the [expansion of the universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expansion_of_the_universe) might use the analogy of a [balloon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Balloon) inflating with dots drawn on its surface. Specifically, **[metaphors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metaphor) in informational texts compare two unrelated subjects to highlight a shared characteristic or clarify a difficult idea.** When an [economist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economics) describes a [market crash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stock_market_crash) as an "[avalanche](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Avalanche)," they are mapping the sudden, cascading, and destructive momentum of [snow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snow) onto financial data.

![To grasp the figurative metaphor of an economic "avalanche," a reader must first comprehend the literal reality of the phenomenon: a sudden, compounding, and highly destructive collapse.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Loose_snow_and_slab_avalanches_near_mt_shuksan.jpg)

> **Crucial Pedagogical Rule:** **Understanding the literal meaning of an informational text is a fundamental [prerequisite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prerequisite) for analyzing deeper figurative nuances.** A student cannot understand why the market crash is an "avalanche" (figurative) if they do not first comprehend the literal mechanics of what an avalanche actually is.

## The Logic of Deduction: Explicit Facts vs. Implicit Inferences

Once the vocabulary is decoded, reading informational text becomes a rigorous exercise in drawing logical conclusions. This requires navigating the boundary between what the author says and what the author means.

**[Explicit information](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Explicit_knowledge) is clearly and directly stated by the author in the informational text.** It requires no guesswork; the data is on the page. However, texts are rarely exhaustive. **Drawing inferences requires readers to identify [implicit information](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tacit_knowledge) not explicitly stated in an informational text.** 

**Implicit information must be logically deduced from the context, tone, and specific details of an informational text.** Notice the inclusion of *tone*. **An author's [tone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tone_%28literature%29) in an informational text provides crucial clues for drawing accurate inferences about the author's underlying perspective.** If an author uses words like "[bureaucratic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bureaucracy) labyrinth" or "endless [red tape](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_tape)," their critical tone allows the reader to infer a profound frustration with government inefficiency, even if the author never explicitly states, "I dislike the government."

![The term "red tape" literally originated from the actual red ribbons historically used to bind government documents, but authors rely on its implicit meaning to signal frustration with bureaucratic inefficiency.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/NARA_Backstage_Pass_(2011-08)_-_14.jpg)

### The Mechanics of a Valid Inference

**An inference is a logical conclusion drawn by combining textual clues with the reader's [prior knowledge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schema_%28psychology%29).** It is not a guess. 

1. **Valid Inferences:** To be mathematically sound in literary terms, **valid inferences must be firmly grounded in specific, [observable details](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observation) found within the informational text.** If a text notes that a specific species of [frog](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frog) is suddenly absent from a local pond immediately following the construction of a [chemical plant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_plant), we can validly infer that the plant's [runoff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_runoff) likely impacted the frog population.

![A valid inference connects textual clues—such as a chemical plant's proximity to a body of water—with a reader's prior knowledge of how surface runoff logically pollutes local ecosystems.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Stormwater_Diagram.svg)

2. **Overreaching:** This is the most common error novice readers make. **Overreaching occurs when a reader's inference goes beyond what can be logically supported by the available textual evidence.** Using the frog example, inferring that the chemical plant is *intentionally* trying to destroy local wildlife is an overreach. The text supports ecological impact, not corporate [malice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malice_%28law%29).

As students process both the stated and unstated facts, they must engage in two distinct [cognitive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cognition) tasks:
*   **[Synthesizing information](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synthesis) involves combining explicit details and drawn inferences to form a comprehensive understanding of a text.** It is the mental combining of the puzzle pieces to see the whole picture.
*   Conversely, when tasked with reporting *only* the facts, they must produce an **[objective summary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abstract_%28summary%29) of an informational text, [which] deliberately excludes the reader's personal opinions or inferences.** 

## The Weight of Proof: Selecting and Evaluating Textual Evidence

Every analytical claim about a text is merely an unsupported [hypothesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypothesis) until evidence is provided. **[Textual evidence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evidence) consists of specific details from a text used to support an argument, analysis, or interpretation.** 

### Types of Evidence
Informational texts deploy distinct categories of evidence, each serving a unique [rhetorical function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhetoric):

*   **[Anecdotes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anecdote) are short personal stories often used as [qualitative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qualitative_research) textual evidence in informational texts to illustrate a broader point.** They provide human resonance.
*   **[Statistical data](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistics) functions as [empirical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empirical_evidence) textual evidence in informational texts to build logical arguments.** They provide objective, measurable scale. 

When you or your students cite these texts, you have two primary vehicles for delivery:
1.  **[Direct quotations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quotation) serve as exact textual evidence to validate an interpretation of an informational text.** You use these when the author's specific phrasing is irreplaceable.
2.  **[Paraphrasing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paraphrase) involves restating an author's ideas in the reader's own words while retaining the original meaning.** Because it condenses information, **a paraphrase can serve as broad textual evidence when summarizing a lengthy section of an informational text.**

### Assessing the Strength of Evidence

Not all evidence is created equal. **Evaluating an informational text requires assessing whether the author provides sufficient textual evidence to support the main [thesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thesis_statement).** As a teacher, you will constantly ask students: *Does this quote actually prove your point?*

| Evidence Quality | Definition & Characteristics |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Strong Textual Evidence** | **Strong textual evidence directly relates to and definitively supports the central claim being made about a text.** It leaves no [logical gaps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logic) between the quote and the assertion. |
| **Weak Textual Evidence** | **Weak textual evidence may be factually correct while completely failing to support the specific inference or analysis being presented.** A student might quote a true [statistic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistic) from the text, but if it does not prove their specific claim, the evidence fails. |
| **Corroborating Evidence** | **[Corroborating evidence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corroborating_evidence) involves finding multiple pieces of textual evidence that support the exact same inference or conclusion.** This makes an argument virtually unassailable. |

## The Pedagogical and Professional Imperative

Dropping a quotation into an essay is not analysis; it is [transcription](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transcription_%28linguistics%29). **Thorough [textual analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Close_reading) requires explicitly explaining the logical connection between a cited piece of evidence and the inference it supports.** This "connective tissue" is the core of effective analytical writing. You must train students to write the "because" — explaining *how* the text proves the claim.

Why does this specific pedagogical approach matter? It is legally and professionally encoded into your [curriculum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curriculum). 

> **The Curriculum Standard:** **The [Common Core State Standards](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Common_Core_State_Standards_Initiative) for [English Language Arts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Language_arts) require students to cite strong and thorough textual evidence to support analysis of what a text says explicitly** as well as inferences drawn from it.

Furthermore, mastering the distinction between strong and weak evidence is paramount for your own licensure. **The [Praxis 5038 exam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Praxis_test) tests a candidate's ability to identify the most relevant quotation to support a given analytical claim about a passage.** You will be presented with a claim and four factually accurate quotations from a text. Only one will be *the most relevant*. 

By understanding the precise architecture of literal and figurative meaning, the rigorous logic required to deduce implicit information, and the forensic process of evaluating textual evidence, you equip yourself not just to pass your exams, but to cultivate a generation of highly critical, discerning readers.
