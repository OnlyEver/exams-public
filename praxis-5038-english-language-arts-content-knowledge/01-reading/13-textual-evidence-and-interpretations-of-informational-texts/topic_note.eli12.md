Imagine you're trying to figure out if your friend is actually good at a new video game, or just bragging. You wouldn't just take their word for it, right? You would want to look at their exact score, see their win rate, and watch how they play. Reading information—like a science article or a history book—works the exact same way! When you read non-fiction, you can't just guess what the author means. You have to be a detective, figuring out exactly how the author builds their ideas, finding their hidden hints, and pointing to the exact sentences that prove your points.

As you learn to read like a pro, your goal is to stop just letting words wash over you. Instead, you'll become an active investigator. To do this, you need to understand how meanings, clues, and proof all work together.

## The Architecture of Meaning: Literal and Figurative Foundations

Before you can dig into the deep arguments of a text, you need to understand the words themselves. 

**Literal meaning refers to the exact, dictionary-definition meaning of words and phrases in an informational text.** If an article about basketball says a player "scored 30 points," the literal meaning is exactly that—they put the ball in the hoop enough times to add up to 30. Sometimes, texts use tough words you don't know. When that happens, **context clues surrounding an unfamiliar word help a reader deduce the word's literal meaning in an informational text.** It's just like guessing what a new power-up does in a game by watching what happens right after you grab it.

But authors who write facts don't just stick to boring dictionary definitions. **Figurative meaning involves words or phrases that depart from everyday literal language to create comparison, emphasis, or clarity.** 

Why use creative comparisons in a serious article? Because we understand weird or new things by comparing them to things we already know! **Informational texts frequently use figurative language such as analogies to explain complex or abstract concepts to the reader.** For example, **metaphors in informational texts compare two unrelated subjects to highlight a shared characteristic or clarify a difficult idea.** If an author says a soccer player's kick was a "rocket," they are comparing a fast-moving ball to a spaceship to show incredible speed.

> **Crucial Rule to Remember:** **Understanding the literal meaning of an informational text is a fundamental prerequisite for analyzing deeper figurative nuances.** This means you must know what a real rocket is (literal) before you can understand why a fast soccer ball is being called one (figurative).

## The Logic of Deduction: Explicit Facts vs. Implicit Inferences

Once you know what the words mean, reading becomes a puzzle where you figure out what the author is *really* trying to say. This requires looking at the facts and doing some smart thinking.

**Explicit information is clearly and directly stated by the author in the informational text.** It requires no guesswork; it is right there on the page. If the text says, "The pizza costs \$15," that's explicit. But texts don't spell out everything for you. **Drawing inferences requires readers to identify implicit information not explicitly stated in an informational text.** 

**Implicit information must be logically deduced from the context, tone, and specific details of an informational text.** Think of tone as the author's attitude. **An author's tone in an informational text provides crucial clues for drawing accurate inferences about the author's underlying perspective.** If an author writes, "The supposedly simple math test felt like climbing Mount Everest in flip-flops," their complaining tone helps you infer they thought the test was unfairly hard, even if they never openly state, "I disliked the test."

### The Mechanics of a Valid Inference

**An inference is a logical conclusion drawn by combining textual clues with the reader's prior knowledge.** It is not a random guess! 

1. **Valid Inferences:** To be correct, **valid inferences must be firmly grounded in specific, observable details found within the informational text.** 

Let's look at how combining facts leads to an inference using a step-by-step math example. Imagine a sports article says a player scored 12 points in the first half of the game, and 8 points in the second half. 
Step 1: Identify the explicit fact from the first half (12).
Step 2: Identify the explicit fact from the second half (8).
Step 3: Add the two exact facts together (12 + 8 = 20).
Step 4: Combine this with your prior knowledge (you know the player needed exactly 25 points today to break a world record).
Step 5: Draw the logical inference: The player did not break the record, because 20 is less than 25. 

2. **Overreaching:** This is a big mistake readers sometimes make. **Overreaching occurs when a reader's inference goes beyond what can be logically supported by the available textual evidence.** If an article notes that school lunches are unpopular, inferring that the cooks are intentionally trying to poison kids is an overreach. The evidence supports the idea that the food tastes bad, not that anyone is being evil!

As you read, you'll do two big things in your brain:
*   **Synthesizing information involves combining explicit details and drawn inferences to form a comprehensive understanding of a text.** This is like putting all the puzzle pieces together to see the whole picture.
*   Conversely, if someone asks you to report *only* the facts, you must produce an **objective summary of an informational text, [which] deliberately excludes the reader's personal opinions or inferences.** 

## The Weight of Proof: Selecting and Evaluating Textual Evidence

Whenever you make a claim about a text, it's just an unsupported guess until you prove it. **Textual evidence consists of specific details from a text used to support an argument, analysis, or interpretation.** 

### Types of Evidence
Authors use different types of evidence to prove their points:

*   **Anecdotes are short personal stories often used as qualitative textual evidence in informational texts to illustrate a broader point.** (Like telling a quick story about the time you lost your weekly allowance to show why saving money is important).
*   **Statistical data functions as empirical textual evidence in informational texts to build logical arguments.** (Like pointing out that 80% of the school voted for longer recess). 

When you want to share proof from a text, you can do it in two main ways:
1.  **Direct quotations serve as exact textual evidence to validate an interpretation of an informational text.** You use these when the author's words are perfect just the way they are.
2.  **Paraphrasing involves restating an author's ideas in the reader's own words while retaining the original meaning.** Because it shrinks things down, **a paraphrase can serve as broad textual evidence when summarizing a lengthy section of an informational text.**

### Assessing the Strength of Evidence

Not all evidence is good evidence. **Evaluating an informational text requires assessing whether the author provides sufficient textual evidence to support the main thesis.** You have to constantly ask yourself: *Does this actually prove the point?*

| Evidence Quality | Definition & Characteristics |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Strong Textual Evidence** | **Strong textual evidence directly relates to and definitively supports the central claim being made about a text.** It’s like showing a video of you crossing the finish line first to prove you won a race. |
| **Weak Textual Evidence** | **Weak textual evidence may be factually correct while completely failing to support the specific inference or analysis being presented.** If you claim a movie is super funny, but your evidence is "it was exactly 90 minutes long," that's true, but it's weak because it doesn't prove it is funny. |
| **Corroborating Evidence** | **Corroborating evidence involves finding multiple pieces of textual evidence that support the exact same inference or conclusion.** It's like having three different friends all confirm you scored the winning goal. |

## The Pedagogical and Professional Imperative

Just dropping a quote into your homework isn't enough. **Thorough textual analysis requires explicitly explaining the logical connection between a cited piece of evidence and the inference it supports.** You have to explain the "because"—*how* does the quote actually prove your point? 

Why do we care so much about this? Because it is exactly what you are expected to learn in school! 

> **The School Standard:** **The Common Core State Standards for English Language Arts require students to cite strong and thorough textual evidence to support analysis of what a text says explicitly** as well as the inferences you draw from it.

Even teachers have to master this! Before they can teach, they take tests too. **The Praxis 5038 exam tests a candidate's ability to identify the most relevant quotation to support a given analytical claim about a passage.** They will be given a claim and four true quotes, and they have to pick the absolute best one.

By understanding exactly how literal and figurative words work, how to figure out hidden meanings, and how to pick the strongest proof, you become an incredibly smart reader who can never be fooled!
