Listen to me, grab that pretzel, sit down, and focus, because when you finally step into that secondary ELA classroom, you aren't just going to be a teacher. You are going to be a chief detective at a sprawling, chaotic, high-stakes true-crime scene, and your students are thirty rogue, wild-card rookies who want to jump to the craziest conclusions possible. Teaching kids how to read a historical manifesto or a heavy scientific journal is exactly like investigating a multi-million-dollar heist. The author is the mastermind, they’ve left behind a very specific trail of clues, and your job is to train these kids to stop hallucinating evidence and start doing actual forensic work! 

Before you can even begin to crack the case, you have to establish the bare-bones facts of the crime scene. You have to tell them that literal meaning refers to the exact, dictionary-definition meaning of words and phrases in an informational text. It is the physical broken glass; it is the exact model of the blue getaway car. If the text says the sky was dark, it just means the sky lacked light. But what happens when the mastermind uses weird, specialized jargon? Your rookies will panic. You have to show them how context clues surrounding an unfamiliar word help a reader deduce the word's literal meaning in an informational text. It’s like looking at the shape of the dust around a stolen painting to figure out exactly what kind of frame used to hang there! 

But these authors—especially the non-fiction ones—are theatrical masterminds! They don't just stick to the dry, literal facts because human brains get bored. Figurative meaning involves words or phrases that depart from everyday literal language to create comparison, emphasis, or clarity. You might ask your kids, why is an astrophysicist writing about the universe using flowery poetry? Because informational texts frequently use figurative language such as analogies to explain complex or abstract concepts to the reader. It is exactly like explaining a highly technical digital cyber-heist by comparing it to a guy picking a rusty padlock on a backyard shed! It makes the invisible visible. More specifically, metaphors in informational texts compare two unrelated subjects to highlight a shared characteristic or clarify a difficult idea. If an economist calls a sudden market crash an "avalanche," they are taking the terrifying, uncontainable, destructive momentum of a mountain of snow and slapping it right onto financial data. But here is the absolute pedagogical trapdoor you will fall through if you aren't careful: understanding the literal meaning of an informational text is a fundamental prerequisite for analyzing deeper figurative nuances. If your student doesn't know what a literal avalanche is—if they think it's a type of tropical fruit—they are never going to understand the metaphor about the economy! You cannot decode the metaphor if you cannot read the literal crime scene.

Once the literal foundation is set, the investigation heats up, and you have to navigate what the suspect said versus what they actually meant. First, you have the smoking gun. Explicit information is clearly and directly stated by the author in the informational text. The author literally wrote "the mayor stole the funds." Open and shut. But the real juicy stuff, the massive conspiracy, is hidden beneath the surface. Drawing inferences requires readers to identify implicit information not explicitly stated in an informational text. To get there, implicit information must be logically deduced from the context, tone, and specific details of an informational text. Think of tone as the suspect's shifting eyes and sweaty brow in the interrogation room! An author's tone in an informational text provides crucial clues for drawing accurate inferences about the author's underlying perspective. If the author describes a new city policy using words like "bureaucratic circus" and "elected clowns," that wildly sarcastic tone lets you infer they absolutely despise the local government, even if they never explicitly typed the words "I hate the mayor." 

But your students are going to act like feral conspiracy theorists. You have to rein them in! You have to remind them that an inference is a logical conclusion drawn by combining textual clues with the reader's prior knowledge. It is a mathematical formula: clues on the page plus what I already know equals the truth. To survive cross-examination, valid inferences must be firmly grounded in specific, observable details found within the informational text. If the author notes that a factory dumped green sludge into the river, you can validly infer the local fish are in deep trouble. But kids love to go completely off the rails. Overreaching occurs when a reader's inference goes beyond what can be logically supported by the available textual evidence. It is inferring that because the factory dumped green sludge, mutant alien lizards are running the corporation! No, Jimmy, put down the red string! That is an overreach! You have to train them to build the entire corkboard properly. Synthesizing information involves combining explicit details and drawn inferences to form a comprehensive understanding of a text. They are looking at the stated facts, the unstated clues, and weaving them together to see the whole matrix. And then, sometimes, you just need them to report the raw facts to the chief of police without any drama. An objective summary of an informational text deliberately excludes the reader's personal opinions or inferences. Just tell me what happened, no spin, no personal feelings about the author's bad haircut!

Eventually, this investigation is going to trial, and a claim without evidence is just you screaming in the courthouse parking lot. Textual evidence consists of specific details from a text used to support an argument, analysis, or interpretation. What kind of evidence are we bagging and tagging? Sometimes the author relies on human drama to sway the jury. Anecdotes are short personal stories often used as qualitative textual evidence in informational texts to illustrate a broader point. It’s the tearful sob story from a single victim on the witness stand to make the jury feel the sheer emotional impact of the crime. Other times, it’s cold, hard reality. Statistical data functions as empirical textual evidence in informational texts to build logical arguments. That is the undeniable DNA test, the forensic accounting, the cold math! 

When your students are presenting this proof, they have two primary weapons. Direct quotations serve as exact textual evidence to validate an interpretation of an informational text. This is playing the uncut audio recording of the suspect confessing to the crime. It is irrefutable because it is the author's exact voice. But sometimes that recording is three hours long! Paraphrasing involves restating an author's ideas in the reader's own words while retaining the original meaning. Because it condenses the timeline of events, a paraphrase can serve as broad textual evidence when summarizing a lengthy section of an informational text. 

But listen to me—take another sip of your drink, because this is where students will drive you to the brink of insanity. You have to grade the quality of their proof! Evaluating an informational text requires assessing whether the author provides sufficient textual evidence to support the main thesis. Did the prosecutor bring enough proof to convict? Strong textual evidence directly relates to and definitively supports the central claim being made about a text. It is the perfect puzzle piece sliding flawlessly into the empty space. Conversely, weak textual evidence may be factually correct while completely failing to support the specific inference or analysis being presented. This is the student proudly holding up a valid unpaid parking ticket to prove the suspect committed a multi-million-dollar bank robbery. Yes, the parking ticket is real, it's explicitly in the text, but it absolutely does not prove the heist! And when you hit the absolute jackpot of proof? Corroborating evidence involves finding multiple pieces of textual evidence that support the exact same inference or conclusion. Five separate witnesses from five different vantage points all pointing to the exact same guy! 

But you cannot just dump the murder weapon on the judge's desk, yell "Boom," and walk out of the courtroom! Thorough textual analysis requires explicitly explaining the logical connection between a cited piece of evidence and the inference it supports. You have to be the lawyer explaining exactly *why* the bloody glove matters! This connective tissue isn't just my unhinged pedagogical ranting—it is literally the law of the land! The Common Core State Standards for English Language Arts require students to cite strong and thorough textual evidence to support analysis of what a text says explicitly, as well as the invisible implications they dug up. And guess what? This is your final boss battle, too. The Praxis 5038 exam tests a candidate's ability to identify the most relevant quotation to support a given analytical claim about a passage. You are going to be sitting at a computer, staring at four quotes that are all completely factually accurate and all pulled directly from the text, and you have to pick the one piece of evidence that actually convicts the mastermind. Master this, and you won't just pass your exam; you'll turn a room full of chaotic teenagers into the most ruthless, brilliant textual detectives on the planet. Now finish your drink, you've got lesson plans to write!
