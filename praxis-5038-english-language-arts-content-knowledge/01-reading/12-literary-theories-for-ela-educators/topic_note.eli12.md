Imagine looking at a book through different colored glasses. If you wear blue glasses, certain parts of the story stand out. If you wear red glasses, different parts jump out at you! These glasses are like literary theories. Think of it this way: **literary theory provides a systematic framework for analyzing literature**. It's basically a set of rules or a "lens" you use to read a story. On the flip side, **literary criticism is the practical application of literary theory to specific texts**. That just means putting on a specific pair of glasses and actually reading a book—like *Harry Potter* or *The Hunger Games*—to see what you notice!

## The Text as an Isolated Object: Formalism and New Criticism

A long time ago, people only cared about the author's life or the history of a book. But then, readers decided to look *only* at the words on the page. **Formalism focuses entirely on the structural purposes of a text**, like the rhythm of a poem or how the sentences are built. To do this perfectly, **formalist critics ignore the author's biography during literary analysis**. It doesn't matter if the author was rich, poor, happy, or sad. Also, **formalist critics ignore the historical context of a text during literary analysis**. It doesn't matter what year it was written. The book is totally on its own!

**New Criticism is a specific type of Formalism popular in the mid-twentieth century**. It is still how you take reading tests today! **New Criticism treats a literary work as a self-contained aesthetic object**. Imagine a story is a snow globe; you only look at what's inside the glass. Because of this, **close reading is the primary analytical method used in New Criticism**. You zoom in and read the words super carefully.

**New Critics coined the term intentional fallacy**. What does that mean? Well, **intentional fallacy is the mistaken belief that an author's intention defines the primary meaning of a text**. If you say, "I think the author meant this," a New Critic will say, "We don't care what they meant; we only care about what the words actually say!" Also, **New Critics coined the term affective fallacy**. **Affective fallacy is the mistaken belief that a text's meaning is derived from the reader's emotional response**. If a story makes you cry, that's just a feeling. It doesn't mean "sadness" is the true, hidden meaning of the story.

## The Reader Enters the Equation: Reader-Response Theory

If a video game is turned off, is it still fun? Some critics say a book is just ink on paper until a reader's brain turns it on. **Reader-response theory argues that a text has no inherent meaning outside of the reader's interpretation**. Because everyone is different, **reader-response criticism analyzes how a reader's background influences their understanding of a text**. A kid from the city and a kid from a farm might read the exact same poem and picture totally different things!

> **The Transactional Nature of Reading**
> A very smart teacher named **Louise Rosenblatt developed the transactional theory of reading**. **The transactional theory of reading posits that meaning is created through a dynamic interaction between the reader and the text**. It's like playing tennis—the book hits the ball to you, and your brain hits it back!

But this doesn't mean you can just make up wild rules. A scholar named **Stanley Fish introduced the concept of interpretive communities to reader-response theory**. **Interpretive communities are groups of readers sharing similar reading strategies and cultural expectations**. For example, if you and all your friends love anime, you are an interpretive community. You will all probably notice the same superhero tropes in a graphic novel!

### Contrasting the Foundational Lenses

| Feature | New Criticism | Reader-Response Theory |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Locus of Meaning** | The text itself (like a locked box). | The team-up between the reader and the book. |
| **Role of the Reader** | Act like a detective finding clues. | Use your own life to build the meaning. |
| **Fallacies to Avoid** | Intentional Fallacy, Affective Fallacy. | Believing there is only one "right" answer. |

## Power, Ideology, and Identity: The Sociological Lenses

Some theories want to zoom way out and look at society. They ask: Who gets to be the boss? Who gets left out?

### Marxism and Economic Determinism
**Marxist literary criticism analyzes literature through the lens of class conflict**. It looks at the battle between rich people and working people in stories. **Marxist criticism examines how texts reflect the ideological control of the dominant social class** (which is a fancy way of saying it shows how the wealthy rule-makers stay in charge). To figure this out, **Marxist critics evaluate the economic conditions portrayed within a literary work**.

To understand this, let's look at a quick math example of how economic conditions work for a character who does chores for a rich neighbor:
1. The character works all day and is promised \$20.
2. The rich neighbor charges the character \$14 as a "fee" for renting the lawnmower.
3. We subtract the neighbor's fee from the pay: 20 - 14 = 6.
4. The character is only left with \$6.

A Marxist critic uses this kind of math detail to prove how the rich neighbor keeps all the power in the story!

### Feminist and Queer Theory
**Feminist criticism analyzes how literature reinforces the oppression of women** (like when stories only let girls be "damsels in distress"). At the same time, **Feminist criticism analyzes how literature subverts patriarchal structures** (like when a girl character becomes the hero and breaks the rules). So, **feminist literary critics examine the representation of gender roles in texts**.

Going even further, **Queer theory challenges heteronormative assumptions present in literary texts**. ("Heteronormative" is just a big word for assuming everyone in a story fits a standard, traditional mold). **Queer theory examines the socially constructed nature of sexual identity in literature**, looking at how society decides what is "normal" and how stories can break those boundaries.

### Critical Race Theory and Postcolonialism
**Critical Race Theory examines how literature reflects systemic racism in society**. Systemic racism means unfair rules that are baked into the system. **Critical Race Theory analyzes the representation of racial power dynamics in texts**, asking who gets treated fairly and who doesn't.

When looking at the whole world, **postcolonial criticism analyzes literature produced by cultures subjected to imperial rule**. Imperial rule is when a powerful country bullies and takes over a weaker country. So, **postcolonial critics examine the power dynamics between colonizers and colonized peoples in literature**. A really famous thinker here is Edward Said. **Edward Said's concept of Orientalism is a foundational text in postcolonial literary criticism**. **Edward Said's Orientalism describes how Western literature historically stereotyped Eastern cultures**, often making them look bad just so the bullying countries could pretend they were doing a good thing.

## History Re-Imagined: New Historicism

A scholar named **Stephen Greenblatt is widely considered the founder of New Historicism**. This theory thinks history and books are a two-way street. **New Historicism assumes that a literary text is shaped by its historical context** (meaning the time period changes the book). But it also **assumes that a literary text actively shapes its historical context** (meaning a great book can actually change the real world!). Because of this, **New Historicism analyzes texts alongside other cultural documents from the same time period**. That's like reading a superhero comic and a real-world newspaper from 1940 at the exact same time to see how they match up.

## The Architecture of Meaning: Structuralism to Deconstruction

How do human brains build stories? Some thinkers say it's like using building blocks.

### Structuralism
**Structuralism analyzes literature by examining underlying systems of signs**. Think of a "sign" as a Lego piece. **Structuralist literary critics seek to identify universal narrative structures common to all stories**. For example, almost every movie has a "hero's journey" where the main character goes on a quest.

To do this, **Structuralist critics frequently analyze texts by identifying binary oppositions**. What are those? **Binary oppositions are pairs of related terms that are opposite in meaning** (like good/evil, light/dark, or Jedi/Sith). Structuralists believe our brains organize all stories using these opposites.

### Deconstruction and Post-Structuralism
But what if the building blocks are wobbly? **Post-structuralism asserts that fixed underlying structures of meaning do not exist**. The world is just too messy for neat little categories.

**Deconstruction is a literary theory associated with the philosopher Jacques Derrida**. He said that words are tricky. **Deconstruction argues that language is inherently unstable**. Because words can mean many things, **Deconstructionists seek to expose inherent contradictions within a literary text**. Basically, **Deconstructionist critics analyze how a text subverts its own apparent meaning**. They try to prove that a book accidentally argues against itself! It's like finding a glitch in a video game.

## Deep Minds, Deep Roots, and the Physical Earth

Finally, let's look at theories that dig into our brains and our planet.

### Psychoanalytic Criticism
**Psychoanalytic criticism applies the psychological theories of Sigmund Freud to literature**. Freud was a famous doctor of the mind. Instead of looking at money or society, **psychoanalytic critics analyze the unconscious desires of literary characters**. They want to know what a character secretly wants but won't admit. **Psychoanalytic criticism explores the presence of the id, ego, and superego in literary characters**. (The *id* is the part of your brain that says "I want pizza now!", the *superego* says "It's not dinner time yet," and the *ego* is the referee that makes the final choice).

### Archetypal Criticism
Another mind doctor named Carl Jung looked at the whole human race. **Carl Jung's concept of the collective unconscious heavily influenced archetypal literary criticism**. He believed all humans share a giant, invisible memory cloud. Because of this, **Archetypal criticism identifies recurring myths across different cultures in literature** (like the story of a great flood). Also, **Archetypal criticism identifies recurring character types across different cultures in literature** (like the trickster or the wise old mentor). **Northrop Frye was a prominent theorist in the field of archetypal criticism**, and he loved finding these repeating patterns.

### Ecocriticism
What about the actual planet? **Ecocriticism examines the relationship between literature and the physical environment**. Instead of only caring about the humans in the story, **ecocritical analysis focuses on representations of nature within literary texts**. Does the story treat the forest like a beautiful home, or just a place to chop down trees for wood?

Whenever you read a book in English class, remember these tools. Whether you are hunting for clues like a New Critic, or thinking about fairness like a Marxist, literary theory helps you become a super-smart reader and a great thinker!
