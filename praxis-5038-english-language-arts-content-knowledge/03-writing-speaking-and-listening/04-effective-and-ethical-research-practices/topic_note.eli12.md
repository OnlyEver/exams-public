Imagine you have the whole world's information right in your pocket. Knowing how to do good research isn't just about following boring rules for school. It's about figuring out what is real, where ideas come from, and how to share your own thoughts fairly. Let's look at how information is organized, checked, and credited!

## The Architecture of Inquiry: Formulating and Finding 

Before you ever type a website address, you need to know what you are actually looking for. The biggest mistake is starting too big. If you pick a topic like "Video Games" or "World War II," it's like trying to eat an entire pizza in one giant bite.

**An effective research question must be specific enough to be answered thoroughly within the scope of a given assignment.** To achieve this, **broad research topics must be narrowed by adding specific constraints such as time periods, geographical locations, or specific demographic groups.** For example, instead of just "video games," you might research "how puzzle video games helped teenagers in Japan in the 2020s."

To see how this works, let's use a step-by-step math calculation to show how narrowing a topic makes your workload easier:
1. Start with a huge, broad topic (e.g., 1000 ideas about sports).
2. Narrow it down by adding a location constraint (1000 / 10 = 100 ideas about sports in California).
3. Narrow it down further by adding a time period constraint (100 / 10 = 10 ideas about sports in California in 2023).
4. Now you only have 10 great ideas to read through instead of 1000!

But when you start searching, watch out for a brain trap. **Confirmation bias is the human tendency to search for, interpret, or favor information that aligns with preexisting beliefs during research.** If you already think a weekly allowance should be \$20, you might only search for "why kids need big allowances" instead of "average weekly allowance for kids." This ruins your research because you only see what you want to see!

### Precision Searching with Boolean Operators

Computers don't think like humans. To talk to them effectively, we use special commands. **Boolean operators are specific words placed between search terms to refine digital database search results.** Think of them like cheat codes for searching:

*   **The Boolean operator AND narrows search results by requiring all specified terms to be present in the returned documents.** (For example, searching `pizza AND pineapple` forces the computer to only find pages that mention both words).
*   **The Boolean operator OR broadens search results by returning documents containing any of the specified terms.** This is great for words that mean the same thing (For example, `sneakers OR tennis shoes`).
*   **The Boolean operator NOT narrows search results by excluding documents that contain a specific term.** If you want to research the wild cat but keep seeing cars, you can search `jaguar NOT car`.

## The Taxonomy of Evidence: Source Types and Credibility

Not all information is equal. We sort evidence by how close it is to the actual event. 

1.  **Primary sources provide firsthand evidence, original data, or direct testimony concerning a historical topic or phenomenon.** This is like a player's actual jersey worn in a big game, a real diary entry, or a live video recording.
2.  **Secondary sources analyze, interpret, or evaluate primary sources.** This is like a sports commentator on TV breaking down a player's game tape.
3.  **Tertiary sources compile and summarize established information from primary and secondary sources.** Things like encyclopedias and textbooks live here. They are good for a quick summary, but usually not the final stop for a big research project.

### Evaluating the Signal from the Noise

How do you know if a website is telling the truth? 

The very best information goes through a strict grading process. **Scholarly peer review is a quality control process where a manuscript is evaluated by experts in the same field before publication.** It's exactly like having your math homework checked by three math teachers before you are allowed to turn it in. 

For regular internet sites, you can use a checklist. **The CRAAP test is a heuristic used to evaluate source credibility based on Currency, Relevance, Authority, Accuracy, and Purpose.** (A "heuristic" is just a fancy word for a mental shortcut or tool). Let's look at two of the most important parts:
*   **Currency in source evaluation refers to the timeliness of the information and whether the content reflects recent developments in the field.** A review of a video game from 2005 isn't "current" if you want to know about the newest gaming console!
*   **Authority in source evaluation refers to the credentials, qualifications, and professional affiliations of the author or publisher.** Basically, who is writing this, and are they an expert?

One quick clue for authority is the website's web address:
*   **A domain name ending in .gov indicates that a website is exclusively sponsored by a United States government entity.**
*   **A domain name ending in .edu indicates that a website is affiliated with a recognized higher education institution**, like a college.

But don't just trust a website because it looks nice! You need to check the author's background. **Lateral reading is the practice of evaluating a digital source's credibility by opening new tabs to search for information about the source's publisher or author.** Instead of just reading the site's own "About Me" page, you open a new tab and Google the author to see if they are actually a trusted expert or just someone making things up!

## The Ethics of Intellect: Synthesis, Plagiarism, and Law

Research isn't just a scavenger hunt where you copy and paste quotes. The real goal is mixing ideas together. **Synthesizing information involves combining facts and ideas from multiple disparate sources to form a new conclusion or unified perspective.** It’s like making a smoothie: you blend strawberries, bananas, and yogurt together to make a brand-new, delicious flavor. 

When you mix ideas, you must be fair. **Plagiarism is the ethical violation of presenting someone else's work, words, or ideas as one's own original work.** That is cheating. Did you know you can even cheat off yourself? **Self-plagiarism occurs when a student submits their own previously graded work for a new assignment without instructor permission.** 

There are also legal rules about what you are allowed to use. Thankfully, school projects get special permission! **Fair use is a legal doctrine permitting limited, transformative use of copyrighted material without acquiring permission from the rights holders.** This means you can use a tiny clip of a song for a school presentation. Also, really old things eventually become free for anyone to use. **Public domain works are creative materials that are not protected by intellectual property laws and can be used freely without copyright infringement.**

### Integrating the Voices of Others

When you want to include someone else's brilliant idea in your essay, you have three choices:

1.  **Quoting**: Using their exact words.
2.  **Paraphrasing**: **Paraphrasing involves restating a specific passage from a source into the researcher's own words and sentence structure.** This isn't just changing one or two words. You have to rebuild the sentence entirely! Remember, **a paraphrased passage must still include a proper citation attributing the ideas to the original source.** Even if the words are yours, the idea belongs to them.
3.  **Summarizing**: **Summarizing involves condensing the main ideas of a larger text into a shorter overview in the researcher's own words.** This is like telling your friend what a two-hour movie was about in just thirty seconds. 

> **The Exception to Citation:**
> Usually, you have to give credit for everything you look up. But there is one exception. **Common knowledge refers to established facts that are widely known and undisputed across many sources.** (For example: "The Earth orbits the Sun"). **Information classified as common knowledge does not require a citation in academic research.**

## The Cartography of Citation: Formatting and Mechanics

Different subjects in school use different rulebooks for how to give credit to authors:

| Citation Style | Primary Subject | Key Features |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **MLA** (Modern Language Association) | English classes | **The Modern Language Association (MLA) format is the standard citation style used in English literature and the humanities.** It cares most about the author's name and the page number. |
| **APA** (American Psychological Association) | Social sciences (like psychology) | **The American Psychological Association (APA) format is the standard citation style heavily used in the social sciences.** It cares a lot about the year things were published! |
| **Chicago Manual of Style** | History | **The Chicago Manual of Style is a citation format commonly used for history disciplines that frequently utilizes footnotes or endnotes.** (Those are the tiny little numbers at the bottom of a page). |

### MLA Formatting: The Standard for English Teachers

Since we are in English class, we will focus on MLA!

#### In-Text Citations
When you write an essay, you put a tiny note in parentheses right after you use a fact. **An MLA in-text citation typically requires the author's last name and the page number from which the information was taken** (like this: `(Smith 42)`). As a quick comparison, **an APA in-text citation typically requires the author's last name and the year of publication** (like this: `(Smith, 2023)`).

A great trick to make your writing flow smoothly is to introduce the author right in your sentence. **A signal phrase is a clause that introduces a quotation, paraphrase, or summary by explicitly naming the author of the source material.** 
*   *Example:* As author John Smith says, "Pizza is great" (42). 
Notice the rule there: **If a signal phrase names the author in an MLA formatted paper, the parenthetical citation at the end of the sentence only requires the page number.**

Sometimes you need to change a quote slightly to make it fit your sentence. 
*   **An ellipsis (...) is a punctuation mark used within a direct quotation to indicate that words have been omitted from the original text.** (Like skipping a boring part of a sentence).
*   **Brackets ([ ]) are punctuation marks used within a direct quotation to indicate that words have been added or modified by the researcher for grammatical clarity.** (Like changing "he" to "[the dog]" so the reader knows exactly who is being talked about).

If a quote is super long, you need a special rule. **Block quotations in MLA format are used for direct quotes that exceed four lines of prose or three lines of poetry.** When this happens, you change how it looks on the page: **Block quotations in MLA format are indented half an inch from the left margin in their entirety and do not use enclosing quotation marks.**

#### The Works Cited Page

At the very end of your paper, you create a list of everywhere you got your facts. **An MLA Works Cited page is alphabetized by the authors' last names.** But what if you don't know the author? **If a source lacks a known author, an MLA Works Cited entry begins with the title of the source and is alphabetized by that title.**

To make this list easy to read, **an MLA Works Cited entry uses a hanging indent to format entries that extend beyond a single line of text.** What does that mean? **A hanging indent is a formatting style that indents all lines of a citation entry half an inch from the left margin except for the first line.** 

Every citation is built like a puzzle using the exact same pieces. **The nine core elements for an MLA Works Cited entry are Author, Title of source, Title of container, Other contributors, Version, Number, Publisher, Publication date, and Location.**

Let's talk about titles and containers:
*   **In MLA format, the title of an independent, standalone source such as a book, film, or entire website is italicized.** (Like *Harry Potter*).
*   **In MLA format, the title of a short work such as an article, essay, or poem contained within a larger work is placed in quotation marks.** (Like a single chapter inside a book).

What is a container? **A container in MLA format refers to the larger whole that holds a smaller source, such as an academic journal holding an article or a database holding a journal.** Think of it like your backpack holding a folder, which holds your homework!

#### The Annotated Bibliography

Sometimes your teacher will ask you to explain your sources before you write the essay. **An annotated bibliography is a structured list of citations followed by a brief descriptive and evaluative paragraph for each source.** It’s a way of proving to your teacher that you actually read the websites and books, and that they are totally trustworthy! 

By learning all these skills, you aren't just following strict rules. You are learning how to find the truth, give fair credit, and become an awesome researcher!
