Imagine spending \$60 on a new video game, but the game only shows you your score right as the screen says "GAME OVER." You wouldn't know if you were doing well or making mistakes along the way! In school, testing you only at the end of a long unit is just as bad. Teaching reading and writing is a big job. To help you level up, teachers use different kinds of check-ins (called assessments). Tests and grades aren't just about giving you a number; they help teachers see exactly how your brain is growing when you read, write, speak, and listen.

## The Architecture of Assessment: Purpose and Measurement

Before we look at specific tools, let's group them by when they happen and why.

At the very beginning of a new topic, teachers give **diagnostic assessments**. Diagnostic assessments are administered before a lesson begins to determine a student's baseline knowledge. It's like doing a quick warm-up drill at soccer practice so the coach knows what you already know how to do.

Once learning starts, teachers use **formative assessment**. Formative assessment involves ongoing checks of student understanding during the learning process.

> **The Core Directive of Formative Assessment**
> The primary purpose of formative assessment is to inform and adjust future instruction. Formative assessments are generally considered low-stakes due to carrying little or no point value toward a final grade.

If formative assessment is tasting the pizza sauce while you're still cooking it, **summative assessment** is serving the finished pizza to a judge. Summative assessment evaluates student learning at the end of an instructional unit by comparing it against a standard. Summative assessments are considered high-stakes due to having a significant impact on a student's final grade. Final exams and end-of-unit essays are common examples of summative assessments.

When looking at scores, teachers can view them in two different ways. **Criterion-referenced assessments** measure a student's performance against a predetermined set of learning goals. You either know the skill or you don't. 

Here is a quick math example for scoring a criterion-referenced test:
Suppose you take a spelling test with 25 questions, and you get 20 correct. How does the teacher find your percentage?
1. Write your correct answers over the total questions as a fraction: 20/25.
2. Divide the top number by the bottom number to get a decimal: 20 / 25 = 0.80.
3. Multiply that decimal by 100 to get your percentage: 0.80 * 100 = 80%.

The other way to look at scores is using **norm-referenced assessments**. Norm-referenced assessments compare a student's performance against the performance of a statistically selected peer group. This tells you if you are in the top 10% of kids your age, like comparing your high score on a game to the global leaderboards.

No matter the test, teachers need two important things from it: **validity** and **reliability**.
*   **Validity** in assessment refers to the degree to which an assessment actually measures what it claims to measure. (For example, a math test is not a valid way to measure your reading skills!)
*   **Reliability** in assessment refers to the consistency of the results produced by the instrument over time. If a test is reliable, you would get a similar score if you took it again tomorrow.

## Evaluating Writing: Rubrics, Conferences, and Portfolios

Grading writing can be tricky because it's a matter of opinion. Two teachers might like different things about the same story. To fix this, teachers use rubrics. **Rubrics increase assessment reliability by providing clear standardized criteria for evaluating subjective student work.** (Subjective means it's based on personal opinion, like art or writing).

### The Taxonomy of Rubrics

There are a few different types of rubrics, just like there are different tools in a toolbox:

| Rubric Type | Definition & Function | Trade-offs |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Holistic** | **Holistic rubrics provide a single comprehensive score based on an overall impression of a student's performance.** | They are fast to grade, but they don't tell you exactly what you need to fix. |
| **Analytic** | **Analytic rubrics break down an assignment into specific constituent skills to provide individual scores for each category.** (Constituent skills just means the smaller parts, like spelling, creativity, and grammar). | **Analytic rubrics provide more detailed feedback to students about specific strengths than holistic rubrics**, but **analytic rubrics take more time for teachers to score than holistic rubrics.** |
| **Single-Point** | **A single-point rubric outlines only the target expectations for an assignment.** | **Single-point rubrics leave open space on either side of the target expectations for individualized teacher comments.** |

When using an analytic rubric, teachers often use the **Six Plus One Traits of Writing model**. The Six Plus One Traits of Writing model evaluates writing based on ideas, organization, voice, word choice, sentence fluency, conventions, and presentation.

When checking "conventions" (like grammar), teachers want to see how you write normally. **Assessing grammar in context involves evaluating a student's application of grammatical rules within their own writing**, instead of just filling out a boring worksheet.

### The Art of Conferring

The best way a teacher can help you is by talking to you. **Conferring is a formative assessment technique involving a one-on-one conversation between a teacher and a student.**

The architecture of an effective writing conference includes research, decision, teaching, and linking phases.
1.  **Research:** During the research phase of a writing conference, the teacher asks the student questions to understand their current writing struggles.
2.  **Decision:** The teacher decides on the main thing to help you with.
3.  **Teaching:** The teacher shows you how to do it.
4.  **Linking:** You agree to try it in your own writing.

> **Warning: The Copyediting Trap**
> **Ineffective writing conferences involve the teacher copyediting the entire student draft.** (Copyediting means fixing every single mistake for you, which doesn't help your brain learn!). Instead, **effective writing conferences focus on teaching the writer one or two specific skills.**

Because writing takes time to get good at, teachers use portfolios. **Portfolio assessment involves collecting a student's work over time to demonstrate growth.** Just like a photo album! **Portfolio assessments are frequently used to evaluate writing development throughout a school year.**

## Decoding the Reader: Assessing Comprehension and Fluency

Reading happens inside your head, so teachers have to find clever ways to see if you actually get what you are reading.

To find out what level book you should start with, teachers use **Informal Reading Inventories**, which are diagnostic assessments used to determine a student's independent reading level.

To check on you while you are learning, teachers use reading conferences. **In a reading conference, the teacher typically asks the student to read aloud to assess fluency.** (Fluency is how smooth you sound). While you read, teachers use **running records**, which are formative assessments used to evaluate a student's oral reading fluency in real time.

If you mess up a word, the teacher does a **miscue analysis**. **Miscue analysis involves recording the specific errors a student makes while reading aloud.** Why do they do this? **Teachers use miscue analysis to identify a student's specific decoding struggles**, like if you always get stuck on words with "th" or "ch."

### Assessing Comprehension

Reading words perfectly doesn't matter if you don't understand the story. To check your understanding, teachers use these tricks:

*   **Cloze Procedure:** **A cloze procedure is a reading assessment where words are strategically omitted from a passage.** (Omitted means taken out). **In a cloze procedure, students must fill in the missing words to demonstrate reading comprehension.** This is like a fill-in-the-blank game. **Cloze reading assessments evaluate a student's ability to use context clues to decode meaning.**
*   **Think-Aloud Protocols:** **Think-aloud protocols require students to verbalize their thought processes while reading.** This just means talking about what you are thinking! **Teachers use think-aloud protocols to formatively assess a student's reading comprehension strategies.**
*   **Retelling:** **Retelling is an assessment strategy where a student orally recounts a story to demonstrate sequencing skills** (putting events in the right order).

## The Ecology of Discourse: Assessing Speaking and Listening

Talking and listening are super important in class.

**Assessing speaking skills requires evaluating vocal delivery elements like volume and articulation.** (Articulation means speaking clearly so people understand you).
Also, **assessing speaking skills requires evaluating physical delivery elements like eye contact and posture.**

But listening is just as important! **Listening assessments often measure a student's ability to summarize information presented orally by peers.** If your friend talks about their favorite movie and you can't repeat the main idea back, you weren't really listening.

### Structured Discourse Models

Teachers use fun setups to test these skills:

*   **Socratic Seminars:** **A Socratic seminar is a formal discussion method used to assess a student's ability to listen actively.** Instead of just shouting opinions, **Socratic seminars are used to assess a student's ability to respond to peers using textual evidence** (proving your point using the book).
*   **Fishbowl Discussions:** **A fishbowl discussion involves a small group conversing in the center of the room while an outer circle of students observes.** It's like watching fish in a tank! **Teachers use fishbowl discussions to assess the observation skills of the outer circle of students.**

While you talk, teachers take notes. **Anecdotal notes are brief informal written records of teacher observations during class activities.** **Teachers frequently use anecdotal notes to document student speaking skills during group discussions.** Also, when a teacher asks the whole class a question, they use **wait time**. **Wait time is an assessment technique where the teacher pauses after asking a question to allow students cognitive processing time.** (That just means giving your brain a few seconds to think of the answer).

## Fostering Metacognition and Daily Checks

The best teachers teach you how to check your own work.

**Peer assessment requires students to evaluate each other's work based on a provided rubric.**
**Self-assessment promotes metacognition by requiring students to evaluate their own learning processes.** (Metacognition is a big word that means "thinking about your own thinking!").

At the very end of class, teachers often use **exit tickets**. **Exit tickets are quick formative assessments completed by students at the end of a lesson.** It's like a fast checkout line at the grocery store to prove you learned the day's lesson.

Finally, to test everything at once, teachers use **performance-based assessments**. **Performance-based assessments require students to demonstrate knowledge by creating a product or performing a task.** Instead of a boring test, you might build a podcast or act out a play!
