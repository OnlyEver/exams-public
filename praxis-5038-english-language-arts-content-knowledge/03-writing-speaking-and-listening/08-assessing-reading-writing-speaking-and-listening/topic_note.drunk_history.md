Listen to me, grab your pretzel and look me right in the eyes, because you are about to step into a secondary English classroom, and if you don't understand how to measure what’s going on inside those kids' heads, you’re flying a 747 blindfolded. Teaching ELA isn't just grading papers. It’s a full-blown culinary psychological heist! You are trying to capture the invisible ghosts of reading and writing and force them into the physical world so you can measure them. 

Let’s start in the kitchen. Before you even turn on the stove, you have to open the fridge. That’s your **diagnostic assessment**. You administer these bad boys before a lesson even begins to determine a student's baseline knowledge. You can't bake a cake if you don't know whether you're starting with flour or sawdust. 

Once the cooking starts, you're constantly tasting the soup, right? That is **formative assessment**. It involves ongoing checks of student understanding *during* the learning process. The absolute primary purpose of formative assessment is to inform and adjust your future instruction. If the soup needs salt, you add salt *now*, not next week! Because it’s just a taste test, formative assessments are generally considered low-stakes due to carrying little or no point value toward a final grade. 

But eventually, the restaurant opens. The food critic sits down. You serve the meal. That is **summative assessment**. It evaluates student learning at the end of an instructional unit by comparing it against a standard. There are no do-overs here; summative assessments are considered high-stakes due to having a significant impact on a student's final grade. Your final exams and end-of-unit essays are common examples of summative assessments. 

Now, how is that food critic judging you? If they are using a **criterion-referenced assessment**, they measure your performance against a predetermined set of learning goals. Did the souffle rise? Yes or no. But if they use a **norm-referenced assessment**, they are comparing your performance against the performance of a statistically selected peer group. You didn't just bake a souffle; you baked a better souffle than students in the 85th percentile of bakers nationwide!

No matter what kitchen tool you use, it must have two things: validity and reliability. Think of a meat thermometer. **Validity** refers to the degree to which an assessment actually measures what it claims to measure. If your thermometer measures the *color* of the meat instead of the *temperature*, it’s invalid! **Reliability**, on the other hand, refers to the consistency of the results produced by the instrument over time. If you poke that turkey three times and get 165 degrees, 40 degrees, and 900 degrees, your thermometer has zero reliability. 

When it comes to writing, assessing is wildly subjective. I might love a kid's essay, you might hate it. To stop ourselves from grading on pure "vibes," we use rubrics! **Rubrics increase assessment reliability by providing clear standardized criteria for evaluating subjective student work.** But you've got to pick the right weapon for the job. 

If you want a Yelp review, you use a **holistic rubric**. These provide a single comprehensive score based on an overall impression of a student's performance. It’s fast, but it doesn't tell the kid *why* they got three stars. 

If you want the Michelin Guide inspector’s clipboard, you use an **analytic rubric**. These break down an assignment into specific constituent skills to provide individual scores for each category. They provide way more detailed feedback to students about specific strengths than holistic rubrics, but—and hear my warning, my friend—analytic rubrics take *more time* for teachers to score. You will be grading until midnight. 

When you want to go minimalist, you use a **single-point rubric**. This beauty outlines *only* the target expectations for an assignment down the middle of the page. Why? Because single-point rubrics leave open space on either side of the target expectations for individualized teacher comments. It’s like a custom-tailored suit—room to note exactly where they exceeded expectations or fell short.

When you're building that analytic rubric, you’ll probably use the infinity stones of English: **The Six Plus One Traits of Writing model**. This bad boy evaluates writing based on ideas, organization, voice, word choice, sentence fluency, conventions, and presentation. And when you're looking at that "conventions" trait, do not just hand them a worksheet to circle commas! **Assessing grammar in context** involves evaluating a student's application of grammatical rules within their *own* writing. If they can't use a semicolon while driving their own essay, they don't really know how to use it!

But essays take time. What about the trenches? What about the daily grind? You need a sideline huddle. That’s **conferring**, a formative assessment technique involving a one-on-one conversation between a teacher and a student. 

The architecture of an effective writing conference is like planning a bank heist. It has four phases: research, decision, teaching, and linking. First, during the **research** phase, the teacher asks the student questions to understand their current writing struggles—you're casing the joint! Then, the **decision** phase: you pick your target. Then the **teaching** phase, where you model a strategy to crack the safe. Finally, the **linking** phase, where the student commits to applying the strategy to their ongoing work. 

Now, listen carefully, because rookie teachers fall into a trap here. **Effective writing conferences focus on teaching the writer one or two specific skills.** That's it! **Ineffective writing conferences involve the teacher copyediting the entire student draft.** If you fix all their spelling mistakes for them, you just robbed the bank *for* them! They learn nothing!

To see if they actually become master thieves over the long haul, you use **portfolio assessment**, which involves collecting a student's work over time to demonstrate growth. It's the Rocky training montage. Because writing is developmental, portfolio assessments are frequently used to evaluate writing development throughout a school year. 

Now, let's talk about the silent ghost: Reading. How do you measure something that happens silently inside a skull? 

First, to find their weight class, you use **Informal Reading Inventories**, which are diagnostic assessments used to determine a student's independent reading level. 

To formatively assess them, you pull them into a reading conference, where the teacher typically asks the student to read aloud to assess fluency. While they talk, you become a live sports broadcaster taking **running records**—formative assessments used to evaluate a student's oral reading fluency in real time. If they stumble, you perform a **miscue analysis**, which involves recording the specific errors a student makes while reading aloud. Teachers use miscue analysis to identify a student's specific decoding struggles. Are they guessing based on the first letter? Tripping on a vowel team? The tape doesn't lie!

But decoding isn't comprehension. Parrots can decode! To see if they understand, we set up a linguistic escape room called a **cloze procedure**. This is a reading assessment where words are strategically omitted from a passage. In a cloze procedure, students must fill in the missing words to demonstrate reading comprehension. By doing this, cloze reading assessments evaluate a student's ability to use context clues to decode meaning. 

You can also make them do the director's commentary track. **Think-aloud protocols** require students to verbalize their thought processes while reading. Teachers use think-aloud protocols to formatively assess a student's reading comprehension strategies. Or, just have them recount the plot of the movie! **Retelling** is an assessment strategy where a student orally recounts a story to demonstrate sequencing skills. 

Finally, we have the gladiator arena: Speaking and Listening. 

**Assessing speaking skills** requires evaluating vocal delivery elements like volume and articulation (are they mumbling into their shoes?), but it also requires evaluating physical delivery elements like eye contact and posture. 

But listening? That's sneaky. **Listening assessments often measure a student's ability to summarize information presented orally by peers.** If they can't accurately repeat the argument their classmate just made, they weren't listening, they were just waiting to talk!

To formalize this, we throw them into a **Socratic seminar**. This is a formal discussion method used to assess a student's ability to listen actively. The catch? Socratic seminars are used to assess a student's ability to respond to peers using *textual evidence*. It’s a polite cage match where the only weapon allowed is page numbers! 

Alternatively, use a **fishbowl discussion**, which involves a small group conversing in the center of the room while an outer circle of students observes. It's the Colosseum! Teachers use fishbowl discussions to assess the observation skills of the *outer* circle of students, making sure the audience is analyzing the gladiators. 

While they're talking, you're taking **anecdotal notes**, which are brief informal written records of teacher observations during class activities. Teachers frequently use anecdotal notes to document student speaking skills during group discussions. And when you ask a question to the whole room, you must use **wait time**, an assessment technique where the teacher pauses after asking a question to allow students cognitive processing time. Let the espresso shot pull! Don't rush it!

Eventually, you have to hand the keys to the asylum over to the inmates. **Peer assessment** requires students to evaluate each other's work based on a provided rubric. **Self-assessment** promotes metacognition by requiring students to evaluate their own learning processes—forcing them to look in the mirror and ask *why* their souffle collapsed. 

Before they escape for the day, the bouncer checks their ID: **Exit tickets** are quick formative assessments completed by students at the end of a lesson. 

And at the very end of the year, when you want to see if they can survive the real world, you hit them with **performance-based assessments**, which require students to demonstrate knowledge by creating a product or performing a task. Don't just tell me how to sail the ship—build the boat, navigate the storm, and bring me back the treasure! 

That, my friend, is how you assess an English class! Now, drink your beer, we've got lesson plans to write!
