Listen to me, grab your drink and look me in the eyes, because you are about to walk into a secondary English classroom, but what you’re really walking into is the most chaotic, high-stakes, Michelin-starred kitchen on the planet. You aren’t just a teacher; you are an executive chef trying to teach novice cooks how to butcher a 300-pound bluefin tuna of academic English while the restaurant is actively on fire. If you don't understand the cognitive architecture of how the human brain actually processes and cooks up a new language, you are going to serve raw chicken to the masses! 

To survive the dinner rush, you need to know the godfathers of the kitchen. Back in the late 1970s and early 1980s, Stephen Krashen developed the Monitor Model of second language acquisition. This is your kitchen bible. The Monitor Model of second language acquisition consists of five distinct hypotheses that dictate how the brain digests words. First up, The Acquisition-Learning hypothesis distinguishes between subconscious language acquisition—which is like organically learning how to chop an onion by just feeling the rhythm of the knife—and conscious language learning, which is painfully memorizing the exact angle and physics of the blade from a textbook. Next is The Monitor hypothesis. This posits that consciously learned language rules only serve as an editor to correct output. It’s that little nagging voice in your head going, "Wait, is it 'who' or 'whom'?" before you plate the dish. Then you have The Natural Order hypothesis, which argues that the acquisition of grammatical structures follows a predictable sequence. You can't force a student to master the past-perfect subjunctive if their brain is still just figuring out basic plural nouns—you can't bake the cake before you mix the batter! 

Now, pay attention to the most famous one: Stephen Krashen's Comprehensible Input hypothesis posits that learners acquire language best when receiving input slightly above the learners' current language level. It’s known as the i+1 model. The i+1 model represents language input that is one step beyond a learner's current linguistic competence. You don't hand a kid a copy of *Beowulf* in Old English; you give them something they mostly get, plus a little extra spice they have to reach for. And finally, The Affective Filter hypothesis states that variables such as motivation, self-confidence, and anxiety play a facilitative role in second language acquisition. If Gordon Ramsay is screaming in your face, you freeze! A high affective filter prevents comprehensible input from reaching the language acquisition device. If they are terrified, they learn nothing.

But Krashen wasn't the only health inspector in town. In 1985, Merrill Swain introduced the Output Hypothesis. The Output Hypothesis suggests that producing language forces learners to recognize linguistic gaps. It’s one thing to watch a master chef fold a dumpling; it’s another thing when you try to fold it yourself and it explodes. The Output Hypothesis suggests that producing language helps learners move from semantic processing to syntactic processing—meaning they stop just stringing words together to survive and start actually using correct grammatical structures. Following this, Richard Schmidt proposed the Noticing Hypothesis in 1990. The Noticing Hypothesis suggests that input does not become intake for language learning unless the learner consciously notices the input. They have to see the specific technique you are using, or it just washes over them like background noise.

You have to track your kitchen staff because language acquisition occurs in predictable stages from pre-production to advanced fluency. When they first arrive, they are in the pre-production stage. The pre-production stage of language acquisition features minimal language comprehension and is characterized by a lack of verbal production. We call this the silent period, a phase in early second language acquisition where learners focus on listening and comprehension rather than speaking. Do not force them to talk! They are just absorbing the smells of the kitchen. Next is the early production stage. The early production stage of language acquisition involves limited language comprehension, and students in the early production stage of language acquisition typically produce one-word or two-word responses. "Knife." "Hot." Then they hit the speech emergence stage. The speech emergence stage of language acquisition features good language comprehension, and students in the speech emergence stage of language acquisition use simple sentences with frequent grammatical errors. They are cooking, but they are burning a few pans. After that, the intermediate fluency stage of language acquisition features excellent language comprehension, and students in the intermediate fluency stage of language acquisition use complex sentences with few grammatical errors. Finally, the advanced fluency stage of language acquisition is characterized by a near-native level of speech and comprehension. They are ready for their own cooking show.

But wait! Jim Cummins introduced the distinction between Basic Interpersonal Communicative Skills and Cognitive Academic Language Proficiency in 1979. This is the difference between the front-of-house host and the back-of-house executive chef. Basic Interpersonal Communicative Skills refer to the social, conversational language used in everyday interactions. This is chatting at the lockers. Basic Interpersonal Communicative Skills rely heavily on context clues, gestures, and visual support, and because of that, Basic Interpersonal Communicative Skills typically take English language learners one to three years to develop. But the classroom demands Cognitive Academic Language Proficiency, which refers to the formal, academic language needed for success in school settings. Analyzing themes in literature? That's CALP, and Cognitive Academic Language Proficiency is highly context-reduced and relies primarily on linguistic cues for meaning. There are no hand gestures to save you in a five-paragraph essay! Because it’s so complex, Cognitive Academic Language Proficiency typically takes English language learners five to seven years to fully develop.

How do you get them there? You have to build a bridge, my friend! The Zone of Proximal Development describes the space between what a learner can do independently and what the learner can do with guidance. To get them across that zone, you use scaffolding. Scaffolding involves providing temporary support to a student to help the student achieve a learning goal. You hold their hand while they chop, but eventually, you step back. Scaffolding is gradually removed as a student becomes more independent in a learning task. 

What does this scaffolding look like? It looks like wait time! Wait time involves pausing after asking a question to give students time to process and formulate a response. Don’t rush them; the soup needs to simmer. You use graphic organizers! Graphic organizers help English language learners visualize relationships between concepts, and graphic organizers help English language learners structure thoughts before writing. You hand out sentence frames. Sentence frames provide structured templates that help students articulate thoughts, and critically, sentence frames guide students in using academic language correctly. You want a multi-sensory kitchen! Multimodal instruction uses visual, auditory, and kinesthetic elements to reinforce language comprehension. For example, real-time captioning during video viewing provides dual-modality input for language learners. They hear it and see it! You can also use realia. Realia refers to objects and materials from everyday life used as teaching aids. Bring in an actual train ticket when reading a story about a journey! Realia improves students' understanding of concepts by connecting abstract vocabulary to concrete objects. When they get stuck, use peer tutoring. Peer tutoring pairs students together to practice language skills in a low-stakes, supportive environment. And you must model the madness! Think-alouds model cognitive processes by having a teacher verbalize thoughts while reading a text or solving a problem. 

You also have to stock the pantry with vocabulary. Now, incidental vocabulary learning occurs when students acquire new words naturally through reading and listening—just picking up spices as they go. But sometimes you need explicit vocabulary instruction. Explicit vocabulary instruction involves directly teaching specific words and word-learning strategies. Let's look at the inventory tiers. Tier 1 vocabulary words are basic, everyday words that typically do not require explicit instruction. Words like "dog" or "run." Tier 2 vocabulary words are high-frequency words found across various academic disciplines, like "analyze" or "synthesize." Tier 2 vocabulary words often require explicit instruction for full comprehension. Then you have the weird, exotic spices: Tier 3 vocabulary words are low-frequency, domain-specific words related to a particular subject area, like "iambic pentameter." 

To teach these words, you give them the tools to decipher the recipes. Morphological analysis involves breaking down words into root words, prefixes, and suffixes to determine word meaning. Context clues are hints found within a sentence or passage that a reader can use to understand the meaning of new or unfamiliar words. You also rely on cognate awareness. Cognate awareness is the ability to recognize words in two languages that share a similar meaning, spelling, and pronunciation. "Familia" and "Family!" It’s the exact same ingredient, just a different brand label! Put these words everywhere. Word walls are visual displays of high-frequency or unit-specific vocabulary words in the classroom. Semantic mapping is a visual strategy for vocabulary expansion that displays how words are related to one another. Semantic Feature Analysis uses a grid to help students compare the traits of related vocabulary words. And my absolute favorite tool is The Frayer Model. The Frayer Model is a graphic organizer used for vocabulary building. The Frayer Model prompts students to define a target vocabulary word, prompts students to describe the characteristics of a target vocabulary word, and requires students to provide examples and non-examples of a target vocabulary word. It corners the word like a rat in the pantry and forces it to give up all its secrets!

When you write the menu for the day, you need dual objectives. Content objectives state what students will know and be able to do at the end of a lesson. Language objectives outline the specific language skills students will need to learn to accomplish the lesson's content goals. You have to teach the history of the Civil War *and* the past tense simultaneously! To do this, you use Sheltered Instruction. Sheltered Instruction integrates language and content instruction to make academic content comprehensible for English language learners. The gold standard here is SIOP. The Sheltered Instruction Observation Protocol model provides a framework for organizing and delivering sheltered instruction. The Sheltered Instruction Observation Protocol model was developed by researchers Jana Echevarría, MaryEllen Vogt, and Deborah J. Short. The Sheltered Instruction Observation Protocol model consists of eight interrelated components that ensure your lesson doesn't fall apart like a bad flan. 

There are other wild methods, too. Content and Language Integrated Learning involves teaching subjects such as science or history through a foreign or second language. Communicative Language Teaching emphasizes interaction as both the means and the ultimate goal of language study—get them talking to each other! Task-based Language Teaching focuses on the use of authentic language to complete meaningful tasks, like having them plan a budget for a fake heist. To support this, you use authentic texts. Authentic texts are materials created for native speakers of a language rather than for language teaching purposes. You can also get physical. Total Physical Response was developed by psychologist James Asher in the 1960s. Total Physical Response is a language teaching method that coordinates language and physical movement. You yell "Jump!" and they jump. The verb lives in their muscles!

When they are reading the recipes, mix it up. Extensive reading involves reading large quantities of self-selected, easy-to-understand texts for pleasure. Just gorging on easy books. But intensive reading involves the careful, detailed reading of short texts to extract specific information, and intensive reading involves the detailed reading of short texts to analyze language structures. You break that paragraph down to the molecular level! Use reciprocal teaching. Reciprocal teaching is a reading comprehension strategy where students take turns leading discussions about a text, and reciprocal teaching incorporates the four strategies of predicting, questioning, clarifying, and summarizing. Use the Language Experience Approach. The Language Experience Approach uses students' personal experiences and spoken language as the basis for reading and writing instruction. If they survived a crazy storm, they write about the storm! And use dictogloss! Dictogloss is a classroom dictation activity where learners reconstruct a text rather than copying the text down word for word. Dictogloss encourages students to use grammatical knowledge to recreate the meaning of a spoken text. 

Now, when they are mixing their native language with English, don't panic; it's a feature, not a bug! Translanguaging is the process by which multilingual speakers utilize their entire linguistic repertoire to make meaning and communicate. They are using every tool in their cognitive kitchen. Code-switching is the practice of alternating between two or more languages or varieties of language in conversation. Language transfer occurs when learners apply knowledge from the learners' native language to a second language. Positive language transfer happens when structures in the native language facilitate the acquisition of the second language. But watch out for the burns! Negative language transfer occurs when structures in the native language cause errors in the second language. Because of this, negative language transfer is also known as language interference. As they learn, they create an interlanguage. Interlanguage is the emerging linguistic system developed by a second language learner who has not yet become fully proficient. Within this chaotic middle-ground, overgeneralization in language acquisition occurs when a learner applies a grammatical rule to situations where the rule does not apply. They learn the "-ed" rule and suddenly they "goed" to the store and "eated" an apple! If you don't step in, fossilization occurs when a second language learner's linguistic errors become a permanent part of the learner's language use. It hardens like concrete in the pan!

How do you fix it without raising that affective filter and making them cry into the soup? Corrective feedback! Explicit correction involves a teacher clearly indicating that a student's utterance was incorrect and providing the correct form. Boom, direct, sometimes harsh. Better yet, recasts involve a teacher reformulating a student's incorrect utterance into the correct form without explicitly pointing out the error. "I eated the apple!" "Oh, you ate the apple? That's great!" It’s smooth. Or you use metalinguistic feedback. Metalinguistic feedback involves a teacher providing comments or questions related to the grammatical correctness of a student's utterance. You tap your chin and say, "Is that how we form the past tense for irregular verbs?" You make them fix their own dish! Now finish your drink, tie your apron tight, and get into that classroom—those kids are hungry for fluency!
