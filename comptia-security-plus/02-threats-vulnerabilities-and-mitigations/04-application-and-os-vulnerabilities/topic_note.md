Imagine a [high-speed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed) [manufacturing robot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Industrial_robot) programmed to [assemble](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Assembly_line) [automotive engines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine). It does exactly what it is told, down to the [micrometer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Micrometre), [billions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Billion) of times a [second](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second). But it possesses absolutely zero [common sense](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Common_sense). If a factory worker mistakenly hands the robot a [steel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steel) [bolt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bolt_%28fastener%29) that is ten times too large, the robot will not pause to consider the absurdity of the situation. It will use its immense [hydraulic force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydraulics) to drive that oversized bolt into the [engine block](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engine_block), inevitably shattering the entire [assembly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Assembly_line). 

This is precisely how [computers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer) operate, and it is the fundamental reality you must manage as an [IT professional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information_technology). [Operating systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operating_system) and [applications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Application_software) are immensely powerful, yet entirely [literal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Literal_and_figurative_language). When we study [cybersecurity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_security) [vulnerabilities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vulnerability_%28computing%29), we are not studying "broken" systems; we are studying systems that are obeying flawed [instructions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Machine_code) perfectly. The vulnerabilities we will explore—from [memory corruption](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Memory_corruption) to [web application](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Web_application) [injections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Code_injection)—represent the exact moments where the rigid, literal nature of [computing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computing) collides with the chaotic, malicious ingenuity of [human](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human) [attackers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Threat_actor). 

## The Physics of [Software](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Software): [Memory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_memory) and [Logic Flaws](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logic_error)

To understand how an [attacker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Threat_actor) can [hijack](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exploit_%28computer_security%29) a [server](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Server_%28computing%29) you manage, you must first understand how [software](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Software) stores [data](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_%28computing%29) in [memory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_memory). [Applications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Application_software) [allocate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Memory_management) specific chunks of memory—called [buffers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_buffer)—to hold [variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variable_%28computer_science%29), [user inputs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Input_%28computer_science%29), and operational instructions.

### [Buffer Overflows](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buffer_overflow) and [Integer Overflows](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer_overflow)
A **[buffer overflow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buffer_overflow)** occurs when a [program](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_program) writes more [data](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_%28computing%29) to a [memory block](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Block_%28data_storage%29) than the memory block is [allocated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Memory_management) to hold. Imagine pouring a [gallon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gallon) of [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water) into a [pint glass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pint_glass). The water does not simply vanish; it spills over the sides onto whatever sits next to the glass. In a [computer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer), writing data past the allocated [bounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bounds_checking) of a [buffer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_buffer) can [overwrite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Overwriting) adjacent [memory locations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Memory_address). 

![A conceptual diagram of a buffer overflow, where excess data spills past the allocated bounds of Buffer A, overwriting the adjacent memory space of Buffer B.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Buffer_overflow_basicexample.svg)

Why does this matter to you as a [security professional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_security)? Because adjacent [memory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_memory) often contains the very [instructions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Machine_code) the [CPU](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_processing_unit) is about to process. Overwriting adjacent memory via a [buffer overflow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buffer_overflow) can alter the [execution flow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Control_flow) of a program. [Attackers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Threat_actor) [exploit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exploit_%28computer_security%29) buffer overflows to execute [arbitrary malicious code](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arbitrary_code_execution) on a target system. Even if the attacker's code does not perfectly [execute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Execution_%28computing%29), the [memory corruption](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Memory_corruption) resulting from buffer overflows can cause [system crashes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crash_%28computing%29) and [denial of service](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Denial-of-service_attack) (DoS), rendering your [infrastructure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IT_infrastructure) unavailable.

Sometimes, a [buffer overflow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buffer_overflow) is triggered indirectly through [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics). An **[integer overflow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer_overflow)** occurs when an [arithmetic operation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic) produces a value larger than the maximum representable value of the [data type](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_type). Think of a [car](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Car) [odometer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Odometer) rolling past `999,999` back to `000,000`. If an [application](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Application_software) calculates a [memory allocation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C_dynamic_memory_allocation) size and triggers an integer overflow, it might allocate a tiny [buffer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_buffer) for a massive amount of [data](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_%28computing%29). Consequently, integer overflows can lead to [memory corruption](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Memory_corruption) and subsequent [buffer overflow vulnerabilities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buffer_overflow).

![Mechanical odometers provide a real-world visualization of integer overflow: when a maximum value is exceeded, the counter resets to zero because it lacks the capacity to represent the next higher digit.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Odometer_rollover.jpg)

### [Null Pointer Dereferences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Null_pointer)
[Memory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_memory) is accessed via "[pointers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pointer_%28computer_programming%29)"—[variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variable_%28computer_science%29) that point to specific [memory addresses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Memory_address). A **[null pointer dereference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Null_pointer)** occurs when an [application](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Application_software) attempts to access memory through a [pointer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pointer_%28computer_programming%29) that has a [null value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Null_pointer) (a value of [zero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/0), meaning it points to nothing). When the [CPU](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_processing_unit) tries to read or write to "nowhere," it panics. Null pointer dereferences typically cause the application to [crash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crash_%28computing%29), creating a reliable [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Attack_vector) for [Denial of Service attacks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Denial-of-service_attack).

![Pointers are variables that store the memory addresses of other data. A null pointer dereference occurs if an application attempts to resolve a pointer that points to a memory address of zero.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Pointers.svg)

## Hijacking the [Process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Process_%28computing%29): [Execution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Execution_%28computing%29) and [Timing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timing_attack)

When an [operating system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operating_system) runs an [application](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Application_software), it creates an [isolated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isolation_%28computer_science%29) [process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Process_%28computing%29). [Security](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_security) depends on that [isolation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isolation_%28computer_science%29). However, [attackers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Threat_actor) have developed sophisticated ways to subvert active processes without leaving traces on the [hard drive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hard_disk_drive).

### [Memory Injection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Code_injection)
**[Memory injection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Code_injection)** involves inserting [malicious code](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malware) into the [memory space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Address_space) of a running [process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Process_%28computing%29). Because the [malicious payload](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Payload_%28computing%29) lives solely in [RAM](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Random-access_memory) rather than as a [file](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_file) on the [hard drive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hard_disk_drive), attackers use memory injection techniques to evade file-based [antivirus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antivirus_software) detection. 

Once injected, the [malware](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malware) adopts the [identity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Process_identifier) of the host [process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Process_%28computing%29). [Memory injection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Code_injection) allows [malicious code](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malware) to [execute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Execution_%28computing%29) with the [security privileges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Privilege_%28computing%29) of the compromised process. A common manifestation of this is **[Dynamic Link Library (DLL) injection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DLL_injection)**, a technique where a malicious [DLL](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynamic-link_library) is forced to load into the [address space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Address_space) of a target [process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Process_%28computing%29), subtly hijacking its [functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subroutine).

### [Race Conditions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Race_condition) and [TOCTOU](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time-of-check_to_time-of-use)
Systems process [millions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Million) of [tasks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Task_%28computing%29) a [second](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second), and [timing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time) is everything. A **[race condition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Race_condition)** occurs when the behavior of a [system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_system) depends on the uncontrolled sequence or timing of [events](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Event_%28computing%29). [Attackers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Threat_actor) [exploit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exploit_%28computer_security%29) race conditions to [escalate privileges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Privilege_escalation) or access [unauthorized files](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_file) by slipping [malicious actions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malware) into [microsecond-long](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microsecond) gaps in system operations.

> **[Time-of-check to time-of-use (TOCTOU)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time-of-check_to_time-of-use)** is a specific, highly prevalent type of [race condition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Race_condition) [vulnerability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vulnerability_%28computing%29). 

A [TOCTOU](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time-of-check_to_time-of-use) vulnerability happens when a [system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_system) verifies an [access condition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Access_control) prior to [executing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Execution_%28computing%29) an action. Imagine a [bouncer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bouncer_%28doorman%29) checking your [ID](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Identity_document) at the front door of a [club](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nightclub), after which you walk down a long hallway to the [bar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bar_%28establishment%29). If you hand your 21+ [wristband](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wristband) to an [underage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minor_%28law%29) friend in that hallway, you have [exploited](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exploit_%28computer_security%29) the gap between the check and the use. In a [TOCTOU exploit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time-of-check_to_time-of-use), an [attacker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Threat_actor) alters the validated condition between the [validation step](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_validation) and the [execution step](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Execution_%28computing%29). 

## [Operating System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operating_system) [Vulnerabilities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vulnerability_%28computing%29) & [Authentication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Authentication)

The [Operating System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operating_system) (OS) is the [ground truth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ground_truth) of a [computer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer). If the OS is compromised, every [application](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Application_software) running on top of it is instantly compromised.

At the core of the OS is the [kernel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kernel_%28operating_system%29). **[Operating system kernel vulnerabilities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kernel_%28operating_system%29)** are highly critical because the kernel has unrestricted access to all [system hardware](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_hardware). This is why [patch management](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patch_%28computing%29) is a [cornerstone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cornerstone) of your daily [IT operations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information_technology_operations): **[unpatched](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patch_%28computing%29) [operating systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operating_system)** expose systems to [exploitation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exploit_%28computer_security%29) via publicly known [vulnerabilities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vulnerability_%28computing%29). 

![The operating system kernel serves as the critical bridge between applications and hardware. Exploiting kernel vulnerabilities grants an attacker total control over the system's ground truth.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Kernel_Layout.svg)

Even a fully [patched](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patch_%28computing%29) [OS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operating_system) can be ruined by bad settings. **Misconfigured [operating system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operating_system) [permissions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File-system_permissions)** can allow [unauthorized users](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Authorization) to modify sensitive [system configuration files](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Configuration_file). When [attackers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Threat_actor) [exploit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exploit_%28computer_security%29) these misconfigurations, their goal is usually **[privilege escalation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Privilege_escalation)**—which involves an attacker gaining higher-level [access rights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Access_control) than initially [authorized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Authorization).
*   **[Vertical privilege escalation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Privilege_escalation)** occurs when a lower-privileged [user](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/User_%28computing%29) (like a guest) gains access to functions reserved for [administrators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/System_administrator).
*   **[Horizontal privilege escalation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Privilege_escalation)** occurs when a [user](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/User_%28computing%29) accesses the [data](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_%28computing%29) of another user possessing the same [privilege level](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Privilege_%28computing%29) (e.g., User A accessing User B's private [inbox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Email_box)).

![Privilege escalation allows attackers to bypass normal security boundaries. Vertical escalation grants higher administrative rights, while horizontal escalation accesses peer user data.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Privilege_Escalation_Diagram.svg)

### [Pass-the-Hash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pass_the_hash)
[Authentication systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Authentication) rarely store [passwords](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Password) in [plaintext](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plaintext); they store [cryptographic hashes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cryptographic_hash_function). **[Pass-the-hash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pass_the_hash)** is an [authentication attack](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Authentication) where an [attacker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Threat_actor) extracts a stored [password hash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cryptographic_hash_function) and uses the [hash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hash_function) to [authenticate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Authentication) directly to a [service](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Windows_service). Like presenting a [valet ticket](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Valet_parking) to get a [car](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Car), the [system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_system) only checks if you have the ticket. Therefore, [pass-the-hash attacks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pass_the_hash) bypass the need for an attacker to know the [plaintext](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plaintext) [password](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Password).

![Authentication systems securely store passwords as cryptographic hashes. In a pass-the-hash attack, adversaries bypass the plaintext password entirely by authenticating directly with the extracted hash.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Cryptographic_Hash_Function.svg)

## [Web Application](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Web_application) [Vulnerabilities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vulnerability_%28computing%29): Trusting the Untrustworthy

As an [IT professional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information_technology), you will spend massive amounts of [time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time) securing [web servers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Web_server). [Web applications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Web_application) are inherently exposed; they exist to accept [input](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Input_%28computer_science%29) from strangers on the [internet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internet). 

### [SQL Injection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SQL_injection) (SQLi)
[Databases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Database) are the engines of the [modern web](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Wide_Web). **[SQL injection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SQL_injection) (SQLi)** occurs when an [application](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Application_software) improperly [sanitizes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_sanitization) [user input](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Input_%28computer_science%29) before sending the input to a [database](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Database). Instead of supplying a [name](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Name) or an [email](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Email), the [attacker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Threat_actor) supplies [SQL commands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SQL). 

[Attackers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Threat_actor) use [SQL injection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SQL_injection) to alter the [logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logic) of [database queries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Query_language). A successful SQL injection attack allows [unauthorized viewing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Authorization) of [database contents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Database), ripping away the [confidentiality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information_security) of [millions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Million) of [users](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/User_%28computing%29). Furthermore, SQL injection vulnerabilities allow attackers to [modify](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Create%2C_read%2C_update_and_delete) or [delete](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_erasure) [database records](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Record_%28computer_science%29) entirely.

**Defense:** You defend against [SQLi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SQL_injection) using **[parameterized queries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prepared_statement)**, which prevent SQL injection by treating [user input](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Input_%28computer_science%29) strictly as [data](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_%28computing%29) rather than [executable code](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Executable). If an attacker inputs `DROP TABLE Users`, the parameterized query ensures the [database](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Database) simply [logs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Log_file) a user whose literal name is "DROP TABLE Users" rather than [executing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Execution_%28computing%29) the destructive command.

### [Cross-Site Scripting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross-site_scripting) (XSS)
While [SQLi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SQL_injection) attacks the [database](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Database), [XSS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross-site_scripting) attacks the [user](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/User_%28computing%29). **[Cross-Site Scripting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross-site_scripting) (XSS)** occurs when an [application](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Web_application) includes untrusted data in a [web page](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Web_page) without proper [validation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_validation) or [escaping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Escape_character). XSS attacks [execute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Execution_%28computing%29) [malicious](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malware) [client-side scripts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Client-side_scripting) within the [web browser](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Web_browser) of the victim. Because the [script](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scripting_language) runs in the user's browser, [attackers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Threat_actor) use XSS to steal [session cookies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HTTP_cookie) and [hijack user sessions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Session_hijacking).

![In a Cross-Site Scripting (XSS) attack, malicious client-side scripts are injected to steal sensitive session cookies, allowing the attacker to hijack the victim's session.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Cookie-theft.svg)

There are three primary variants of XSS:
1.  **[Reflected XSS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross-site_scripting):** Involves the immediate echoing of [malicious input](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malware) back to the [browser](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Web_browser) within the [web application response](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HTTP) (often via a manipulated [URL link](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/URL)).
2.  **[Stored XSS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross-site_scripting):** Occurs when a [malicious script](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Client-side_scripting) is permanently saved on the [target web server](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Web_server) (such as in a [forum post](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internet_forum) or [comment section](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blog_comment_hosting_service)), [executing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Execution_%28computing%29) automatically whenever *any* [user](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/User_%28computing%29) views the infected [page](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Web_page).
3.  **[Document Object Model (DOM) based XSS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Document_Object_Model):** Occurs when the [vulnerability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vulnerability_%28computing%29) exists entirely within the [client-side code](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Client-side_scripting), manipulating the [DOM environment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Document_Object_Model) in the user's [browser](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Web_browser) without the [payload](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Payload_%28computing%29) ever reaching the [server](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Server_%28computing%29).

**Defense:** **[Output encoding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Character_encoding)** is a primary [defensive measure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information_security) against [Cross-Site Scripting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross-site_scripting) (XSS) attacks. Output encoding converts [untrusted input](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Input_%28computer_science%29) into a safe form to prevent [browsers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Web_browser) from executing the input as [code](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Source_code) (e.g., converting `<script>` into `&lt;script&gt;`).

### [Forgery Attacks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forgery): [CSRF](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross-site_request_forgery) vs. [SSRF](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Server-side_request_forgery)

Understanding the direction of trust is critical for differentiating [forgery attacks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forgery):

| Attack Type | Mechanism | Exploit Target |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **[Cross-Site Request Forgery (CSRF)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross-site_request_forgery)** | Forces a [logged-in user](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Login) to send a [forged](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forgery) [HTTP request](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HTTP) to a [vulnerable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vulnerability_%28computing%29) [web application](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Web_application). | [CSRF attacks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross-site_request_forgery) exploit the trust a web application has in the [authenticated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Authentication) [browser](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Web_browser) of a [user](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/User_%28computing%29). |
| **[Server-Side Request Forgery (SSRF)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Server-side_request_forgery)** | Occurs when a [web application](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Web_application) fetches a [remote resource](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_network) based on attacker-supplied [URLs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/URL). | [SSRF vulnerabilities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Server-side_request_forgery) allow an [attacker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Threat_actor) to interact with [internal network resources](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intranet) normally protected by a [firewall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Firewall_%28computing%29). |

*In short: [CSRF](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross-site_request_forgery) uses the user's [browser](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Web_browser) as a weapon against the [server](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Server_%28computing%29). [SSRF](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Server-side_request_forgery) uses the [public-facing server](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Web_server) as a weapon against its own [internal network](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intranet).*

### [Directory Traversal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Directory_traversal_attack)
[Web servers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Web_server) are designed to serve [files](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_file) from a specific [root directory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Root_directory). **[Directory traversal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Directory_traversal_attack)** vulnerabilities allow an [attacker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Threat_actor) to read [arbitrary files](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_file) on the [server](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Server_%28computing%29) running an [application](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Application_software) (such as `/etc/passwd` on [Linux](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux)). Directory traversal attacks commonly use dot-dot-slash sequences (`../`) to navigate outside the intended [web document root directory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Root_directory). 

## The Broader Landscape: [OWASP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OWASP) Design Failures

Beyond specific [code flaws](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Software_bug), systemic [architectural and design flaws](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Secure_by_design) plague [applications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Application_software). The [Open Worldwide Application Security Project](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OWASP) (OWASP) categorizes several pervasive issues:

*   **[Broken Access Control](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Access_control):** An [OWASP vulnerability category](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OWASP) where [users](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/User_%28computing%29) can act outside of intended [permissions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File-system_permissions), bypassing [security checks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Security_controls) entirely.
*   **[Cryptographic Failures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cryptography):** Occur when sensitive [application data](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_%28computing%29) is [transmitted](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_transmission) or [stored](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_storage) without proper [encryption](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Encryption), allowing [man-in-the-middle interception](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Man-in-the-middle_attack) or [data breaches](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_breach).
*   **Security Misconfiguration:** Occurs when [security settings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_security) are poorly defined or left at default [vulnerable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vulnerability_%28computing%29) values (such as leaving default [admin passwords](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Default_password) active).
*   **[Software and Data Integrity Failures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_integrity):** Relate to [application infrastructure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IT_infrastructure) that does not protect against [code modification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Source_code), such as failing to verify the [digital signatures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_signature) of [software updates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patch_%28computing%29) before applying them.

### The Ultimate Baseline Defense: [Input Validation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_validation)
If there is a [golden rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden_Rule) in [application security](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Application_security), it is to never trust [user input](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Input_%28computer_science%29). **[Input validation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_validation)** checks whether provided [data](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_%28computing%29) meets specific criteria before [processing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_processing) the data (e.g., ensuring a [phone number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telephone_number) field contains *only* [integers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer) and is exactly 10 [digits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Numerical_digit) long). 

Implementing **strict [input validation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_validation) on the [server side](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Server-side) mitigates many [injection-based](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Code_injection) [application vulnerabilities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vulnerability_%28computing%29).** It stops the oversized [bolt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bolt_%28fastener%29) before the [robotic arm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robotic_arm) can ever grab it, ensuring the [integrity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_integrity), [availability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High_availability), and [confidentiality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information_security) of the [systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_system) you are trusted to [protect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_security).
