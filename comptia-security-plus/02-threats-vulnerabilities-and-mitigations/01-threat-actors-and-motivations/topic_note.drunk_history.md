Listen to me. Stop looking at your phone, look at your pint glass, and listen. You think passing this Security+ exam and configuring a firewall makes you a network defender? Firewalls are just fences! You need to understand the physics of the people climbing over them. 

In this business, a threat actor is any entity responsible for an event that impacts the security of a system or network. It’s not just a blinking red light on your dashboard or a failed ping; it’s a living, breathing human adversary on the other side of the world—or in the cubicle next to you. To actually make sense of this chaos so you don't lose your mind, threat actors are systematically classified by their level of technical sophistication and available financial resources. 

But here’s the kicker. Before any of them even touch your perimeter, they all start from the exact same playbook. Open-Source Intelligence is frequently used by all classifications of threat actors to gather target information before launching an attack. They are reading your company’s LinkedIn profiles to see who the new, gullible intern is. They are checking your public code repositories. They are scoping out the blueprints and stalking the bouncers before they even try to rob the casino. 

To map out this nightmare, you have to split the world in two: the wolves outside the walls, and the wolves already sitting at your dinner table. External threat actors originate from outside the organization's network boundary, while internal threat actors originate from inside the organization's logical or physical network boundary. 

Let’s look at the external threats first, starting at the absolute apex of the food chain. Nation-state actors are highly skilled cyber attackers sponsored by a national government. We're talking James Bond villains with a platinum credit card backed by a sovereign treasury. Nation-state threat actors possess the highest level of resources and funding among all attacker types. They aren't trying to steal your grandma's debit card. The primary motivation for nation-state attackers is typically political espionage or military advantage. They are playing 4D global chess. Nation-state attackers often seek to steal government secrets or proprietary intellectual property from other nations. Why spend twenty years and billions of dollars developing a stealth bomber when you can just steal the blueprints? Furthermore, state-sponsored actors frequently target critical infrastructure to cause national disruption during geopolitical conflicts. They’ll map out a rival country's power grids, financial sectors, and water plants just to hold them hostage during a physical war. 

Because their operations require absolute, invisible patience, Advanced Persistent Threats are most commonly associated with nation-state threat actors. You know what an APT is? An Advanced Persistent Threat maintains long-term undetected access to a compromised network to continuously extract data. It’s like a highly trained spy living inside the drywall of your IT department for a decade, quietly photocopying every document that passes by without ever making a sound!

Right beneath the governments, you have the digital mafia. Organized crime syndicates operate as highly structured groups of professional cybercriminals. They literally have corporate HR departments, paid time off, and 24/7 customer support desks for their victims! The primary motivation for organized crime threat actors is financial gain. It is pure, ruthless capitalism. To squeeze that cash out of the world, organized crime actors frequently deploy ransomware to extort money from targeted organizations. They encrypt your entire database, throw away the key, and demand a massive payout. Because ransomware is so insanely lucrative, organized crime syndicates often possess significant financial resources to purchase undocumented zero-day exploits. They buy the digital equivalent of a skeleton key that seamlessly unlocks any vault!

Wait, who are they buying these exploits from? The arms dealers! Vulnerability brokers are threat actors who discover zero-day vulnerabilities specifically to sell the exploit details to other attackers. They don’t pull the trigger; they just build the gun. A vulnerability broker's primary motivation is direct financial compensation. They find a structural flaw in a major operating system, keep it quiet, and sell it to the highest bidder—usually a nation-state or the mob—for maybe \$500,000!

But what if the attack isn't coming from a government or the mob? What if it's coming from across the street? Competitor threat actors attack rival organizations to gain an unfair market advantage. The primary motivation for competitor threat actors is corporate espionage. Imagine a rival tech startup trying to steal your algorithm. Competitor threat actors actively seek to steal proprietary intellectual property or confidential customer data from business rivals. And if they can't steal from you, they'll just try to break your kneecaps. Competitors sometimes fund external attacks against rivals to disrupt the rival organization's business operations, like secretly paying a mercenary to take down your e-commerce site on Black Friday so your customers flock to them instead!

Now, take away the money, take away the corporate espionage, and what do you have left? Pure, unfiltered ideology. Hacktivists are threat actors driven by political, social, or ideological beliefs. The primary motivation for a hacktivist is to promote a specific cause or publicize a grievance. They want a megaphone and an audience. Because of this, hacktivists frequently use website defacement to spread a political message directly to the public—literally spray-painting their manifesto over your corporate homepage. If that doesn't get your attention, hacktivists often utilize Distributed Denial of Service attacks to disrupt target organizations associated with opposing ideologies. They will flood your servers with so much junk traffic that your network just gives up and chokes!

Finally, at the very bottom of the external threat barrel, you have the amateurs. Unskilled attackers are threat actors who lack deep technical knowledge of cybersecurity. Because they can't code their way out of a wet paper bag, unskilled attackers rely entirely on pre-written scripts and automated tools to launch cyber attacks. Because of this, unskilled attackers are historically referred to in the cybersecurity industry as script kiddies. They aren't backed by governments or the mob; unskilled attackers typically lack external funding and advanced computing resources. The motivation for unskilled attackers often includes bragging rights or simple curiosity. They’re just teenagers rattling the doorknobs of the internet to see what opens, but if you leave a door unlocked, they will absolutely wander in and accidentally set the kitchen on fire!

Okay, take a breath. Take a sip of that beer. Because here is the part that is going to keep you up at night. The perimeter is a lie. The greatest danger might be the guy who pours your coffee in the breakroom! An insider threat is a security risk originating from within the targeted organization. Why are they so deadly? Because insider threats bypass traditional perimeter security controls because the actors already operate inside the network boundary. Your multi-million dollar firewall is useless because insider threats already possess authorized access to the targeted organization's systems and physical facilities. They have the keycards! They have the passwords! And they are everywhere: insider threats include current employees, former employees, and trusted contractors.

Sometimes, the internal destruction is entirely on purpose. The motivation for a malicious insider threat is frequently revenge against an employer. Maybe they got passed over for a promotion, or they know they are getting fired on Friday. In response, malicious insider threats deliberately sabotage corporate systems or steal sensitive data. They will wipe the main servers, delete the backups, and walk out the front door smiling. 

But malice isn't required for a total catastrophe. An unintentional insider threat occurs when an employee accidentally exposes sensitive data through negligence or poor security practices. Bob in accounting falling for a phishing email and accidentally handing his credentials to a Russian syndicate is just as devastating as a coordinated heist!

And then there's the administrative nightmare of rogue tech. Employees typically deploy Shadow IT to bypass corporate restrictions for convenience or perceived efficiency. Shadow IT refers to information technology systems deployed without the explicit knowledge or approval of the organizational IT department. The marketing team thinks the corporate file-sharing limits are "too annoying," so they secretly set up their own unencrypted cloud server. It sounds innocent, but Shadow IT creates severe internal vulnerabilities by bypassing corporate security policies and monitoring. You cannot patch, defend, or monitor a rogue server that you don't even know exists! 

So, look at all these maniacs. When they finally strike, what are their actual operational goals? You can boil their actions down to three catastrophic outcomes:

First, they steal. Data exfiltration is a primary actor motivation involving the unauthorized transfer of sensitive information out of an organization's network. They pack your customer records or military secrets into a digital suitcase and sneak it out the back door. 

Second, they squeeze you. Blackmail and extortion involve threatening to release stolen data or disrupt systems unless the victim pays a ransom. Pay the mob their money, or your company's deepest secrets go live on the dark web tomorrow morning.

Third, they just want to watch the world burn. Disruption as a motivation aims to intentionally halt business operations or take critical systems offline. It’s the hacktivist bringing down your website, or the nation-state turning off the city's power grid. 

That’s the reality of the network, my friend! You aren't just managing routers and updating software patches. You are defending the castle against a literal army of spies, mobsters, furious rivals, and clueless employees! Now finish your drink, we have to go configure some access control lists before someone burns the casino down.
