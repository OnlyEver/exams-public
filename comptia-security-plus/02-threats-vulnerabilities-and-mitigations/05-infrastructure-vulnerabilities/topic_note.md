A bridge may have newly paved [asphalt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asphalt_concrete), but if the [steel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steel) [girders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Girder) beneath are quietly [rusting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rust), the entire structure will inevitably collapse under load. [Digital infrastructure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IT_infrastructure) operates on the exact same physical and logical principles. We spend vast amounts of time polishing the [application layer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Application_layer)—securing [user interfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/User_interface) and tuning [web firewalls](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Web_application_firewall)—while the foundational [hardware](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_hardware), [virtualization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virtualization) layers, and third-party [dependencies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dependency_%28computer_science%29) are often neglected. If an [attacker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Threat_actor) compromises the underlying [host](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Host_%28network%29), the [security](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_security) of the applications running on top of it drops to zero. To defend a [network](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_network), you must intimately understand the [vulnerabilities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vulnerability_%28computing%29) baked into the devices, [hypervisors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypervisor), and [cloud environments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cloud_computing) that form the bedrock of modern computing.

![Rackmount servers in a modern data center. Just as a physical bridge relies on steel girders, digital applications rely on underlying hardware infrastructure that must be continuously secured and maintained.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Wikimedia_Foundation_Servers-8055_35.jpg)

## The Decay of Silicon and Code: Hardware & Legacy Systems

When you unbox a new piece of infrastructure—a [router](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Router_%28computing%29), a [server](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Server_%28computing%29), or an [IoT device](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internet_of_things)—it is inherently vulnerable. **Default system configurations** often prioritize [usability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Usability) over security by keeping unnecessary [network services](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Network_service) enabled, ensuring the device "just works" when plugged in. Similarly, **default hardware configurations** frequently leave easily guessable [administrative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/System_administrator) [credentials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Credential) active upon installation. 

To counteract this out-of-the-box fragility, [systems administrators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/System_administrator) must engage in **[hardening](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hardening_%28computing%29)**. Hardening reduces infrastructure vulnerabilities by disabling unnecessary network services. True hardening involves applying secure configuration [baselines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baseline_%28configuration_management%29) to infrastructure devices and absolutely requires changing default administrative [passwords](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Password) on newly deployed infrastructure devices before they ever touch a production network.

But what happens as that hardware ages? Time is the enemy of security. Hardware and [software](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Software) pass through distinct [lifecycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Software_release_life_cycle) phases that dictate their [risk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Risk_management) profile:

*   **[End of Life (EOL)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/End-of-life_product):** This indicates that a [manufacturer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manufacturing) no longer produces or sells a specific hardware or software product. The product is retired from the catalog, though [support](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Technical_support) may temporarily continue.
*   **[End of Support (EOS)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Technical_support):** This means the manufacturer no longer provides technical support for a specific product. Unpatched security vulnerabilities accumulate in End of Support systems because the [vendor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vendor_%28supply_chain%29) does not release fixes for new [threats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Threat_%28computer%29).
*   **[End of Service Life (EOSL)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/End-of-life_product):** The terminal stage. End of Service Life (EOSL) systems no longer receive [security patches](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patch_%28computing%29) from the original manufacturer. 

![A typical product lifecycle timeline showing the progression from general availability to End of Life (EOL). Operating systems past these milestones stop receiving crucial security patches, rendering them highly vulnerable.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/ProductEndOfLifeCycle.png)

When you discover an EOSL system in your environment, your immediate instinct should be to replace it. However, reality is rarely that simple. You will frequently encounter **[legacy systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legacy_system)**, which are outdated computing technologies that remain in operation because they perform essential [business functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Business_function). 

Why do organizations keep them? First, upgrading legacy systems is often difficult due to strict software dependencies on outdated [application frameworks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Application_framework). If a critical [manufacturing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manufacturing) control program only runs on [Windows Server 2003](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Windows_Server_2003), upgrading the [OS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operating_system) breaks the factory. Second, the high financial cost of replacing specialized hardware often forces organizations to continue using legacy systems.

![An ATM running the unsupported Windows XP operating system in 2018. Organizations frequently maintain highly vulnerable legacy systems in production because they perform specialized or critical business functions.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Windows_XP_sighted_'in_the_wild'_on_a_cash_point%2C_3_August_2018.jpg)

Because you cannot patch these systems, you must isolate them. **[Network segmentation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Network_segmentation)** reduces the risk of legacy system compromise by isolating vulnerable devices from the rest of the network, ensuring that if they are breached, the attacker cannot pivot to modern systems.

### Beneath the Operating System: Firmware Vulnerabilities
Below the [operating system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operating_system) lies the [firmware](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Firmware). **Firmware vulnerabilities** exist in the low-level [code](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Source_code) that controls the basic functions of hardware devices. Because this code sits below the OS, [malware](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malware) residing in firmware is nearly invisible to traditional [antivirus software](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antivirus_software). 

![A ROM BIOS chip on a motherboard. Vulnerabilities residing in this low-level firmware bypass operating system controls, making them uniquely dangerous and difficult to detect.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/AMI_486DX_EISA_BIOS_20051109.jpg)

Updating hardware firmware requires specific vendor patches to resolve low-level security flaws. However, if the device has aged out, **end of life firmware** cannot receive security updates to address newly discovered hardware vulnerabilities. This leaves the hardware permanently exposed to attackers who understand its lowest-level [instruction sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Instruction_set_architecture). 

Worse still are **[zero-day vulnerabilities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero-day_%28computing%29)**—software flaws that are completely unknown to the original software vendor. Because the vendor is unaware of the flaw, zero-day vulnerabilities currently lack any available official security patch from the [developer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Software_developer). Defending against zero-days requires robust [defense-in-depth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Defense_in_depth_%28computing%29), as no patch can save you from a flaw nobody knows exists.

## The Phantom Perils: Virtualization Vulnerabilities

To maximize hardware efficiency, we use [virtualization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virtualization). A **[hypervisor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypervisor)** is the software responsible for creating and running [virtual machines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virtual_machine) on physical hardware. Think of the hypervisor as an apartment building manager, and the Virtual Machines (VMs) as the individual apartments. 

In a properly functioning environment, the tenants cannot access each other's apartments. However, **inadequate isolation** between virtual machines can lead to unauthorized access to resources across a virtualized environment. 

The nightmare scenario for any systems administrator is a **[Virtual Machine (VM) escape](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virtual_machine_escape)**. A VM escape occurs when an attacker breaks out of an isolated guest operating system to interact directly with the host hypervisor. A successful VM escape allows an attacker to potentially compromise all other virtual machines running on the same physical host. If the hypervisor falls, the entire infrastructure falls. Therefore, regularly patching the hypervisor software is the primary mitigation against Virtual Machine escape vulnerabilities.

![A logical diagram distinguishing between Type-1 (bare-metal) and Type-2 (hosted) hypervisors. A Virtual Machine escape occurs when an attacker breaks the isolation boundary between the virtual environment and the underlying hypervisor.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Hyperviseur.svg)

Virtualization also introduces unique [data retention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_retention) risks. **[Data remnants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_remanence)** in virtualization occur when a deleted virtual machine's [storage space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_data_storage) is reallocated without being securely wiped. If a new VM spins up and claims that physical disk space, a clever attacker could scrape the unallocated space to recover passwords or [cryptographic keys](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cryptographic_key) left behind by the previous tenant.

## Someone Else's Computer: Cloud Vulnerabilities

The [cloud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cloud_computing) is simply virtualization deployed at an industrial scale. The convenience of the cloud masks its immense complexity, and misconfigurations are rampant. 

Consider storage. **[Cloud storage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cloud_storage) misconfigurations** are a leading cause of [data breaches](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_breach) due to unintentional public exposure of sensitive data. An administrator toggles a single setting incorrectly on an [AWS S3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amazon_S3) bucket, and suddenly a [database](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Database) of proprietary customer information is indexed by public [search engines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Search_engine).

### APIs and Access Control
Cloud environments run on **[Application Programming Interfaces (APIs)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/API)**, which allow different software components to communicate and share data in cloud environments. APIs are the connective tissue of modern infrastructure. 

*   **Authentication Failures:** **Insecure cloud APIs** without proper [authentication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Authentication) expose backend cloud infrastructure to unauthorized access. If an API accepts commands without verifying *who* is sending them, it acts as an unlocked [backdoor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Backdoor_%28computing%29).
*   **Rate Limiting Failures:** Cloud APIs lacking [rate limiting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rate_limiting) are highly vulnerable to automated [brute-force attacks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brute-force_attack) and [resource exhaustion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Denial-of-service_attack). An attacker can hammer the API with millions of requests a second, either cracking a password or crashing the service.

Behind the APIs lies the logic of permissions. **[Identity and Access Management](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Identity_management) (IAM) misconfigurations** in cloud environments often result from assigning overly permissive roles to [users](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/User_%28computing%29). But humans aren't the only entities needing permissions. Assigning overly broad IAM permissions to automated cloud applications creates significant security vulnerabilities. If an automated [script](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scripting_language) that only needs to read a database is given "Full Admin" privileges, a compromise of that script grants the attacker total control over the environment.

### The Financial Attack Vector and Multi-Tenancy
The cloud is designed to [scale infinitely](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalability) based on demand. While great for handling a sudden surge in legitimate traffic, cloud elasticity can be exploited in a **Denial of Wallet** attack by triggering automatic resource scaling that incurs massive financial costs. An attacker sends bogus traffic, your [auto-scaler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autoscaling) spins up fifty new expensive servers to handle it, and you are left with a \$100,000 monthly bill.

![Diagram illustrating horizontal versus vertical scaling. Cloud elasticity relies on automated horizontal scaling, which attackers can maliciously trigger to cause massive financial strain in a Denial of Wallet attack.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Horizontal_vs_vertical_scaling.svg)

Finally, remember that the [public cloud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_cloud) is a shared environment. **[Multi-tenancy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multitenancy)** in public clouds introduces the risk of cross-tenant attacks if strict logical isolation fails. Just as with localized VM escape, a flaw in [AWS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amazon_Web_Services), [Azure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microsoft_Azure), or [GCP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Google_Cloud_Platform) could theoretically allow an attacker operating their own cloud instance to peek into yours.

## The Trojan Horse: Supply Chain Vulnerabilities

You can build a fortress, but if you buy your locks from a compromised locksmith, your walls are useless. A **[supply chain attack](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supply_chain_attack)** compromises a primary target by exploiting vulnerabilities in the target's third-party vendors. 

Because large [enterprises](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Business) have massive security budgets, attackers often go after the smaller companies that service them. A supply chain attack can leverage vulnerabilities in an organization's managed service providers. **[Managed Service Providers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Managed_services) (MSPs)** are frequent targets of supply chain attacks because their access to multiple client networks provides a high-value pivot point. Why hack fifty individual companies when you can hack the single [IT](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information_technology) firm that holds the administrative keys to all fifty?

### Poisoning the Well: Software and Hardware
Supply chain attacks occur in both the digital and physical realms:

*   **Software Supply Chain:** These attacks involve injecting malicious code into trusted [open-source](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_source) [libraries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Library_%28computing%29). Developers unknowingly pull this corrupted code into their applications. Consequently, software supply chain attacks can compromise the distribution of legitimate software updates. The infamous [SolarWinds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SolarWinds_hack) breach was exactly this—a trusted update mechanism turned into a malware distribution network.
*   **Hardware Supply Chain:** Hardware supply chain vulnerabilities include the insertion of malicious [microchips](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integrated_circuit) into devices before final delivery. Alternatively, hardware supply chain attacks can involve installing compromised firmware onto devices during the manufacturing process. By the time the server arrives at your loading dock in shrink-wrap, it is already compromised.

### Supply Chain Defenses
How do we trust what we buy? First, we demand transparency. A **[Software Bill of Materials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Software_bill_of_materials) (SBOM)** documents all open-source and third-party components within a specific application. An SBOM helps security teams identify hidden supply chain vulnerabilities within application dependencies. If a new vulnerability is announced in a obscure [logging](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logging_%28software%29) library, you check your SBOM to see if your software relies on it.

To ensure files haven't been tampered with, we rely on [cryptography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cryptography). **[Cryptographic code signing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Code_signing)** helps mitigate software supply chain attacks by verifying the authenticity of software updates. Furthermore, cryptographic code signing guarantees the [integrity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_integrity) of an application file to ensure the file was not altered in transit. If the [digital signature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_signature) doesn't match the developer's [public key](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public-key_cryptography), the system refuses to install the update.

![Diagram showing how digital signatures function. Cryptographic code signing ensures software supply chain integrity by allowing the receiver to mathematically verify the update's origin and confirm it was not tampered with.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Private_key_signing.svg)

Finally, organizations utilize **Vendor Risk Management** programs. These programs assess the [cybersecurity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_security) posture of third-party suppliers to mitigate supply chain risks before contracts are even signed. 

## The Enemy Within: Shadow IT

You cannot protect what you cannot see. **[Shadow IT](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shadow_IT)** involves employees using unapproved cloud services without the knowledge of the IT department. A [marketing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marketing) team frustrated by corporate bureaucracy might independently purchase a cloud [file-sharing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File_sharing) service to collaborate with external vendors. Shadow IT also includes the deployment of unauthorized hardware devices on the corporate network, like a developer plugging a personal, unpatched [Wi-Fi router](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wireless_router) into a sensitive [subnet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subnetwork).

![A consumer-grade wireless router. Plugging unauthorized hardware into a corporate subnet introduces major vulnerabilities by completely circumventing established security architectures and monitoring tools.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/D-Link_DI-524.jpg)

Shadow IT introduces infrastructure vulnerabilities by completely bypassing organizational security controls. Because these systems are unvetted, they do not undergo hardening, they lack proper IAM controls, and they are not tracked for EOL/EOS status. Ultimately, Shadow IT creates blind spots in network visibility for security [monitoring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Network_monitoring) teams, rendering millions of dollars of security [architecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Network_architecture) blind to the rogue infrastructure operating right beneath it.
