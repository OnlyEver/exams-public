When an [architect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Architect) designs a [suspension bridge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suspension_bridge), they do not simply start [welding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Welding) [steel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steel) over a river. They construct a [mathematical model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_model), translating physical tension, wind resistance, and [gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity) into a [system of equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/System_of_equations). A [mathematical model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_model) functions exactly like a [blueprint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blueprint). It strips away the chaotic noise of reality—the [rust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rust), the changing weather, the traffic delays—and isolates the essential mechanisms governing a system. 

In a real-world [mathematical model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_model), the [input variable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dependent_and_independent_variables) of a [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_%28mathematics%29) represents the [independent quantity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dependent_and_independent_variables)—the condition we measure, control, or observe unfolding, such as time elapsed or items produced. The [output variable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dependent_and_independent_variables) of a [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_%28mathematics%29) represents the [dependent quantity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dependent_and_independent_variables), changing in response to the input. For your future [middle school](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_school) students, the world is a chaotic place. Teaching them to analyze [mathematical models](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_model) is teaching them to find the hidden architecture beneath a [cell phone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mobile_phone) data plan, the [trajectory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trajectory) of a tossed [basketball](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basketball), or the compounding spread of a [viral video](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viral_video). 

![The mathematical function can be visualized as a machine: it takes a specific input variable, applies a consistent mathematical rule, and produces a corresponding output.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Function_machine2.svg)

## The Language of Models: Function Notation

To effectively read these mathematical [blueprints](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blueprint), we must first master the language they are written in. Many students enter [middle school](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_school) viewing the [equals sign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equals_sign) merely as a command to "compute the answer." [Function notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_notation) shifts this paradigm, allowing us to describe complex relationships with high precision.

**[Function notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_notation) $f(x)$ represents the specific output value of a [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_%28mathematics%29) named $f$ corresponding to an input value of $x$.** It is not [multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication). It is a [mapping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Map_%28mathematics%29). 

![Function notation describes a strict mapping, where each element in the input domain is uniquely assigned to a corresponding output value.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Function_color_example_3.svg)

When we write an [equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equation) in the form $f(a) = b$, it signifies that an input of $a$ produces an output of $b$ within the given mathematical context. If $C(t)$ models the cost in [dollars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_dollar) of renting a facility for $t$ hours, the statement $C(4) = 150$ is a complete, self-contained sentence: "Renting the facility for 4 hours costs \$150." The notation encapsulates both the independent and dependent quantities in a single, readable expression.

## The Dials and Knobs: Understanding Parameters

If [variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variable_%28mathematics%29) $x$ and $y$ are the moving parts of our machine, [parameters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parameter) are the settings on the control board. **[Parameters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parameter) in a [mathematical model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_model) are [constants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constant_%28mathematics%29) that define the specific characteristics and boundaries of a [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_%28mathematics%29) for a particular scenario.** While [variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variable_%28mathematics%29) change continuously *within* a specific situation, [parameters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parameter) remain locked in place for that situation, dictating how the [variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variable_%28mathematics%29) interact. 

Let's look at the two foundational models you will teach: [linear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_function) and [exponential functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponential_function).

### Linear Models: $f(x) = mx + b$
[Linear models](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_function) describe relationships built on constant [addition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Addition) or [subtraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subtraction). 
*   The [parameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parameter) $b$ in the [linear model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_function) $f(x) = mx + b$ represents the initial value of the output when the input quantity is exactly zero. If you are tracking a [fundraiser](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundraising), this might be your starting seed money before any sales are made.
*   The [parameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parameter) $m$ in the [linear model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_function) $f(x) = mx + b$ represents the constant [rate of change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rate_%28mathematics%29) of the output with respect to the input. It is the steady heartbeat of the model—how much the dependent variable shifts for every single-unit step of the independent variable.

![Linear models are defined by a constant average rate of change, meaning the dependent variable shifts by the exact same amount for every unit step of the independent variable.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Linear_Function_Graph.svg)

### Exponential Models: $f(x) = a(b)^x$
[Exponential models](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponential_function) describe relationships built on constant [multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication), commonly seen in [population growth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Population_growth), [compound interest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compound_interest), or [radioactive decay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radioactive_decay).
*   The [parameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parameter) $a$ in the [exponential model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponential_function) $f(x) = a(b)^x$ represents the initial amount of the dependent variable when the input is zero.
*   The [parameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parameter) $b$ in the [exponential model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponential_function) $f(x) = a(b)^x$ represents the constant multiplier or [growth factor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponential_growth) per unit of the input variable. If $b = 1.05$, the quantity is growing by 5% per unit of input; if $b = 0.5$, the quantity is halving.

![Exponential growth, such as the replication of a bacterial colony, relies on a constant multiplier rather than constant addition, resulting in a rapidly accelerating curve.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/E.coli-colony-growth.gif)

| Model Type | Formula | Initial Value Parameter | Growth Parameter | Mechanism of Growth |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **[Linear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_function)** | $f(x) = mx + b$ | $b$ | $m$ | Constant [addition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Addition)/[subtraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subtraction) |
| **[Exponential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponential_function)** | $f(x) = a(b)^x$ | $a$ | $b$ | Constant [multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication) |

## The Pulse of the Function: Average Rate of Change

How fast is a system evolving? To answer this, we look at the [rate of change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rate_%28mathematics%29). 

> The **[average rate of change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Difference_quotient)** of a [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_%28mathematics%29) $f(x)$ over a [closed interval](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interval_%28mathematics%29) $[a, b]$ is calculated using the formula:
> $$\frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b - a}$$

![The average rate of change over an interval is calculated as the change in the dependent variable (vertical axis) divided by the change in the independent variable (horizontal axis), representing the slope of the secant line.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Wiki_slope_in_2d.svg)

This formula is simply the change in output divided by the change in input. Consequently, the units for an [average rate of change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Difference_quotient) are always the units of the output variable divided by the units of the input variable (e.g., [dollars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_dollar) per hour, miles per gallon, [bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacteria) per day).

Interpreting the result of this calculation provides immediate insight into the behavior of the model over that specific interval:
*   A **positive** [average rate of change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Difference_quotient) over an interval indicates that the function's output increases overall from the start of the interval to the end.
*   A **negative** [average rate of change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Difference_quotient) over an interval indicates that the function's output decreases overall from the start of the interval to the end.
*   An [average rate of change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Difference_quotient) of **zero** over an interval indicates that the function's starting output and ending output for that interval are identical. (Note: The function may have fluctuated wildly in between, but the net change is zero).

The defining characteristic of [linear functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_function) is that they have an identical, constant [average rate of change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Difference_quotient) across any chosen interval of their [domain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_of_a_function). Whether you measure the [slope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slope) between $x=1$ and $x=2$, or between $x=10$ and $x=100$, the result is exactly the same: the [parameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parameter) $m$. 

In contrast, [non-linear functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonlinear_system) (like exponential or [quadratic models](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadratic_function)) have [average rates of change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Difference_quotient) that vary depending on the specific interval chosen. The growth [accelerates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acceleration) or decelerates as the input progresses.

## Thresholds of the Model: Understanding Intercepts

Intercepts are the critical thresholds where [mathematical models](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_model) cross the [axes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system), translating to powerful real-world milestones.

### The y-intercept
The [y-intercept](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Y-intercept) of a [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_%28mathematics%29) is the single output value generated when the input variable is equal to exactly zero. Because [functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_%28mathematics%29) can only have one output for any given input, there can be at most one [y-intercept](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Y-intercept). In contextual time-based models, the [y-intercept](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Y-intercept) typically represents the starting point or initial measurement of a scenario before any [time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time) elapses. It is the "$t=0$" moment.

### The x-intercepts
The [x-intercepts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-intercept) of a [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_%28mathematics%29) are the specific input values that result in an output of exactly zero. Unlike the [y-intercept](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Y-intercept), a [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_%28mathematics%29) can have multiple [x-intercepts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-intercept). In contextual physical models, an [x-intercept](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-intercept) often represents the specific time or condition when a quantity is fully depleted (a [bank account](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bank_account) hitting \$0) or reaches ground level (a falling object hitting the earth).

![The graph of a quadratic polynomial clearly displays its key thresholds: the single $y$-intercept where the input is zero, and the $x$-intercepts where the output crosses the axis to equal exactly zero.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Polynomialdeg2.svg)

## Arcs, Gravity, and Quadratics

When an object is thrown, launched, or dropped, its [trajectory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trajectory) is governed by [gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity). This behavior cannot be modeled linearly (because [gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity) [accelerates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acceleration) the object) nor exponentially. It requires a [quadratic model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadratic_function).

The standard form of a [quadratic function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadratic_function) is $f(x) = ax^2 + bx + c$. Here, the [parameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parameter) $c$ specifies the exact [y-coordinate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system) of the [y-intercept](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Y-intercept). If you evaluate $f(0)$, the $ax^2$ and $bx$ terms vanish, leaving only $c$. 

To find the [x-intercepts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-intercept) of a [quadratic model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadratic_function), we must determine when the output is zero. The [x-intercepts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-intercept) of a [quadratic model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadratic_function) can be determined by setting the [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_%28mathematics%29) equal to zero and solving using [factoring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Factorization), [completing the square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Completing_the_square), or the [quadratic formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadratic_formula). 

A [quadratic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadratic_function) [mathematical model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_model) will display zero, one, or two [real](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number) [x-intercepts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-intercept) depending entirely on the value of the equation's [discriminant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discriminant) ($b^2 - 4ac$). 
*   If the [discriminant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discriminant) is positive, there are two real [x-intercepts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-intercept).
*   If the [discriminant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discriminant) is zero, there is exactly one real [x-intercept](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-intercept) (the [vertex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_%28curve%29) rests on the x-axis).
*   If the [discriminant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discriminant) is negative, there are zero real [x-intercepts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-intercept) (the curve never crosses the axis).

### Modeling Projectile Motion

One of the most profound applications of [quadratic functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadratic_function) your students will encounter is the [projectile motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projectile_motion) model. This model predicts the height $h$ of an object at any given time $t$. 

In a [quadratic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadratic_function) [projectile motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projectile_motion) model $h(t) = -16t^2 + v_0t + h_0$, the [parameters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parameter) carry highly specific physical meanings:
*   The [parameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parameter) $h_0$ represents the initial height of the object in [feet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foot_%28unit%29). This is the [y-intercept](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Y-intercept).
*   The [parameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parameter) $v_0$ represents the initial vertical [velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity) of the object in [feet per second](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foot_per_second). 

But what about the [leading coefficient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coefficient)? The [constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constant_%28mathematics%29) $-16$ in the standard [English-unit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_units) [projectile motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projectile_motion) [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_%28mathematics%29) $h(t) = -16t^2 + v_0t + h_0$ is derived from half the [acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acceleration) due to [Earth's gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity_of_Earth) measured in [feet per second squared](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foot_per_second_squared). Earth's [gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity) pulls objects downward at approximately $32 \text{ ft/s}^2$. The [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics) [equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equation) for position relies on $\frac{1}{2}gt^2$, and half of $-32$ is $-16$. 

If you are teaching in a [science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Science) context, you will likely use the [metric system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_system). The [constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constant_%28mathematics%29) $-4.9$ in the standard metric-unit [projectile motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projectile_motion) [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_%28mathematics%29) $h(t) = -4.9t^2 + v_0t + h_0$ is derived from half the [acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acceleration) due to [Earth's gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity_of_Earth) measured in [meters per second squared](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metre_per_second_squared) ([gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity) is $9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$, and half of that is $4.9$).

When we set this projectile [equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equation) to zero and solve for the [x-intercepts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-intercept), we are asking the model: *"At what time $t$ is the height $h$ equal to zero?"* In almost all real-world contexts, one solution will be negative (representing a theoretical time before the launch, which we discard as outside the practical [domain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_of_a_function)) and one will be positive. The positive [x-intercept](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-intercept) of a [quadratic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadratic_function) [projectile motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projectile_motion) model represents the total amount of time the object remains in the air before hitting the ground.

![The parabolic arc of projectile motion is modeled using quadratic equations. The positive x-intercept of this model dictates the total flight time before the object hits the ground.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Ideal_projectile_motion_for_different_angles.svg)

By mastering these [parameters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parameter), rates, and intercepts, you equip your students not just to pass an exam, but to mathematically read the [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics) and phenomena of the world around them.
