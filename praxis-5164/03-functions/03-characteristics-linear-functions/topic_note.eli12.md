When you stare at a math graph, you might just see a frozen picture on a piece of paper. But it's actually a snapshot of something moving! It could be a draining health bar in a video game or your allowance growing in a piggy bank. When something changes at a steady, perfectly predictable pace, it draws a straight line on a graph. By figuring out how steep that line is, where it starts, and where it stops, you can actually predict the future of whatever that line represents. Let's learn how to read the secrets of straight lines!

## The Anatomy of Constant Change: Slope

At the very heart of a straight line is its "slope." 

The slope of a linear function represents the constant rate of change between the dependent variable (like your total money) and the independent variable (like the number of weeks that have passed). 

In fancy math terms, the slope of a linear function represents the marginal change in the output for each one-unit change in the input. In everyday words, that just means how much your total changes every time you take one single step forward. If you get 3 extra lives for every level you beat, the marginal change is 3 lives per 1 level. 

The slope tells you the angle of the line and which way it's pointing:

*   A positive slope indicates that the value of the dependent variable increases as the independent variable increases. (Like earning XP points in a game: as time goes on, your points go up, making the line go up and to the right!)
*   A negative slope indicates that the value of the dependent variable decreases as the independent variable increases. (Like eating slices of pizza: as the minutes tick by, the amount of pizza left goes down, making the line drop.)
*   A horizontal line on a coordinate plane has a slope exactly equal to zero. This means nothing changes at all. If a video game always costs \$50 no matter what year it is, the line stays completely flat.
*   A vertical line on a coordinate plane has an undefined slope. Since there is zero side-to-side movement, finding the slope would mean dividing by zero, which breaks the rules of math! 

### Calculating Slope Across Multiple Representations

You can figure out the slope whether you are looking at a picture, a math equation, or a chart. 

**From a Graph:**
If you are looking at a picture of a line, the slope of a graphed line is defined as the vertical change divided by the horizontal change. You might hear your teacher call this "rise over run." You simply count how many steps you go up or down, and divide it by how many steps you go sideways.

**From Two Points:**
If you only know the coordinates of two dots on a line, you can use a formula! The formula to calculate the slope given two points (x1, y1) and (x2, y2) is m = (y2 - y1) / (x2 - x1). 

Here is how you do it step-by-step:
1. Pick your two points. Let's use (1, 4) and (3, 10).
2. Subtract your y-values (the second numbers): 10 - 4 = 6.
3. Subtract your x-values (the first numbers) in the same order: 3 - 1 = 2.
4. Divide the y-difference by the x-difference: 6 / 2 = 3. Your slope is 3!

**From a Table:**
How do you know if a chart of numbers will draw a straight line? A data table represents a linear function if the ratio of the change in y to the change in x is constant across all data pairs. 

To calculate the slope from a data table, divide the difference between any two y-values by the difference between their corresponding x-values. 

Let's try it step-by-step:
1. Pick any two rows from a table, for example: an input (x) of 2 gives an output (y) of 8, and an input (x) of 5 gives an output (y) of 17. 
2. Find the difference in the y-values: 17 - 8 = 9.
3. Find the difference in the x-values: 5 - 2 = 3.
4. Divide: 9 / 3 = 3. The slope is 3! No matter which two rows you pick from a linear table, you will always get the exact same answer.

## Intercepts: The Anchors of a Mathematical Model

If the slope tells us *how* things are changing, the intercepts tell us *where* the story starts and ends. They anchor the math to real life.

### The Y-Intercept
The y-intercept of a graph is the exact point where the line intersects the y-axis (the vertical line that goes straight up and down on your paper). Because this point is locked onto that center line, the x-coordinate of any y-intercept is always exactly zero. 

*   **Algebraic Calculation:** To calculate the y-intercept of an equation algebraically, substitute zero for the x-variable and solve for the y-variable.
    1. Take an equation like y = 4x + 10.
    2. Swap the x for a zero: y = 4(0) + 10.
    3. Multiply: 4 times 0 is 0. 
    4. Add: 0 + 10 = 10. The y-intercept is 10!
*   **Modeling Context:** In mathematical models, the y-intercept typically represents the initial value or starting amount of the dependent variable. If you start a new racing game with 50 bonus coins before you even drive, that starting number of 50 is your y-intercept. 

### The X-Intercept
The x-intercept of a graph is the exact point where the line intersects the x-axis (the horizontal line that goes left to right across your paper). Because it sits flat on that center line, the y-coordinate of any x-intercept is always exactly zero.

*   **Algebraic Calculation:** To calculate the x-intercept of an equation algebraically, substitute zero for the y-variable and solve for the x-variable.
    1. Take an equation like 2x + 6 = y.
    2. Swap the y for a zero: 2x + 6 = 0.
    3. Subtract 6 from both sides to get the x-part alone: 2x = -6.
    4. Divide by 2: x = -3. The x-intercept is -3!
*   **Modeling Context:** In mathematical models, the x-intercept often represents the point in time or the condition when the dependent variable reaches a value of zero. If your phone battery is dropping steadily, the x-intercept is the exact minute your battery hits 0% and your phone dies!

## Proportional Relationships: The Origin of Scaling

There is a super special type of line you will see a lot in middle school. 

A proportional relationship is a specific type of linear function that always passes through the origin. The origin point on a standard coordinate plane is the ordered pair (0, 0). That is the exact dead-center of your graph! 

In real life, the point (0, 0) on the graph of a proportional relationship indicates that an input of zero produces an output of zero. If you buy zero candy bars, you pay exactly zero dollars. 

The equation for a proportional relationship is written in the form y = kx. Notice there is no "+ anything" at the end, because it starts exactly at zero!

In the proportional equation y = kx, the variable k represents the constant of proportionality. Because this type of line starts at zero, the constant of proportionality in a proportional relationship is mathematically equal to the slope of the line. They are the exact same number!

### The Unit Rate
When you deal with proportional things, you will often hear about the "unit rate." The unit rate of a proportional relationship describes the amount of the dependent variable associated with exactly one unit of the independent variable. For example, if 4 concert tickets cost \$80, the unit rate is what just *one* ticket costs (\$20). 

Just like we learned earlier, the unit rate of a proportional relationship is equivalent to the constant of proportionality. 

**Finding the Unit Rate:**
1.  **Look at the number 1:** On the graph of a proportional relationship, the y-coordinate of the point (1, y) represents the unit rate. If you go right to the number 1 on the bottom of the graph and look straight up to the line, whatever y-number you land on is your unit rate!
2.  **Use any point on the line:** For any point (x, y) on the graph of a proportional relationship, the ratio y/x equals the unit rate. 
    1. Find a point, like (3, 15). 
    2. Put the y over the x: 15 / 3.
    3. Divide them: 15 / 3 = 5. Your unit rate is 5!

## Parallel and Perpendicular Systems

When you draw more than one straight line on the same piece of paper, the way they act around each other is all about their slopes.

*   **Parallel Lines:** Parallel lines on a coordinate plane have identical slopes. Think of a train track: the two rails go in the exact same direction, at the exact same angle. Because their slopes are perfectly identical, they will never crash into each other! 
*   **Perpendicular Lines:** Perpendicular lines on a coordinate plane have slopes that are negative reciprocals of one another. That means they cross to make a perfect plus-sign (a 90-degree corner). 
    1. To find a negative reciprocal, start with a slope, like 3/4.
    2. Flip the fraction upside down: 4/3.
    3. Change the sign. Since it was positive, make it negative: -4/3.

| **Standard Form** | Ax + By = C | In the standard form of a linear equation Ax + By = C, the slope of the line is equal to -A/B.<br><br>In the standard form of a linear equation Ax + By = C, the y-coordinate of the y-intercept is equal to C/B.<br><br>In the standard form of a linear equation Ax + By = C, the x-coordinate of the x-intercept is equal to C/A. |
| **Point-Slope Form** | y - y1 = m(x - x1) | The point-slope form of a linear equation is written as y - y1 = m(x - x1).<br><br>In the point-slope form y - y1 = m(x - x1), the variables x1 and y1 represent the coordinates of a specific known point on the line. ### Summary for Praxis 5164 Application
When you approach a test or a tricky word problem about straight lines, play detective and look for hidden clues in the words. If a story mentions an object hitting "the ground" or a tank being "empty," it is secretly testing your knowledge of the x-intercept (where y is exactly zero). If the story mentions an "initial fee" or "starting balance," it is testing the y-intercept (where x is exactly zero). If the problem says something happens "per unit" or "each week," it is handing you the slope. Once you learn to translate these normal words into math, you will see the geometry of constant change happening everywhere around you!
