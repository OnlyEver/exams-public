A linear function is the ultimate middle school drama—it’s a living, breathing reality TV show of predictability, and if you can read the math, you can predict exactly when the whole system is going to implode.

Let’s start with the heartbeat of the whole operation: the slope. The slope of a linear function represents the constant rate of change between the dependent variable and the independent variable. It’s the engine of the chaos! In the trenches of your future classroom, the slope represents the marginal change in the output for each one-unit change in the input. Think about confiscating cell phones. For every one unit of time that passes—say, a single class period—the number of confiscated phones in your desk drawer goes up by exactly two. That marginal change? That’s your slope. 

When you look at a graph, the slope of a graphed line is defined as the vertical change divided by the horizontal change. You’ll hear people say "rise over run," but I want you to think of it as the rise in cafeteria drama over the run of the school week. If things are getting worse, you’ve got a positive slope. A positive slope indicates that the value of the dependent variable increases as the independent variable increases. Days go by, stress goes up. But what if you’re bribing them with Jolly Ranchers? A negative slope indicates that the value of the dependent variable decreases as the independent variable increases. As the number of days increases, the remaining candy in your jar plummets! 

Now, imagine silent reading time. Pure, glorious, unbroken silence. The volume level doesn't change no matter how many minutes tick by. A horizontal line on a coordinate plane has a slope exactly equal to zero. It’s flatlining. But then—disaster! A 7th grader drops a hydro flask on the tile floor during a major test. A vertical line on a coordinate plane has an undefined slope. There is zero horizontal movement, time practically stops, and mathematically, you’re dividing by zero. It’s a total societal collapse, an undefined event. 

So how do you prove the rate of this chaos to the principal? If you have specific incidents, the formula to calculate the slope given two points $(x_1, y_1)$ and $(x_2, y_2)$ is $m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$. You’re just taking the difference in the drama and dividing it by the difference in time. And what if they hand you a spreadsheet of behavior logs? To calculate the slope from a data table, divide the difference between any two y-values by the difference between their corresponding x-values. It doesn’t matter which days you pick, because a data table represents a linear function if the ratio of the change in y to the change in x is constant across all data pairs. If that ratio holds steady, you’re dealing with a perfectly linear escalation. 

But every reality show has to start somewhere, right? The cast has to arrive at the house. That’s your y-intercept. The y-intercept of a graph is the exact point where the line intersects the y-axis. Because the y-axis is the absolute vertical spine of your coordinate plane, the x-coordinate of any y-intercept is always exactly zero. You haven't moved left or right; you are at time zero. In mathematical models, the y-intercept typically represents the initial value or starting amount of the dependent variable. It’s the \$500 budget the PTA gave you on day one. If you want to crack this algebraically when staring at an equation, to calculate the y-intercept of an equation algebraically, substitute zero for the x-variable and solve for the y-variable. 

And on the flip side, what happens when the money runs dry? You hit the floor. The x-intercept of a graph is the exact point where the line intersects the x-axis. Because the x-axis is flat on the ground, the y-coordinate of any x-intercept is always exactly zero. In mathematical models, the x-intercept often represents the point in time or the condition when the dependent variable reaches a value of zero. It is the exact minute your \$500 budget is entirely gone. Boom. Zero dollars. To calculate the x-intercept of an equation algebraically, substitute zero for the y-variable and solve for the x-variable. 

But wait, let’s talk about the unicorns. The purest kids in the school. The ones with no hidden agendas and no starting baggage. I’m talking about a proportional relationship! A proportional relationship is a specific type of linear function that always passes through the origin. The origin point on a standard coordinate plane is the ordered pair $(0, 0)$. In the real world, the point $(0, 0)$ on the graph of a proportional relationship indicates that an input of zero produces an output of zero. You buy zero pizzas for the end-of-year party, you pay exactly zero dollars. No delivery fee, no y-intercept funny business! 

Because there’s no initial value clogging things up, the equation for a proportional relationship is written in the form $y = kx$. In the proportional equation $y = kx$, the variable $k$ represents the constant of proportionality. And here is the beautiful secret: the constant of proportionality in a proportional relationship is mathematically equal to the slope of the line. It’s the same beast, just in its purest form! 

When you’re haggling for those pizzas, you’re dealing with the unit rate. The unit rate of a proportional relationship describes the amount of the dependent variable associated with exactly one unit of the independent variable. How much for just *one* pizza? By definition, the unit rate of a proportional relationship is equivalent to the constant of proportionality. If you want to spot it instantly on a visual, just look at the graph of a proportional relationship, the y-coordinate of the point $(1, y)$ represents the unit rate. But you don’t even need the number one! For any point $(x, y)$ on the graph of a proportional relationship, the ratio $y/x$ equals the unit rate. If you see they charged you \$60 for 5 pizzas, you divide 60 by 5, and bam, your unit rate is 12. 

Now, when you sit down for this Praxis exam, they’re going to disguise these lines in different outfits like they’re pulling a bank heist. You need to know the three forms! 

First, the classic getaway car: Slope-Intercept Form. In the slope-intercept form $y = mx + b$, the coefficient $m$ represents the slope of the line. Meanwhile, in the slope-intercept form $y = mx + b$, the constant term $b$ represents the y-coordinate of the y-intercept. It is all right there in plain sight. 

Second, the hostage negotiation: Standard Form. It looks like $Ax + By = C$. They use this when they’re hiding the slope and intercepts in plain sight. But you’re a math teacher, you have the decoding ring! In the standard form of a linear equation $Ax + By = C$, the slope of the line is equal to $-A/B$. To find where it crosses the walls, in the standard form of a linear equation $Ax + By = C$, the y-coordinate of the y-intercept is equal to $C/B$. And to find where it hits the floor, in the standard form of a linear equation $Ax + By = C$, the x-coordinate of the x-intercept is equal to $C/A$. You just kick down the door and take what you need!

Third, the forensic evidence: Point-Slope Form. You only have a single footprint and the speed the suspect was running. The point-slope form of a linear equation is written as $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$. In the point-slope form $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$, the variables $x_1$ and $y_1$ represent the coordinates of a specific known point on the line. You plug in that one piece of evidence, attach your slope $m$, and you can build the entire reality from that single moment in time.

And finally, you have to watch out for how these functions interact with each other in the wild hallway of the coordinate plane. If you have two kids running at the exact same speed down the hall but they started at different lockers, they are parallel lines on a coordinate plane have identical slopes. They will never touch. But what if they are sprinting on a collision course? Perpendicular lines on a coordinate plane have slopes that are negative reciprocals of one another. If one kid is moving at a slope of $2/3$, and the other is moving at a slope of $-3/2$, they are going to T-bone perfectly at a 90-degree angle right outside the gym.
