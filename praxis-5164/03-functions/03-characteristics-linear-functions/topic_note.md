A student staring at a [graph of a linear function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_of_a_function) is often just looking at a snapshot of a moving object, a draining tank, or a growing bank account frozen in [two dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two-dimensional_space). To teach the characteristics of [linear functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_function) is to teach the mathematics of predictability. When a phenomenon changes at a constant rate—whether it is the steady burn of a candle, the descent of an airplane, or the accumulation of a flat monthly streaming fee—it traces a [straight line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_%28geometry%29) across a [coordinate plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system). By isolating a line's [slope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slope), its starting point, and where it terminates, we transition from merely observing a graph to predicting the future behavior of the system it represents. For middle school students, this is the crucial bridge between concrete [arithmetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic) and abstract [algebraic reasoning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra). 

![Graphs of linear equations visually represent a constant rate of change across a Cartesian coordinate plane.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Linear_Function_Graph.svg)

## The Anatomy of Constant Change: Slope

At the heart of every linear function is its **[slope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slope)**. 

> The slope of a linear function represents the constant rate of change between the [dependent variable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dependent_and_independent_variables) and the [independent variable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dependent_and_independent_variables). 

In practical terms, the slope represents the [marginal change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marginal_concept) in the output for each one-unit change in the input. If a student is taking a [standardized test](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standardized_test) and scores three points for every correct answer, the "marginal change" is three points per one unit of correct answers. The slope dictates the [angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle) and direction of the line:

*   A **positive slope** indicates that the value of the dependent variable increases as the independent variable increases. (Think of an hourly wage: as hours worked increase, total pay increases.)
*   A **negative slope** indicates that the value of the dependent variable decreases as the independent variable increases. (Think of a gift card: as the number of purchases increases, the remaining balance decreases.)
*   A **[horizontal line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horizontal_and_vertical)** on a coordinate plane has a slope exactly equal to [zero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/0). If you plot a [constant function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constant_function) like $y = 5$, the output never changes, no matter the input. 
*   A **[vertical line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horizontal_and_vertical)** on a coordinate plane has an undefined slope. Because there is no horizontal movement, calculating the rate of change requires [dividing by zero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_by_zero), which is mathematically [undefined](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Undefined_%28mathematics%29).

![Because a vertical line has zero horizontal movement, calculating its slope requires division by zero, an undefined mathematical operation that often results in a calculator error.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/TI86_Calculator_DivByZero.jpg)

### Calculating Slope Across Multiple Representations

To effectively teach linear functions, you must be able to translate the concept of slope across [graphs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_of_a_function), [formulas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formula), and [tables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Table_%28information%29). 

**From a Graph:**
The slope of a graphed line is defined as the vertical change divided by the horizontal change. You will often hear this described to students as "rise over run." It is a visual [measurement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Measurement) of how many units you must travel up or down before moving one unit to the right.

![The geometric interpretation of slope relies on "rise over run," visually representing the change in y divided by the change in x.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Wiki_slope_in_2d.svg)

**From Two Points:**
When given the [coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system) of two distinct [points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_%28geometry%29), the formula to calculate the slope given two points $(x_1, y_1)$ and $(x_2, y_2)$ is:
$$m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$$

**From a Table:**
How do we know if a set of [data](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data) is linear just by looking at a table? A data table represents a linear function if the [ratio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ratio) of the change in y to the change in x is constant across all data pairs. To calculate the slope from a data table, [divide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_%28mathematics%29) the difference between any two y-values by the difference between their corresponding x-values. It does not matter which two rows you pick; in a linear relationship, that ratio will relentlessly remain the same.

## Intercepts: The Anchors of a Mathematical Model

If the slope tells us *how* a system is changing, the intercepts tell us *where* that system begins and ends. Intercepts anchor the theoretical line to concrete reality.

### The Y-Intercept
The **[y-intercept](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/y-intercept)** of a graph is the exact point where the line [intersects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line%E2%80%93line_intersection) the [y-axis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system). Because the y-axis is the vertical center line of the coordinate plane, the x-coordinate of any y-intercept is always exactly zero. 

*   **[Algebraic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra) Calculation:** To calculate the y-intercept of an [equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equation) algebraically, substitute zero for the x-variable and [solve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equation_solving) for the y-variable.
*   **Modeling Context:** In [mathematical models](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_model), the y-intercept typically represents the initial value or starting amount of the dependent variable. If an [electrician](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrician) charges a flat \$50 fee just to show up, plus \$75 per hour of work, the initial value of \$50 is the y-intercept. 

### The X-Intercept
The **[x-intercept](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/x-intercept)** of a graph is the exact point where the line intersects the [x-axis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system). Because the x-axis is the horizontal center line of the coordinate plane, the y-coordinate of any x-intercept is always exactly zero.

*   **Algebraic Calculation:** To calculate the x-intercept of an equation algebraically, substitute zero for the y-variable and solve for the x-variable.
*   **Modeling Context:** In mathematical models, the x-intercept often represents the point in time or the condition when the dependent variable reaches a value of zero. If a 100-gallon water tank is draining at a rate of 5 gallons per minute, the x-intercept represents the exact minute the tank is entirely empty.

## Proportional Relationships: The Origin of Scaling

There is a profoundly important sub-category of linear functions that students encounter early in their algebraic careers: [proportional relationships](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proportionality_%28mathematics%29). 

A proportional relationship is a specific type of linear function that always passes through the [origin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Origin_%28mathematics%29). The origin point on a standard coordinate plane is the [ordered pair](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordered_pair) $(0, 0)$. In an applied context, the point $(0, 0)$ on the graph of a proportional relationship indicates that an input of zero produces an output of zero. If you buy zero apples, you pay zero dollars. 

> The equation for a proportional relationship is written in the form $y = kx$. 

In the proportional equation $y = kx$, the [variable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variable_%28mathematics%29) $k$ represents the **[constant of proportionality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proportionality_%28mathematics%29)**. You will notice this equation lacks the $+ b$ seen in slope-intercept form; that is because the y-intercept ($b$) is zero. Consequently, the constant of proportionality in a proportional relationship is mathematically equal to the slope of the line.

![The graph of a proportional relationship always intersects the origin (0, 0), indicating that an input of zero yields an output of zero.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Proportional_variables.svg)

### The Unit Rate
When dealing with proportional relationships, we often use the term **[unit rate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rate_%28mathematics%29)**. The unit rate of a proportional relationship describes the amount of the dependent variable associated with exactly one unit of the independent variable. 

Crucially, the unit rate of a proportional relationship is equivalent to the constant of proportionality (and therefore, the slope). 

**Finding the Unit Rate graphically and algebraically:**
1.  **Look for the x-value of 1:** On the graph of a proportional relationship, the y-coordinate of the point $(1, y)$ represents the unit rate. 
2.  **Use any point:** For any point $(x, y)$ on the graph of a proportional relationship, the ratio $y/x$ equals the unit rate. If a student sees the point $(4, 12)$ on a proportional graph, calculating $12 / 4$ reveals a unit rate of $3$. 

## Parallel and Perpendicular Systems

When multiple linear functions interact on a single coordinate plane, their slopes dictate their [geometric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry) relationship.

*   **[Parallel Lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_%28geometry%29):** Parallel lines on a coordinate plane have identical slopes. If two runners are moving at the exact same constant [speed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed) but start at different positions, their distance-over-time graphs will never intersect. 
*   **[Perpendicular Lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular):** Perpendicular lines on a coordinate plane have slopes that are [negative reciprocals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplicative_inverse) of one another. If one line has a slope of $2/3$, a line perpendicular to it must have a slope of $-3/2$. This mathematical inversion creates a precise 90-[degree](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_%28angle%29) intersection.

![Parallel lines maintain the exact same slope, while perpendicular lines possess slopes that are negative reciprocals.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Slopes_of_Parallel_and_Perpendicular_Lines.svg)

## The Three Forms of a Linear Equation: A Quick Reference

As a teacher, you will need to rapidly identify and extract intercepts and slopes depending on how a linear equation is presented. The form chosen is usually the one most convenient for the information initially available.

| Equation Form | Formula | Key Extraction Facts |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Slope-Intercept Form** | $y = mx + b$ | The [coefficient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coefficient) $m$ represents the slope of the line.<br><br>The [constant term](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constant_term) $b$ represents the y-coordinate of the y-intercept. |
| **Standard Form** | $Ax + By = C$ | Often used to model constraints (e.g., buying $x$ child tickets and $y$ adult tickets for a total of $C$).<br><br>The slope of the line is equal to $-A/B$.<br><br>The y-coordinate of the y-intercept is equal to $C/B$.<br><br>The x-coordinate of the x-intercept is equal to $C/A$. |
| **Point-Slope Form** | $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$ | Often used when modeling experimental data where a specific moment in [time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time) is recorded alongside the rate of change.<br><br>The variables $x_1$ and $y_1$ represent the coordinates of a specific known point on the line. The variable $m$ remains the slope. |

### Summary for Praxis 5164 Application
When you approach [selected-response questions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiple_choice) or numeric-entry items regarding linear functions, always look for the hidden givens. A prompt mentioning "the ground" or "empty" is testing your knowledge of the x-intercept ($y=0$). A prompt mentioning an "initial fee" or "starting balance" is testing the y-intercept ($x=0$). If the problem states "per unit," it is handing you the slope. Master these translations between narrative text, algebraic form, and [graphing calculator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graphing_calculator) displays, and you will teach your future students to see the invisible [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry) of constant change that surrounds them every day.
