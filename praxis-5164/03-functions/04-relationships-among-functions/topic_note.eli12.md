Think of a math function like a video game. It has three main parts: the game code (the equation), the stats menu (the table), and what you actually see moving on your screen (the graph). To really "beat" math, you just need to learn how to translate freely among these three representations. Instead of just crunching numbers, you are learning to read the DNA of an equation and instantly predict what its visual reality will look like on a coordinate plane! When you can look at a simple table of numbers and visualize the shape it makes before even touching a graphing calculator, you have unlocked the true connection between algebra and geometry.

## The Anatomy of Linear Functions

Before we build complex rollercoasters, we need to understand a simple, straight road. The defining characteristic of a linear function is its unwavering consistency. It never speeds up or slows down!

### Reading the Ledger: Tables and Intercepts
We can diagnose how a function behaves just by looking at its outputs (the answers you get). 

A table of values represents a linear function if the first differences between consecutive y-values are constant for equally spaced x-values. This constant rate of change is the heartbeat of a straight line! 

Let's look at how to find this out step-by-step using an allowance example:
1. First, check that your x-values (like the weeks you do chores) are equally spaced: Week 1, Week 2, Week 3, Week 4. They go up by exactly 1 each time!
2. Next, look at your y-values (the money in your piggy bank): 10, 15, 20, 25.
3. Now, find the "first differences" by subtracting the consecutive y-values: 15 - 10 = 5. Then 20 - 15 = 5. Then 25 - 20 = 5.
4. Because the difference is always a constant 5, you know for sure this table represents a linear function!

When looking at any table, you also want to look for the "anchors," which are the places where the line crosses the grid axes. 
* The y-intercept of a function's graph corresponds to the ordered pair in a table where the x-value is exactly zero. (Like starting out with \$20 before you even earn an allowance).
* The x-intercepts of a function's graph correspond to the ordered pairs in a table where the y-value is exactly zero. (This is when your piggy bank is totally empty). 

If you learn to immediately scan a table for zeros, you can instantly find where the graph crosses the axes!

### Measuring the Incline: Slope
That constant pattern we just saw in the table is officially called "slope." Think of it like the steepness of a skateboard ramp. The slope of a linear function can be calculated from a table or graph by dividing the change in vertical y-values by the corresponding change in horizontal x-values between any two points. 

Here is how to calculate it step-by-step:
1. Pick any two points on your line. Let's use (2, 8) and (4, 14).
2. Find the change in the vertical y-values (how much it rises): 14 - 8 = 6.
3. Find the change in the horizontal x-values (how much it runs): 4 - 2 = 2.
4. Divide the vertical change by the horizontal change: 6 / 2 = 3. 
Your slope is 3! Every time you move over 1 space, you go up 3 spaces.

### The Equations: Blueprints of a Line
Straight lines have really simple blueprints. You'll mostly see them in two forms:

> **Slope-Intercept Form:** y = mx + b
> The slope-intercept form of a linear equation is y = mx + b. It is famous because it is so useful! In the slope-intercept form y = mx + b, the variable m represents the slope of the line, which tells you how steep it is. Also, in the slope-intercept form y = mx + b, the variable b represents the y-coordinate of the y-intercept. This tells you exactly where the line starts on the vertical axis.

> **Point-Slope Form:** y - y1 = m(x - x1)
> The point-slope form of a linear equation is y - y1 = m(x - x1). This is a great tool for building a line when you don't know where it started! In the point-slope form y - y1 = m(x - x1), the variables x1 and y1 represent the coordinates of a specific known point on the line. Once you know the slope and just one single point, you can write the equation without having to figure out the y-intercept first.

## The Architecture of Quadratics

When the rate of change is no longer constant, the straight line bends. The graph of a quadratic function is a U-shaped curve called a parabola. Imagine the path of a basketball flying through the air toward the hoop!

How do we spot a U-shape just by looking at a table of numbers? A table of values represents a quadratic function if the second differences between consecutive y-values are constant for equally spaced x-values.

Let's test this out step-by-step:
1. Check your x-values: 1, 2, 3, 4. (They go up by 1, so they are equally spaced).
2. Look at your y-values: 2, 5, 10, 17. 
3. Find the *first* differences by subtracting consecutive y-values: 5 - 2 = 3. 10 - 5 = 5. 17 - 10 = 7. (Since 3, 5, and 7 are different, this is NOT a straight line).
4. Now, find the *second* differences by subtracting those first answers: 5 - 3 = 2. 7 - 5 = 2. 
5. Because the second differences are constantly 2, we know this is a quadratic function!

Quadratic functions can be written in a few different ways. The specific format tells you what part of the U-shape you are looking at right away.

| Form | Equation | Key Insights Revealed |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Standard** | y = ax^2 + bx + c | The standard form of a quadratic equation is y = ax^2 + bx + c. In the standard quadratic form y = ax^2 + bx + c, the variable c represents the y-coordinate of the y-intercept. Also, the axis of symmetry of a parabola given in standard form y = ax^2 + bx + c is the vertical line defined by the equation x = -b / (2a). That line cuts the parabola perfectly in half! |
| **Factored / Intercept** | y = a(x - p)(x - q) | The factored form or intercept form of a quadratic equation is y = a(x - p)(x - q). In the factored quadratic form y = a(x - p)(x - q), the variables p and q represent the x-coordinates of the x-intercepts. These are the exact spots where the U-shape hits the ground (the x-axis). |
| **Vertex** | y = a(x - h)^2 + k | The vertex form of a quadratic equation is y = a(x - h)^2 + k. In the quadratic vertex form y = a(x - h)^2 + k, the variables h and k represent the coordinates of the parabola's vertex at point (h, k). This is the very bottom tip or very top peak of the curve! |

You might notice the letter "a" is in every single equation. The variable a in the quadratic vertex form y = a(x - h)^2 + k determines the direction of the parabola's opening and its vertical stretch or compression. 

It acts like a steering wheel:
* A positive value of the leading coefficient a in a quadratic equation causes the parabola to open upward, making a shape like a smiley face that has a lowest point.
* A negative value of the leading coefficient a in a quadratic equation causes the parabola to open downward, making a shape like an umbrella that has a highest peak.

## Foundational Parent Functions

Besides lines and parabolas, there are other basic shapes that you will use to build big equations. Every complicated function is just a dressed-up version of a simple "parent" function.

**The Absolute Value Function**
The parent absolute value function is f(x) = |x|. Absolute value just measures how far away a number is from zero, which means the answer can never be negative! Because of this, the graph of the parent absolute value function f(x) = |x| has a V-shape with its vertex situated at the origin (0,0). The right side goes up like a normal line, and the left side bounces off the bottom and goes up too, making a perfect "V".

**The Square Root Function**
The parent square root function is f(x) = √(x). This one has strict rules. You can't take the square root of a negative number in normal math! Because of this rule, the domain of the parent square root function f(x) = √(x) is restricted to all non-negative real numbers. Visually, the graph of the parent square root function f(x) = √(x) begins at the origin (0,0) and curves outward into the first quadrant, slowly getting flatter and flatter like an airplane taking off but leveling out.

## The Geometry of Algebra: Function Transformations

One of the coolest "cheat codes" in math is that the rules for moving a straight line are the exact same rules for moving a U-shape, a V-shape, or a square root curve. We call these universal movement rules **transformations**. 

### Translations: Shifting the Grid
A translation is just sliding the shape around without rotating it. 

A vertical translation shifts the entire graph of a function upward or downward without altering the graph's overall shape or orientation. This happens when you add or subtract a number at the very end of the equation.
* Adding a positive constant k to the output of a function, written as f(x) + k, translates the graph vertically upward by k units. (Like taking an elevator up).
* Subtracting a positive constant k from the output of a function, written as f(x) - k, translates the graph vertically downward by k units. (Taking the elevator down).

A horizontal translation shifts the entire graph of a function to the left or right without altering the graph's overall shape or orientation. This happens when you change the input (the x) directly, inside parentheses. But watch out, horizontal movement feels a little backwards!
* Subtracting a positive constant h from the input variable of a function, written as f(x - h), translates the graph horizontally to the right by h units. 
* Adding a positive constant h to the input variable of a function, written as f(x + h), translates the graph horizontally to the left by h units.

Why is it backwards? Imagine a video game character who normally jumps at x = 0. If the game code suddenly says f(x - 3), the character has to walk 3 steps to the right (to positive 3) just to make 3 - 3 = 0 so they can jump! 

### Reflections: The Mathematical Mirror
Reflections create mirror images. If you slap a negative sign onto the equation, it flips the graph over entirely!
* A reflection of a function over the x-axis transforms the symbolic representation of the function f(x) into -f(x). When you do this, multiplying a function's entire output by negative one visually flips the graph vertically across the horizontal x-axis. A U-shape pointing up turns into a U-shape pointing down.
* A reflection of a function over the y-axis transforms the symbolic representation of the function f(x) into f(-x). In this case, multiplying a function's input variable by negative one visually flips the graph horizontally across the vertical y-axis. The left side and the right side swap places!

### Dilations: Stretches and Compressions
Finally, you can stretch or squish a graph like it's made of slime by multiplying it by a special number.
* A vertical stretch occurs when a function is multiplied by a constant factor a, provided the absolute value of a is greater than 1 (like 2, 5, or -4). The graph gets pulled tall and skinny because the answers are growing super fast!
* A vertical compression occurs when a function is multiplied by a constant factor a, provided the absolute value of a is strictly between 0 and 1 (like 1/2 or 0.25). The graph gets pushed down so it looks wide and flat, because the answers are growing much slower.

By learning these cheat codes, you never have to memorize a billion different graphs. You just learn the basic shapes and how to drive them around the screen!
