Imagine your principal hands you a giant spreadsheet. It has the test scores, favorite lunch foods, and attendance records of four hundred kids in your grade. Staring at that huge wall of numbers and words tells you almost nothing. Our brains aren't built to read endless rows of data; we are visual creatures! To really understand the story hidden in those numbers, we need to turn them into pictures, like lines, shapes, and slices.

Before you draw anything, you have to figure out what kind of information you are looking at. **Categorical data represents characteristics or groups.** Think of things like your favorite video game console or how you get to school (bus, car, or walking). On the other hand, **numerical data represents measurable or countable quantities.** This would be things like the exact weight of your backpack or the number of points you scored in a basketball game. Knowing the difference between these two types of data is super important. You can't calculate the "average" favorite color, just like you wouldn't put fifty different test scores into their own separate word categories.

## The Architecture of Categories

When we work with categorical data, our goal is to show how big each group is. We turn counts into visual pictures that help us see the differences between groups.

### Bar Graphs and Dot Plots
The easiest way to show a count is by using length. **Bar graphs display the frequencies of categorical data using distinct rectangular bars.** If you want to see how many kids ride the bus, walk, or get dropped off, you draw a separate bar for each group. **The spaces between bars in a bar graph emphasize that the categories are discrete.** "Discrete" just means separate. There is no halfway point between a kid who rides the bus and a kid who walks, so the visual gap between the bars proves they are totally different groups.

If you don't have a lot of data, you can use a simpler method. **Dot plots represent the frequency of numerical or categorical data using individual dots stacked along an axis.** If twelve kids choose band as their favorite class, you just stack twelve dots above the word "Band." It is a quick and easy way to count!

### Circle Graphs
Sometimes, we care more about seeing slices of a whole pizza than counting the exact number of pepperoni slices. **Circle graphs display categorical data as proportional parts of a whole.** They make you look at the data as fractions of one big group. Because the entire circle represents everyone you asked, **the sum of all percentage values in a complete circle graph is exactly 100 percent.**

Drawing a circle graph is like doing a geometry puzzle. **The central angle of a sector in a circle graph is directly proportional to the relative frequency of that category.** 

Here is how you figure out the exact angle for a category that takes up 25% of the data:
1. Turn the percentage into a decimal. (25% becomes 0.25).
2. Remember that a full circle is always 360 degrees.
3. Multiply the decimal by the total degrees in the circle (0.25 * 360 = 90). 
The slice of the circle will be exactly 90 degrees!

## Unpacking Numerical Distributions

When data is numerical, it naturally flows along a number line. We are no longer looking at separate groups; we are looking at how the numbers are spread out.

### Preserving the Raw Data
Sometimes, when we make a chart, we don't want to lose the actual, real numbers. **Stem-and-leaf plots display the frequency distribution of numerical data while preserving the exact original data values.** If you got scores of 82, 85, and 88 on your tests, the "stem" is the 80s (written as an 8), and the "leaves" are the 2, 5, and 8. You get to see the shape of the data, but you can still read every single original score perfectly.

### Grouping the Data: Histograms
For huge sets of numbers—like the time it takes 200 kids to run a race—plotting every single decimal would look super messy. We need to organize it. **Histograms display the frequencies of continuous numerical data.** 

To build a histogram, you chop the number line into chunks. **Data in a histogram is grouped into contiguous intervals called bins.** ("Contiguous" just means the bins are touching side-by-side). Unlike a bar graph, a histogram has no spaces between its columns because time and distance never stop flowing. A kid could finish the race in 15 seconds, 15.1 seconds, or 15.11 seconds. 

To keep the chart fair, **the bins in a histogram must be of equal width.** If one bin holds ten seconds of time and another bin holds only two seconds, the chart will look confusing and wrong. Once your bins are set up fairly, **the height of a bin in a histogram represents the frequency of data values within that interval.** If the bin is tall, a lot of kids finished the race in that time chunk!

### Summarizing Spread: Boxplots
Sometimes we just want to ignore the messy details and look at the middle of the data and the extremes. **Boxplots display the distribution of numerical data using a five-number summary.** 

> **The Five-Number Summary:**
> **The five-number summary for a boxplot consists of the minimum, first quartile, median, third quartile, and maximum.**

This tool is a really smart way to draw data. **The box in a boxplot spans from the first quartile to the third quartile.** The width of this box is very important: **the length of the box in a boxplot represents the interquartile range.** That sounds like a fancy phrase, but **the interquartile range contains the middle 50 percent of the numerical data values.** If you want to know how the "average" group of kids did, ignoring the few kids who got perfect scores or really low scores, you just look at the box!

Sticking out from the box are lines we call whiskers. **The whiskers in a standard boxplot extend from the quartiles to the minimum and maximum values.** But what if one kid scores 1,000,000 points on a video game, while everyone else scores around 100? Drawing a giant whisker all the way to 1,000,000 would ruin the picture. Because of this, **outliers in a boxplot are often plotted as individual data points beyond the reach of the whiskers.**

## Modeling Numerical Relationships

Often, we want to know how two different things affect each other. Do kids who spend more hours studying get better test scores? Does a heavier backpack mean you can't jump as high?

When both things we are measuring are numbers, we graph them on a grid. **Scatterplots display data points on a coordinate plane to model the relationship between two continuous numerical variables.** Each dot is one kid, but measured in two ways (like hours studied on the bottom line, and test score on the side line). 

If a pattern shows up in the cloud of dots, we draw a line to show what is happening. **A trend line on a scatterplot models the general direction of the plotted data points.** It helps us guess what might happen next!

If one of the numbers we are tracking is time (like days of the week), we connect the dots. **Line graphs display continuous numerical data over a continuous variable such as time.** Connecting the dots makes sense here because the time between Tuesday and Wednesday really happened, and the line shows how things changed during that time.

## Two-Way Tables: The Hidden Math of Associations

What if we want to find patterns, but our data is made of categories instead of numbers? You can't put "Choir" and "Soccer" on a number line. Instead, we make a grid. **Two-way tables summarize the frequencies of two categorical variables within a single dataset.**

Imagine you ask 200 middle schoolers two questions: Are you in the school band? (Yes/No) and Are you on the honor roll? (Yes/No).

| | Honor Roll (Yes) | Honor Roll (No) | **Row Totals** |
| :--- | :---: | :---: | :---: |
| **Band (Yes)** | 40 | 10 | **50** |
| **Band (No)** | 60 | 90 | **150** |
| **Column Totals**| **100** | **100** | **200** |

### Decoding the Frequencies
Every single number in this table has a special job. 
*   **Joint frequencies in a two-way table represent the count of data points belonging to a specific row category and a specific column category simultaneously.** (For example, the 40 kids who are *both* in Band and on the Honor Roll at the same time).
*   **Marginal frequencies in a two-way table represent the total sum of counts for a single row or a single column.** These are the numbers sitting in the margins (the edges) of the table. (Like the 50 total kids in Band, or the 100 total kids on the Honor Roll).
*   **The grand total in a two-way table is the sum of all joint frequencies.** This is the big number at the very bottom right—the 200 kids you talked to!

### Establishing Proportions with Relative Frequencies
Looking just at the plain numbers can trick you. If 60 kids who *aren't* in band make the honor roll, and only 40 band kids make it, does that mean skipping band makes you smarter? Nope! There are just way more non-band kids in the school. To be fair, we have to turn these counts into fractions or percentages.

**Relative frequency is the ratio of a specific frequency to a designated total count.** By comparing the smaller group to the total, we make everything fair to compare.

1.  **A joint relative frequency is calculated by dividing a joint frequency by the grand total.**
    *   Step 1: Find the joint frequency (Band AND Honor Roll is 40).
    *   Step 2: Find the grand total (200).
    *   Step 3: Divide them (40 / 200 = 0.20, which is 20%).
2.  **A marginal relative frequency is calculated by dividing a marginal frequency by the grand total.** 
    *   Step 1: Find the marginal frequency (Total kids in Band is 50).
    *   Step 2: Find the grand total (200).
    *   Step 3: Divide them (50 / 200 = 0.25, which is 25%).

The coolest trick we have is something called a conditional relative frequency. It lets us look at just one specific group of kids. **A conditional relative frequency is calculated by dividing a joint frequency by a marginal frequency.**

Let's ask a really smart question: *If* a kid is in the band, what are the chances they are on the honor roll? We completely ignore the grand total of 200. We only care about the 50 band kids.
*   Step 1: Find the joint frequency of kids in Band AND Honor Roll (40).
*   Step 2: Find the marginal frequency of total kids in Band (50).
*   Step 3: Divide them (40 / 50 = 0.80, which is 80%).

Now, what *if* a kid is NOT in the band?
*   Step 1: Find the joint frequency of kids NOT in Band AND on the Honor Roll (60).
*   Step 2: Find the marginal frequency of total kids NOT in Band (150).
*   Step 3: Divide them (60 / 150 = 0.40, which is 40%).

### Proving an Association
We just discovered a really cool secret in the numbers! **Comparing conditional relative frequencies determines whether a statistical association exists between two categorical variables.** 

If being in the band had absolutely nothing to do with getting good grades, the percentages would be almost exactly the same for both groups. But in statistics, **an association exists between two categorical variables if the conditional relative frequencies differ significantly across categories.** 

Because 80% of band kids are on the honor roll, and only 40% of non-band kids are on the honor roll, there is a strong association! Being in the band and making the honor roll are mathematically connected in this group of kids.

This is the superpower of math. Statistics isn't just about drawing boring charts; it is the best way to cut through a confusing pile of numbers and discover the awesome truth about how things in our world are connected!
