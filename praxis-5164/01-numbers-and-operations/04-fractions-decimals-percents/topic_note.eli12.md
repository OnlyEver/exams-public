Imagine you are organizing a gaming tournament with your friends. One friend says they have completed 1/3 of the main quest. Another friend says their character is 50% leveled up. A third friend says they have 0.75 of the coins needed to buy a rare skin. To figure out who is the furthest along, you need a way to translate all these different numbers into the same language! 

Fractions, decimals, and percents are exactly like this. They are not entirely different types of math. Instead, they are just three different ways to talk about the exact same thing: parts of a whole. Once you learn how they connect, figuring out pizza slices, leveling up in games, or calculating your allowance becomes super easy.

## The Architecture of Base-10 Equivalence

Let's look at the rules of our number system. The word "percent" literally means "out of one hundred." Because of this, the percent symbol indicates division by one hundred. It is a quick visual clue telling you that the number is the top part of a fraction (the numerator), and the bottom part (the denominator) is exactly 100.

Because our entire number system is built on tens, moving between decimals and percents is super fast. 

To convert a percent to a decimal, divide the percent value by one hundred. Since our numbers work in tens, dividing a number by one hundred is mathematically equivalent to moving the number's decimal point two places to the left. 

Let's do this step-by-step:
1. Start with your percent, like 45%.
2. Drop the percent sign, leaving just 45.
3. Imagine the invisible decimal point at the very end: 45.0
4. Move that decimal point two places to the left to get your answer: 0.45.

Working backward is just as easy. To convert a decimal to a percent, multiply the decimal value by one hundred. Multiplying a number by one hundred is mathematically equivalent to moving the number's decimal point two places to the right.

Let's do this step-by-step:
1. Start with your decimal, like 0.82.
2. Move the decimal point two places to the right to get 82.
3. Add the percent sign to get your final answer: 82%.

To tie this directly to fractions: to convert a percent to a fraction, write the percent value as the numerator over a denominator of one hundred. For example, 25% simply becomes the fraction 25/100!

## Terminating vs. Repeating Decimals: The Base-10 Filter

Fractions are really just division problems waiting to be solved. To convert a fraction to a decimal, divide the fraction's numerator by the fraction's denominator. 

When you divide the top by the bottom, you will notice something interesting. Some fractions divide perfectly and stop (like a game ending naturally), while others generate digits that march on forever. This isn't random! It happens because of how our base-10 number system is built. 

The number 10 is built by multiplying exactly two prime numbers: 2 and 5. Because of this rule, a fraction produces a terminating decimal if the prime factorization of the simplified fraction's denominator contains only twos and fives. ("Terminating" just means it naturally stops.)

Let's verify this step-by-step:
1. Look at the fraction 3/40.
2. Break down the bottom number (40) into prime numbers: 40 = 2 x 2 x 2 x 5.
3. Because you only see 2s and 5s in that breakdown, dividing 3 by 40 will give you a decimal that stops perfectly (0.075).

On the flip side, a fraction produces a repeating decimal if the prime factorization of the simplified fraction's denominator contains any prime factors other than two or five. If a 3, 7, or 11 sneaks into the bottom number's prime building blocks, the division will never finish cleanly!

A repeating decimal features a sequence of one or more digits that repeats infinitely. Since you can't write digits forever, mathematicians use a special symbol. The vinculum is a horizontal bar placed over the repeating digits of a decimal to indicate infinite repetition (for example, writing 0.3 with a little line over the 3).

### Reversing the Translation: Decimals to Fractions

If you have a decimal that stops, turning it back into a fraction is a breeze. To convert a terminating decimal to a fraction, use the digits of the decimal as the numerator and the place value of the final digit as the denominator.

Let's turn 0.48 into a fraction step-by-step:
1. Look at the digits "48". This becomes your top number (numerator).
2. Look at the very last digit. The "8" is in the hundredths place.
3. Use 100 as your bottom number (denominator). Your fraction is 48/100.

But wait, you aren't quite finished! The fractional equivalent of a terminating decimal must often be simplified by dividing the numerator and denominator by their greatest common factor. 

Let's simplify step-by-step:
1. Start with 48/100.
2. Find the biggest number that divides evenly into both (the greatest common factor is 4).
3. Divide the top by 4: 48 divided by 4 = 12.
4. Divide the bottom by 4: 100 divided by 4 = 25.
5. Your beautiful, simplified fraction is 12/25.

## Capturing Infinity: The Algebra of Repeating Decimals

Turning a *repeating* decimal back into a fraction feels like a magic trick. You can't just put it over 10 or 100 because the numbers never end. Instead, we use a little algebra to chop off the endless tail.

Here is the exact step-by-step trick to do it:
1. The first step in algebraically converting a repeating decimal to a fraction is to set a variable equal to the repeating decimal. 
   Let x = 0.454545...
2. To shift the decimal point past one full repeating sequence, multiply the initial decimal equation by ten raised to the power of the repeating sequence length. Because "45" is a two-digit repeating pattern, we multiply by 10 to the power of 2 (which is 100).
   100x = 45.454545...
3. Subtracting the original repeating decimal equation from the shifted decimal equation cancels out the infinitely repeating fractional part.
   Let's line it up:
   100x = 45.454545...
   Minus x = 0.454545...
   This leaves us with: 99x = 45
4. Solve for x by dividing both sides by 99. The answer is 45/99!

This neat algebraic trick gives us two amazing shortcuts you can use anytime:
* A repeating decimal with exactly one repeating digit immediately following the decimal point equals a fraction with that repeating digit over nine. (For example, 0.777... is just 7/9).
* A repeating decimal with exactly two repeating digits immediately following the decimal point equals a fraction with those two digits over ninety-nine. (For example, 0.4545... is 45/99, which then simplifies to 5/11).

## Translating Fractions to Percents

There are two great ways to turn a fraction into a percent. Think of these like two different paths to clear a level in a game.

**Path 1:**
To convert a fraction to a percent, first convert the fraction to a decimal, then multiply the resulting decimal by one hundred.

Let's do this step-by-step for the fraction 3/5:
1. Divide 3 by 5 to get the decimal: 0.6.
2. Multiply 0.6 by 100 by moving the decimal two spaces right: 60.
3. Add your percent sign: 60%.

**Path 2:**
A fraction can be converted to a percent by solving a proportion where the fraction equals an unknown value divided by one hundred. 

Let's do this step-by-step for the fraction 3/5:
1. Set up the problem like this: 3/5 = x/100.
2. Ask yourself: "What do I multiply the bottom number (5) by to get 100?" The answer is 20.
3. Multiply the top number (3) by that exact same amount (20). 3 times 20 = 60.
4. Your answer is 60%.

## The Visual Grammar of Equivalence

Sometimes you need to literally *see* the math to understand it. Here are the best visual tools for seeing how fractions, decimals, and percents match up.

**The Hundredths Grid**
A hundredths grid is a visual model consisting of a ten-by-ten square used to represent parts of a whole. Imagine a big square cake sliced into 100 tiny, perfectly equal pieces.
* In a hundredths grid, shading exactly one square represents one percent of the whole.
* Because it is 1 out of 100, in a hundredths grid, shading exactly one square represents the decimal value 0.01.
* Written as a fraction, in a hundredths grid, shading exactly one square represents the fraction 1/100.

Now, what if you shade a whole row?
* In a hundredths grid, shading an entire row of ten squares represents ten percent of the whole.
* Mathematically, in a hundredths grid, shading an entire row of ten squares represents the decimal value 0.1.
* And finally, in a hundredths grid, shading an entire row of ten squares represents the fraction 1/10.

**Other Visual Models**
* A number line visually demonstrates the equivalence of fractions, decimals, and percents by plotting equivalent values at the exact same physical point. This proves that 1/2, 0.5, and 50% aren't three separate things floating around—they are just different names for the exact same spot on the line.
* A tape diagram is a rectangular visual model divided into equal-sized segments to represent proportional relationships between fractions and percents. Think of it like a progress bar downloading a game. If the 100% full bar is chopped into 4 equal chunks, each block visually represents exactly 25%.
* A pie chart represents parts of a whole by dividing a circle into sectors whose central angles are proportional to given fractions or percents. Just like slicing a real pizza, if you want a 25% slice, the angle at the point of that slice has to be exactly 90 degrees (which is one-fourth of a 360-degree circle).

## The Fundamental Lexicon: Canonical Equivalencies

Just like memorizing the controls for a new video game, memorizing the most common fraction, decimal, and percent match-ups makes everything faster. You won't have to stop and calculate them every time!

At the very foundation of all of this: the integer one is mathematically equivalent to the percent 100%. (One whole pizza is 100% of the pizza!)

Here are the main match-ups you should lock into your memory:
* The fraction one-half is mathematically equivalent to the decimal 0.5.
* The decimal 0.5 is mathematically equivalent to the percent 50%.
* The fraction one-fourth is mathematically equivalent to the decimal 0.25.
* The decimal 0.25 is mathematically equivalent to the percent 25%.
* The fraction three-fourths is mathematically equivalent to the decimal 0.75.
* The decimal 0.75 is mathematically equivalent to the percent 75%.
* The fraction one-fifth is mathematically equivalent to the decimal 0.2.
* The decimal 0.2 is mathematically equivalent to the percent 20%.
* The fraction one-eighth is mathematically equivalent to the decimal 0.125.
* The decimal 0.125 is mathematically equivalent to the percent 12.5%.

And don't forget the most famous repeating fraction! 
* The fraction one-third is mathematically equivalent to the repeating decimal 0.333...
* Which also means the repeating decimal 0.333... is mathematically equivalent to the repeating percent 33.333...%

## Expanding the Boundaries: Extremes and Edge Cases

Sometimes math goes past the normal limits of 0 and 1. We usually think of a fraction or percent as being smaller than a whole, but that isn't always true.

What happens if you have more than 100%? A percent value strictly greater than one hundred represents a numerical value greater than one whole. For example, if you collect 150% of the coins you need for an item, you have enough for the item PLUS half of another one!

Matching this rule, a decimal value strictly greater than one translates to an improper fraction or a mixed number.

Let's translate the decimal 1.5 step-by-step:
1. Look at the whole number part before the decimal. That gives you a big "1".
2. Turn the 0.5 part into a fraction: 1/2.
3. Put them together to get the mixed number 1 1/2. (You could also write this as the improper fraction 3/2).

On the totally opposite side, we have tiny, microscopic numbers. A percent value strictly less than one represents a numerical value less than 0.01.

This is a sneaky trap! If an app charges a fee of 0.5%, that is NOT 50%. It is only half of a single percent.

Let's convert it to a decimal step-by-step to see how small it is:
1. Start with 0.5%.
2. Drop the percent sign: 0.5.
3. Move the decimal point two places to the left: 0.005.

Seeing the huge difference between 50% (0.5) and 0.5% (0.005) is super important. Recognizing this will keep you from getting tricked when saving your allowance or managing your own money someday!
