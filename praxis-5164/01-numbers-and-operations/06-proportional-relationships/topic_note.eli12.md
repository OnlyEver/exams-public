Think about your favorite video game. If your character runs twice as fast, they cover twice the distance in the exact same amount of time. If you want to make a recipe for slime bigger, you have to add the exact same extra amount of glue and baking soda, or the slime is totally ruined. 

The physical world relies on amounts growing and shrinking perfectly together. In math, we call this a proportional relationship. A proportional relationship exists between two variable quantities when they maintain a constant ratio. This guide is going to help you crack the code of how things scale up, shrink down, and handle money like a pro!

## The Architecture of Proportionality

Before we can solve puzzles, we have to know how to write down our clues. We use ratios to compare amounts. 
* Ratios can be written numerically using a fraction bar (like y/x).
* Ratios can be written using a colon symbol between the two compared quantities (like y:x).
* Ratios can be written using the English word 'to' between the two compared quantities (like y to x).

When two of these comparisons are perfectly balanced, we call it a proportion. A proportion is a mathematical equation stating that two distinct ratios are entirely equivalent (for example, 1/2 = 2/4).

Sometimes, a piece of the puzzle is missing. When that happens, cross-multiplication is an algebraic method used to solve for an unknown value within two equivalent proportional ratios. 

Here is how you cross-multiply step-by-step:
1. Write down your two equivalent ratios with your unknown value: x/4 = 3/6.
2. Multiply the top-left number by the bottom-right number: x * 6 = 6x.
3. Multiply the bottom-left number by the top-right number: 4 * 3 = 12.
4. Set those two answers equal to each other: 6x = 12.
5. Divide both sides by 6 to solve for the missing piece: x = 2.

### The Constant of Proportionality and Unit Rates

At the heart of all this math is a special, unchanging number. The constant of proportionality is the unchanging ratio between two variables in a proportional relationship. Because mathematicians love shortcuts, the constant of proportionality is conventionally represented by the lowercase variable k.

If you have a dependent variable y (like how much money you make) and an independent variable x (like how many hours you work), they are locked together. In a proportional relationship between a dependent variable y and an independent variable x, the relationship is expressed by the algebraic equation y = kx. Because this is a straight, direct link, direct variation is a specific mathematical term representing a direct proportional relationship.

If you need to find that secret k number, it is easy! The constant of proportionality k can be calculated by dividing the dependent variable y by the independent variable x (which looks like k = y/x).

This k number is also super useful in real life. The constant of proportionality is numerically equivalent to the unit rate of a proportional relationship. A unit rate is a mathematical comparison of two different quantities where the second quantity is exactly one unit (like driving 50 miles per 1 hour). 

Sometimes, figuring out the unit rate means dealing with a complex fraction. A complex fraction is a fraction containing another fraction in its numerator. A complex fraction is also a fraction containing another fraction in its denominator. (So, a fraction stacked on top of a fraction!). 

Here is how you solve it step-by-step. Unit rates can be calculated from complex fractions by multiplying the numerator fraction by the reciprocal of the denominator fraction.
1. Write out the complex fraction: (1/2) / (3/4).
2. Keep the numerator fraction exactly the same: 1/2.
3. Find the reciprocal of the denominator fraction by flipping it upside down: 3/4 becomes 4/3.
4. Multiply the numerator fraction by this reciprocal: 1/2 * 4/3.
5. Multiply the top numbers: 1 * 4 = 4.
6. Multiply the bottom numbers: 2 * 3 = 6.
7. Simplify your answer: 4/6 becomes 2/3!

Why do we care about unit rates? Because they save you money at the store! When comparing unit prices, the item with the mathematically lower unit price is considered the better financial value.

### Constants in the Physical World

These constants are literally holding the universe together:
* **Physics:** Distance is directly proportional to travel time when an object is moving at a perfectly constant speed.
* **Measurement:** The mathematical conversion factor between two different units of measurement serves as a direct constant of proportionality (like exactly 12 inches in 1 foot).
* **Geometry:** The constant of proportionality relating the geometric circumference of a circle to its diameter is Pi!

## Multiple Representations: Tables and Graphs

You can spot proportional relationships in a few different ways. 

**In a Table:** 
A table of variables represents a proportional relationship if the quotient of the dependent and independent variables is constant across all rows. If even one row gives you a different division answer, the relationship is broken!

**On a Graph:**
The graph of any proportional relationship is strictly a straight line. No wiggles, no curves! Also, the graph of a proportional relationship always passes through the geometric origin. The origin of a coordinate plane is the ordered pair (0, 0). 

On a proportional graph, the origin point indicates that zero units of the independent variable correspond to exactly zero units of the dependent variable. (If you do zero chores, you earn zero allowance!). 

There is one more magic trick on the graph. On a graph of a proportional relationship, the specific coordinate point (1, r) identifies the unit rate r. If you find where the x-axis is 1, the y-number right above it tells you the unit rate!

### The Inverse Reality: Inverse Proportionality

What if things move in opposite directions? In an inversely proportional relationship involving positive quantities, one quantity decreases at the exact rate the other quantity increases. (Think about sharing a pizza: as the number of friends increases, the number of slices you get decreases!). 

In an inversely proportional relationship, the product of the two variables is always a constant mathematical value. If you multiply them together, the answer never changes. When you graph this, it doesn't look like a straight line anymore. The geometric graph of an inversely proportional relationship forms a specific curve called a hyperbola.

## Geometry and Scale

Whenever you shrink or grow a picture, you are using a scale factor. A scale factor is the ratio of the length of a side of one figure to the length of the corresponding side of a similar figure.

The number you choose for your scale factor decides what happens:
* A scale factor strictly greater than 1 creates an enlarged version of an original geometric figure (making it bigger).
* A scale factor strictly between 0 and 1 creates a proportionally reduced version of an original geometric figure (making it smaller).
* A scale factor of exactly 1 produces a figure mathematically congruent to the original figure (which means it is exactly the same size and shape).

You use these everywhere in life! 
* A scale drawing is a proportional two-dimensional representation of an actual object (like a paper blueprint for a treehouse).
* A scale model is a proportional three-dimensional representation of an actual object (like a toy car).
* The scale of a map is a ratio comparing the measured distance on the map to the actual physical distance on the ground.

## The Mathematics of "Per Centum"

You probably see the % symbol every day! A percent is a specialized ratio representing a specific part out of exactly one hundred. The mathematical percent symbol is denoted by %. The word percent is derived from the Latin phrase per centum. The Latin phrase per centum translates directly to "by the hundred."

You can easily switch back and forth between decimals and percents:
* To convert a decimal to a percent, the decimal value must be multiplied by 100.
* To convert a percent to a decimal, the numerical percent value must be divided by 100.

Here is step-by-step how to change 45% into a decimal:
1. Write down the number: 45.
2. Divide it by 100 (which is like moving the decimal point two spots to the left).
3. Your new decimal is 0.45.

When solving math problems, you have two great tools. The percent equation defines the partial amount as being equal to the percent expressed as a decimal multiplied by the whole amount. The percent proportion is an equation setting the ratio of the part to the whole equal to the percent value divided by 100 (Part/Whole = %/100).

### Percent Change, Difference, and Error

Be careful! Not all percent problems are the same.

**1. Percent Change:**
Percent change is the ratio of the absolute amount of change to the original amount expressed as a percentage. The percent change formula requires dividing the absolute difference between the new and original values by the original starting value.
* A percent increase happens when the final measured value is strictly greater than the initial starting value. A one hundred percent increase of any positive quantity exactly doubles the original starting quantity.
* A percent decrease happens when the final measured value is strictly less than the initial starting value. A fifty percent decrease of any positive quantity exactly halves the original starting quantity.

**2. Percent Difference:**
Percent difference is the absolute difference between two numerical values divided by their mathematical average. Percent difference is utilized when comparing two values where neither value is definitively designated as the original starting value (like comparing the heights of two mountains).

**3. Percent Error:**
Nobody is perfect when measuring in science! Absolute error is the positive mathematical difference between a flawed measured value and an actual correct value. Percent error is the ratio of the absolute error of a measurement to the exact actual value expressed as a percentage.

## Financial Mathematics in the Classroom

When you start buying your own things, you need to understand money math!

### Markups, Taxes, Tips, and Commissions
Stores have to make money, so they use markups. A markup is a specific financial amount added to an original wholesale cost to determine a final retail selling price. The final price after a markup can be found by multiplying the original price by one plus the markup rate expressed as a decimal.

Other times, extra money gets added on top of the price:
* Sales tax is a percentage of a purchase price added to the total cost of an item or service. Just like a markup, the total cost of an item including sales tax can be calculated by multiplying the original price by one plus the tax rate expressed as a decimal.
* A gratuity is a percentage of a service cost added to the final consumer bill (like when you pay a waiter). The term tip is mathematically synonymous with a gratuity.
* A commission is a fee paid to a salesperson calculated strictly as a percentage of their total sales amount.

Here is step-by-step how to find the total of a \$20 meal with a 15% tip:
1. Turn the 15% rate into a decimal by dividing by 100: 0.15.
2. Add that decimal to the number one: 1 + 0.15 = 1.15.
3. Multiply the original price by your new number: 20 * 1.15.
4. The final bill is \$23.

### Discounts and Markdowns
Everyone loves a sale! A discount is a specific financial amount subtracted from an original price to determine a final sale price. The term markdown is mathematically synonymous with a discount.

The final sale price of an item with a discount can be calculated by multiplying the original price by one minus the discount rate expressed as a decimal. 

Here is how you do it step-by-step for a \$50 jacket on sale for 30% off:
1. Turn the 30% discount into a decimal: 0.30.
2. Subtract that decimal from the number one: 1 - 0.30 = 0.70.
3. Multiply the original price by this number: 50 * 0.70.
4. The final sale price is \$35.

Watch out for store tricks! Adding two consecutive percent discount rates together does not yield the correct total overall discount percentage. A 20% off coupon on top of a 30% off sale does *not* mean 50% off! This is because consecutive percent discounts are calculated sequentially based on each newly reduced price rather than the original starting price.

### The Mechanics of Interest

When you put money in the bank, the bank pays you extra money called interest just for keeping it there! 

Simple interest is an interest amount calculated strictly on the initial principal amount of a loan or investment. Because it only looks at the starting money, simple interest grows an account balance at a constant linear mathematical rate.

The simple interest formula is Interest equals Principal multiplied by Rate multiplied by Time. (I = P * R * T).
* In the simple interest formula, the Principal represents the initial amount of money borrowed or invested.
* In the simple interest formula, the Rate must be expressed as a decimal or fraction representing the annual interest rate.
* In the simple interest formula, the Time represents the duration of the loan or investment strictly measured in years.

Here is a step-by-step example. You deposit \$100 at a 5% interest rate for 3 years:
1. Identify the Principal: 100.
2. Change the 5% Rate to a decimal: 0.05.
3. Identify the Time in years: 3.
4. Multiply Principal by Rate: 100 * 0.05 = 5.
5. Multiply that answer by Time: 5 * 3 = 15.
6. The interest earned is \$15!

Just remember, the simple interest formula calculates only the specific interest earned or owed rather than the total account balance. If you want to know how much money you have in total, the total accrued amount in a simple interest account is the sum of the initial principal and the accumulated simple interest. (So \$100 + \$15 = \$115 total).

Finally, the most powerful math in banking is compound interest. Compound interest is calculated on the initial principal and also on the accumulated interest from previous periods. This means your money earns money, and then *that* extra money starts earning even more extra money!
