Listen to me, rookie. Slide your drink over and pay attention, because middle schoolers are beautiful, chaotic little anarchists, but the universe they operate in is governed by one iron-clad law: a proportional relationship exists between two variable quantities when they maintain a constant ratio. It is the golden rule of the cafeteria. If there are two gossips for every three athletes at table four, that ratio holds no matter how many tables you push together. We call the puppet master behind this the constant of proportionality, which is the unchanging ratio between two variables in a proportional relationship. In the underground syndicate of middle school algebra, this constant of proportionality is conventionally represented by the lowercase variable $k$. 

It’s the invisible string. And here’s the beautiful part—the constant of proportionality is numerically equivalent to the unit rate of a proportional relationship. A unit rate is simply a mathematical comparison of two different quantities where the second quantity is exactly one unit. Three confiscated cell phones per *one* homeroom. When you have a proportional relationship between a dependent variable $y$ and an independent variable $x$, the relationship is expressed by the algebraic equation $y = kx$. That is direct variation, which is a specific mathematical term representing a direct proportional relationship. To find the mastermind, the constant of proportionality $k$ can be calculated by dividing the dependent variable $y$ by the independent variable $x$. 

Now, fire up your on-screen graphing calculator, because you need to see the fingerprint of this crime. The graph of any proportional relationship is strictly a straight line. It never bends. Furthermore, the graph of a proportional relationship always passes through the geometric origin. The origin of a coordinate plane is the ordered pair $(0, 0)$. On a proportional graph, the origin point indicates that zero units of the independent variable correspond to exactly zero units of the dependent variable. Zero effort yields zero drama. If you take one step onto that line, you find the smoking gun: on a graph of a proportional relationship, the specific coordinate point $(1, r)$ identifies the unit rate $r$. If you interrogate the numbers and build a table of variables, that table represents a proportional relationship only if the quotient of the dependent and independent variables is constant across all rows. One row with a broken quotient, and the entire alibi collapses.

But sometimes the math gets messy. I'm talking about a turducken of disaster known as a complex fraction. A complex fraction is a fraction containing another fraction in its numerator. Or, heaven forbid, a complex fraction is a fraction containing another fraction in its denominator. How do you survive this? Unit rates can be calculated from complex fractions by multiplying the numerator fraction by the reciprocal of the denominator fraction. Flip the bottom one upside down and multiply to end the madness. You need this survival skill because on a teacher's salary, you'll be comparing unit prices at the grocery store. When comparing unit prices, the item with the mathematically lower unit price is considered the better financial value.

If you are trying to figure out how many bulk glue sticks you can afford, and you have an unknown value within two equivalent proportional ratios, you unleash cross-multiplication. Cross-multiplication is an algebraic method used to solve for an unknown value within two equivalent proportional ratios. Because that is what a proportion is: a proportion is a mathematical equation stating that two distinct ratios are entirely equivalent. And these ratios wear disguises. Ratios can be written numerically using a fraction bar. Ratios can be written using a colon symbol between the two compared quantities. And ratios can be written using the English word 'to' between the two compared quantities. Three outfits, same suspect.

Speaking of building things, let's talk about the geometry of their art projects. When they resize a drawing, they use a scale factor. A scale factor is the ratio of the length of a side of one figure to the length of the corresponding side of a similar figure. If a kid's ego has a scale factor strictly greater than $1$, it creates an enlarged version of an original geometric figure. But if you hand them a pop quiz, that scale factor is strictly between $0$ and $1$, creating a proportionally reduced version of an original geometric figure. They shrink. A scale factor of exactly $1$ produces a figure mathematically congruent to the original figure—a perfect clone. This maps reality. A scale drawing is a proportional two-dimensional representation of an actual object, like a blueprint for a prank. A scale model is a proportional three-dimensional representation of an actual object, like a papier-mâché volcano. And if they draw an escape route, the scale of a map is a ratio comparing the measured distance on the map to the actual physical distance on the ground.

Now, brace yourself for the ultimate middle school weapon: the percent. A percent is a specialized ratio representing a specific part out of exactly one hundred. It’s an ancient concept. The word percent is derived from the Latin phrase *per centum*, and the Latin phrase *per centum* translates directly to 'by the hundred'. The mathematical percent symbol is denoted by $\%$. To drag these numbers into the light, to convert a decimal to a percent, the decimal value must be multiplied by $100$. To force them back underground, to convert a percent to a decimal, the numerical percent value must be divided by $100$. 

You have two interrogation techniques here. The percent equation defines the partial amount as being equal to the percent expressed as a decimal multiplied by the whole amount. Alternatively, the percent proportion is an equation setting the ratio of the part to the whole equal to the percent value divided by $100$. 

When you grade their disastrous science fair projects, you will calculate how badly they messed up using absolute error, which is the positive mathematical difference between a flawed measured value and an actual correct value. Then, to document the catastrophe, you find the percent error, which is the ratio of the absolute error of a measurement to the exact actual value expressed as a percentage. But what if you are measuring how much worse your own life got this semester? You use percent change. Percent change is the ratio of the absolute amount of change to the original amount expressed as a percentage. The percent change formula requires dividing the absolute difference between the new and original values by the original starting value. A percent increase happens when the final measured value is strictly greater than the initial starting value. A percent decrease happens when the final measured value is strictly less than the initial starting value. Keep your intuition sharp: a one hundred percent increase of any positive quantity exactly doubles the original starting quantity, and a fifty percent decrease of any positive quantity exactly halves the original starting quantity. 

Now, if two teachers in the lounge are arguing over whose homeroom is louder, use percent difference. Percent difference is the absolute difference between two numerical values divided by their mathematical average. Why the average? Because percent difference is utilized when comparing two values where neither value is definitively designated as the original starting value. 

Let's talk about retail warfare, because your paycheck depends on it. The world robs you with markups. A markup is a specific financial amount added to an original wholesale cost to determine a final retail selling price. The final price after a markup can be found by multiplying the original price by one plus the markup rate expressed as a decimal. Then the government gets you. Sales tax is a percentage of a purchase price added to the total cost of an item or service. Just like a markup, the total cost of an item including sales tax can be calculated by multiplying the original price by one plus the tax rate expressed as a decimal. 

If you are lucky, you score a discount, which is a specific financial amount subtracted from an original price to determine a final sale price. If the store calls it a markdown, don't panic—the term markdown is mathematically synonymous with a discount. The final sale price of an item with a discount can be calculated by multiplying the original price by one minus the discount rate expressed as a decimal. But beware the retail heist of the 'additional percent off' rack. Consecutive percent discounts are calculated sequentially based on each newly reduced price rather than the original starting price. Adding two consecutive percent discount rates together does not yield the correct total overall discount percentage. A $20\%$ off coupon on a $30\%$ off sweater does not equal a $50\%$ discount! It's a trap!

And when you finally go out to eat to cry about your grading backlog, you leave a gratuity, which is a percentage of a service cost added to the final consumer bill. The term tip is mathematically synonymous with a gratuity. Hopefully, the waiter isn't also earning a commission, which is a fee paid to a salesperson calculated strictly as a percentage of their total sales amount.

Eventually, you’ll need a loan just to buy dry-erase markers. Welcome to simple interest. Simple interest is an interest amount calculated strictly on the initial principal amount of a loan or investment. The simple interest formula is Interest equals Principal multiplied by Rate multiplied by Time. In the simple interest formula, the Principal represents the initial amount of money borrowed or invested. In the simple interest formula, the Rate must be expressed as a decimal or fraction representing the annual interest rate. In the simple interest formula, the Time represents the duration of the loan or investment strictly measured in years. Watch your back here: the simple interest formula calculates only the specific interest earned or owed rather than the total account balance. To find out how entirely broke you are, the total accrued amount in a simple interest account is the sum of the initial principal and the accumulated simple interest. Because it only looks at the principal, simple interest grows an account balance at a constant linear mathematical rate. Compound interest, on the other hand, is a monster. Compound interest is calculated on the initial principal and also on the accumulated interest from previous periods. It feeds on its own chaos.

Finally, what happens when things move in opposition? What about the twilight zone? In an inversely proportional relationship, the product of the two variables is always a constant mathematical value. In an inversely proportional relationship involving positive quantities, one quantity decreases at the exact rate the other quantity increases. The faster you sprint down the hallway, the less time it takes to catch the troublemaker. When you map that madness on your graphing calculator, the geometric graph of an inversely proportional relationship forms a specific curve called a hyperbola. It swoops away from the axes like a guilty seventh grader avoiding eye contact. 

These constants of proportionality are everywhere, holding the fabric of reality together. In geometry, the constant of proportionality relating the geometric circumference of a circle to its diameter is Pi. In physics, distance is directly proportional to travel time when an object is moving at a perfectly constant speed. And in your everyday teacher life, making sense of the madness, the mathematical conversion factor between two different units of measurement serves as a direct constant of proportionality. Learn the ratios, rookie. They're the only thing keeping the walls from caving in.
