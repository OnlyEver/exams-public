A naked number is like a ghost. If someone says "7," it doesn't really mean much on its own. Seven what? Seven slices of pizza? Seven extra lives in a video game? Seven days until your birthday? But the moment you write "7 meters," that number suddenly has size, shape, and a place in the real world. In math, you will learn to stop just moving plain numbers around and start dealing with real-world stuff. When you learn how to track and change units (like turning feet into inches), you are learning how to make sure your math actually makes sense in real life—whether you are figuring out how much water fills a swimming pool or checking to see if a news graph is trying to trick you!

## The Algebra of Reality: Units in Arithmetic and Formulas

If you are just doing regular math, 3 + 4 is always 7. But in the real world, 3 slices of pizza plus 4 gummy bears does not make 7 "pizza-bears."

When we mix different measurements together, we have to follow strict rules about their units.

### Addition and Subtraction

If you want to add or subtract real things, they have to be exactly the same type of thing, measured in exactly the same way. **Adding quantities requires the quantities to be expressed in the exact same units.**

You also cannot subtract things that don't match. **Subtracting quantities requires the quantities to be expressed in the exact same units.**

Imagine you are building a wooden ramp for your skateboard. You have a board that is 2 meters long, and you need to cut off 15 centimeters. You can't just do 2 - 15 = -13! Here is how you solve it step-by-step:

1. Look at your two units: meters and centimeters. They do not match.
2. Change the 2 meters into centimeters so they match. (2 meters = 200 centimeters).
3. Subtract the numbers: 200 - 15 = 185.
4. Keep the same unit. Your answer is 185 centimeters.

### Multiplication and Division

Multiplying and dividing are totally different. You are allowed to mix and match units, but when you do, you create something brand new!

* **Multiplying quantities with different units creates a new derived unit.** If you multiply how many hours you work by how many dollars you make per hour, you get a brand new unit: total dollars!
* **Dividing quantities with different units creates a new derived unit.** If you divide a distance (miles) by a time (hours), you get a new unit for speed: miles per hour.

When we multiply the *same* kind of unit, like length, we completely change dimensions (from lines to flat spaces to 3D shapes):

* **Squaring a linear measurement produces a measurement in square units.** This helps you find the area of a flat space, like a rug. Here is how you do it step-by-step for a rug that is 5 meters by 5 meters:
  1. Identify your length (5 meters) and width (5 meters).
  2. Multiply the numbers together: 5 x 5 = 25.
  3. Multiply the units together: meters x meters = square meters. Your area is 25 square meters.
* **Cubing a linear measurement produces a measurement in cubic units.** This helps you find volume, like how much space is inside a shipping box. Here is how you do it for a box where every side is 2 centimeters:
  1. Identify your length, width, and height (2 cm, 2 cm, and 2 cm).
  2. Multiply the numbers: 2 x 2 x 2 = 8.
  3. Multiply the units: cm x cm x cm = cubic centimeters. Your volume is 8 cubic centimeters.

### Units as Inherent Properties of Variables

In basic algebra, a letter like *x* is just a secret number waiting to be found. But in science and real-world math, **a variable representing a physical measurement in a mathematical formula inherently includes a specific unit.** It's not just a blank letter; it's a letter that expects a certain unit!

Let's look at the science formula for Potential Energy (how much energy a roller coaster has at the top of a hill): PE = mgh.

Here, *g* stands for gravity. On Earth, gravity is always measured in meters and seconds. Because the *g* has meters and seconds built into it, the mass (*m*) and the height (*h*) have to match those rules. Here is how you handle it:

1. Check the units built into the constant gravity (*g*), which uses meters and seconds.
2. Make sure your mass (*m*) is measured in kilograms.
3. Make sure your height (*h*) is measured in meters.
4. Plug the numbers into the formula. Remember: **Substituting values into a formula requires aligning the input units with the units expected by the formula constants.**

## The Geometry of Data: Scales and Graphical Interpretation

A graph is a super cool tool that turns numbers into a picture. But for the picture to be helpful and honest, the edges of the graph (the axes) have to be drawn carefully.

### Building the Axes

When you start drawing a graph, your first job is figuring out how to space out your numbers. **The scale of a graph determines the numerical interval represented by the distance between consecutive tick marks.** (Tick marks are those little lines on the edge of the graph).

To be mathematically correct, **a valid coordinate plane axis must maintain a constant numerical interval between equally spaced tick marks.** If you are counting your allowance, you can't label the lines 0, 10, 20, 100, 500. You have to jump by the exact same amount every time (like counting by 10s or 20s).

But here is a neat trick: the bottom line (horizontal) and the side line (vertical) don't have to match each other! **The horizontal axis and the vertical axis on a single graph can use entirely different numerical scales.** You might count days by 1s on the bottom, and count dollars by 10s on the side.

Why do we do this? Because **an appropriate graph scale allows the entire dataset to be plotted within the designated display area.** That way, your dots don't all get squished into a tiny, unreadable corner of your paper!

### Interpreting Graph Dimensions

When a graph uses two different units (like Distance in meters on the side, and Time in seconds on the bottom), the physical shape of the graph actually tells a story.

* **The units of the vertical axis and horizontal axis determine the unit of the slope of a graph.** The slope is how steep the line is.
* **The unit of the slope of a graph is calculated by dividing the vertical axis unit by the horizontal axis unit.** Here is how to find it step-by-step:
  1. Look at the unit on the side vertical axis (like meters).
  2. Look at the unit on the bottom horizontal axis (like seconds).
  3. Divide the vertical unit by the horizontal unit. Your slope's unit is meters per second!
* **The units of the vertical axis and horizontal axis determine the unit of the area under a graphed curve.**
* **The unit of the area under a curve is calculated by multiplying the vertical axis unit by the horizontal axis unit.** Here is the step-by-step:
  1. Look at the unit on the vertical axis.
  2. Look at the unit on the horizontal axis.
  3. Multiply them together to find the unit for your area!

### Visual Manipulation and Misleading Graphs

Your brain makes quick judgments when it sees steep lines or tall bars. Because of this, people can easily use graphs to trick you. You need to know how to spot these traps!

1. **Changing the numerical scale of an axis alters the visual steepness of a graphed line without changing the mathematical slope.** If someone stretches out the side axis, a slow, boring increase will suddenly look like a massive, explosive spike, even though the math hasn't changed at all!
2. **A bar graph or line graph that omits the origin on the vertical axis can create a misleading visual comparison.** The origin is the zero mark. If a video game company makes \$98,000 one year and \$99,000 the next year, they might start the graph at \$97,000 instead of 0. This makes the second bar look twice as tall as the first one, tricking you into thinking they doubled their money!

## Dimensional Analysis: Multiplying by One

Sometimes, a measurement is given in a unit you don't want. **Dimensional analysis is a mathematical method used to convert a quantity from one unit of measurement to another.** That's just a fancy way of saying "translating units."

The secret weapon for this is a **conversion factor**, which **is a ratio expressing how many of one unit are equal to another unit.** For example, 12 inches is the exact same thing as 1 foot.

Why are we allowed to multiply our numbers by these conversion factors? Because **the numerator and denominator of a valid conversion factor represent the exact same physical quantity.** (The numerator is the top of a fraction, and the denominator is the bottom). Since 12 inches and 1 foot are the exact same size, putting one on top of the other is mathematically equal to 1!

**Multiplying any measurement by a valid conversion factor is mathematically equivalent to multiplying the measurement by the number one.** The actual size of the object isn't growing or shrinking; we are just changing its name.

### The Algebra of Canceling Units

The greatest trick in translating units is treating the words themselves (like "inches" or "feet") as if they were variables in algebra. **In dimensional analysis calculations, units are treated as algebraic variables.**

This means you can cross them out! **Units present in both the numerator and denominator of a dimensional analysis expression cancel each other out algebraically.**

Let's convert 60 inches into feet step-by-step:
1. Write down what you are starting with: 60 inches.
2. Multiply it by your conversion factor: (1 foot / 12 inches). Notice how "inches" is on the bottom now!
3. Multiply the top numbers: 60 x 1 = 60.
4. Divide by the bottom number: 60 / 12 = 5.
5. Because "inches" was on the top to start, and on the bottom of the fraction, you can cross the word "inches" out completely!
6. Keep the word "feet" since it didn't get crossed out. Your answer is 5 feet.

### Complex Conversions: Rates, Area, and Volume

Things get a little trickier when you are converting speeds, flat areas, or 3D volumes. You can't just use one simple conversion factor.

* **Converting a rate requires applying separate conversion factors for the numerator unit and the denominator unit.** Let's say you want to turn 60 miles per hour into feet per second.
  1. Write down 60 miles / 1 hour.
  2. Pick a conversion factor to turn miles into feet (5280 feet / 1 mile) and cross out the word "miles."
  3. Pick a totally different conversion factor to turn hours into seconds (1 hour / 3600 seconds) and cross out the word "hours."
  4. Multiply your surviving numbers to get your final answer in feet per second!
* **Converting an area measurement requires applying a linear conversion factor twice.** Let's turn 1 square yard into square feet.
  1. Remember that a square yard is just a flat box that is 1 yard long and 1 yard wide.
  2. Change the length: 1 yard becomes 3 feet.
  3. Change the width: 1 yard becomes 3 feet.
  4. Multiply length times width: 3 feet x 3 feet = 9 square feet. You don't just multiply by 3, you multiply by 3 twice!
* **Converting a volume measurement requires applying a linear conversion factor three times.** Let's turn a cubic yard into cubic feet.
  1. A cubic yard is a 3D box: 1 yard long, 1 yard wide, and 1 yard tall.
  2. Change all three of those yards into 3 feet.
  3. Multiply all three sides: 3 feet x 3 feet x 3 feet = 27 cubic feet.

## The Rosetta Stone of Measurement: Essential Constants

To be awesome at translating units, there are a few facts you should memorize perfectly.

### The Customary System

This is the system mostly used in the United States, based on old historical measurements.

| Dimension | Customary Fact |
| :--- | :--- |
| Length | **One foot is equal to exactly 12 inches.** |
| Length | **One yard is equal to exactly 3 feet.** |
| Length | **One mile is equal to exactly 5280 feet.** |

### Time

Time is the same everywhere in the world! It's built on a system of 60s created by ancient people thousands of years ago.

| Dimension | Time Fact |
| :--- | :--- |
| Time | **One minute is equal to exactly 60 seconds.** |
| Time | **One hour is equal to exactly 60 minutes.** |
| Time | **One hour is equal to exactly 3600 seconds.** (Because 60 x 60 = 3600). |

### The Metric System (SI)

The metric system is awesome because it is built like our normal numbering system. **The metric system uses a base-10 mathematical structure to convert between units of the same type.** This means you can just slide the decimal point around!

It uses special code words (prefixes) at the beginning of words to tell you how big they are:
* **The metric prefix kilo multiplies a base unit by 1000.**
* **The metric prefix centi multiplies a base unit by 0.01.** (Just think of cents! One cent is 0.01 of a dollar).
* **The metric prefix milli multiplies a base unit by 0.001.**

Here is how you use those code words in real life:
* Because of the "kilo" part, **one kilometer is equal to exactly 1000 meters.**
* Because of the "centi" part, **one meter is equal to exactly 100 centimeters.**
* Because of the "kilo" part again, **one kilogram is equal to exactly 1000 grams.**

### Bridging the Systems

If you need to switch between the U.S. Customary system and the Metric system, there is one super important bridge you need to know:
* **One inch is exactly equal to 2.54 centimeters.**

When you master how these units work together, you get a real superpower. You can always tell if your math is right just by watching how the unit words cross out, combine, and change! If the final units make sense, you know your math is telling the truth.
