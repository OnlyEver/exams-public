Imagine you are making the perfect batch of slime. The recipe calls for 3 bottles of glue and 2 cups of shaving cream. If you want to make a bigger batch and decide to just add 1 extra bottle of glue and 1 extra cup of shaving cream, your slime will be ruined! It will be a sticky mess. Why? Because the ingredients in a recipe are tied together. You cannot just add random amounts; you have to scale them up together, multiplying them so they grow at the same speed. 

Learning how numbers grow together is like unlocking a superpower. It helps you understand everything from how fast your video game character is running, to how to find the best deal on pizza, to understanding how shapes work. 

## The Anatomy of a Ratio

To understand how to compare numbers like this, we need to know what a ratio is. **A ratio is a multiplicative comparison of two quantities.** That just means it tells us how much of one thing we have for every amount of another thing, and they grow by multiplying, not adding.

When we talk about ratios, the order of the items is super important! **The ratio of quantity A to quantity B is not equal to the ratio of quantity B to quantity A unless the two quantities are equal.** For example, imagine your school has 10 teachers and 200 students. A ratio of 10 teachers to 200 students makes total sense. But a ratio of 200 teachers to 10 students? That would be crazy—imagine 20 teachers watching over just 1 student! Order matters.

### Expressions of Ratios
There are three ways we can write down this comparison:
1. **Ratios can be expressed mathematically using a colon.** (Example: 3:2)
2. **Ratios can be expressed verbally using the word 'to'.** (Example: "3 to 2")
3. **Ratios can be expressed numerically using fraction notation.** (Example: 3/2)

### Parts and Wholes
When we look at groups of things, we can compare them in different ways. 
* **A ratio can describe a part-to-part relationship between subsets within a whole.** For example, comparing 3 bottles of glue to 2 cups of shaving cream. You are comparing one part of the recipe to another part.
* **A ratio can describe a part-to-whole relationship comparing a subset to the entire group.** For example, comparing 3 bottles of glue to all 5 ingredients in the bowl.

> **A Quick Trick for Percentages**
> **A percentage is a specific type of part-to-whole ratio where the whole is defined as exactly 100.** Because percentages always look at the *whole* group, **converting a part-to-part ratio to a percentage requires first calculating the total sum of the parts to represent the whole.** 
> 
> Let's say an animal shelter has a ratio of 3 cats to 1 dog. What percentage of the animals are cats?
> 1. Add the parts together to find the whole group: 3 cats + 1 dog = 4 total animals.
> 2. Write the part-to-whole ratio as a fraction: 3 cats / 4 total animals.
> 3. Convert that fraction into a percentage by making the bottom number 100: Since 3/4 equals 75/100, the shelter is 75% cats!

### The Universal Constants of Ratios
Ratios are so special that they are built into nature and shapes. If you measure any perfect circle, **the ratio of the circumference of any Euclidean circle to its diameter is exactly equal to the mathematical constant pi.** It doesn't matter if it is a tiny coin or a giant steering wheel, that ratio never changes! Similarly, in nature and art, **the golden ratio is an irrational number approximately equal to 1.618.** These are famous, unbreakable rules of math.

## Rates and the Power of 'Per'

When we use ratios to compare things that are measured in completely different ways, we call it a rate. **A rate is a ratio that compares two quantities measured in different units.** 

If you are playing a racing game and drive 150 miles in 3 hours, you are comparing distance (miles) to time (hours). **Speed is a rate that compares the distance an object travels to the time elapsed.** Scientists use rates all the time, too. In science class, you might learn that **density is a physical rate that compares the mass of a substance to its volume.** 

### The Unit Rate
Rates become really helpful when we shrink them down to a single unit. **A unit rate is a rate that compares a quantity to exactly one unit of another quantity.** 

When we talk out loud, **the word 'per' is used in unit rates to indicate a mathematical comparison to exactly one unit.** You hear this all the time: miles *per* hour, points *per* game, or dollars *per* slice of pizza. To figure this out, **the unit rate associated with the ratio A to B is the numerical value of A divided by B.**

One of the coolest ways to use unit rates is when you are spending your allowance. **Unit price is a unit rate representing the monetary cost of exactly one unit of an item.** This helps you find the best deals! **Dividing the total price of an item by the total quantity of the item yields the unit price.** 

Let's say 5 apples cost \$4.00. How much is just one apple?
1. Find your total price (\$4.00) and your total quantity (5 apples).
2. Divide the total price by the total quantity: 4.00 / 5.
3. Your answer is your unit price: \$0.80 per apple.

### Navigating Complex Fractions
Sometimes the real world doesn't give us easy, whole numbers. You might see rates like walking 1/2 of a mile in 1/4 of an hour. When a fraction has another fraction inside it, we call it a complex fraction. **Complex fractions are fractions in which the numerator, the denominator, or both contain fractions.**

**Calculating the unit rate of quantities measured in fractions requires evaluating a complex fraction using division.** Let's figure out how fast you were walking!

1. Set up your division problem with your fractions: (1/2) / (1/4).
2. To divide fractions, you flip the second fraction upside down and multiply instead: 1/2 * 4/1.
3. Multiply the top numbers together (1 * 4 = 4).
4. Multiply the bottom numbers together (2 * 1 = 2).
5. Divide your final fraction to get your unit rate: 4 / 2 = 2 miles per hour.

## The Algebra of Equivalence

In math, it is really important to know when two different-looking things actually mean the exact same thing. **Equivalent ratios are distinct ratios that express the same multiplicative relationship between two quantities.** For example, scoring 2 points in 3 games is the exact same pattern as scoring 4 points in 6 games. 

How do we create these matching patterns?
* **Multiplying both terms of a ratio by the same non-zero number generates an equivalent ratio.**
* **Dividing both terms of a ratio by the same non-zero number generates an equivalent ratio.**

When we put an equals sign between two ratios, we are doing algebra! **A proportion is an algebraic equation stating that two ratios are mathematically equivalent.** (Like writing x / 5 = 4 / 10).

To solve these puzzles, we use a neat shortcut. **Cross-multiplication is an algebraic method used to solve for an unknown term in a proportion.** Here is how to use the shortcut:

1. Write your proportion: x / 5 = 4 / 10.
2. Multiply diagonally across the equals sign (cross-multiply): x * 10 = 5 * 4.
3. Do the multiplication you know: 10x = 20.
4. Divide both sides by 10 to find the missing piece: x = 2.

We also use this kind of matching math with pictures. Think about zooming in on a picture on your phone. **A scale factor is a ratio used to proportionally enlarge or reduce the size of a geometric figure.** If you zoom in by a scale factor of 3, every single part of the picture gets multiplied by 3 so it doesn't look stretched out or weird.

## Visualizing the Multiplicative World

Before we do fancy algebra, it helps to draw pictures. Here are three awesome ways to draw out a math problem:

| Visual Model | Definition & Best Use Case |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Tape Diagram** | **A tape diagram is a visual model consisting of segmented rectangles used to represent the relative sizes of quantities in a ratio.** *Best for:* Parts of the same whole, like mixing 3 blocks of yellow paint and 2 blocks of blue paint. |
| **Double Number Line** | **A double number line diagram uses two parallel number lines with aligned tick marks to model equivalent rates.** *Best for:* Looking at two different units growing together, like miles on the top line matching up with hours on the bottom line. |
| **Ratio Table** | **A ratio table is a structured mathematical display organizing equivalent ratios into corresponding rows or columns.** *Best for:* Keeping your numbers perfectly organized so you can spot missing pieces or get ready to graph them. |

## Graphing Proportional Relationships

The coolest thing you can do with a ratio table is draw it on a graph! 

When two things grow together perfectly, we can write an equation for them that looks like y = kx. **In the proportional equation y = kx, the constant of proportionality k represents the unit rate.** 

If you take your numbers and plot them on a graph, you will always notice two amazing things:
1. **The graph of a proportional relationship on a Cartesian coordinate plane is always a straight line.** It will never zigzag or curve!
2. **The graph of a proportional relationship on a Cartesian coordinate plane always passes through the origin.** The origin is the point (0,0). This makes sense: if you buy 0 slices of pizza, it costs 0 dollars.

> **Reading the Graph**
> The tilt of the line tells us a secret. **The slope of a graphed proportional relationship represents the unit rate of the relationship.** 
> 
> If you want to find out the unit rate just by looking at a graph, look for the number 1 on the bottom line (the x-axis) and see how high the line goes up. **The coordinate pair (1, r) on the graph of a proportional relationship indicates that the value r is the unit rate.** Because the bottom line shows exactly 1 unit, whatever the line hits on the side is your "per 1" amount!

Learning ratios means learning the secret code of math. Whether you are finding out which snack is cheaper, measuring how fast a roller coaster goes, or graphing a perfectly straight line, you are using the exact same skills!
