Math is like a multiplayer video game. For everyone to play together, you all need the exact same rules. When you see a math problem or a real-life situation involving numbers, the order you do the steps isn't just about what you feel like doing; it follows strict game rules. Without these rules, a single math problem could give you six different answers! That would be super confusing. We are going to look at exactly how to solve problems step by step, so you always get the winning answer and understand why numbers work the way they do.

## The Syntax of Calculation: Order of Operations

The order of operations determines the specific sequence of steps for evaluating mathematical expressions. It is like a cheat code that guarantees everyone gets the exact same answer. For example, if you have 8 - 2 x 3, the rules make sure everyone gets 2, instead of 18. You might have heard of the trick "PEMDAS." Sometimes this memory trick confuses people, so let's break down the real rules of the game.

### Step 1: Grouping Symbols (The Overrides)
Grouping symbols are evaluated first in the mathematical order of operations. Think of them as the ultimate VIP pass. They explicitly tell you, "Compute the value inside here before doing anything else."

You probably know the normal ones like parentheses (), brackets [], and curly braces {}. But you have to watch out for the sneaky ones! Grouping symbols include parentheses, brackets, braces, fraction bars, radical symbols, and absolute value bars. 

*   **Fraction Bars:** A fraction bar acts as a grouping symbol requiring the numerator (top) and denominator (bottom) to be fully evaluated before division.
    *   Example: (10 + 2) / (5 - 1)
    *   Step 1: Do the math on the top part first. 10 + 2 = 12.
    *   Step 2: Do the math on the bottom part next. 5 - 1 = 4.
    *   Step 3: Now divide the top answer by the bottom answer. 12 / 4 = 3.
*   **Radical Symbols (Roots):** Radical symbols act as grouping symbols requiring the radicand (the expression underneath the root) to be evaluated before extracting the root.
    *   Example: square root of (16 + 9)
    *   Step 1: Do the math inside the root symbol first. 16 + 9 = 25.
    *   Step 2: Now find the square root of 25. The answer is 5.
*   **Absolute Value Bars:** Absolute value bars act as grouping symbols requiring the expression inside to be evaluated before finding the absolute value.
    *   Example: |3 - 8|
    *   Step 1: Do the math inside the bars. 3 - 8 = -5.
    *   Step 2: Take the absolute value (which makes it positive). The answer is 5.

### Step 2: Exponents and Roots
Exponents and roots are evaluated second in the mathematical order of operations. Once all the VIP groups are taken care of and squished down to single numbers, you handle any little numbers floating at the top (exponents) and their opposites (roots).

### Step 3: Multiplication and Division
Multiplication and division are evaluated third in the mathematical order of operations.

> **Crucial Warning:** Multiplication does not take mathematical precedence over division. 

This is where the PEMDAS trick messes people up. Kids think "M" comes before "D", so multiply always comes first. That is totally false! Multiplication and division hold equal rank. Because they are equal, multiplication and division are evaluated sequentially from left to right as they appear in the mathematical expression. Just like reading a book!
*   Example: 12 / 3 x 2
*   Step 1: Read left to right. The division comes first. 12 / 3 = 4.
*   Step 2: Take that answer and multiply. 4 x 2 = 8.

### Step 4: Addition and Subtraction
Addition and subtraction are evaluated last in the mathematical order of operations.

> **Crucial Warning:** Addition does not take mathematical precedence over subtraction. 

Exactly like their teammates above, addition and subtraction are perfectly equal. Addition and subtraction are evaluated sequentially from left to right as they appear in the mathematical expression.
*   Example: 10 - 4 + 2
*   Step 1: Read left to right. The subtraction is first. 10 - 4 = 6.
*   Step 2: Take that answer and add. 6 + 2 = 8.

## The Arithmetic of Signs

In middle school, you will deal with negative numbers a lot. You can't just think about "holding 5 apples" anymore. Imagine positive numbers are money you have, and negative numbers are money you owe.

### Addition and Subtraction
*   **Adding Negatives:** Adding two negative numbers results in a negative sum. If you owe a friend \$10 and you borrow \$5 more, your debt just increases.
    *   Example: (-10) + (-5)
    *   Step 1: Combine the amounts you owe. 10 + 5 = 15.
    *   Step 2: Keep the negative sign because it is money owed. The answer is -15.
*   **Mixed Signs in Addition:** The sign of the sum of a positive number and a negative number matches the sign of the number with the larger absolute value. Think of this like a tug-of-war.
    *   Example: -12 + 7
    *   Step 1: Look at who is pulling harder. 12 is bigger than 7, so the negatives win the tug-of-war.
    *   Step 2: Find the difference between 12 and 7. 12 - 7 = 5.
    *   Step 3: Give the answer the winner's sign. The answer is -5.
*   **Subtracting Negatives:** Subtracting a negative number is mathematically equivalent to adding the corresponding positive number. If someone takes away (subtracts) a debt (a negative), your money actually goes up! 
    *   Example: 8 - (-3)
    *   Step 1: Change subtracting a negative into adding a positive. It becomes 8 + 3.
    *   Step 2: Add normally. The answer is 11.

### Multiplication and Division
The rules here are super easy if you think about matching pairs:
*   Multiplying or dividing two numbers with the identical sign results in a positive number. If they match, they are positive!
    *   Example: (-4) x (-5)
    *   Step 1: Multiply 4 x 5 = 20.
    *   Step 2: Since both numbers were negative (identical signs), the answer is positive 20.
*   Multiplying or dividing two numbers with opposite signs results in a negative number. If they don't match, they are negative!
    *   Example: (-20) / 4
    *   Step 1: Divide 20 / 4 = 5.
    *   Step 2: Since one is negative and one is positive (opposite signs), the answer is -5.

## The Landscape of Real Numbers

Numbers come in different types, sort of like different classes of video game characters. 

### Defining the Categories

**Rational Numbers:** A rational number can be expressed as the ratio of two integers where the denominator is not equal to zero. These are friendly, predictable numbers. They can be whole numbers, clean fractions like 1/2, or decimals that end or repeat nicely. 
*Fact:* Zero is a rational number, because you can write it as the fraction 0/1.

**Irrational Numbers:** An irrational number cannot be expressed as a simple fraction of two integers. These numbers are messy. They are decimals that go on forever and never repeat a pattern, like the famous number Pi (3.14159...) or the square root of 2.

### The Chemistry of Combining Numbers

What happens when we mix these numbers together? It helps to think of irrational numbers like spilled glitter—if you mix it with something clean, the whole thing usually gets messy!

**Combining Rationals:**
*   The sum of two rational numbers is always a rational number. 
*   The product of two rational numbers is always a rational number. 
If you add or multiply two clean, predictable numbers together, you just get another clean number.

**Mixing Rationals and Irrationals:**
*   The sum of a rational number and an irrational number is always an irrational number. If you add 3 (clean) to Pi (messy glitter), the answer is still a messy decimal.
*   The product of a non-zero rational number and an irrational number is always an irrational number. Multiplying 2 x (square root of 5) just makes a bigger messy number.
*   *The Super Power:* The product of zero and any irrational number is exactly zero. Because zero is a rational number, this is the one single time the messy glitter is completely wiped out!

**Combining Irrationals with Irrationals:**
Sometimes when you mix two messy numbers, the messes perfectly cancel each other out! 
*   The sum of two irrational numbers can result in an irrational number. 
    *   Example: Pi + Pi 
    *   Step 1: Add them together to get 2 x Pi. Still messy!
*   The sum of two irrational numbers can result in a rational number. 
    *   Example: (3 + square root of 2) + (3 - square root of 2)
    *   Step 1: The positive root and negative root cancel each other out.
    *   Step 2: Add 3 + 3 to get 6. Clean!
*   The product of two irrational numbers can result in an irrational number.
    *   Example: (square root of 2) x (square root of 3)
    *   Step 1: Multiply 2 x 3 = 6. 
    *   Step 2: Keep it under the root to get square root of 6. Still messy!
*   The product of two irrational numbers can result in a rational number.
    *   Example: (square root of 2) x (square root of 2)
    *   Step 1: Multiply 2 x 2 = 4 inside the root.
    *   Step 2: Find the square root of 4, which is 2. Clean!

## Translating the Real World: Word Problems

Math word problems are really just a translation game. You just have to translate English sentences into Math sentences. Here is a handy dictionary to help you:

| English Phrase | Mathematical Translation |
| :--- | :--- |
| **"Sum of"** | The phrase "sum of" in a word problem indicates the operation of mathematical addition. |
| **"Difference between"** | The phrase "difference between" in a word problem indicates the operation of mathematical subtraction. |
| **"Product"** | The word "product" in a word problem indicates the operation of mathematical multiplication. |
| **"Quotient"** | The word "quotient" in a word problem indicates the operation of mathematical division. |
| **"Per"** | The word "per" in a word problem indicates the operation of mathematical division. For example, miles *per* hour means miles / hours. |
| **"Of"** | The word "of" in a word problem frequently indicates the operation of multiplication when used alongside fractions or percentages. For example, "half *of* \$50" means 1/2 x 50. |

### The "Less Than" Trap
There is a trick that catches almost everyone off-guard. 

> **Translation Warning:** The phrase "less than" in a word problem dictates reversing the order of the terms in a subtraction expression.

If someone asks, "What is 5 less than 12?", you might want to read left-to-right and write 5 - 12. But wait! If you have \$12 and I give you \$5 less than that, we are starting with 12 and taking 5 away.
*   Step 1: Identify the starting amount (12).
*   Step 2: Identify what is being taken away (5).
*   Step 3: Write it backwards from how it sounds: 12 - 5.

### Putting It All Together in Context

Let's practice with a real-world story, like buying tablets for your school. 

*"A school buys tablets for \$300 each. The school receives a \$50 discount per tablet. If they buy half of a 40-unit shipment, and then pay a flat \$100 shipping fee for the whole order, write an expression for the total cost."*

Here is how your new vocabulary dictates the setup:
*   Step 1: Handle the price of one tablet. The word "discount" means we subtract. We write (300 - 50). We use parentheses (grouping symbols) because we have to find the new price *before* we buy a bunch of them.
*   Step 2: Handle the number of tablets. The word "of" next to a fraction implies multiplication. We write 1/2 x 40.
*   Step 3: Multiply the new tablet price by the amount of tablets. That looks like (300 - 50) x (1/2 x 40).
*   Step 4: Handle the shipping fee. A "flat fee" means we do a final addition at the very end. We add + 100.

The final math sentence is: [(300 - 50) x (1/2 x 40)] + 100. 

By knowing your game rules (order of operations), your money rules (positive and negative signs), your character types (rational and irrational numbers), and your translator dictionary, you will be able to easily write and solve any math challenge that comes your way!
