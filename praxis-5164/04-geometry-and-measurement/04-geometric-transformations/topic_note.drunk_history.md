Every time you manipulate a shape, a geometric transformation maps an original figure called a preimage onto a new figure called an image. Think of the preimage as the raw, exposed informant spilling their guts, and the image as their brand-new, post-makeover identity living quietly in Omaha. Our job is to move them without breaking them. 

When we are dealing with our VIP informants, we use the gold standard: rigid transformations. A rigid transformation preserves the absolute distance between any two points on a geometric figure, meaning our guy doesn’t mysteriously lose an inch of height in transit. Because the side lengths are locked down, a rigid transformation preserves the angle measures of a geometric figure. His elbows bend the exact same way. It goes even deeper into his cellular geometry—a rigid transformation preserves the collinearity of points from the preimage to the image, meaning if three freckles were in a perfectly straight line before, they stay in a perfectly straight line after. It even maps parallel lines on a preimage to parallel lines on the resulting image! The mafia calls these untouchable movements "isometries." Rigid geometric transformations are also referred to mathematically as isometries. If you apply a sequence consisting solely of rigid transformations, it maps a preimage to a geometrically congruent image. It’s the exact same guy, just standing in a different part of the grid! 

Now, how do we move him? The cleanest getaway is the translation. A translation is categorized as a rigid transformation because it slides a figure along a straight path without changing the orientation or size of the geometric figure. It’s a smooth, silent glide out the back door. But you can’t just tell a getaway driver to "drive." A translation is uniquely specified by a translation vector. This vector is your encrypted GPS coordinate; a translation vector defines both the horizontal shift and the vertical shift applied to a preimage. In the digital language of your calculator, a translation by $a$ units horizontally and $b$ units vertically maps a coordinate point $(x, y)$ to the point $(x + a, y + b)$. And because it’s just a smooth glide, a translation preserves the geometric orientation of a polygon. Geometric orientation refers to the arrangement of points in a specific clockwise or counterclockwise sequence around a polygon perimeter. If his pockets went watch, wallet, keys clockwise, they still go watch, wallet, keys. 

But sometimes, the feds need to use a decoy. Enter the reflection. A reflection is categorized as a rigid transformation, but it creates the ultimate evil twin! A reflection flips a geometric figure across a specific line to create a mirror image. It’s completely anchored by that boundary—a reflection transformation is uniquely specified by a single line of reflection. Imagine this line is a massive, impenetrable pane of glass. This line of reflection acts as the exact perpendicular bisector of the segment connecting a preimage point to the corresponding image point. And what happens to the dust sitting directly on that glass? Nothing! Every point located precisely on the line of reflection acts as a fixed point during a reflection transformation. A fixed point is a coordinate location that maps to its exact original position after a geometric transformation. It refuses to budge. Now, when you create an evil twin, things get weird. A reflection reverses the geometric orientation of a polygon! His clockwise pockets are now counterclockwise. Left is right! You need to have the algebraic codes for these mirrors memorized for your exam. A reflection across the x-axis maps a coordinate point $(x, y)$ to the point $(x, -y)$. A reflection across the y-axis maps a coordinate point $(x, y)$ to the point $(-x, y)$. If the mirror is tilted, like a reflection across the linear equation $y = x$, it maps a coordinate point $(x, y)$ to the point $(y, x)$. And a reflection across the linear equation $y = -x$ maps a coordinate point $(x, y)$ to the point $(-y, -x)$. There’s also the ultimate trapdoor trick: a point reflection across the origin maps a coordinate point $(x, y)$ to the point $(-x, -y)$, essentially pulling the shape through the origin and turning it inside out.

The third isometry is for the dramatic reveals. A rotation is categorized as a rigid transformation because a rotation turns a geometric figure around a fixed point by a specific angle measurement. Think of a supervillain spinning around in a massive leather desk chair. To pull this off, a rotation transformation is specified by a center of rotation, an angle of rotation, and a direction of rotation. The chair’s bolted base doesn’t move, meaning the exact center of rotation acts as a fixed point during a rotation transformation. Because the guy is just spinning, a rotation preserves the geometric orientation of a polygon. But beware the coordinate plane’s twisted sense of direction! Math rebels against the clock. A positive angle of rotation in the coordinate plane dictates a counterclockwise turning direction, while a negative angle of rotation in the coordinate plane dictates a clockwise turning direction. If your center is the origin, you must know the coordinates of the spin. A rotation of 90 degrees counterclockwise about the origin maps a coordinate point $(x, y)$ to the point $(-y, x)$. A rotation of 180 degrees about the origin maps a coordinate point $(x, y)$ to the point $(-x, -y)$—which, if you’re paying attention, is the exact same coordinate mapping as that point reflection across the origin we just talked about! Two different aliases, same dead drop. A rotation of 270 degrees counterclockwise about the origin maps a coordinate point $(x, y)$ to the point $(y, -x)$. And because it's a circle, a rotation of 90 degrees clockwise about the origin produces identical coordinates to a rotation of 270 degrees counterclockwise. 

What if you stumble onto a crime scene and just see the preimage and a rotated image, but the chair is missing? How do you find the center of rotation? You do the forensic geometry! The geometric center of rotation lies exactly on the perpendicular bisector of every segment connecting a preimage point to the corresponding rotated image point. You draw segments connecting the "before" and "after" points, find their perpendicular bisectors, and boom—the intersection coordinate of two perpendicular bisectors of segments connecting preimage points to corresponding rotated image points reveals the center of rotation. 

Now, let's say the mob is closing in and witness protection isn't enough. We need a sci-fi mutation. We need the Dilation Ray! A dilation is a non-rigid transformation that changes the overall size of a geometric figure. It blows them up or shrinks them down! But it’s not a funhouse mirror; it keeps their DNA intact. A dilation preserves the proportional shape and angle measures of a geometric figure. Just like our rigid friends, a dilation preserves the collinearity of points from the preimage to the resulting image, and maps parallel lines on a preimage to parallel lines on the resulting image. It even preserves the geometric orientation of a polygon! To fire this ray gun, a dilation transformation is specified by a center of dilation and a numerical scale factor. The blast originates from the nozzle, so the exact center of dilation acts as a fixed point during a dilation transformation. If you fire from the origin, it’s simple: a dilation with the center at the origin and a scale factor of $k$ maps a coordinate point $(x, y)$ to the point $(kx, ky)$. 

The power dial on this ray gun is $k$. The scale factor of a dilation is the ratio of a side length of the image to the corresponding side length of the preimage. Because it’s a multiplier, the distance between any two points on a dilated image equals the corresponding preimage distance multiplied by the absolute value of the scale factor. If you crank it up, a dilation produces an enlargement of the preimage if the absolute value of the scale factor is strictly greater than one. If you want a pocket-sized informant, a dilation produces a reduction of the preimage if the absolute value of the scale factor is strictly between zero and one. If you panic and fire a dilation with a scale factor of exactly one, it maps the preimage onto identical image coordinates—nothing happens! But here is the craziest setting: a dilation with a negative scale factor produces an image that is rotated 180 degrees around the center of dilation relative to the preimage. It shrinks them, pulls them completely through the nozzle of the gun, and blows them out the back upside down! 

When you use the dilation ray on infinite lines, the geometry gets hauntingly beautiful. A dilation maps a line not passing through the center of dilation to a strictly parallel line. It just pushes the whole line away or pulls it closer. But a dilation maps a line passing through the center of dilation directly onto that exact same line; it just stretches endlessly along itself. If you walk onto a scene and need to locate the unseen dilation ray, it’s incredibly easy. Straight lines connecting corresponding vertices on a dilated preimage and its image will always intersect at the specific center of dilation. Just follow the laser beams backward! And remember, because the size changed but the angles didn't, a sequence of transformations containing at least one dilation produces an image that is geometrically similar to the preimage, not congruent. 

In the real world, a heist is never just one move. The composition of geometric transformations involves the successive mathematical application of two or more transformations. You translate, then reflect, then rotate. But this is the most critical rule of the game: the composition of geometric transformations is fundamentally not a commutative mathematical operation! You cannot put your shoes on and then your socks! Reversing the execution order of transformations in a composition sequence frequently alters the final coordinate positions of the image. You will send your witness to the bottom of the ocean instead of Paris! 

Sometimes, composition sequences create terrifying illusions, specifically the double-mirror tricks. The composition of two separate reflections across parallel lines is mathematically equivalent to a single translation. It bounces off one wall, off the other, and boom, it just looks like it slid over. And how far did it slide? The overall distance of the translation resulting from two reflections across parallel lines is exactly twice the distance separating the parallel lines. If the mirrors aren't parallel? The composition of two separate reflections across intersecting lines is mathematically equivalent to a single rotation! The specific angle of the rotation resulting from two reflections across intersecting lines is exactly twice the angle formed between the intersecting lines. 

So, as the detective, how do you look at the final, mangled composition sequence and know if a mirror was used? You check their pockets! Comparing the orientation of a preimage and an image helps mathematically identify the presence of a reflection in a transformation sequence. An image demonstrating reversed orientation relative to the preimage indicates that an odd number of reflections occurred in the overall transformation sequence. One, three, five flips—they are inside out. But an image demonstrating identical orientation relative to the preimage indicates that an even number of reflections occurred in the overall transformation sequence. They flipped inside out, and then flipped right side back in!
