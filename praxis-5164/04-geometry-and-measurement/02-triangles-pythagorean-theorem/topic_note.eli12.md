If you hand a kid three random Popsicle sticks and ask them to build a flat, closed triangle, they will quickly learn something cool about math: it has strict rules, just like a video game. You cannot force any three random sticks to close perfectly into a triangle. But when they do close, that triangle is super strong! Unlike a square built of sticks, which can easily flop over into a slanted shape if you push on it, a triangle’s sides lock its angles into place forever. This super-strong grip is why every bridge, crane, and skateboard ramp in the world is built using triangles. 

Let us look at the awesome rules of the triangle, from the basic rules of how they are built to the famous magic trick known as the Pythagorean theorem!

## The Architecture of a Triangle: Existence and Classification

Before we do any math, we have to know if a triangle is even allowed to exist. A triangle is a perfect team-up between three side lengths and three inside angles. 

### The Rule of Angles
A flat triangle exists on a very strict points budget. **The sum of the interior angles of any planar triangle is always exactly 180 degrees.** If you are playing a trivia game and see a question where the given interior angle measures do not sum exactly to 180 degrees, the answer is always a hard "no." **No triangle can be formed if the given interior angle measures do not sum exactly to 180 degrees.**

If you take one side of a triangle and draw a straight line extending out of it, you make an "exterior" (outside) angle. **The measure of an exterior angle of a triangle equals the sum of the measures of the two remote interior angles.** (Remote interior angles are just the two inside angles that are furthest away from it). 

Here is an example of finding an exterior angle:
1. Identify the two remote interior angles (for example, 50 degrees and 60 degrees).
2. Add them together: 50 + 60 = 110.
3. The exterior angle is exactly 110 degrees!

### Classifying by Side Lengths
Triangles are grouped into different families based on matching sides. In math, when sides match perfectly, we call them "congruent." 
*   **Scalene triangles are defined as triangles having zero congruent sides.** Because the side lengths tell the angles how wide to open, **scalene triangles have three interior angles of completely distinct measures** (none of them match).
*   **Isosceles triangles are defined as triangles having at least two congruent sides.** Their matching sides create a cool mirror effect: **the angles opposite the congruent sides in an isosceles triangle are structurally congruent to each other.**
*   **Equilateral triangles are defined as triangles having exactly three congruent sides.** 

Because an equilateral triangle has 3 matching sides, it must share its total angle budget equally. **Every equilateral triangle has three interior angles that each measure exactly 60 degrees.**
Here is the math:
1. Start with the total degrees for any triangle: 180.
2. Divide by the 3 equal angles: 180 / 3.
3. The answer is exactly 60 degrees.

> **The Big Rule:** In *any* triangle, the sides and angles are linked. Think of a door—opening it just a tiny bit creates a small gap, while opening it super wide creates a huge gap. Because of this, **the shortest leg of any triangle is always positioned strictly opposite the smallest interior angle.** 

### The Triangle Inequality Theorem
How do we know if three sticks can even touch to make a triangle? **The Triangle Inequality Theorem dictates that the sum of any two side lengths of a triangle must be strictly greater than the third side length.**

**To form a valid triangle, the longest side must be strictly less than the sum of the two shorter sides.** If a friend tries to build a triangle with sides that are 2 inches, 3 inches, and 6 inches, the 2-inch and 3-inch sides will just fold flat against the 6-inch side and never touch. **No triangle can be formed if the sum of the two shorter given side lengths is exactly equal to the longest given side length.** (They would just lay perfectly flat on top of the long side!)

If you know two sides of a triangle and need to find the mystery third side, there is a cool trick: **The length of any unknown side of a triangle must be strictly greater than the positive difference of the other two known side lengths.**

Here is how you find the safe zone for a mystery side if your known sides are 5 and 8:
1. Find the positive difference (subtract the smaller from the larger): 8 - 5 = 3.
2. Find the sum (add them together): 8 + 5 = 13.
3. The unknown side must be strictly greater than 3, and strictly less than 13!

## Creating Unique Triangles: The Congruence Postulates

When you give a group of builders specific measurements, you are giving them instructions. Depending on the rules, they might be able to build an infinite number of triangles, exactly one unique triangle, or zero triangles!

### Generating Exactly One Unique Triangle
If you hand out specific combinations of sides (S) and angles (A), the rules of math lock the triangle into place. These specific sets of instructions are called geometric conditions. The following conditions guarantee the formation of **exactly one unique triangle** (every kid in class will draw the exact same perfect clone):
*   **The Side-Side-Side geometric condition guarantees the formation of exactly one unique triangle.**
*   **The Side-Angle-Side geometric condition guarantees the formation of exactly one unique triangle.**
*   **The Angle-Side-Angle geometric condition guarantees the formation of exactly one unique triangle.**
*   **The Angle-Angle-Side geometric condition guarantees the formation of exactly one unique triangle.**

### The Infinite Scaled Clones (AAA)
What happens if you give everyone just the three angles, called the Angle-Angle-Angle given conditions? Think about pinching to zoom in on a photo on a smartphone. The angles stay the same, but the picture can get huge or tiny! Because of this, **the Angle-Angle-Angle given conditions produce an infinite number of scaled similar triangles rather than a single unique triangle.**

### The Ambiguous Case (SSA)
Imagine a broken video game glitch where you press a button and you have no idea what is going to happen. **The Side-Side-Angle given conditions are considered an ambiguous case in geometry.** Because the angle is not locked between the two known sides, the unknown side swings around wildly like a broken door. 
Because of this swinging door, **the Side-Side-Angle conditions can result in exactly zero valid triangles depending on the specific measurements** (the door is too short to reach the wall). Or, **the Side-Side-Angle conditions can result in exactly one unique triangle depending on the specific measurements.** Finally, **the Side-Side-Angle conditions can result in exactly two distinct valid triangles depending on the specific measurements** (the door can click into two totally different spots).

## The Pythagorean Theorem: Nature's Perfect Relationship

When the interior angle of a triangle hits exactly 90 degrees (a perfect corner, like on a piece of paper), a mathematical magic trick happens. **The Pythagorean theorem states that the square of the hypotenuse equals the sum of the squares of the other two sides in a right triangle.** 

**The algebraic formula for the Pythagorean theorem is a^2 + b^2 = c^2.**

Let's look at the parts of this famous formula so we know exactly what is going on:
*   **In the Pythagorean theorem formula a^2 + b^2 = c^2, the variables a and b represent the lengths of the legs of a right triangle.**
*   **The legs of a right triangle are the two shorter sides that intersect to form the 90-degree angle.**
*   **In the Pythagorean theorem formula a^2 + b^2 = c^2, the variable c represents the length of the hypotenuse of a right triangle.**
*   **The hypotenuse is the longest side of a right triangle.**
*   Just like how a door opens widest when pushed the hardest, **the hypotenuse of a right triangle is always located directly opposite the 90-degree angle.**

### Applying the Converse to Classify Triangles
This magic formula also works perfectly in reverse! **The converse of the Pythagorean theorem states that a triangle is a right triangle if the square of the longest side equals the sum of the squares of the other two sides.** 

But what if the math doesn't perfectly balance? You can use the imbalance to classify the triangle without even drawing it! Always test the longest side (c) first:
*   **A triangle is acute if the square of its longest side is strictly less than the sum of the squares of its two shorter sides.** 
*   **A triangle is obtuse if the square of its longest side is strictly greater than the sum of the squares of its two shorter sides.**

Here is an example of testing a triangle with sides 4, 5, and 7:
1. Identify the longest side (c): 7.
2. Identify the two shorter legs (a and b): 4 and 5.
3. Square the longest side: 7 * 7 = 49.
4. Square the shorter sides and add them together: (4 * 4) + (5 * 5) = 16 + 25.
5. Finish the addition: 16 + 25 = 41.
6. Compare them: 49 is strictly greater than 41. Therefore, the triangle is obtuse!

## Integer Magic: Pythagorean Triples

Doing square roots by hand can be annoying. Luckily, we have cheat codes! **Pythagorean triples are sets of three positive integers that perfectly satisfy the Pythagorean theorem.** 

These are clean, whole numbers (no messy decimals). Here are the most famous cheat codes to memorize:
*   **The integer set 3, 4, 5 is a standard Pythagorean triple.** 
*   **The integer set 5, 12, 13 is a standard Pythagorean triple.**
*   **The integer set 8, 15, 17 is a standard Pythagorean triple.**
*   **The integer set 7, 24, 25 is a standard Pythagorean triple.**

The coolest part? You can scale them up! **Multiplying each number in a Pythagorean triple by the same positive integer produces another valid Pythagorean triple.** 

Here is how you scale up the 3-4-5 triple by multiplying it by 10:
1. Multiply the first side: 3 * 10 = 30.
2. Multiply the second side: 4 * 10 = 40.
3. Multiply the third side: 5 * 10 = 50.
4. Your brand new valid triple is 30, 40, 50!

## Special Right Triangles: The Shortcuts of the Universe

While Pythagorean triples are shortcuts for whole number *sides*, special right triangles give us shortcuts for perfect *angles*.

### The 45-45-90 Triangle
Imagine taking a perfectly square piece of pizza and slicing it exactly from corner to corner. You just made one! **A 45-45-90 triangle is a special right triangle with two 45-degree angles and one 90-degree angle.**
*   Because two angles perfectly match, **a 45-45-90 triangle is an isosceles right triangle.**
*   This means **in a 45-45-90 triangle, the two legs are perfectly congruent in length.**
*   Here is the shortcut rule: **In a 45-45-90 triangle, the hypotenuse length is equal to the leg length multiplied by the square root of 2.**

Here is an example to find the hypotenuse if the leg is 6:
1. Identify the leg length: 6.
2. Multiply the leg by the square root of 2: 6 * sqrt(2).
3. The hypotenuse is exactly 6 * sqrt(2)!

### The 30-60-90 Triangle
Now, imagine a perfect equilateral triangle. Drop a straight line down from the top point to slice the bottom edge exactly in half. **A 30-60-90 triangle is a special right triangle with angles measuring 30 degrees, 60 degrees, and 90 degrees.**
*   Because you chopped that bottom edge perfectly in half, **in a 30-60-90 triangle, the hypotenuse is exactly twice the length of the shortest leg.**
*   To find the middle-sized side, use this shortcut: **in a 30-60-90 triangle, the longest leg is equal to the shortest leg multiplied by the square root of 3.**
*   Remember the rule about doors opening? **The longest leg of a 30-60-90 triangle is always positioned strictly opposite the 60-degree angle.** 

Here is how to solve a 30-60-90 triangle if the shortest leg is 5:
1. Identify the shortest leg: 5.
2. Find the hypotenuse by multiplying by 2: 5 * 2 = 10.
3. Find the longest leg by multiplying the shortest leg by the square root of 3: 5 * sqrt(3).
4. The three sides are 5, 5 * sqrt(3), and 10!

## The Cartesian Connection

Have you ever looked at a map in a video game and wondered how far it is to walk from point A to point B diagonally? You can't just count the squares. Instead, you build a right triangle!

**The distance between two points on a Cartesian coordinate plane can be calculated by applying the Pythagorean theorem to a right triangle formed by those points.** 

When you want to find the distance between two points on a graph, the horizontal (left-to-right) distance is just leg "a." The vertical (up-and-down) distance is just leg "b." The diagonal distance across the map is your hypotenuse "c." 

Here is how to find the distance between point (1, 2) and point (4, 6):
1. Find the horizontal leg (a) by subtracting the x-coordinates: 4 - 1 = 3.
2. Find the vertical leg (b) by subtracting the y-coordinates: 6 - 2 = 4.
3. Now use the Pythagorean theorem (a^2 + b^2 = c^2): 3^2 + 4^2 = c^2.
4. Square the numbers: 9 + 16 = 25.
5. Solve for c by figuring out what number squared equals 25: sqrt(25) = 5.
6. The distance between the points is exactly 5 units!

Next time you see a diagonal line on a map, remember: the famous a^2 + b^2 = c^2 formula is the ultimate secret key to unlocking distances anywhere in the universe!
