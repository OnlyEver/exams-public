Imagine a giant video game map. For a long time in math, shapes were just floating around in blank space. But when we put shapes on a grid, suddenly everything becomes a lot easier to figure out! This grid is called the Cartesian coordinate system. It is like a massive sheet of graph paper. The x-axis and y-axis of the Cartesian coordinate system intersect at a right angle. They form a perfect cross. Right in the very middle of this giant map is home base. The coordinates of the origin on the Cartesian plane are (0, 0). 

Because of this grid, a line is not just a drawing; it is a path we can measure with numbers. When we look at how dots and lines connect on this grid, we are just using simple math to measure space!

## The Anatomy of Distance

If you want to know how objects act on our video game map, you have to measure the gaps between them. The shortest path between two points in the coordinate plane is a straight line segment connecting them. No zig-zags, just a straight shot!

When that straight path goes perfectly left-to-right or perfectly up-and-down, measuring is super easy. 
*   The length of a horizontal line segment is the absolute value of the difference between the x-coordinates of the endpoints. That just means you subtract the two x numbers and make sure the answer is positive. Also, because a flat line does not go up or down at all, the slope of any horizontal line on the coordinate plane is exactly zero.
*   The length of a vertical line segment is the absolute value of the difference between the y-coordinates of the endpoints. You subtract the y numbers and keep the answer positive. Because a straight up-and-down line does not move side-to-side, the slope of any vertical line on the coordinate plane is mathematically undefined.

But what if the line is slanted? You cannot just count the squares on the graph paper. 

The distance formula calculates the straight-line distance between two points on the coordinate plane. Here is the rule: The distance formula between two points is the square root of the sum of the squared difference of the x-coordinates and the squared difference of the y-coordinates.

That sounds like a mouthful, but it is actually a secret disguise! The distance formula is a direct algebraic application of the Pythagorean Theorem (a^2 + b^2 = c^2). 

Let's break down how this works step-by-step. Let's find the distance between point (1, 2) and point (4, 6):
1. Find the horizontal difference (subtract the x-coordinates): 4 - 1 = 3. This is the flat bottom of our hidden right triangle.
2. Square that horizontal difference: 3 * 3 = 9.
3. Find the vertical difference (subtract the y-coordinates): 6 - 2 = 4. This is the tall side of our hidden triangle.
4. Square that vertical difference: 4 * 4 = 16.
5. Add those two squared numbers together: 9 + 16 = 25.
6. Take the square root of that sum to find your final distance: the square root of 25 is 5. So, the distance is exactly 5!

## Finding the Balance: The Midpoint

If the distance tells us how far apart two things are, the midpoint tells us where they perfectly balance, like the exact middle of a seesaw. 

The midpoint formula calculates the exact center coordinate point between two coordinate pairs. When you find this exact center, the midpoint of a line segment divides that segment into two smaller segments of exactly equal length.

Finding the middle is just finding the average! 
*   The x-coordinate of a segment midpoint is the arithmetic mean of the endpoints x-coordinates. (Just add the two x numbers and divide by 2).
*   The y-coordinate of a segment midpoint is the arithmetic mean of the endpoints y-coordinates. (Just add the two y numbers and divide by 2).

Let's find the midpoint between (2, 4) and (8, 10):
1. Add the x-coordinates: 2 + 8 = 10.
2. Divide by 2: 10 / 2 = 5. This is your middle x number.
3. Add the y-coordinates: 4 + 10 = 14.
4. Divide by 2: 14 / 2 = 7. This is your middle y number.
5. Put them together: your midpoint is (5, 7).

### Reversing the Process

Sometimes a math problem works backward. You might know where the middle is and where one end is, but you have to find the other end. 

An unknown endpoint coordinate can be calculated algebraically by setting the known midpoint coordinate equal to the average of the known and unknown endpoint coordinates. 

Think of it like getting an allowance. Let's say last week you got \$10 (your known endpoint). You want your average allowance to be \$15 (your known midpoint). How much do you need to get this week (the unknown endpoint) to make that average happen? Let's solve it step-by-step:
1. Set up your average rule: 15 = (10 + unknown) / 2.
2. Multiply both sides by 2 to get rid of the fraction: 15 * 2 = 30. Now we have: 30 = 10 + unknown.
3. Subtract 10 from both sides: 30 - 10 = 20.
4. Your unknown endpoint is 20! You need \$20 this week. This works exactly the same way for finding points on a graph.

## Polygons on the Plane: Perimeter and Area

Once we know how lines work, we can connect them to make shapes, like triangles and squares, which are called polygons. The grid helps us measure the outside edges and the inside space.

### Perimeter
The perimeter of a polygon on the coordinate plane is the sum of the lengths of all its sides. Think of it like a fence around a yard. If the fence goes straight up or down, just count the units! But what if the fence is diagonal? The side lengths of a polygon with slanted sides on the coordinate plane are calculated using the distance formula. You just use our hidden right triangle trick from earlier for each slanted side, and then add all the sides together!

### Area
Area is the amount of flat space inside the shape. Think of it like how much cheese covers a pizza. If the shape is weird or slanted on the grid, there are two cool ways to find the area:

1.  **The Additive Method (Breaking it apart):** The area of a polygon on the coordinate plane can be calculated by decomposing the polygon into smaller non-overlapping rectangles and right triangles. Just chop the weird shape into normal, easy shapes, find the area of each piece, and add them all up.
2.  **The Subtractive Method (The Cookie Cutter):** The area of a polygon on the coordinate plane can be calculated by subtracting the areas of external right triangles from the area of an enclosing bounding box. 

Imagine the bounding box is a big rectangular sheet of cookie dough. Draw a straight, easy-to-measure box around your weirdly shaped cookie. Find the area of the whole dough box. Then, figure out the area of the corner pieces of dough you are going to throw away (the external right triangles). Subtract those corner pieces from the total box, and whatever is left is your perfect cookie!

## The Algebra of Shapes: Classifying Quadrilaterals

A quadrilateral is just any shape with four straight sides. Instead of guessing what a shape is by looking at it, we use the graph to prove it using math!

### The Behavior of Slopes
Slope is just a number that tells us how steep a line is, like a hill in a racing game. 
*   Two distinct non-vertical lines on the coordinate plane are parallel if and only if their slopes are exactly equal. This means they go up and over at the exact same speed and will never crash into each other, like train tracks.
*   Two non-vertical lines on the coordinate plane are perpendicular if the product of their slopes equals negative one. This means they crash perfectly straight into each other like a plus sign (+). For example, a slope of 2/3 and a slope of -3/2 are perpendicular.

Let's check if a slope of 4 and a slope of -1/4 are perpendicular step-by-step:
1. Multiply the first slope by the second slope: 4 * (-1/4).
2. Think of 4 as a fraction: 4/1. Now multiply the top numbers: 4 * -1 = -4. 
3. Multiply the bottom numbers: 1 * 4 = 4. So you have -4 / 4.
4. Divide -4 by 4 to get -1. Since the answer is exactly negative one, the lines are perpendicular!

### Diagonals as the Key to Quadrilaterals
Diagonals are the crisscross lines inside a shape that connect opposite corners. They are the secret cheat codes to figure out what kind of quadrilateral you have!

| If you want to prove... | Then show that... | Using this math tool... |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Parallelogram** | The midpoints of its two diagonals are located at the exact same coordinate point. | The Midpoint Formula |
| **Rectangle** | The lengths of its two diagonals calculated via the distance formula are equal. | The Distance Formula |
| **Rhombus** | Its two diagonals are perpendicular to each other. | Slope Calculations (slope 1 times slope 2 = -1) |

A shape is a parallelogram if its crisscross lines perfectly cut each other in half. A quadrilateral on the coordinate plane is a parallelogram if the midpoints of its two diagonals are located at the exact same coordinate point.

If you already know it is a parallelogram, maybe it is also a rectangle! A parallelogram on the coordinate plane is a rectangle if the lengths of its two diagonals calculated via the distance formula are equal. Think of a rectangular window frame: it only stays perfectly square if the crossbars inside are exactly the same size.

Finally, a parallelogram on the coordinate plane is a rhombus if its two diagonals are perpendicular to each other. A rhombus is a diamond shape where all four outside sides are identical. You prove this by multiplying the slopes of the crisscross lines to see if they equal exactly negative one.

Geometry on a graph is basically a map of clues. Every slanted line is a hidden triangle, every middle point is a perfect average, and every shape can be figured out if you just look at its slopes and lengths!
