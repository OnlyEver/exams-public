But here is where you have to act like the laws of physics. Real-world mathematical contexts often impose domain constraints on variables. Pure algebra doesn't care if a number is negative, but reality does. A domain constraint might restrict a variable representing a physical length to strictly positive numbers. You cannot have a negative four-foot piece of construction paper! Likewise, a domain constraint might restrict a variable representing a count of indivisible objects to whole numbers. You cannot put $2.7$ seventh graders on a school bus! You will get fired! And to keep them honest, dimensional analysis ensures that units match across both sides of a constructed equation. Sometimes the kids are boxed in entirely. A compound inequality is formed by joining two individual inequalities with the word "and" or the word "or." The word "or" in a compound inequality represents the union of two conditions—the cool substitute teacher who lets you go to the library if you have a pass *or* if you just look really stressed out. Eventually, you will unleash them into the matrix of formulas. A literal equation is an equation containing two or more variables. It’s an alphabet soup of doom. Rearranging a formula requires isolating a specific target variable on one side of the equals sign. To do this without blowing up the school, the properties of equality are used to perform inverse operations when rearranging literal equations. It’s like moving heavy furniture—whatever you do to one side of the living room, you do to the other. The addition property of equality allows adding the same variable expression to both sides of an equation to rearrange terms. You just slide that couch across the equals sign. The multiplication property of equality allows multiplying both sides of an equation by the same non-zero variable expression to clear denominators, wiping out fractions like a majestic sponge. And if the formula is a beautifully balanced ratio? Cross-multiplication is an algebraic technique used to isolate variables in rational equations structured as proportions. But what if the variable they need to isolate is scattered everywhere, screaming for help?
