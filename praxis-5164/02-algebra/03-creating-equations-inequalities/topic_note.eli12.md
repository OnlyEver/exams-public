## The Lexicon of Algebraic Translation

Translating verbal phrases to mathematical expressions requires assigning variables to unknown quantities. This is the very first step: if you don't know how many pizza slices are left, you just give that unknown amount a letter name (like "x" or "p") so you can still do math with it!

The basic math words are easy once you know the secret code:
*   The phrase "sum of" indicates the mathematical operation of addition.
*   The phrase "difference between" indicates the mathematical operation of subtraction.
*   The phrase "product of" indicates the mathematical operation of multiplication. 
*   The phrase "quotient of" indicates the mathematical operation of division.

There are also special multipliers that show up a lot in word problems. For example, the phrase "twice a number" translates to multiplying a variable by two (like 2x). On the flip side, the phrase "half of a number" translates to multiplying a variable by one-half (like 1/2 * x).

### The "Less Than" Trap
Subtraction has a sneaky trap that catches a lot of students. In subtraction translations, the phrase "less than" reverses the order of the written terms. 

Think about it like this: if you have \$5 less than your friend's allowance, you don't start with \$5 and take away your friend's money. You start with your friend's money and take away \$5! Because of this rule, the phrase "five less than x" translates to the algebraic expression x - 5. It is NOT 5 - x. 

Finally, the most important word in any math sentence is the verb. The word "is" in a verbal mathematical description translates to an equals sign in an algebraic equation. It is the perfect balance point of the whole problem.

## Constructing Models from Reality

When you read word problems, you are actually building math models of real life. How you put the pieces together is super important.

### Linear and Exponential Models
In linear models (ones that graph as a straight line), word problems usually give you two clues. First, an initial starting value in a real-world scenario translates to the y-intercept constant in a linear equation. Imagine a cell phone plan that charges a base fee of \$20 just to turn the phone on. Second, a constant rate of change in a real-world scenario translates to the slope coefficient in a linear equation. This is like being charged an extra \$5 for every gigabyte of data you use. 

But what if things are growing super fast, like a zombie virus? Creating an exponential equation from a verbal description requires identifying a constant multiplicative growth or decay rate. This means instead of adding the same amount every time, you multiply by the same amount, like the number of zombies doubling every single day or a bouncing ball losing half its height on every bounce.

### The Logic of Consecutive Integers
Sometimes you need to work with numbers that are right next to each other, like 7, 8, and 9. Constructing equations for consecutive integers involves defining a base variable and adding sequential integer values to it. 
*   Because they just go up by 1 each time, variables for three consecutive integers are defined as x, x plus 1, and x plus 2.

If you only want evens or odds, you have to skip numbers. Constructing equations for consecutive even or consecutive odd integers requires adding sequential multiples of two to a base variable. 
*   Variables for three consecutive even integers are defined as x, x plus 2, and x plus 4.

Why do we add 2 for odd numbers? Let's prove it with a step-by-step example:
1.  **Step 1:** Pick an odd base number for x, like 5.
2.  **Step 2:** To find the next odd number, test what happens when you add 1. 5 + 1 = 6. (6 is even, so this fails).
3.  **Step 3:** Test what happens when you add 2. 5 + 2 = 7. (7 is odd, so this works perfectly!).
4.  **Step 4:** Test what happens when you add 4. 5 + 4 = 9. (9 is odd, so this is the next one after 7!).
Even if you start with an odd number, the jump to the next one is always an even jump!

### Domain Constraints and Dimensional Analysis
Math equations have to obey the rules of the real world. Real-world mathematical contexts often impose domain constraints on variables. A domain constraint is just a rule that says what kind of numbers are allowed to be used. 

*   For example, a domain constraint might restrict a variable representing a physical length to strictly positive numbers. You can't have a piece of wood that is -3 inches long!
*   Also, a domain constraint might restrict a variable representing a count of indivisible objects to whole numbers. "Indivisible" means you can't break it apart. You can't invite 4.5 friends to a party or buy 2.3 puppies!

When you build an equation correctly, the units (like miles, hours, or dollars) have to balance out. Dimensional analysis ensures that units match across both sides of a constructed equation. If you multiply your speed (miles per hour) by your time (hours), the "hours" cross out, leaving you with just "miles." If the other side of your math problem measures gallons of milk, you know right away you made a mistake!

## The Architecture of Inequalities

Life isn't always exact. We have budgets (you can spend up to \$10) and roller coaster rules (you must be this tall to ride). Creating an inequality involves identifying a strict or non-strict boundary condition for an unknown quantity. A strict boundary means it can't equal the number (like "strictly greater than"). A non-strict boundary means it is allowed to equal the number.

Here is your translation guide for setting up boundaries:

*   The phrase "at least" translates to a greater than or equal to inequality symbol (>=).
*   The phrase "no less than" translates to a greater than or equal to inequality symbol (>=).
*   The phrase "at most" translates to a less than or equal to inequality symbol (<=).
*   The phrase "no more than" translates to a less than or equal to inequality symbol (<=).

### Reversing the Inequality
There is one massive rule you must remember: in a constructed inequality, multiplying or dividing both sides by a negative value reverses the direction of the inequality symbol. 

Why does this happen? Let's look at it step-by-step using a number line:
1.  **Step 1:** Write down a true statement with basic numbers, like 2 < 5 (2 is smaller than 5).
2.  **Step 2:** Multiply both sides by -1.
3.  **Step 3:** The left side becomes -2, and the right side becomes -5.
4.  **Step 4:** Picture a number line. Because -5 is further to the left (colder/smaller) than -2, the number -5 is now the smaller value.
5.  **Step 5:** Change the symbol so the statement stays true: -2 > -5. Multiplying by a negative flipped them around like a mirror, so the sign must flip too!

### Compound Inequalities
Sometimes you have two rules going on at the exact same time. A compound inequality is formed by joining two individual inequalities with the word "and" or the word "or".

*   The word "and" in a compound inequality represents the intersection of two conditions. This means BOTH rules must be true at the same time. Think of getting a kids' meal at a restaurant: your age must be >= 0 AND <= 12.
*   The word "or" in a compound inequality represents the union of two conditions. This means AT LEAST ONE rule has to be true. Think of a free bus ride: you get to ride free if your age is < 5 OR your age is >= 65.

## Fluidity in Form: Rearranging Literal Equations

Math uses a lot of formulas. A literal equation is an equation containing two or more variables. Famous examples include the area of a rectangle (A = l * w) or the formula to change temperatures from Celsius to Fahrenheit. It is mostly letters!

Sometimes you need to shuffle a formula around to find a different piece of missing information. Rearranging a formula requires isolating a specific target variable on one side of the equals sign. "Isolating" just means getting that letter completely by itself.

We do this by using the fair rules of math. The properties of equality are used to perform inverse operations when rearranging literal equations. "Inverse operations" are just opposite actions, like undoing addition with subtraction.

### Properties of Equality in Action
The addition property of equality allows adding the same variable expression to both sides of an equation to rearrange terms. If you want to move something out of the way, just add or subtract it from both sides so the equation stays balanced like a seesaw!

The multiplication property of equality allows multiplying both sides of an equation by the same non-zero variable expression to clear denominators. Denominators are the bottom numbers in fractions. If you don't like dealing with fractions, just multiply everything by that bottom number to make the fractions disappear! 

When you have two fractions equal to each other (like a/b = c/d), there is a great shortcut. Cross-multiplication is an algebraic technique used to isolate variables in rational equations structured as proportions. It is really just a super-fast way of using the multiplication property on both sides at the same time!

### Advanced Manipulations and Hidden Dangers
Sometimes formulas are tricky puzzles, and you have to be extra careful:

1.  **Factoring Out the Target:** What if the letter you want is stuck in two different places? Isolating a variable that appears in multiple terms of an algebraic expression requires factoring out that variable.
    *   **Step 1:** Start with an equation where 'x' is in two spots: a*x + b*x = 10.
    *   **Step 2:** Pull the 'x' out to the front like pulling a matching block out of two bags: x * (a + b) = 10.
    *   **Step 3:** Now you can safely divide both sides by the group (a + b) to get 'x' completely alone!
2.  **The Zero-Division Rule:** There is a secret rule whenever you divide. Dividing both sides of a formula by a variable expression requires the assumption that the expression does not equal zero. Why? Because dividing by zero breaks the universe! You can't share 10 slices of pizza with 0 people.
3.  **Fake Answers:** Be super careful with square roots. Squaring both sides of an equation containing a radical can introduce extraneous solutions that do not satisfy the original constructed equation. "Extraneous" is just a fancy math word for "fake." A radical is a square root symbol.
    Let's see how a fake answer happens step-by-step:
    *   **Step 1:** Start with the problem: square root of x = -4. (Wait, square roots can't give negative answers in normal math, so this shouldn't have an answer!)
    *   **Step 2:** But let's say you try to solve it anyway by squaring both sides: (square root of x) * (square root of x) = (-4) * (-4).
    *   **Step 3:** Multiply it out. The left side becomes x, and the right side becomes 16. So, x = 16.
    *   **Step 4:** Now check your answer in the original problem. Does the square root of 16 equal -4? No, it equals positive 4! The math tricked us and handed us a fake solution.
