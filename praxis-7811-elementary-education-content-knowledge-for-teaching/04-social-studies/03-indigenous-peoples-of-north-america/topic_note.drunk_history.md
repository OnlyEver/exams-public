Slide your margarita over here and listen to me, because you are about to step into a 4th-grade classroom, and you hold the literal fabric of history in your dry-erase-marker-stained hands. If you are going to be an elementary school teacher, you have to be ready for the fact that a solid half of your students are going to walk in with their brains full of Thanksgiving-play nonsense. You have to save them. We are talking about the Indigenous Peoples of North America, and it is a saga of brilliant engineering, political drama, absolute tragedy, and unyielding survival. 

Let's start with the ultimate classroom hazard. A common elementary student misconception is that Native Americans were a single monolithic group with identical customs. You ask an eight-year-old to imagine a Native American, and they mash together a feathered headdress, a desert mesa, and a totem pole into one giant historical smoothie. You have to grab that smoothie and dump it in the sink! You must drill into their heads that Native American cultures prior to European contact were highly diverse. They were the ultimate environmental hackers. Pre-contact Native American societies adapted housing and diets to specific regional environments. It was like the most intense, high-stakes game of HGTV Extreme Habitats.

Let’s take a road trip across pre-contact America. Up north? The Arctic. It’s a frozen wasteland where you can't grow a single carrot. So what do they do? They look at the freezing ocean and say, "Dinner." Arctic Indigenous groups relied on hunting marine mammals such as seals and whales. And for shelter, groups like the Inuit constructed igloos from snow and ice for winter shelter. They literally used the freezing environment to insulate themselves from the freezing environment! 

Now, swing over to the Pacific Northwest. Picture billionaire real-estate vibes—lush forests, endless ocean. Indigenous peoples of the Pacific Northwest relied heavily on salmon fishing. Because they had this unlimited fish buffet, they didn't have to pack up and move. They settled down and flexed their carpentry skills. Pacific Northwest tribes constructed large plank houses from cedar trees. Giant, multi-family mansions made of premium cedar! 

Head down to the blazing hot American Southwest. You try surviving in a desert! But the Pueblo peoples of the American Southwest built multi-story adobe dwellings. Adobe clay absorbs the blazing heat during the day and releases it into the freezing desert at night—it’s natural air conditioning! And because you can't just wish for rain, Southwest Indigenous groups utilized complex irrigation systems to farm in arid climates. They were terraforming the desert!

Then we hit the Midwest, which brings us to your next major pedagogical hurdle. A common elementary student misconception is the belief that all Native Americans lived in tipis. No! Tipis were primarily used as portable shelters by nomadic tribes of the Great Plains. Why were they portable? Because they were on the ultimate, perpetual road trip. Great Plains tribes depended heavily on bison for food, clothing, and shelter. If the bison moved, you moved! And let me drop a spoiler alert for later: the introduction of horses by Spanish explorers drastically changed the hunting practices of Great Plains tribes. It was like trading in a rusty bicycle for a Ferrari. Suddenly, they could track and hunt those herds at lightning speed.

Over in the Eastern Woodlands, they were pulling off the greatest group project in agricultural history. Eastern Woodlands tribes cultivated corn, beans, and squash together. This is a biological masterpiece! Cultivating corn, beans, and squash together is known as the Three Sisters farming technique. The corn is the scaffolding, the beans provide the nitrogen fertilizer, and the squash leaves act as a giant weed-blocking tarp. Flawless execution. 

They also had political setups that would make modern diplomats weep. The Iroquois Confederacy was a political alliance among five, later six, Native American nations in the Eastern Woodlands. It was essentially an ancient United Nations. But here’s a pro-tip for your teacher credibility: "Iroquois" is a colonizer name. Haudenosaunee is the traditional autonym for the people commonly known as the Iroquois. Teach your kids the real names! This alliance didn’t just vibe together; the Iroquois Confederacy established an oral constitution known as the Great Law of Peace. Because they didn't write it on parchment, some people dismiss it, but that's wildly ignorant. Indigenous storytelling traditions were used to pass down history and cultural values across generations. It was a rigorous, highly disciplined method of encrypting and storing data in the human mind. 

And don't sleep on the Southeast! The Mississippian culture in the Southeast built large earthen mounds for religious and ceremonial purposes. We are talking about massive dirt skyscrapers and sprawling urban city-states that rivaled European cities at the time. 

Speaking of Europe, here comes the collision. When the Europeans showed up, it sparked a chaotic, global swap meet. The Columbian Exchange facilitated the transfer of plants, animals, and diseases between the Americas and the Eastern Hemisphere. And it was an apocalyptic disaster for Native peoples. European diseases such as smallpox decimated Indigenous populations due to a lack of natural immunity. 

But you have to teach your students that "the Europeans" weren't just one big invading monolith either. European colonial powers had different primary goals when interacting with Native Americans. It was like a terrible reality TV show where everyone has a different secret agenda. The French? They were the hustlers. French colonists generally focused on establishing fur trading networks with Native American tribes, which meant they actually had to build somewhat cooperative relationships. The Spanish? They were the hardcore zealots. Spanish colonizers frequently sought to convert Native Americans to Catholicism through the mission system. But they also wanted free labor, so the Spanish encomienda system forced Native Americans into hard labor on agricultural estates. And the English? The English were the ultimate nightmare roommates. English colonists primarily focused on acquiring land for permanent settlements. They didn't want to trade; they just wanted your house. 

But Native Americans weren't just sitting there letting it happen! They were playing 4D chess. Native American tribes frequently formed strategic alliances with European powers to gain trade advantages, pitting the French against the English to survive. 

Then comes the birth of the United States, and this is where you need to teach your kids about sovereignty. The United States government initially interacted with Native American tribes by signing treaties. You do not sign a treaty with your neighborhood watch; you sign a treaty with a country! Therefore, early United States treaties legally recognized Native American tribes as sovereign nations. 

Down in the Southeast, the colonizers slapped a wildly condescending label on the Cherokee, Chickasaw, Choctaw, Creek, and Seminole. The Five Civilized Tribes was a term used by European Americans to describe the Cherokee, Chickasaw, Choctaw, Creek, and Seminole because these nations adapted to white culture—farming, centralized government, courts—to try and avoid getting wiped out. The Cherokee even hacked the English language! The Cherokee Nation developed a written syllabary to record their language. One guy basically sat down and invented an alphabet from scratch! Sequoyah created the Cherokee syllabary in the early nineteenth century, and suddenly his entire nation was literate. 

Did playing by the rules save them? No. Enter President Andrew Jackson, the ultimate villain of this unit. The Indian Removal Act was signed into law by President Andrew Jackson in 1830. This piece of legislation was pure theft. The Indian Removal Act authorized the federal government to force Native American tribes out of the southeastern United States. 

The Cherokee fought back the "civilized" way: they sued! And they won! In the 1832 Worcester versus Georgia decision, the Supreme Court ruled that states had no jurisdiction over Native American lands. The highest court in the land said, "Georgia, back off, this is a sovereign nation." But here is where the system completely broke down. President Andrew Jackson ignored the Supreme Court ruling in Worcester versus Georgia. He essentially told the Chief Justice, "You made the rule, let's see you enforce it."

What followed is pure horror. The Trail of Tears was the forced relocation of Native American nations to territory west of the Mississippi River. The Cherokee, Muscogee, Seminole, Chickasaw, and Choctaw nations were forced to march along the Trail of Tears. (Notice I said Muscogee there—that’s the actual name for the Creek!). Thousands of Native Americans died from exposure, disease, and starvation during the Trail of Tears. And where were they marching to? The destination for the forced relocation during the Trail of Tears was designated as Indian Territory. Today, the designated Indian Territory from the 1830s relocation corresponds mostly to the modern state of Oklahoma. 

And the government didn't stop at stealing land. Later on, assimilation policies aimed to strip Native Americans of their cultural identities. They tried to scrub the "Native" out of the Native Americans through boarding schools, banning languages, and outlawing religions. 

So how do you teach this heavy, immense history without crushing a ten-year-old's spirit? You fix your pedagogy. First off, a common elementary student misconception is that Native Americans existed only in the past. If you only talk about Indigenous people in November or when teaching the 1800s, you are contributing to their erasure! Elementary pedagogy emphasizes teaching Native American history through the continuous presence and modern contributions of Indigenous peoples. Show them Native astronauts, artists, and politicians! 

Second, you have to get the colonizers' voices out of the center of the room. Elementary education emphasizes using primary sources to teach students about Native American perspectives. Let them read the actual treaties. Let them look at Sequoyah's syllabary. 

And please, for my sanity, throw away whatever crusty, culturally insensitive textbook is sitting in your supply closet. The National Museum of the American Indian provides educational resources to replace stereotypical narratives with historically accurate accounts. Use their stuff! 

You are the gatekeeper to their understanding of the world. Teach the sovereignty, teach the tragedy, but above all, teach the unyielding survival of these nations. Now drink that margarita and go write your lesson plan!
