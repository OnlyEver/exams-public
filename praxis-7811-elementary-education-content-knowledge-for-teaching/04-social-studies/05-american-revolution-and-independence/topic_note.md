# Teaching the [American Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Revolution): A Masterclass in Cause, Effect, and Human Nature

Welcome, future [educators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teacher)! Pull up a chair. You are preparing to teach one of the most exciting, dynamic, and misunderstood periods in [history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History): the [American Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Revolution). 

If you walk into a [fifth-grade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fifth_grade) classroom and start rattling off dates, [treaties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty), and [tax](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tax) acts, you are going to lose them. Their eyes will glaze over. Why? Because to a ten-year-old, [1776](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1776) feels like the time of the [dinosaurs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dinosaur). 

But if you frame the [American Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Revolution) around a concept every [elementary student](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_school) understands intimately—**fairness**—you will have them on the edge of their seats. The [American Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Revolution) wasn't just a [political](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics) shift; it was a colossal argument over what is fair, who gets to make the rules, and what happens when the people making the rules stop listening to the people following them. 

Let's break down the Content Knowledge for Teaching (CKT) you need to master this topic, looking not just at *what* happened, but *how* to make your students understand *why* it mattered.

---

## Part 1: The Bill Comes Due (Causes of the Revolution)

To understand the [Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Revolution), we have to start before it. Imagine you and your students are working on a massive class project. You spend weeks building it, and it costs a lot of money. When it’s done, the [principal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Head_teacher) walks in and says, "Great job! Now, I need you all to pay me back for the supplies." Your students would be furious! This is exactly what happened between [Great Britain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Great_Britain) and the [American colonies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thirteen_Colonies).

The stage is set when **the [French and Indian War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_and_Indian_War) concluded in [1763](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1763)**. [Great Britain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Great_Britain) won, securing massive new territories in [North America](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_America). But [wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/War) are astronomically expensive, and **the [French and Indian War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_and_Indian_War) left [Great Britain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Great_Britain) with significant [national debt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_debt_of_the_United_Kingdom)**. 

![Map showing massive British territorial gains in North America following the French and Indian War in 1763. Defending this vast new territory required troops and money, leading directly to the taxation of the American colonies.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/NorthAmerica1762-83.png)

From [London](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/London)'s perspective, the solution was obvious. The [British military](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Armed_Forces) had just spent years defending the [American colonies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thirteen_Colonies) from the [French](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_France). Therefore, **the [British Parliament](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliament_of_Great_Britain) believed the [American colonies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thirteen_Colonies) should contribute financially to the [French and Indian War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_and_Indian_War) debt**. 

### The Spark: Taxes and the Outrage of Unfairness

To pay down this debt, [Parliament](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliament_of_Great_Britain) began passing [laws](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statute). 
1. **The [Stamp Act of 1765](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stamp_Act_1765) levied a [direct tax](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direct_tax) on [printed materials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Printing) in the [American colonies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thirteen_Colonies).** If you wanted a [legal document](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legal_document), a [newspaper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newspaper), or even a deck of [playing cards](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Playing_card), you had to pay a [tax](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tax) to get a special [stamp](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Revenue_stamp). 

![A printed notice of the Stamp Act of 1765. Colonists were enraged not just by the financial cost, but by the lack of colonial representation in the Parliament that levied it.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Parliament_Stamp_Act1765.jpg)

2. A couple of years later, **the [Townshend Acts of 1767](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Townshend_Acts) placed British [taxes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tax) on essential imported goods in the [American colonies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thirteen_Colonies)**, such as [glass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glass), [lead](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lead), [paint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paint), [paper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paper), and [tea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tea).

> **Pedagogical Pivot: The Big Misconception**
> Your students will naturally think, *"The [colonists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thirteen_Colonies) were mad because they had to pay [taxes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tax), and nobody likes taxes."* **Stop them right there.** This is a massive [historical misconception](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_common_misconceptions). 
> The [colonists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thirteen_Colonies) were actually fine with paying taxes to run their local towns and colonies. Their anger was about *who* was doing the taxing. Because they had no representatives sitting in [Parliament](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliament_of_Great_Britain) in [London](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/London), **American colonists adopted the phrase "[no taxation without representation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/No_taxation_without_representation)" to protest British [tax policies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tax_policy).**

You must ensure your students understand this crucial CKT concept: **[Colonists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thirteen_Colonies) believed only their elected [colonial assemblies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colonial_government_in_the_Thirteen_Colonies) possessed the legitimate authority to levy [taxes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tax).** It wasn’t about the money; it was about the *right* to take the money.

## Part 2: From Snowballs to Harbor Tea (The Escalation)

When people feel unheard, they get loud. Tensions in the colonies, especially in [Boston](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boston), reached a boiling point. 

On **[March 5, 1770](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/March_5)**, a rowdy crowd of colonists began taunting and throwing [ice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ice) and [snowballs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snowball) at [British soldiers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Army) guarding the [Custom House](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Custom_House%2C_Boston). The situation spiraled out of control, and **the [Boston Massacre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boston_Massacre) occurred on [March 5, 1770](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/March_5), 1770**, when **[British soldiers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Army) fired into a crowd of protesting colonists**, killing five people. 

*Classroom Strategy:* Show your students [Paul Revere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Revere)’s famous [engraving](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engraving) of the [Boston Massacre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boston_Massacre). Ask them, "Does this look like a fair fight? Does it look like the soldiers were scared, or does it look like they are villains?" This is a brilliant way to teach students about *[propaganda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propaganda)* and how [images](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Image) are used to shape [public opinion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_opinion).

![Paul Revere's highly sensationalized engraving of the Boston Massacre. This image is a prime pedagogical tool for teaching students about historical propaganda and how media shapes public opinion.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Boston_Massacre_high-res.jpg)

### The Boston Tea Party

Fast forward to **[December 16, 1773](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/December_16), 1773**. [Parliament](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliament_of_Great_Britain) had passed the [Tea Act](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tea_Act), which actually lowered the price of [tea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tea) but maintained the hated [tax](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tax), essentially forcing colonists to buy [British East India Company](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_India_Company) tea. The colonists saw right through it. 

**The [Boston Tea Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boston_Tea_Party) served as a direct colonial protest against the British [Tea Act](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tea_Act).** On that chilly night, **members of the [Sons of Liberty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sons_of_Liberty) dumped British tea into [Boston Harbor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boston_Harbor) during the [Boston Tea Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boston_Tea_Party).** 

![Depiction of the Boston Tea Party. This deliberate destruction of corporate property by the Sons of Liberty was the catalyst for the severe British punishments known as the Intolerable Acts.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Boston_Tea_Party_w.jpg)

How did [Great Britain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Great_Britain) react? Like a very angry parent. 
**The [British Parliament](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliament_of_Great_Britain) passed the [Coercive Acts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intolerable_Acts) to punish [Massachusetts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Province_of_Massachusetts_Bay) colonists for the [Boston Tea Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boston_Tea_Party).** They closed [Boston Harbor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boston_Harbor) and suspended [self-government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-governance) in [Massachusetts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Province_of_Massachusetts_Bay). These [laws](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law) were so harsh and suffocating that **American colonists referred to the British [Coercive Acts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intolerable_Acts) as the [Intolerable Acts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intolerable_Acts).** 

### The Perspectives Table

When teaching, use a [table](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Table_%28information%29) like this to help students see the escalating conflict from both sides:

| Event | British Perspective | Colonial Perspective |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **[Taxation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tax)** | "We protected you. It's time to pay your fair share for the [war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_and_Indian_War)." | "You can't tax us unless we have a voice in [Parliament](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliament_of_Great_Britain)." |
| **[Boston Massacre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boston_Massacre)** | "Our [soldiers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Army) were surrounded by a dangerous, violent [mob](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crowd) and panicked." | "Bloodthirsty soldiers murdered unarmed citizens in cold blood." |
| **[Boston Tea Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boston_Tea_Party)**| "This is destruction of [private property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Private_property) and an act of lawless [vandalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vandalism)." | "This is a principled stand against tyrannical corporate [monopolies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monopoly) and illegal [taxes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tax)." |
| **[Intolerable Acts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intolerable_Acts)**| "We must restore [law and order](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law_and_order_%28politics%29) in [Massachusetts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Province_of_Massachusetts_Bay)." | "You are treating us like [slaves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slavery) and stripping away our fundamental [rights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rights)." |

Because of these [Intolerable Acts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intolerable_Acts), the colonies realized they could no longer just complain individually. They had to unite. **The [First Continental Congress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_Continental_Congress) convened in [1774](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1774) to coordinate colonial resistance against the [Intolerable Acts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intolerable_Acts).** They started organizing [boycotts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boycott) and forming [militias](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Militia).

## Part 3: The Point of No Return

Things finally spilled over into open [warfare](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/War). **Armed conflict in the [American Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War) began with the [Battles of Lexington and Concord](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battles_of_Lexington_and_Concord) in [1775](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1775)**. The "[Shot Heard 'Round the World](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shot_heard_round_the_world)" had been fired. 

But here's a fascinating thing to teach your students: *Even after the shooting started, most colonists did not want independence!* They just wanted [Great Britain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Great_Britain) to treat them fairly as [British citizens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_nationality_law). 

So, what changed their minds? A brilliant piece of writing. 

**[Thomas Paine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Paine) published the influential [political pamphlet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pamphlet) *[Common Sense](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Common_Sense)* in [1776](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1776).** Paine wrote in plain, powerful language that everyday people could understand. He basically said, "Why is a tiny [island](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Island) thousands of miles away ruling a massive [continent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent)? This makes no sense!" **The pamphlet *[Common Sense](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Common_Sense)* successfully persuaded many American colonists to support immediate independence from [Great Britain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Great_Britain).** It was the viral [social media post](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_media) of its era.

![The cover of Thomas Paine's pamphlet "Common Sense," which used plain, accessible language to successfully argue that it was absurd for a distant island to rule an entire continent.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Commonsense.jpg)

## Part 4: The Breakup Letter (The Declaration of Independence)

Once the colonists decided to break away, they needed to explain *why* to the world. 

**The [Second Continental Congress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Continental_Congress) formally adopted the [Declaration of Independence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Declaration_of_Independence) on [July 4, 1776](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Independence_Day_%28United_States%29).** 
You must know the architecture of this document inside and out. **[Thomas Jefferson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Jefferson) served as the primary author of the [Declaration of Independence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Declaration_of_Independence).** But Jefferson didn't invent these ideas out of thin air. **The [political philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_philosophy) of English [philosopher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosopher) [John Locke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Locke) heavily influenced the text of the [Declaration of Independence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Declaration_of_Independence).**

![John Trumbull's famous 1819 painting depicting the Committee of Five presenting their draft of the Declaration of Independence to the Second Continental Congress in Philadelphia.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Declaration_of_Independence_(1819)%2C_by_John_Trumbull.jpg)

[John Locke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Locke) believed in the "[social contract](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_contract)." Think of it like this for your students: We all agree to follow the rules of the [classroom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classroom), and in exchange, the [teacher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teacher) keeps the classroom safe and fair. If the teacher starts acting like a cruel [dictator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dictator), the [contract](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contract) is broken. 

Jefferson took Locke's ideas and laid out a masterclass in [political theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_philosophy). You need to know these foundational arguments:
1. **The [Declaration of Independence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Declaration_of_Independence) asserts that all individuals are endowed by their creator with [unalienable rights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_rights_and_legal_rights).** (Unalienable means rights that cannot be taken away by any human [government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government)).
2. What are they? **The [Declaration of Independence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Declaration_of_Independence) explicitly identifies [life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_to_life) as an unalienable right.**
3. **The [Declaration of Independence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Declaration_of_Independence) explicitly identifies [liberty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liberty) as an unalienable right.**
4. **The [Declaration of Independence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Declaration_of_Independence) explicitly identifies the [pursuit of happiness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life%2C_Liberty_and_the_pursuit_of_Happiness) as an unalienable right.**
5. Next, it explains the purpose of government: **The [Declaration of Independence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Declaration_of_Independence) asserts that governments derive their legitimate authority from the [consent of the governed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consent_of_the_governed).** (The people give the government its power).
6. And the ultimate hammer blow: **The [Declaration of Independence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Declaration_of_Independence) argues that citizens possess the [right to alter or abolish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_of_revolution) an oppressive government.** 

After laying out this beautiful philosophy, Jefferson brings the receipts. **The [Declaration of Independence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Declaration_of_Independence) contains a specific list of [colonial grievances](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grievances_of_the_United_States_Declaration_of_Independence) directed at [King George III](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_III).** (He [quartered troops](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quartering_Acts) in our homes, he cut off our trade, he imposed [taxes without consent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/No_taxation_without_representation)). 

> **Why This Matters to Your Students Today**
> The words of the Declaration were imperfectly applied in [1776](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1776)—they excluded [women](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_the_American_Revolution), [enslaved people](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slavery_in_the_colonial_history_of_the_United_States), and [Indigenous peoples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States). However, those very words became the ultimate tool for future [justice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Justice). You must teach that **the [Declaration of Independence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Declaration_of_Independence) established a foundational philosophical framework for future [democratic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democracy) and [civil rights movements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civil_rights_movements).** When [Abraham Lincoln](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abraham_Lincoln) spoke at [Gettysburg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gettysburg_Address), or when [Martin Luther King Jr.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martin_Luther_King_Jr.) stood on the steps of the [Lincoln Memorial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lincoln_Memorial), they were cashing the "promissory note" written by Jefferson in the Declaration.

## Part 5: The All-Star Team (Key Figures)

While Jefferson was writing, the actual [war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War) was raging. For your [exam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Test_%28assessment%29), you need to understand the distinct roles of key individuals. It wasn't just a bunch of guys in [wigs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wig) doing the same job. 

### The Glue: George Washington
**[George Washington](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Washington) served as the [Commander-in-Chief](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commander-in-chief) of the [Continental Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continental_Army) during the [American Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War).** 
*Pedagogical note:* Students often think Washington was a great [general](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_officer) because he won all his battles. He didn't! He lost a lot of them. His true brilliance was in his [resilience](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Psychological_resilience) and his connection with his men. **[George Washington](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Washington)'s military leadership helped maintain the unity of the [Continental Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continental_Army) through severe hardships** (like the freezing, starving [winter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Winter) at [Valley Forge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Valley_Forge)). He kept the [army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Army) from giving up, and as long as the army existed, the [revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Revolution) was alive.

![George Washington and the Marquis de Lafayette at the brutal winter encampment of Valley Forge. Washington's greatest military strength was his resilience and ability to keep his starving, freezing army unified.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Washington_and_Lafayette_at_Valley_Forge.jpg)

### The Diplomat: Benjamin Franklin
Meanwhile, across the [ocean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean), a different kind of war was being fought. [America](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States) could not beat the [British Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Empire) alone. They needed money, [ships](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ship), and trained soldiers. 
Enter the charming, brilliant [inventor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inventor) and [diplomat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diplomat), [Benjamin Franklin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benjamin_Franklin). **[Benjamin Franklin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benjamin_Franklin) secured a crucial [military](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Military) and [financial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finance) [alliance with France in 1778](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_of_Alliance_%281778%29).** 
Why is this important? Because without [France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_France), the revolution fails. **[French military support](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/France_in_the_American_Revolutionary_War) proved essential to the American military victory over [Great Britain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Great_Britain).** The French provided the [navy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Navy) that trapped the British, and the funds that kept Washington's army supplied. 

![Benjamin Franklin leveraged his immense popularity and diplomatic charm in Paris to secure the French naval and financial support that proved essential to American victory.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Joseph_Siffrein_Duplessis_-_Benjamin_Franklin_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg)

## Part 6: Victory and Recognition

The climax of the war came in [Virginia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virginia). Trapped by Washington's forces on land and the [French Navy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Navy) by sea, the British surrendered. **The [Continental Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continental_Army) achieved a decisive final military victory over [British forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Army) at the [Battle of Yorktown](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siege_of_Yorktown) in [1781](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1781).** 

But the war didn't officially end the day the guns stopped firing. It took years of [diplomacy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diplomacy) to untangle the mess. Finally, **the [Treaty of Paris of 1783](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_of_Paris_%281783%29) formally ended the [American Revolutionary War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War).**

The most important clause of that treaty? The validation of everything they had fought for: **[Great Britain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Great_Britain) officially recognized the [United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States) as an [independent nation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sovereign_state) in the [1783 Treaty of Paris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_of_Paris_%281783%29).**

![Benjamin West's 1783 painting of the American delegation at the Treaty of Paris negotiations. The painting remains famously unfinished because the British delegates refused to pose for it.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Treaty_of_Paris_by_Benjamin_West_1783.jpg)

### Concluding Thoughts for the Future Teacher

As you prepare for the [Elementary Education](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_education) CKT [exam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Test_%28assessment%29), remember the story arc:
1. It starts with **[Debt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Debt)** ([French and Indian War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_and_Indian_War), [1763](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1763)).
2. It moves to **Unfairness** ([Stamp Act](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stamp_Act_1765), [Townshend Acts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Townshend_Acts), [Taxation without Representation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/No_taxation_without_representation)).
3. It boils into **Protest and Punishment** ([Boston Massacre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boston_Massacre), [Tea Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boston_Tea_Party), [Intolerable Acts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intolerable_Acts)).
4. It breaks into **War and Philosophy** ([Lexington & Concord](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battles_of_Lexington_and_Concord), *[Common Sense](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Common_Sense)*, the [Declaration of Independence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Declaration_of_Independence)).
5. It is sustained by **Leadership and Alliances** (Washington's [perseverance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perseverance), Franklin's [French alliance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_of_Alliance_%281778%29)).
6. It concludes with **Victory and Sovereignty** ([Yorktown](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siege_of_Yorktown), [Treaty of Paris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_of_Paris_%281783%29)).

When you explain the [American Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Revolution), you aren't just teaching [history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History). You are teaching your students the mechanics of standing up to power, the meaning of fundamental [rights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rights), and the profound responsibility of [self-government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-governance). Master these facts, understand *why* they connect, and you will be ready to inspire the next generation of citizens!
