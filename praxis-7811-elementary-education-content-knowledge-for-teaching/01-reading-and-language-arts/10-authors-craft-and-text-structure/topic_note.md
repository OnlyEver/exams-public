Welcome to the machinery of [reading](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reading)! If you want to teach a child how a [clock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clock) works, you can’t just teach them to tell time. You have to open the back, show them the [gears](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gear), and explain how the turning of one cog moves another. 

Reading is exactly the same. As a [teacher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teacher), you are moving beyond simply asking students, *"What does this text mean?"* Your job is to help them open the back of the book and see the gears. You are asking, *"How did the author build this [meaning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meaning_%28linguistics%29)? What tools did they use to make you feel that way?"*

This is the study of **Author's Craft and Text Structure**. We are going to break down how authors use [language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Language), organize their ideas, utilize [visual tools](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visual_communication), and present their perspectives. More importantly, we’re going to look at *how eight-year-olds process these concepts*, where they trip up, and how you can guide them to true [comprehension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reading_comprehension).

Let’s dive into the gears.

---

## Part 1: The Magic of Words (Word Choice, Tone, and Mood)

Before an author can build a text, they need materials. An author's primary building materials are [words](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Word). But words are tricky things; they carry baggage. 

### Denotation vs. Connotation

When we teach [vocabulary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vocabulary), we start with the [dictionary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dictionary). But we cannot stop there. To understand an author's craft, students must understand the dual nature of words:

> **[Denotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Denotation)** is the literal, dictionary definition of a word. 
> **[Connotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connotation)** represents the emotional or cultural associations attached to a specific word.

Imagine an author describing a [house](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/House). They could call it a *[shack](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shack)*, a *[cabin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Log_cabin)*, or a *[cottage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cottage)*. All three words share a similar denotation: a small, simple dwelling. But the *connotation* is wildly different! A "cottage" makes you think of cozy fires and [fairy tales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fairy_tale). A "shack" makes you think of splintered wood and [poverty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poverty). When you teach your students to look for connotation, you are teaching them to look for the author's hidden [fingerprints](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fingerprint).

### Tone and Mood (The Classic Mix-Up)

Here is where [word choice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diction) does its heaviest lifting. Words create tone and mood. 

> An **author's [tone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tone_%28literature%29)** is the writer's [attitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Attitude_%28psychology%29) toward the subject matter or the [audience](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Audience). 
> **[Mood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mood_%28literature%29)** is the emotional [atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_%28literature%29) a text creates for the reader.

Think of Tone as the author's [voice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Writer%27s_voice), and Mood as the feeling in your stomach as you read it. If an author writes, *"The bleak, gray sky stared down unblinkingly at the ruined fields,"* the author's **tone** is pessimistic and grim. The **mood** created for the reader is gloomy and [depressing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Depression_%28mood%29).

**🚨 The Classroom Reality:** **[Elementary students](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_school) frequently confuse an author's tone with the reader's mood.** 
Why? Because children are beautifully [egocentric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egocentrism)! If a text makes a student feel sad (mood), they assume the author *is* sad (tone). 
*How to teach it:* Ask your students to separate the sender from the receiver. "Tone is the author's [microphone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microphone); Mood is your [headphones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Headphones). What is the author saying into the mic, and how does it make you feel when it hits your ears?"

### Sensory Details and Figurative Language

Authors don’t just want to tell you something; they want you to experience it. **Sensory details help authors create vivid [mental images](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mental_image) for readers by appealing to the [five human senses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sense)** ([sight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visual_perception), [sound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hearing), [smell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Olfaction), [touch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somatosensory_system), [taste](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taste)). When an author describes the "sharp, biting tang of a cold orange," they are forcing your brain's [sensory cortex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primary_somatosensory_cortex) to fire.

When literal sensory details aren't enough, authors bend reality. **[Figurative language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Literal_and_figurative_language) involves non-literal word uses to achieve specific [stylistic effects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stylistics).** It is the art of saying one thing and meaning another to create a deeper understanding. You will teach three heavy hitters:

| Term | Definition | Pedagogical Example to Use |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **[Simile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simile)** | Compares two distinctly different things using the words "like" or "as". | *"The classroom was as quiet as a [tomb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tomb)."* Kids easily spot the "as," but make sure they understand *why* it's quiet. |
| **[Metaphor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metaphor)** | Makes a direct comparison between two distinct things *without* using the words "like" or "as". | *"The classroom was a [zoo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zoo)."* Explain to your students that the room isn't literally full of [monkeys](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monkey); the author is highlighting the chaos! |
| **[Personification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Personification)**| Gives human characteristics, emotions, or actions to non-human entities or objects. | *"The wind whispered through the trees."* Have students act this out. Can the wind really talk? No, but giving it a voice makes the [setting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Setting_%28narrative%29) feel alive. |

## Part 2: The Architecture of Information (Text Structure)

If words are the [bricks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brick), **text structure refers to how information is logically organized within a written passage.** As a teacher, you must help students recognize the "[blueprint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blueprint)" of a text because understanding *how* a text is built helps students predict *what* they are about to read.

Let’s look at the five primary blueprints an author might use:

### 1. Chronological Text Structure
This structure **presents events in the exact time order the events occurred.** You will teach your students to look for **signal words like "first," "next," and "finally"** which indicate a [chronological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chronology) text structure. This is the blueprint for [biographies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biography), historical accounts, and [recipes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recipe).

### 2. Cause and Effect Text Structure
This structure **explains why an [event](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Event_%28philosophy%29) happened and what resulted from the event.** It answers the "Why?" and the "So what?". You'll teach students to hunt for **signal words like "consequently," "therefore," and "as a result"** to indicate a [cause and effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Causality) text structure.

**🚨 The Classroom Reality:** **Elementary students often misidentify a sequential [narrative text](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Narrative) as a cause and effect informational text.** 
*Why?* Imagine a story that goes: *"The boy lost his shoe. Next, he walked home. Finally, he ate [dinner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dinner)."* Because events happen one after the other, a child might think, *"He ate dinner BECAUSE he lost his shoe!"* 
*How to teach it:* You must actively train your students to distinguish between *narrative [sequence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sequence)* (time passing) and *factual [causation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Causality)* (one thing forcing the other to happen). Push them to find those specific signal words ("therefore", "consequently") before they declare a text cause and effect!

### 3. Compare and Contrast Text Structure
This structure **highlights the similarities and differences between two or more subjects.** Are we reading about [frogs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frog) and [toads](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toad)? [Alligators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alligator) and [crocodiles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crocodile)? 
*The tool of the trade:* **[Venn diagrams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venn_diagram) are standard visual tools used to teach compare and contrast text structures.** If you read a text and can instantly see a Venn diagram forming in your mind, you are reading compare and contrast.

### 4. Problem and Solution Text Structure
Here, the author **introduces an issue and outlines one or more ways to resolve the issue.** This is heavily used in [persuasive texts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persuasion) and [science writing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Science_journalism) (e.g., "The [ocean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ocean) is filling with [plastic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plastic_pollution), so scientists invented a new [biodegradable material](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodegradation)..."). 

### 5. Descriptive Text Structure
Sometimes, an author just wants to paint a picture of a single thing. A **descriptive text structure provides detailed [characteristics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Characteristic) or features of a specific topic without comparing the topic to anything else.** Think of a paragraph detailing the physical traits of a [Blue Whale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blue_whale). It’s not a story, it’s not solving a problem—it’s just a beautifully detailed snapshot.

## Part 3: The Dashboard of a Book (Text Features)

Imagine buying a brand new [car](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Car), sitting in the driver's seat, ignoring the [dashboard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dashboard), the mirrors, and the [navigation screen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Automotive_navigation_system), and just staring blankly out the [windshield](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Windshield). That is what a child is doing when they read an informational text while ignoring the text features. 

> **Text features are visual or organizational elements independent of the main text body that enhance reader comprehension.**

Here is the dashboard you must teach them to use:

*   **[Table of Contents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Table_of_contents):** Lists major text sections and corresponding page numbers at the *beginning* of a book. (The map).
*   **[Index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Index_%28publishing%29):** Provides an alphabetical list of specific topics and corresponding page numbers at the *end* of a book. (The [search engine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Search_engine)).
*   **[Glossary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary):** Provides definitions for domain-specific vocabulary words found within a specific text. (The built-in dictionary).
*   **Headings and Subheadings:** Organize text content into manageable topical sections for the reader. (The road signs).
*   **[Captions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photo_caption):** Short descriptions placed directly underneath or beside an image to explain the image's context. 
*   **[Sidebars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sidebar_%28publishing%29):** Distinct text boxes that provide supplementary information related to the main text topic. 

**🚨 The Classroom Reality:** **Elementary students often skip informational text features unless specifically prompted by an educator.** 
To an eight-year-old, a sidebar looks like an [advertisement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Advertising) on a [website](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Website): something to be ignored to get to the "real" text. They will look at a [photograph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photograph) of a [volcano](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volcano), but they won't read the caption explaining *which* volcano it is. 

*How to teach it:* **[Explicit instruction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direct_instruction) on text features requires teachers to model how to use [visual aids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visual_aid) *before* reading the main text.** You cannot just say, "Read chapter four." You must put the book under the [document camera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Document_camera) and say, "Before we read a single paragraph, let's look at this photograph. Let's read the caption together. Let's look at this bold heading. What do we think this page is going to be about?" You have to prove to them that the dashboard is useful!

## Part 4: Who is Talking and Why? (Point of View & Purpose)

We’ve looked at the words, the structure, and the features. Now we must look at the person standing behind the text. 

### Point of View (POV)

> **[Point of view](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_of_view_%28literature%29) determines the [narrator's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Narrator) perspective through which a story or information is told.**

Whose eyes are we looking through? 
*   **[First-person point of view](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First-person_narrative)** uses [pronouns](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pronoun) like "I" and "we" to narrate a story from a character's direct perspective. We are inside their head. 
*   **[Third-person limited point of view](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Third-person_narrative)** reveals the internal thoughts and feelings of *only one character*. The narrator is outside the story, hovering over one character's shoulder.
*   **[Third-person omniscient point of view](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Omniscient_narrator)** reveals the internal thoughts and feelings of *multiple characters*. The narrator is a god-like figure who knows everything everyone is thinking ("omniscient" = all-knowing).

**🚨 The Classroom Reality:** **Students often incorrectly identify third-person narratives as first-person narratives when character [dialogue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dialogue) contains the word "I".**
*Imagine this text:* The little boy looked at his mother. "I want a [cookie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cookie)," he said. He felt incredibly hungry.
*The student's brain:* "Aha! I see the word 'I'! It's first-person!"
*How to teach it:* You must teach students to look at the narration *outside* the [quotation marks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quotation_mark). The dialogue belongs to the characters, but the POV belongs to the *narrator*. If the narrator is calling the character "he," it's third-person, regardless of what the character says out loud.

### Author's Purpose and Argument

Why did the author bother writing this text at all? **An author's purpose is the primary reason the author wrote the text.** We generally categorize these into the easy-to-remember [acronym](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acronym) **PIE**: **Common author purposes include to [persuade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persuasion), to [inform](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information), and to [entertain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entertainment).**

When an author's purpose is to *persuade*, they must build an [argument](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Argument). You will teach your students that **an argument structure requires a main claim supported by [logical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logic) reasons and factual [evidence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evidence).** 
*   **Claim:** The author's main stance (e.g., "Schools should serve [pizza](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pizza) every day.")
*   **Reason:** Why they believe it (e.g., "Because it increases student attendance.")
*   **Evidence:** The [data](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data) proving it (e.g., "A study showed attendance rose by 15% on pizza days.")

## Part 5: When Authors Collide (Comparing Texts)

Finally, we reach the summit of elementary [reading comprehension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reading_comprehension): synthesis. 

**Comparing texts involves analyzing how two different authors approach the exact same topic or theme.** This is an incredible [critical thinking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Critical_thinking) exercise for young minds. You might hand them two articles about [wolves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolf). One article is written by a [wildlife conservationist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wildlife_conservation). The other is written by a [sheep farmer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sheep_farming). 

What happens? **Two authors writing about the same topic may present conflicting [facts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fact) based on the authors' individual perspectives or [biases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bias).** 

The conservationist’s text might use words with positive *connotations* (majestic, wild, vital), employ a *descriptive* text structure to show the beauty of the wolf, and have a *purpose* to persuade the reader to protect the animal. 

The farmer’s text might use words with negative *connotations* (vicious, dangerous, [pests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pest_%28organism%29)), employ a *problem and solution* text structure (Problem: Wolves eat sheep. Solution: [Fences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fence) and [traps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapping)), and present entirely different "facts" about wolf [behavior](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethology).

When you teach a student to compare these texts, you are teaching them that everything they read is constructed by a [human being](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human) with a perspective. You are empowering them to look past the surface, identify the author's craft, and think for themselves. 

***

**Your Takeaway as a Teacher:**
When you sit for your [Content Knowledge exam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Praxis_test), remember that you are not just being tested on whether *you* know what a metaphor or an index is. You are being tested on whether you understand how a child encounters these concepts. Look for the [distractor answers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiple_choice) that highlight common student errors (like confusing mood and tone, or getting tricked by the word "I" in dialogue). Understand the gears, know where the kids get their fingers pinched, and you will do brilliantly.
