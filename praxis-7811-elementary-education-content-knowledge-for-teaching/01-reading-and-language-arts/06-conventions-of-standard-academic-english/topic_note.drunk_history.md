Barkeep, slide another ginger ale over here! My friend here is student-teaching second grade, and she has looked into the abyss. You’ve been grading their writing, haven't you? I can see the thousand-yard stare. You’re holding these papers, looking at a sentence like "I runned fast too the house because the dog's barked," and you are wondering if human language was a mistake. 

It wasn’t! You just have to stop looking at their writing as a list of errors and start seeing it as a chaotic, unlicensed construction site. These kids are operating heavy linguistic machinery without a permit! Our job isn't to shut down the site; it’s to teach them the building codes. 

Let's start from the ground up with the raw materials. I’m talking about orthography. Orthography isn't just a fancy word to throw around at faculty meetings; orthography is the conventional spelling system of a language. It’s the zoning laws of the alphabet. Your kids are starting with the absolute basics, the CVC word. A CVC word consists of a consonant, a vowel, and a consonant. Think of "cat" or "pig." It’s a little linguistic shack. Because it's so small, CVC words typically contain a short vowel sound. 

But then, the kids want to build something bigger. They want a long vowel. Enter the CVCe spelling pattern! This involves a consonant, a vowel, a consonant, and a final silent "e". I call that final "e" the mob boss. He doesn't say a word, but the final silent "e" in a CVCe word usually signals the preceding vowel to represent a long vowel sound. He just stands at the end of "cake" and forces the "a" to yell its own name! Sometimes, though, vowels unionize. That's your vowel teams. Vowel teams consist of two or more consecutive vowels representing a single vowel sound. They share the workload—like in "boat," two letters clock in, but only one sound gets made. And then, you have the absolute rogue contractor: the r-controlled vowel. An r-controlled vowel represents a sound heavily influenced by the consonant "r" immediately following the vowel. The "r" completely hijacks the vowel, tosses it in the trunk, and takes over the sound in words like "car" or "bird."

Now, when you want to add onto these houses—say, an addition on the back—you have spelling codes to follow! If you have a little CVC shack and you want to add a vowel suffix, you can't just slap it on; the roof will cave in and "hopping" becomes "hoping." That's why doubling the final consonant before adding a vowel suffix is a common spelling rule for CVC words. You have to reinforce the wall! What if the house ends in a "y"? Changing a final consonant "y" to "i" before adding a suffix is a common English spelling rule. The "y" is too unstable to hold the new addition. And what about that mob boss, the silent "e"? He hates company. The final silent "e" is typically dropped before adding a suffix beginning with a vowel. You gotta evict him before you can build "baking."

But spelling is just the paint. Let's look at the plumbing: morphology! Morphology is the study of the internal structure of words. We are dealing with morphemes here, my friend. A morpheme is the absolute smallest unit of meaning in a language. You crack a word open, and these are the gears inside. The main engine is the base word, which functions as a standalone meaningful word. You can take "build" and it works all by itself. But sometimes you dig up a root word. A root word carries the primary meaning of a word, sure, but English root words often require affixes to function as complete words. Think of the root "struct." It means to build, but you can’t just walk up to someone and say, "I struct." You look insane! You need attachments! 

A prefix is an affix attached to the beginning of a base word or root, like a front porch. A suffix is an affix attached to the end of a base word or root, like a back deck. But suffixes come in two drastically different flavors, and your kids are going to mix them up. First, you have inflectional suffixes. These just slap a new coat of paint on the house—inflectional suffixes indicate grammatical features such as tense, number, or degree. Critically, inflectional suffixes never change the core part of speech of the base word. A "cat" is a noun, "cats" are nouns. "Walk" is a verb, "walked" is a verb. 

On the other hand, derivational affixes are total structural demolitions! Derivational affixes create new words by altering the core meaning of the base word, and because of this, derivational affixes often change the part of speech of the base word. You take a verb like "teach," slap on "-er", and boom, it's a noun! 

This brings me back to that kid who wrote, "I runned fast." You probably circled it in red ink and wept. Stop crying! That kid is a genius! They learned the inflectional rule that "-ed" means past tense, and they applied it! Elementary students frequently overgeneralize the "-ed" suffix to form the past tense of irregular verbs. The words "goed" and "runned" represent common student errors of overgeneralizing the "-ed" past tense suffix. They didn't fail at English; English failed them by being an ancient, irregular, rule-breaking mess! You just gently pull them aside and say, "I love your logic, kiddo, but 'run' is a rebel. We have to memorize him."

Alright, we’ve got the parts. Let's frame the house! Let's talk syntax. To build a sentence, kids need to know the difference between a pile of lumber and a load-bearing wall. A phrase is just a group of related words lacking a subject-verb combination. "Under the bed." Lovely imagery, but it goes nowhere! An independent clause, however, contains a subject and a verb. It has an engine! And because it has an engine, an independent clause expresses a complete thought. It can stand proudly on its own. Now, a dependent clause also contains a subject and a verb, but it's a cliffhanger. Because it has a subordinating word tied to it, a dependent clause does not express a complete thought. "Because I said so..." Because you said so, what?! You can't just leave a dependent clause hanging in the wind; it's an attached garage without a house!

Once they know this, they can build the four models of houses. A simple sentence consists of exactly one independent clause. Just one strong, sturdy cabin. Because it's simple, a simple sentence contains no dependent clauses. Then they get ambitious and build townhomes: a compound sentence contains two or more independent clauses. Again, a compound sentence contains no dependent clauses. But how do you connect those two independent clauses? You can't just smash them together! Independent clauses in a compound sentence are often joined by a coordinating conjunction. You know them, the FANBOYS! Coordinating conjunctions include for, and, nor, but, or, yet, and so. 

Then we have the architectural marvels. A complex sentence contains one independent clause and at least one dependent clause. A house with an attached garage! And for the grand finale, the mansion: a compound-complex sentence contains two or more independent clauses and at least one dependent clause. 

But what happens when the kids play architect without a license? Disaster. You get a sentence fragment, which is an incomplete sentence punctuated as a complete sentence. Usually, sentence fragments occur when a dependent clause is punctuated as a standalone sentence. They write, "When the dog barked." Period. Crash! The house falls down. Or, they go the other way and build a run-on sentence, which incorrectly fuses two independent clauses without appropriate punctuation. Two massive houses slammed into each other with no hallway! And the worst offense of all? The comma splice. A comma splice occurs when two independent clauses are joined with only a comma. Listen to me: a comma is a piece of Scotch tape! You cannot use a comma to hold two skyscrapers together! 

Let's check the electricity inside the house—the verbs! Verbs control the time zone. Present tense verbs describe an action happening currently. Past tense verbs describe an action that has already occurred. Future tense verbs describe an action that will happen at a later time. Simple, right? But time is a fluid dimension! Sometimes things are ongoing. The progressive verb tense indicates an ongoing continuous action. To build it, the progressive verb tense uses a form of the "be" verb along with a present participle ending in "-ing". ("I am teaching.") And when something is totally wrapped up? The perfect verb tense describes completed actions. The perfect verb tense uses a form of the auxiliary verb "have" along with a past participle. ("I have survived grading.")

Here is where the plumbing absolutely explodes: subject-verb agreement. The rule is foolproof on paper. Subject-verb agreement requires a singular subject to be paired with a singular verb form, and subject-verb agreement requires a plural subject to be paired with a plural verb form. So why do your fifth graders constantly write, "The box of crayons are broken"? Because of the mirage! Intervening prepositional phrases between a subject and verb frequently cause subject-verb agreement errors among students! The kids see the plural word "crayons" right next to the verb and they panic. You have to train them to cross out the intervening phrase "of crayons" so they can see the true, singular load-bearing beam: the box!

Finally, we finish the house with landscaping and security cameras. Punctuation and capitalization! Capitalization is required for the first word of every sentence. We know this. And capitalization is required for all proper nouns, because proper nouns designate specific names of people, places, and organizations. They are VIPs! And book titles? Even trickier. The first and last words of a title must be capitalized. Furthermore, important words within a title must be capitalized. But the little guys hiding in the bushes? Articles, short prepositions, and coordinating conjunctions in the middle of a title are typically not capitalized. 

Then we have commas, the yield signs of our grammatical roads. A comma separates items in a series of three or more items. A comma precedes a coordinating conjunction linking two independent clauses—that’s the driveway between the townhomes! And a comma follows an introductory phrase or dependent clause at the beginning of a sentence. 

When your kids start writing dialogue, things get cinematic. Quotation marks enclose the exact words spoken by someone in direct speech. But kids always throw the trash in the street instead of the bin! You have to hammer this into their little heads: commas and periods are placed inside closing quotation marks in standard American English. Keep the punctuation inside the fence!

And I saved the most dangerous tool for last. The chainsaw. The apostrophe. It has exactly two jobs in this world. First, an apostrophe indicates possession. "The dog's bone." Second, an apostrophe indicates omitted letters in a contraction. "Don't." That is it! But what do the kids do? They run through the streets revving the chainsaw! Students frequently misapply apostrophes to make regular nouns plural. You walk into a grocery store and see a sign that says "Apple's \$1.00" and you want to scream! Plurals do not get apostrophes! 

And the absolute deadliest trap on the construction site? "Its" versus "It's". Students often confuse the possessive pronoun "its" with the contraction "it's". They see that a dog has a bone, they want to show possession, so they slap an apostrophe in there. No! The pronoun "its" does not use an apostrophe to show possession! It is an independent pronoun, it needs no help! On the other hand, the contraction "it's" represents the phrase "it is" or "it has". It's just two words squished together in a trash compactor with the apostrophe marking the casualty!

So next time you sit down with a stack of papers, don't look at them as a pile of mistakes. Look at them as a booming metropolis built by a bunch of wild, brilliant, totally reckless miniature architects. You just gotta give them the blueprints! Now drink your ginger ale, you’ve got lesson plans to write!
