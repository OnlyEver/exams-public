# The [Physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics) of [Language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Language): A Teacher’s Guide to Form, Function, and [Meaning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meaning_%28linguistics%29)

Welcome! Let’s get right to it. When most people think about teaching [language arts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Language_arts), they picture a strict [grammarian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grammarian) wielding a red pen, hunting for misplaced [commas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comma) and [split infinitives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Split_infinitive). 

But you and I? We are going to look at [language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Language) like [physicists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physicist). 

[Language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Language) is not just a list of arbitrary rules; it is a living, breathing [ecosystem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem) of human connection. For the [Elementary Education](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primary_education) Content Knowledge for Teaching (CKT) exam, you don't just need to know *what* a [comma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comma) does. You need to know *why* a student used it, *how* it changes the [meaning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meaning_%28linguistics%29) of a [sentence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sentence_%28linguistics%29), and *what* [instructional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pedagogy) choices you can make to help that student unlock their full expressive potential.

Let’s dismantle the engine of the [English language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_language) and see how it actually works.

## Part 1: The [Linguistic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linguistics) [Ecosystem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem) ([Dialects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dialect) and [Registers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Register_%28sociolinguistics%29))

One of the most profound [paradigm shifts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paradigm_shift) you will experience as an [educator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teacher) involves how you view the [language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Language) your students bring into the [classroom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classroom). 

Imagine a student excitedly telling you a story: *"He don't got no pencil."* 
Historically, [schools](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/School) treated this as "broken" [English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_language). It isn't. To understand why, we need to talk about [dialect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dialect).

> **Dialect:** A **[dialect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dialect)** is a [variety of a language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variety_%28linguistics%29) characteristic of a particular [regional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regional_dialect) or [social group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sociolect). 

Here is the most beautiful, foundational truth of [linguistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linguistics): **all linguistic [dialects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dialect) are strictly rule-governed systems of [communication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communication).** When that student says *"He don't got no pencil,"* they are executing the complex, highly structured [grammatical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grammar) rules of their specific [dialect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dialect) with perfect accuracy! Because of this, we know scientifically that **no single [dialect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dialect) is inherently linguistically superior to any other [dialect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dialect).** 

![A map of primary dialect regions in the UK and Ireland, illustrating how linguistic varieties are highly structured and geographically distributed.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Dialects_of_English_in_UK_and_Ireland.svg)

### The Teacher's Role in Dialect
So, what is your job when teaching [Standard English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_English)? Do you just correct them? Absolutely not. 

**[Teachers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teacher) support [language development](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Language_development) by treating students' home [dialects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dialect) as valid [communication systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communication).** 
Think of [language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Language) like a wardrobe. You don't throw away a perfectly good [swimsuit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swimsuit) just because you bought a [tuxedo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuxedo). You just wear them to different events! In the [classroom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classroom), **[Standard English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_English) instruction should frame [Standard English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_English) as an addition to a student's linguistic repertoire**, expanding their wardrobe. Crucially, **[Standard English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_English) instruction should never frame [Standard English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_English) as a replacement for a student's home [dialect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dialect).** 

### Language Registers and Formality
If [dialects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dialect) are the clothes, *[registers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Register_%28sociolinguistics%29)* are the dress codes. A **[language register](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Register_%28sociolinguistics%29)** is the specific level of formality a speaker uses in a given [context](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Context_%28language_use%29). 

There are five primary [language registers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Register_%28sociolinguistics%29) you need to know:

| Register | Definition & Example |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Frozen** | [Language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Language) that rarely or never changes. *([The Pledge of Allegiance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pledge_of_Allegiance), the [Preamble to the Constitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Preamble_to_the_United_States_Constitution))* |
| **Formal** | One-way [communication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communication) with strict rules and [complete sentences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sentence_%28linguistics%29). *([Academic essays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Academic_writing), [keynote speeches](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Keynote))* |
| **Consultative** | Two-way [communication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communication) in a professional or [academic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Academia) setting. *(A [teacher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teacher) talking to a student, a [doctor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physician) talking to a [patient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patient))* |
| **Casual** | [Language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Language) used among peers and friends. Contains [slang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slang) and loose [grammar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grammar). *(Recess conversations, [text messages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Text_messaging))* |
| **Intimate** | Private [communication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communication) reserved for close [family members](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Family) or partners. *([Inside jokes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/In-joke), [pet names](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypocorism))* |

![Schoolchildren reciting the Pledge of Allegiance. Because its wording is strictly preserved and universally repeated without alteration, the Pledge serves as a classic example of the frozen language register.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/PledgeOfAllegiance1899.jpg)

How do we decide which [register](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Register_%28sociolinguistics%29) to use? It’s completely situational. **The required level of formality in [communication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communication) is dictated by the target [audience](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Audience)**, as well as being heavily influenced because **the required level of formality in [communication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communication) is dictated by the specific purpose of the message.** You wouldn't use a [formal register](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Register_%28sociolinguistics%29) to comfort a crying [kindergartener](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kindergarten), and you wouldn't use an [intimate register](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Register_%28sociolinguistics%29) to write a [principal's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Head_teacher) [newsletter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newsletter).

As students navigate these different spaces, they naturally engage in **[code-switching](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Code-switching)**, which is the practice of alternating between two or more [language varieties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variety_%28linguistics%29) in a single [conversation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conversation). A student might use a consultative [register](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Register_%28sociolinguistics%29) with you at the [whiteboard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whiteboard), and instantly [code-switch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Code-switching) into a casual, [regional dialect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regional_dialect) with their best friend on the carpet.

## Part 2: The [Chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemistry) of [Words](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Word) ([Vocabulary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vocabulary) and [Meaning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meaning_%28linguistics%29))

[Words](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Word) are the raw elements of [thought](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thought). When we teach students to [write](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Writing), we are teaching them to select the exact right element for the reaction they want to cause in their [reader](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reading).

### Denotation vs. Connotation
If a student writes, *"The [smell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Olfaction) coming from the [cafeteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cafeteria) was strong,"* we get the basic idea. But "smell" and "strong" are blunt instruments. We want precision. **Precise [vocabulary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vocabulary) reduces [ambiguity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ambiguity) in [written communication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Written_language).**

To get precise, students must master two layers of [meaning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meaning_%28linguistics%29):
1.  **[Denotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Denotation)** is the literal [dictionary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dictionary) definition of a [word](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Word). 
2.  **[Connotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connotation)** refers to the emotional or [cultural](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Culture) associations attached to a specific [word](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Word).

The [words](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Word) *aroma*, *fragrance*, *[odor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Odor)*, and *stench* all share the same basic [denotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Denotation) (a [smell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Olfaction)). But their *[connotations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connotation)* are vastly different! "The *aroma* coming from the [cafeteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cafeteria)" makes you hungry. "The *stench* coming from the [cafeteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cafeteria)" makes you call the [health inspector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Environmental_health_officer). 

### Contextual Word Choices
When [writing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Writing) academically, [word choice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diction) dictates the [register](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Register_%28sociolinguistics%29). For instance, **[academic writing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Academic_writing) typically avoids [colloquialisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colloquialism) to maintain a [formal register](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Register_%28sociolinguistics%29).** 
*   What is it? A **[colloquialism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colloquialism)** is a [word](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Word) or [phrase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phrase) that is not formal or [literary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Literature) (e.g., saying "gonna" or "kid" instead of "going to" or "[child](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Child)"). 
*   Go even further down the informal scale, and you hit [slang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slang). **[Slang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slang) consists of very informal [vocabulary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vocabulary) typically restricted to a particular [context](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Context_%28language_use%29) or group of people** (e.g., a student saying a book is "[bussin'](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slang)"). 

On the other end of the spectrum is [academic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Academia) mastery. When students write a [science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Science) report, teaching them to use [words](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Word) like *[photosynthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photosynthesis)* or *[chrysalis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pupa)* isn't just about [spelling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spelling). **Domain-specific [vocabulary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vocabulary) demonstrates [expertise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expert) in a particular [academic subject](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Academic_discipline).** It proves to the [reader](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reading), "I belong in this [conversation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conversation)."

![A scientific schematic demonstrating photosynthesis. Providing students with domain-specific vocabulary allows them to demonstrate precise knowledge and academic expertise in their writing.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Photosynthesis_en.svg)

## Part 3: The [Physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics) of Pacing ([Punctuation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Punctuation) for Effect)

[Punctuation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Punctuation) is not just a traffic cop telling you where to stop; it is the [sheet music](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sheet_music) of [written language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Written_language). **[Punctuation marks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Punctuation) guide the [reader's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reading) pacing through a [text](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Text_%28literary_theory%29).** Let's look at three powerful tools you can teach young [writers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Writer) to use for [rhetorical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhetoric) effect.

### The Em Dash (—)
Think of the [em dash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dash) as an interruption—a delightful little detour. **An [em dash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dash) indicates a sudden break in a [writer's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Writer) [thought](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thought).** It functions similarly to a [comma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comma), but with much more [theatrical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theatre) flair. In fact, **an [em dash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dash) provides greater emphasis to a [phrase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phrase) than a standard [comma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comma) provides.**
*   *[Comma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comma):* The [monster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monster), terrifying and green, slowly opened the door.
*   *[Em Dash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dash):* The [monster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monster)—terrifying and green—slowly opened the door!

![A typographic comparison showing the em dash relative to the hyphen and en dash. The extended length of the em dash visually signals a distinct break or theatrical interruption in a sentence.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Dash_font_comparison.png)

### The Semicolon (;)
Students often think a [semicolon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semicolon) is just a fancy [period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Full_stop). Not quite! **A [semicolon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semicolon) joins two [independent clauses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Independent_clause) to emphasize a close thematic relationship between those [clauses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clause).** It tells the [reader](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reading), "These two thoughts are so deeply connected, I refuse to put a [full stop](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Full_stop) between them." 
*   *Example:* "The [storm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Storm) raged outside; the [children](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Child) huddled together near the fire."

### The Ellipsis (...)
The [ellipsis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipsis) is the master of time and silence. **An [ellipsis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipsis) can indicate a trailing off of a character's [thought](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thought)** (e.g., *"I thought I locked the door..."*). 
[Pedagogically](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pedagogy), teaching the [ellipsis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipsis) is a great way to help students write [narratives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Narrative) because **an [ellipsis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipsis) can create [suspense](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suspense) by intentionally delaying the end of a [sentence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sentence_%28linguistics%29).** 
*   *Example:* "He reached into the dark box and pulled out..."

![A mural depicting an ellipsis as a digital typing indicator. In both literature and modern digital communication, the ellipsis effectively delays resolution and builds anticipation.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Left_on_read.jpg)

## Part 4: The [Architecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Architecture) of [Thought](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thought) ([Sentence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sentence_%28linguistics%29) Construction)

Once students have the [words](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Word) and the [punctuation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Punctuation), they have to build the house. The way a student constructs a [sentence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sentence_%28linguistics%29) reveals how they are organizing their [thoughts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thought). 

### Expanding and Reducing Sentences
Young [writers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Writer) often write simple, bare-bones [sentences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sentence_%28linguistics%29): *"The dog barked."* 
We want to teach them expansion. **Expanding [sentences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sentence_%28linguistics%29) involves adding descriptive [modifiers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grammatical_modifier) to provide greater detail.** A highly effective, concrete way to teach this is by showing students how **expanding [sentences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sentence_%28linguistics%29) can be achieved by attaching [prepositional phrases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Preposition_and_postposition) to the core [clause](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clause).**
*   *Core [clause](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clause):* The dog barked.
*   *Expanded with [prepositional phrases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Preposition_and_postposition):* The dog barked **at the [mailman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mail_carrier)** **through the screen door**.

![A structural analysis of an English sentence demonstrating how a core clause can be expanded by seamlessly attaching a prepositional phrase.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Constituent_structure_analysis_English_sentence.svg)

On the flip side, as students get older, they sometimes try to sound "smart" by using too many [words](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Word): *"Due to the fact that the dog was in a state of barking..."* 
Here, we teach reduction. **Reducing [sentences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sentence_%28linguistics%29) eliminates [wordiness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Verbosity) to increase [textual](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Text_%28literary_theory%29) clarity.** By teaching them that **removing redundant [phrases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phrase) directly streamlines student [writing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Writing)**, you empower them to get straight to the point.

### Sentence Combining
This is perhaps the most heavily tested [instructional strategy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pedagogy) on your CKT exam. **Sentence combining merges two related [sentences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sentence_%28linguistics%29) using appropriate [conjunctions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conjunction_%28grammar%29).** 

Why do we do this? Because **sentence combining explicitly demonstrates the [logical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logic) relationship between two distinct ideas.** Let's look at the "glue" we use to combine them:
*   **[Coordinating conjunctions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conjunction_%28grammar%29)** (For, And, Nor, But, Or, Yet, So) **give equal weight to multiple ideas within a combined [sentence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sentence_%28linguistics%29).** *(It rained, so we stayed inside.)*
*   **[Subordinating conjunctions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conjunction_%28grammar%29)** (Because, Although, If, Since) **establish unequal importance between two combined ideas.** *(Because it rained, we stayed inside.)* Notice how the rain becomes the cause, dominating the effect!

The pedagogical [research](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Research) here is crystal clear. You need to know that **sentence combining exercises directly improve a student's [syntactic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syntax) maturity.** Furthermore, because they are manipulating ideas rather than just generating new ones from scratch, **sentence combining exercises improve overall [writing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Writing) [fluency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluency).**

### Voice and Pacing
Finally, let's talk about the macro-level of [writing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Writing): flow. If a student writes ten [sentences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sentence_%28linguistics%29) in a row that are all exactly seven [words](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Word) long, the [reader](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reading) will fall asleep. **Varying [sentence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sentence_%28linguistics%29) lengths prevents [written text](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Written_language) from sounding [monotonous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monotone).** 

If you want to teach a [child](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Child) to write a truly thrilling [story](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Narrative), teach them this trick: **placing a short [sentence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sentence_%28linguistics%29) immediately after a series of long [sentences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sentence_%28linguistics%29) creates dramatic [rhetorical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhetoric) emphasis.**
*   *Example:* "The enormous, scaly [dragon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dragon) roared furiously as it soared through the dark, stormy clouds, searching desperately for the [knight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knight) who had stolen its [gold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gold). Then it stopped. It saw him." 

*Boom.* That short [sentence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sentence_%28linguistics%29) hits like a drum!

Lastly, we must look at the "[voice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voice_%28grammar%29)" of the [sentence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sentence_%28linguistics%29). 
*   **[Active voice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Active_voice) generally creates more concise and direct [sentences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sentence_%28linguistics%29) than [passive voice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Passive_voice).** *(The dog bit the man).* 
*   By contrast, **a [passive voice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Passive_voice) [sentence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sentence_%28linguistics%29) places the recipient of the action as the [grammatical subject](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subject_%28grammar%29).** *(The man was bitten by the dog).* 

[Teachers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teacher) often say "never use [passive voice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Passive_voice)." That's incorrect! As [linguistic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linguistics) [physicists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physicist), we know every tool has a purpose. **[Passive voice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Passive_voice) is sometimes used deliberately to shift focus away from the actor.** If a [politician](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politician) messes up, they don't say, "I made mistakes" ([Active](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Active_voice)). They say, "Mistakes were made" ([Passive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Passive_voice)). They use [grammar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grammar) to make the actor magically disappear!

### Conclusion
As a [teacher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teacher), your job is to demystify these forms and functions of [language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Language) for your students. You are not handing down decrees from a [grammar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grammar) [dictionary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dictionary); you are handing them the keys to a [laboratory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laboratory). 

When you honor their home [dialects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dialect), show them the power of [code-switching](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Code-switching), hand them the precise [words](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Word) they need, and show them how to bolt their [sentences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sentence_%28linguistics%29) together with [conjunctions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conjunction_%28grammar%29) and [punctuation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Punctuation), you aren't just teaching [English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_language). You are teaching them how to command the world around them through the power of their own [voice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_voice).

![A visual spectrogram of the human voice. When students learn to master the complex physics of language, they unlock the full potential of their own unique voice.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Human_voice_spectrogram.jpg)
