Listen to me, grab your drink, and look me in the eye. You’re sitting there thinking that teaching elementary writing is going to be some serene, cinematic moment. You think you’re going to hand out cute little prompt journals, and these kids are just going to elegantly whisper their souls onto the page like tiny poets. 

Oh, you beautiful, naive soul. 

Teaching an eight-year-old to write is like running the kitchen of a Michelin-star restaurant right in the middle of a massive, coordinated bank heist. It is high-stakes, the cognitive load is absolutely redlining, and if you don’t understand exactly how the machinery of their brains works, the whole operation goes up in flames. 

You need to understand the architecture of this kitchen, which means you must master the standard stages of the writing process—which include prewriting, drafting, revising, editing, and publishing. But here is the massive, screaming trap most rookie teachers fall into: they teach these stages like a straight, one-way staircase. Wrong! The writing process is recursive rather than strictly linear. You don't just chop, cook, and plate in a neat little row. Writers frequently return to earlier stages of the writing process to refine their work. A kid might be halfway through writing their story, realize their villain has absolutely no motive, and sprint right back into the brainstorming phase. That’s not a malfunction; that’s the beautiful, chaotic reality of a working kitchen!

Let’s start in the pantry: the prewriting stage. This is where the prewriting stage involves generating ideas before composing full sentences or paragraphs. If you ask a kid to stare at a blank page and immediately write a perfect, continuous sentence, their brain will crash like an overheated hard drive. You have to prep the ingredients first. This is why teachers use graphic organizers during prewriting to help students structure their ideas visually. You give them a mind map or a Venn diagram, so they can lay all their raw ingredients out in little prep bowls before they even turn on the stove. 

And before they even chop a single onion, they need to know who is sitting in the dining room! Determining the target audience during prewriting guides a writer's subsequent vocabulary choices. If they are writing a piece about dinosaurs for their kindergarten reading buddies, they are going to use the word "big." If they are writing for the principal, they might use "gargantuan." Who is eating this meal? That audience dictates everything! Furthermore, determining the target audience during prewriting influences the overall tone of the final text. Are we throwing a raucous pizza party, or serving a formal five-course dinner? Tone is decided before the pan gets hot. And while we're on audience, remind your students that adapting their writing style to an audience requires adjusting sentence complexity based on the reader's presumed background knowledge. If your reader doesn't know what a "meteorite" is, you can't just drop it in a complex, multi-clause sentence without explaining it first! 

Once the prep is done, we hit the absolute chaos of the drafting stage. The drafting stage focuses on translating ideas into continuous text. And listen to me—this is critical for your Content Knowledge for Teaching—drafting prioritizes content generation over grammatical perfection. You just need to get the meat on the grill! Do you know what happens if a kid stops to worry about whether "beautiful" has an 'e' or an 'a' while they are having a breakthrough about their main character? The meat burns. Emphasizing perfect spelling during the drafting stage interrupts a student's flow of ideas. You tell them, "Guess the spelling and keep moving! Do not stop stirring the sauce!"

Now comes the absolute hardest part to teach: revising. Revising is like realizing your soup is entirely too thin and deciding to turn it into a stew. It's moving the walls of the restaurant. Revising involves making structural and content changes to improve the clarity of a written text. But here is the psychological roadblock: elementary students often incorrectly equate revising a draft with correcting spelling errors. They think "revising" a completely raw, unseasoned chicken breast just means sprinkling a little spelling-error parsley on top. No! You have to explicitly teach them that adding descriptive details to a narrative draft is a revision strategy. You have to teach them that deleting off-topic sentences from a paragraph is a revision strategy—if there are gummy bears in your spaghetti, pick them out! And you have to show them that reordering paragraphs to improve logical flow is a revision strategy. Put the appetizer before the dessert!

It is only after the structural integrity of the meal is perfect that we move to editing. Editing focuses exclusively on correcting errors in spelling, capitalization, punctuation, and grammar. This is wiping the smudges off the rim of the plate so the presentation is flawless. And don't do all the tasting yourself! Set up a system where peer review provides students with an authentic audience to provide constructive feedback on drafts. When an eight-year-old knows their best friend is going to read their paper, they suddenly care deeply about where that period goes. Finally, we reach publishing. Publishing involves formatting a final written piece to share with an intended audience. It’s the grand reveal, the moment the waiter carries the cloche into the dining room. 

Now, step back for a second. We’ve talked about how to cook, but what kind of restaurant are you running? Because the primary purpose of a text dictates the genre and structure of the writing. Form always follows function. In your classroom, you are essentially juggling three franchises, because the three primary purposes for elementary school writing tasks are to persuade, to inform, and to entertain. 

If they are running the persuasive franchise, persuasive writing requires the clear articulation of claims supported by relevant evidence. "We should have extra recess because science says we need vitamin D." Boom. Claim and evidence. If they are running the informational franchise, informational writing requires the objective presentation of facts and explanations. No opinions, just the cold, hard nutritional label of truth. But if they are running the entertainment franchise? That’s where narrative writing utilizes story elements like character development and plot progression. We need heroes, villains, and a climax!

As the head chef—I mean, teacher—you also control the clock. You have to purposefully vary the types of writing tasks you assign. Sometimes, you are hosting a three-month pop-up restaurant. Extended writing tasks occur over multiple class sessions or weeks. Because you have all this time, extended writing tasks allow for deep topic research, extensive peer review, and multiple draft iterations. It’s the ultimate slow-roast. 

But sometimes, your kids are on a reality TV cooking show and the host just yelled, "Go!" That’s on-demand writing, which requires students to plan, draft, and finalize a text within a single, restricted time frame. It’s pure survival. And other times, you just want to taste their raw instincts, which is where first-draft writing assesses a student's ability to quickly organize and articulate ideas without the benefit of extensive revision. It’s a snapshot of their brain, right in that very second. 

So, how do you actually teach all of this without losing your mind? You need your pedagogical playbook. First, never hide the health inspector’s checklist! Scoring rubrics provide students with explicit quality expectations for a writing task before the drafting stage begins. Give them the rubric on day one! Next, show them what a million-dollar meal looks like. Mentor texts serve as published examples for students to emulate specific writing styles, tones, or structures. You read them a wildly funny Mo Willems book and say, "We are going to steal his recipe for dialogue!"

Since writing happens invisibly inside the skull, you have to drag it out into the light. Teachers use think-aloud strategies to verbally model their internal decision-making during the writing process. You stand at the whiteboard and literally talk to yourself: "Hmm, I want to write 'the dog ran,' but 'ran' is boring. What's a spicier word? Sprinted! Yes!" You are letting them eavesdrop on your brain. 

When they are ready to cook with you, you have two incredibly powerful, distinct tools. Listen to the difference, because this is where teachers mess up. Sometimes, you use shared writing. Shared writing involves the teacher acting as the sole scribe while students contribute ideas aloud. You are holding the only knife. They are shouting out ingredients, but you are doing the dangerous work of spelling and formatting so they can just focus on brilliant ideas. But then there is interactive writing. Interactive writing involves the teacher and students physically sharing the pen to compose a text together. You write the easy sight words, and then you literally hand the marker—you hand over the spatula—to a student so they can sound out and write the tough words right there on the chart paper with you. 

And finally, when the kitchen is bustling and everyone is working on their own dishes, you don't stand at the front of the room shouting into a megaphone. You do writing conferences. Writing conferences provide individualized, targeted teacher feedback to a student on a specific piece of writing. You walk up to one student’s station, look at their draft, and give them one piece of feedback. Just one! You don't critique their entire existence; you just say, "Let's work on adding a little more descriptive detail to this one paragraph," and then you walk away. 

That’s it. That’s the secret. You guide them through the chaos, you show them the tools, you let them get messy in the drafting phase, and you teach them how to rebuild the walls during revision. Now finish your drink. We've got a kitchen to open.
