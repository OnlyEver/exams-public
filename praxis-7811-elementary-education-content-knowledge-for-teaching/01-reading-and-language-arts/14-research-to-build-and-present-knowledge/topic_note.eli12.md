Welcome! Pull up a chair. 

Think about how you learn best. You probably don't want someone just handing you a boring list of facts to memorize. You are naturally curious, always asking "why" and "how." Learning isn't just about someone giving you the answers; it is about getting a flashlight so you can find the answers yourself in the dark! 

That brings us to the awesome, messy, and super important world of research. A great way to start is with **inquiry-based learning**, which means you get to frame the research process around your own student-generated questions. Instead of a teacher saying, "Read page 40 about volcanoes," it's way more fun when you ask, "Why did Pompeii disappear?" and then go find out. Suddenly, you aren't just reading; you are investigating. 

Let's break down how you can build and present your knowledge, and more importantly, how to get unstuck if you get lost.

## Phase 1: Navigating the Jungle of Text

Imagine someone hands you a massive 200-page book on marine biology because you want to know what sea turtles eat. If you don't know how to navigate, you will start on page one and probably give up by page four. You need a map!

**Informational text features help readers quickly locate specific facts within a text.** These are like cheat codes for nonfiction books:
*   A **table of contents lists chapters or sections and their starting page numbers**. It’s like the level-select menu at the very front of a video game. 
*   An **index provides an alphabetical list of topics and their corresponding page numbers** at the very back of the book. (If you just want to find the word "jellyfish," you skip straight to the J's here).
*   **Glossaries define key terms used within a specific text**, serving as a built-in dictionary so you don't have to stop reading to look up a tough word.
*   **Headings and subheadings organize text into identifiable topics for easier skimming.** They act like big, bold street signs that tell you what each section is about.

### Skimming vs. Scanning: The Two Gears of Reading
People often confuse these two, but they are totally different ways to use your brain. 
*   **Skimming involves reading quickly to get a general overview of a text.** (This is like scrolling through a movie catalog just to see if it’s an action movie or a comedy).
*   **Scanning involves reading a text quickly to locate a specific fact or piece of information.** (This is like darting your eyes around a crowded lunchroom looking specifically for your best friend).

> **Pro Tip:** Don't try to read every single word when you should be scanning. Let your eyes "bounce" down the page until you spot that one specific target word!

## Phase 2: Gathering the Gold (Extraction & Organization)

Once you find the right page, how do you get the info out of the book and into your brain? First, **selecting relevant information requires students to match text details to their specific research questions.** If your question is about what turtles eat, you have to completely ignore that super cool paragraph about how deep they can dive. 

Next, you need to write it down. You shouldn't just copy an entire paragraph word-for-word. You need **note-taking strategies that help students extract relevant information without copying text verbatim** (verbatim just means exactly word-for-word). 

To make sense of messy notes, it helps to draw them out. **Graphic organizers visually structure information to help students categorize research findings.** 
*   A classic example is a Venn diagram. **Venn diagrams are graphic organizers used to compare and contrast information from different texts.** If you are reading one book on Xbox and another on PlayStation, a Venn diagram (those two overlapping circles) helps you visually sort out what is unique to each console and what features they share.

### Summarizing vs. Synthesizing
These two ideas are super important, but they are not the same thing. 
*   **Summarizing involves identifying the main ideas and key details of a text in a condensed format.** It’s like giving your friend a quick 30-second recap of a two-hour movie.
*   **Synthesizing requires readers to combine information from multiple sources to form a new understanding.** 

*Pizza Analogy:* Summarizing is just reading off the list of ingredients you have in your fridge. Synthesizing is taking all those different ingredients, putting them together, and baking a brand-new pizza.

## Phase 3: The Detective Work (Analyzing Texts)

Whether you are reading a made-up story like *Percy Jackson* or a real article about spiders, you have to back up what you say. You need to use **textual evidence**, which **consists of specific details from a reading that support an analysis or claim.** Saying "because I said so" doesn't work! You have to prove it with evidence.

But *how* we investigate depends on what kind of writing we are reading:

| Text Type | Analytical Focus |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Narrative Texts** (Fiction / Made-up stories) | **Analyzing narrative texts requires gathering evidence about character motivations and plot development.** (For example: *Why did the villain steal the diamond? What clues in the story prove he was jealous?*) |
| **Informational Texts** (Nonfiction / Real facts) | **Analyzing informational texts requires identifying the author's main point and supporting reasons.** (For example: *What is the author trying to tell us about recycling, and what exact facts do they use to prove it?*) |

## Phase 4: Trust No One? (Evaluating Sources & Bias)

We live on the internet. You can type a question into a search engine and get 4 million results in half a second. How do you know what is actually true? 

Enter the **CRAAP test**. It is an amazing (and funny sounding) acronym used to evaluate sources based on **Currency, Relevance, Authority, Accuracy, and Purpose**. Let's break down the big ones.

### Authority and Domains
**Source credibility depends on the author's expertise and credentials regarding the topic.** (Credibility means how much you can believe them). An astronomer writing about black holes is credible; a famous TikTok dancer writing about black holes is probably not. 

Always look at the end of a website's link (the URL):
*   **The .gov internet domain extension is reserved solely for United States government websites.** 
*   **The .edu internet domain extension is reserved for postsecondary educational institutions** (like colleges and universities).
*   Because there are strict rules for making them, **websites with .gov or .edu domain extensions are generally considered highly credible sources.**
*   Watch out for the **.com**! **The .com internet domain extension represents commercial entities.** (A commercial entity is just a business trying to make money). What does that mean for your research? **Commercial websites may present biased information designed to sell a product or service.** If a .com site says chocolate is the healthiest food on earth, check to see if the website is secretly owned by a candy company!

### Currency and Accuracy
**The publication date of a text determines the currency and potential accuracy of the information.** (Currency just means how current or recent it is). A beautifully written, highly credible science book from 1950 will tell you humans have never been to the moon. The date matters! Also, if you find a totally wild fact, use **cross-checking, which involves verifying a fact by finding the same information in multiple independent sources.** That means checking to see if three totally separate websites agree before you believe it.

### Identifying Bias and Purpose
**Bias is a disproportionate weight in favor of or against a specific idea or thing.** Teaching yourself to spot bias is like getting a superpower. It means noticing when a writer is unfairly playing favorites. 

1.  **Fact vs. Opinion:** First, **distinguishing between verifiable facts and subjective opinions is a foundational skill for identifying bias.** 
    *Step 1:* Find the statement.
    *Step 2:* Ask if it can be proven. (Fact: The video game costs \$60). 
    *Step 3:* Ask if it's just a feeling. (Opinion: The video game is a huge rip-off).
2.  **Loaded Language:** Next, look at the vocabulary. **Authors often demonstrate bias through the use of loaded language.** **Loaded language consists of words with strong emotional connotations** (for example, calling a new school rule a "disastrous nightmare" instead of a "policy change").
3.  **Author's Purpose:** Finally, ask: *Why was this written?* **An author's purpose is the primary reason the author wrote the text** (to persuade you, to inform you, or to entertain you). Ultimately, **identifying an author's purpose helps readers evaluate a source for potential bias.**

## Phase 5: Time Travelers and Commentators (Source Types)

When researching historical events, you need to understand how close the writer actually was to the event. 

*   **A primary source is an original, first-hand account of an event or time period.** The creator was actually there! **Diaries, photographs, and historical letters are examples of primary sources.** 
*   **A secondary source provides interpretation, analysis, or commentary on primary sources.** The creator was *not* there, but they studied it later. **Textbooks and encyclopedias are common examples of secondary sources.**

*Why it matters:* Reading George Washington’s actual handwritten diary (primary source) gives you his raw feelings. Reading a history textbook chapter about George Washington (secondary source) gives you the big-picture view of *why* he wrote in his diary. You need both to get the full story!

## Phase 6: Giving Credit Where It's Due (Academic Integrity)

Finally, we arrive at the golden rule of doing research. If you use someone else's hard work, you have to thank them for it. 

**Plagiarism is the act of using another person's words or ideas without giving proper credit to the original creator.** To stop this from happening, we use a **citation, which attributes a specific piece of information to its original source.** A citation is just a formal way of saying, "I got this idea from this person."

You have two ways to use research in your writing, and both have specific rules:

1.  **Direct Quoting:** **Direct quoting requires copying text exactly as written in the original source.** Because you are borrowing the exact voice of the author, **direct quotes must be enclosed in quotation marks.** 
2.  **Paraphrasing:** **Paraphrasing requires rewriting information from a source into a reader's own words.** 

> **Crucial Warning!**
> A lot of people think that if they change the words (paraphrase), the idea now belongs to them and they don't need a citation. *Wrong!* **Paraphrased information requires a formal citation to avoid plagiarism.** Even if you put the idea in a new outfit, the original idea still belongs to the author who came up with it.

To wrap up an awesome research project, you will put together a **bibliography**, which is **a formatted list of all sources consulted during a research project.** It sits at the very end of your paper like a giant "Thank You" list to all the books and websites that helped you along the way.

### The Takeaway
Learning how to research isn't just about finishing a homework assignment. It is about learning how to locate facts, test if things are true, separate bias from reality, and build your own understanding of the world. It’s teaching your brain how to think for itself. 

Now, go out there and be a brilliant researcher!
