Listen to me, slide that drink out of the way, and look at me. You are about to walk into a classroom of twenty-five elementary schoolers, and right now, to them, the universe operates on pure, unadulterated wizardry. Puddles vanish. Balloons magically grow. Sugar ghosts its way out of their iced tea. Your job isn’t to hand them a textbook; your job is to pull back the curtain on the ultimate reality-TV show of the universe. We are talking about the VIP list of existence. We are talking about matter, and by definition, matter is absolutely anything that has mass and takes up physical space. If it doesn’t have heft and it can’t claim a seat at the table, it doesn’t exist in our physical club. 

Now, when you introduce these kids to the cast of characters, you have to start with the three most common states of matter on Earth: solid, liquid, and gas. Think of them as the three distinct personalities you are going to encounter in your own classroom. A solid is that incredibly stubborn kid who refuses to take off his winter coat no matter where he sits; solids maintain a fixed volume and a fixed shape. They do not care about the chair you put them in; they are who they are. Then you have the liquids. Liquids are the people-pleasers, the followers. Liquids maintain a fixed volume regardless of the container—you can't magically make more of it—but they have no spine of their own, which means liquids take the shape of the container holding the liquid. Pour them into a vase, they act like a vase. Pour them on the floor, they act like a mess. 

And then you have the gases. Oh, the gases. The ultimate divas of the physical world. Gases want all the attention, meaning gases take both the shape and the volume of the container holding the gas. Put a gas in a tiny jar, it behaves. Open the lid in a gymnasium? It will immediately spread out to fill the entire gym. 

Before we zoom in, you have to look at the macroscopic drama: the materials themselves. Look around this bar. Why are the stools made of wood and not, I don't know, gelatin? Because materials can be classified into categories based on observable physical properties like color, texture, hardness, and flexibility. This is the resume for every material on Earth. Ultimately, a material's physical properties determine the material's suitability for specific intended purposes. Wood gets the starring role for building structures due to the hardness and structural integrity of wood. You want a house that stands up? Hire wood. Conversely, metals are highly suitable for cooking pans due to the thermal conductivity of metals. You try to fry an egg on a wooden skillet, and you’re just going to set off the smoke alarm, because wood doesn't pass the heat along. 

But how do we know who gets the job? We hold auditions! Testing material properties involves observing how the material reacts to external forces. You bend it, you smash it, you heat it up. But listen, your kids are going to try to cheat the auditions. Little Timmy is going to try to prove his favorite brand of paper towel is the most absorbent by using a massive sheet of his favorite brand and a microscopic square of the competitor. You have to shut that down and teach them that a fair test of material properties requires keeping all variables constant except the specific material being tested. Same size sheet, same amount of water. Fair is fair in the science court!

Once they understand the big stuff, you introduce them to Lego logic. This is the idea that physical objects can be built by connecting a set of smaller individual pieces. A massive block tower is just a bunch of tiny squares. Therefore, an object built from small pieces can be disassembled into the original separate small pieces. No magic, just mechanics. And here is the beautiful part: those disassembled pieces from a large object can be reassembled into a completely different structural configuration. A castle becomes a spaceship. But here is where your kids will try to gaslight you! The total weight of a set of pieces remains constant regardless of the structural shape formed by the pieces. You’d think that’s obvious, right? Wrong. Elementary students frequently hold the misconception that reshaping a clay ball changes the weight of the clay. They will roll a thick clay ball into a long, skinny snake, look you dead in the eye, and swear on their lives that the snake weighs less because it "looks thinner." You have to slam that clay on a digital scale before and after to break the optical illusion.

Then, my friend, you pull the ultimate heist. You tell them that the entire universe is made of Legos. All matter is composed of individual particles. The major plot twist? The individual particles comprising matter are too small to be seen with the human eye. They are completely invisible undercover agents. So how do you prove they exist? You cast stand-ins. Scientific models can visually represent matter particles that are too small to be seen. You can go low-budget: student-created drawings can serve as scientific models of microscopic matter particles. Just have them draw a billion little dots. Or you can use props: physical manipulatives like plastic building blocks can serve as scientific models of microscopic matter particles. 

Let's talk about the kitchen disasters. You’re going to have a student drop a sugar cube into a glass of iced tea, watch it vanish, and scream, "Teacher, the sugar melted!" Do not let them get away with this! Elementary students frequently confuse the physical process of dissolving with the physical process of melting. You must correct the record. Melting requires a transfer of thermal energy into a solid. It’s a heat bribe! You leave a chocolate bar in the sun, heat enters it, and it breaks down into a liquid. Dissolving, on the other hand, requires the mixing of a solid solute into a liquid solvent. It’s throwing the sugar into a crowded party of water particles. 

In fact, dissolving a solid into a liquid provides observable evidence that matter is made of particles too small to be seen. Why? Because the sugar dissolved in water remains present in the water despite becoming invisible to the human eye. The sugar particles just separated and hid in the microscopic gaps between the water particles! And because we don’t believe in magic, we know the total mass of a solution equals the sum of the mass of the solvent and the mass of the solute. 100 grams of water plus 10 grams of sugar will always equal exactly 110 grams of sweet water. Want to blow their minds and prove the sugar never left? Evaporating the liquid phase from a solution will leave the dissolved solid particles behind. Boil the water away, the water particles flee into the air, and boom—the sugar crystals are sitting right there at the bottom of the pan like they got caught sneaking out.

Speaking of the air, gases are the ghosts of your classroom. Gases are composed of individual physical matter particles, but because they are usually invisible, elementary students frequently hold the misconception that gases do not have mass. They think empty hands mean empty space. You have to put the ghosts on trial. First, prove they take up space. The inflation of a balloon provides observable evidence that invisible air particles take up space. The balloon rubber stretches because billions of tiny physical particles are throwing their shoulders against the walls. Second, prove they have heft. Weighing a deflated basketball and an inflated basketball demonstrates experimentally that air has measurable mass. The pumped-up ball is heavier. Case closed!

Because gases are the divas that spread out, they leave a lot of room between themselves. The physical compressibility of a gas demonstrates that empty space exists between individual gas particles. You can literally squish them closer together in a syringe. But brace yourself, because when you ask a fourth-grader what exists in that empty microscopic space between the gas particles, they will confidently give you the wrong answer. Elementary students frequently hold the misconception that the microscopic empty space between matter particles is filled with air. You must look them in the eye and tell them it is filled with absolutely nothing. The void! Furthermore, elementary students often struggle to understand that air is a mixture of different types of gas particles. They think "Air" is a single brand-name product. You have to explain it’s a chaotic soup of nitrogen, oxygen, and carbon dioxide all crashing into each other.

And how fast are they crashing into each other? That depends entirely on the thermostat! Temperature is just the DJ of the particle dance floor. Heating a substance causes the particles of the substance to move faster. They bump harder, they push outward, it’s a mosh pit. But cooling a substance causes the particles of the substance to move slower. They lose their energy and huddle up close together to survive the cold. 

If you can get your students to see the world like this—to see the invisible particles shivering, sprinting, hiding in liquids, and stretching out rubber balloons—you won’t just be teaching them science. You’ll be giving them the cheat codes to the whole physical universe! Now, are you going to finish that water, or are you just going to let the particles evaporate?
