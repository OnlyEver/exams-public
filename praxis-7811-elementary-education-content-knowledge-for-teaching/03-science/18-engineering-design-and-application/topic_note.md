# [Engineering Design](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering_design_process) and Application: A Teacher’s Guide to How Students Learn to [Solve Problems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Problem_solving)

Welcome! So, you want to teach [engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering) to [elementary students](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primary_education). That’s fantastic, because children are natural [engineers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineer). They love to [tinker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tinkering), break things, and see what happens when they tape a [balloon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Balloon) to a [toy car](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Model_car). But as a teacher, your job isn't just to let them play with tape; it’s to guide their natural [curiosity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curiosity) into the structured, magnificent world of the **[engineering design process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering_design_process)**.

Let’s get one thing straight right away: **the [engineering design process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering_design_process) is an [iterative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iterative_design) cycle rather than a linear sequence of steps.** It loops, it circles back on itself, and it thrives on [learning from mistakes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trial_and_error). Ultimately, **[engineers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineer) use the [engineering design process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering_design_process) to [solve practical problems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Problem_solving)**, and those **engineering design problems are driven by [human needs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_human_needs) and human wants**. Whether it’s inventing a cleaner way to filter [drinking water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drinking_water) (a need) or designing a more comfortable [headphones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Headphones) (a want), [engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering) is about human realities. 

![A municipal water treatment facility. Addressing fundamental human needs, such as filtering clean drinking water on a mass scale, is the primary driver of complex engineering design.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Water_Treatment_Plant.jpg)

Because this is so foundational to how we understand our modern world, **the [Next Generation Science Standards](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Next_Generation_Science_Standards) explicitly integrate [engineering design](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering_design_process) expectations into [kindergarten through twelfth grade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K%E2%80%9312) [science curricula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Science_education).** 

But how do we teach this? Let's dive into the fascinating mechanics of how kids learn [engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering), the [misconceptions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Misconception) they'll bring to your classroom, and how you can guide them to think like true [problem-solvers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Problem_solving).

---

## 1. The [Pedagogy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pedagogy) of [Engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering): No More "Recipe" Building

Before we look at the steps of the cycle, we have to talk about your [instructional choices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Instructional_design). 

**Effective [engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering) instruction requires teachers to facilitate open-ended [problem-solving](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Problem_solving) experiences.** If you hand a child a [Lego](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lego) kit and a 20-page booklet telling them exactly where every piece goes, they are practicing their [fine motor skills](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fine_motor_skill) and their ability to follow directions. But they are *not* [engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering). **Providing step-by-step building instructions eliminates the [problem-solving](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Problem_solving) aspect of the [engineering design process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering_design_process).** True [engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering) requires the student to grapple with the unknown. Give them a goal, give them materials, and let them figure out the path!

---

## 2. Defining the Problem: What Do We Want, and What Are Our Limits?

You can't solve a problem if you don't know the rules of the game. **Defining an [engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering) problem requires identifying the specific criteria for success**, as well as **identifying the specific design constraints.** 

Here is where your students will stumble. **[Elementary students](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primary_education) often hold the [misconception](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Misconception) that design criteria and design constraints are the same thing.** As their teacher, you need to draw a bright, bold line between these two concepts.

> **Criteria** are the required features a successful [engineering design](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering_design_process) must possess. Think of criteria as the "goals" or "wish list." (e.g., *The [bridge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bridge) must hold 5 [toy cars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Model_car), it must span a 12-inch gap, and it must allow [boats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boat) to pass underneath.*)

> **Constraints** are the strict limitations placed on an [engineering design](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering_design_process). Think of constraints as the boundaries you cannot cross. **Common engineering design constraints include time limits, material limits, and cost limits.** (e.g., *You only have 30 minutes, you can only use 10 popsicle sticks, and your [budget](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Budget) is \$5.*)

If a design doesn't meet the criteria, it's not a successful solution. If it breaks the constraints, it's disqualified before it even gets tested.

## 3. Generating Solutions: The Battle Against "Design Fixation"

Once the problem is defined, we move to the [imagination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imagination) phase. **[Brainstorming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brainstorming) is used to generate multiple possible solutions to a single design problem.** 

It is crucial for your students to understand that **multiple distinct solutions can successfully address the exact same [engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering) problem.** One student might build a [suspension bridge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suspension_bridge); another might build an [arch bridge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arch_bridge). Both might successfully hold the 5 [toy cars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Model_car). In engineering, there is rarely just one "right" answer!

![A diagram showing materials under compression, tension, and shear forces. Different bridge designs resolve these physical forces in distinct ways, proving to students that multiple unique solutions can successfully solve the same engineering problem.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/_Compressive_tensile_shear_loading.svg)

### The Trap of Design Fixation
Watch closely when your students start [sketching](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sketch_%28drawing%29) their ideas. **Young students frequently exhibit design fixation by becoming overly attached to their initial solution idea.** They draw their very first thought, declare it a masterpiece, and refuse to consider any other options. Your job as a [facilitator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Facilitator) is to push them to generate *at least* three different ideas before they ever touch the building materials. 

### Evaluating and Choosing the Best Path
How do [engineers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineer) pick which brainstormed idea to actually build? 
1. **[Engineers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineer) eliminate proposed solutions that fail to fit within the established constraints.** (If an idea requires 20 popsicle sticks and you only have 10, throw it out!)
2. **[Engineers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineer) evaluate proposed solutions by comparing those solutions against the established criteria.** 

Inevitably, they will encounter **engineering [trade-offs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trade-off)**, which **occur when improving one aspect of a design worsens another aspect.** For example, using thicker [cardboard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardboard) makes the [bridge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bridge) stronger (a pro), but it also makes the bridge heavier and more expensive (a con). 

![A cross-section of corrugated fiberboard. The fluted internal geometry represents a classic engineering trade-off: it increases the board's structural strength without drastically adding to the material's overall weight or cost.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Corrugated_Cardboard.JPG)

To help students navigate [trade-offs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trade-off) [objectively](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Objectivity_%28philosophy%29), we use a **[decision matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decision_matrix)**. **A decision matrix is a mathematical tool used to systematically compare multiple design solutions against specific criteria.** 

| Design Idea | [Strength](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strength_of_materials) (1-5) | [Cost-Efficiency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cost_effectiveness_analysis) (1-5) | [Aesthetics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aesthetics) (1-5) | **Total Score** |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Idea A ([Arch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arch_bridge))** | 5 | 2 | 4 | **11** |
| **Idea B ([Beam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beam_bridge))** | 3 | 5 | 2 | **10** |
| **Idea C ([Suspension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suspension_bridge))** | 4 | 1 | 5 | **10** |

*By scoring each idea mathematically, students rely on [logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logic) rather than just "going with their favorite."*

## 4. [Prototypes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prototype) and The Magic of the "[Fair Test](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_control)"

Now, it's time to build! The students will create a **[prototype](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prototype)**, which **is a preliminary model used to test an [engineering design](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering_design_process) concept.**

A [prototype](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prototype) isn't the final product; it's a physical question you are asking nature. *Will this work?* **Testing a prototype provides [empirical data](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empirical_evidence) about which parts of the design function correctly**, and just as importantly, **testing a prototype reveals which specific components of the design require modification.** 

When a student's [bridge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bridge) collapses, they will be sad. Frame it differently! **[Failure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Failure) during [prototype](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prototype) testing provides necessary diagnostic data to improve the subsequent design [iteration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iterative_design).** Without failure, we wouldn't know where the design was weak. Failure isn't the opposite of success in [engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering); it's a stepping stone to it.

![The 1940 collapse of the Tacoma Narrows Bridge. For both professional engineers and students, catastrophic structural failures provide invaluable diagnostic data needed to improve the safety and functionality of the next design iteration.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Tacoma-narrows-bridge-collapse.jpg)

### Designing a [Fair Test](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_control)
To gather accurate diagnostic data, students must test their [prototypes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prototype) properly. This requires a **[fair test](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_control)**.

> **A [fair test](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_control) changes exactly one [variable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dependent_and_independent_variables) at a time** and **keeps all non-tested conditions completely [constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Control_variable) across trials.** 

Let's break down the [scientific vocabulary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_terminology) of a fair test that you'll need to teach:
*   **The [independent variable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dependent_and_independent_variables) is the single aspect intentionally changed during an experimental [fair test](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_control).** (e.g., Changing the *thickness of the paper* used for the [bridge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bridge)).
*   **The [dependent variable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dependent_and_independent_variables) is the specific outcome measured during an experimental fair test.** (e.g., Measuring the *amount of [weight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weight)* the bridge can hold before collapsing).
*   **[Controlled variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Control_variable) are the environmental conditions kept identical across all trials of a fair test.** (e.g., Keeping the *distance between the tables* and the *type of weights used* exactly the same).

### The "Change Everything!" [Misconception](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Misconception)
Here is a classic [pedagogical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pedagogy) hurdle: **[Elementary students](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primary_education) frequently attempt to change multiple design [variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dependent_and_independent_variables) simultaneously during testing phases.** 

Imagine a student tests a [paper airplane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paper_plane). It nosedives. So, for the next trial, the student changes the size of the wings, adds a [paperclip](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paper_clip) to the nose, *and* throws it twice as hard. It flies perfectly! But wait... *why* did it fly perfectly? 

**Changing multiple [variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dependent_and_independent_variables) simultaneously prevents the tester from identifying which specific change caused an observed outcome.** Was it the wings? The [paperclip](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paper_clip)? The throw? We don't know! You must train your students to have the patience to change *just one thing at a time*. 

![A schematic of a contest-winning paper glider. Optimizing an aircraft requires a strict fair test; if wing shape, nose weight, and throwing force are all altered simultaneously, the tester cannot isolate which independent variable affected the flight.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/International_contest_paper_glider.svg)

## 5. [Optimization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering_optimization): Making it Better

The final major phase of the [engineering design process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering_design_process) (before the cycle inevitably loops again) is **[optimization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering_optimization)**, which **is the process of modifying a design to make that design as effective or functional as possible.**

How do we optimize? We don't just guess. **Optimizing a design relies entirely on analyzing the [empirical data](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empirical_evidence) collected from [fair tests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_control).** If the data says the [paperclip](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paper_clip) on the nose made the plane fly 3 feet further, we keep the paperclip. If the data says the wider wings caused [drag](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drag_%28physics%29) and slowed it down, we narrow the wings. 

Because [optimization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering_optimization) is [iterative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iterative_design), **students must receive structured opportunities to redesign their [prototypes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prototype) after the initial testing phase concludes.** This is critical for your [lesson planning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lesson_plan)! If your [engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering) activity ends the moment the students test their first [prototype](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prototype), you have robbed them of the most important part of the engineering process: using [empirical data](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empirical_evidence) to make their design better. Always [budget](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Budget) time for "Redesign and Retest."

### The Takeaway for Your Classroom
When you teach [engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering), you are teaching children how to think. You are teaching them that the world is malleable, that [human needs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_human_needs) can be met through [creativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Creativity), and that [failure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Failure) is just data disguised as disappointment. By defining clear criteria and constraints, fighting design fixation, enforcing [fair tests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_control) with strict [variable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dependent_and_independent_variables) control, and always allowing time for [optimization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering_optimization), you will help your students realize their potential as the [architects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Architect) of tomorrow.
