## Part 1: What Exactly *Is* a Force?

When a child shoves a [toy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toy) truck across the carpet or yanks open a heavy door, they are doing [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics). 

> **A [force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force) is defined as a push or a pull acting upon an [object](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_object).** 

[Nature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nature) has a very strict accounting system for these pushes and pulls. When we measure them, we care about two fundamental [properties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_property):
1. **Every force has a specific magnitude representing its strength.** (A gentle tap vs. a mighty kick).
2. **Every force is applied in a specific [direction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direction_%28geometry%29).** (Up, down, left, right).

If you want to know how an object is going to behave, you have to look at all the forces acting on it at once. We call this the **[net force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Net_force)**. 
* **Net force is calculated by adding individual forces that act in the exact same direction.** (If you and a student both push a heavy desk to the right, your forces combine).
* **Net force is calculated by subtracting individual forces that act in perfectly opposite directions.** (If you push right and the student pushes left, the forces fight each other).

### The Touch Requirement: Contact Forces
Many forces in our daily lives are **[contact forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contact_force)**. Simply put, **contact forces require objects to physically touch each other to exert a push or pull.** 

When your students are playing [bumper cars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bumper_cars) or [marbles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marble_%28toy%29), they are observing that **when two objects [collide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collision), the objects exert forces on each other.** But there are two other contact forces you must know intimately:

* **[Friction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friction):** Have you ever noticed how sliding in [socks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sock) is easy on [hardwood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hardwood) but impossible on [carpet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carpet)? That's friction. **Friction is a contact force.** Specifically, **friction is a force that opposes the motion of an object.** It's the universe's [brake pedal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brake). 
* **The [Normal Force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_force):** Put a book on a desk. Why doesn't it fall through the wood? Because the desk is pushing back! **The normal force is an upward contact force exerted by a [solid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid) [surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_%28physics%29) against a resting object.**

## Part 2: The Delicate Dance of Balance 

Here is where student [intuition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intuition) collides dramatically with physics. Let's look at the difference between balanced and unbalanced forces.

### Balanced Forces: The Art of Doing Nothing (or Changing Nothing)
**[Balanced forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanical_equilibrium) occur when multiple forces acting on an object cancel each other out.** Because they cancel, **balanced forces result in a net force of exactly [zero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero).** 

What happens to an object when the net force is zero? Absolutely nothing new. **Balanced forces do not cause any change in the motion of an object.** 
* **An object [at rest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rest_%28physics%29) remains at rest when acted upon exclusively by balanced forces.** 
* Similarly, **an object moving at a [constant speed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed) in a [straight line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_motion) is experiencing balanced forces.** 

This brings us to two of the most critical [pedagogical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pedagogy) moments you will face in your career.

> 🚨 **Common Student Misconception #1: "Still objects have no forces on them."**
> **Many elementary students hold the misconception that completely [stationary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rest_%28physics%29) objects have zero forces acting upon them.** If a kid sees a resting block on the floor, they think, "No forces!" You must guide them to see that [gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity) is pulling the block down, while the [normal force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_force) from the floor is pushing it up. The forces aren't missing; they are just *balanced*. **An object remaining perfectly still provides [observational evidence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empirical_evidence) of balanced forces.**

> 🚨 **Common Student Misconception #2: "To keep moving, you gotta keep pushing."**
> **Many elementary students hold the misconception that a continuous applied force is required to keep an object moving at a constant speed.** They think this because we live in a world filled with friction! If they stop pushing a toy car, friction slows it down. But if we could magically turn off friction (like a [puck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hockey_puck) gliding on infinitely smooth, [frictionless](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frictionless) [ice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ice)), the puck would glide forever at a constant speed *without any extra pushing*. A constant speed in a straight line means the forces are balanced!

### Unbalanced Forces: The Agents of Change
If balanced forces keep things the same, **[unbalanced forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Net_force) occur when the net force acting on an object is greater than zero.**

Because the forces don't cancel out, **unbalanced forces always cause a change in the motion of an object.** 
* **Unbalanced forces can cause a stationary object to start moving.** (**An object starting to move from a stationary position provides observational evidence of unbalanced forces.**)
* **Unbalanced forces can cause a moving object to [speed up](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acceleration).**
* **Unbalanced forces can cause a moving object to [slow down](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deceleration).**
* **Unbalanced forces can cause a moving object to change its direction of travel.**

The rules governing these changes are highly intuitive. First, **a stronger push or pull will cause a greater change in an object's motion than a weaker push or pull applied to the same object.** Second, **the direction of a push or pull determines the direction of the resulting change in an object's motion.** If you push a rolling ball hard from the right, it accelerates to the left.

## Part 3: Spooky Action at a Distance (Non-Contact Forces)

Your students will easily grasp contact forces because they can see and feel the touching. But what about the invisible forces? 

**[Non-contact forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-contact_force) act upon objects over a distance without requiring physical touch.** 

There are three major non-contact forces you need to master for your exam: **[Gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity)**, **[Magnetism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetism)**, and **[Electrostatic force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrostatics)**. Let's break them down.

### 1. Gravity: The Universal Glue
**Gravity is a non-contact force.** At its core, **gravity is an attractive force that pulls any two objects with [mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass) toward one another.**

You and your [coffee cup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coffee_cup) have a gravitational pull toward each other, but it's too incredibly weak to notice. Gravity only becomes obvious when an object is unimaginably huge—like a [planet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planet). 

Here are the rules of gravitational attraction:
* **The gravitational force between two objects increases as the mass of the objects increases.** (More mass = more pull).
* **The gravitational force between two objects decreases as the distance between the objects increases.** (Further away = weaker pull).

Let's clarify some vocabulary that adults get wrong, but your classroom will get right:
* **[Mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass) is the measurement of the amount of [matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matter) contained within an object.** Mass doesn't change just because you go to the [moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moon).
* **[Weight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weight) is the measurement of the gravitational force pulling on an object.** Weight *does* change on the moon because the moon's gravity is weaker.

> 🚨 **Common Student Misconception #3: "Heavy things fall faster!"**
> **Many elementary students hold the misconception that heavier objects always fall faster than lighter objects in the absence of air resistance.** If you drop a [bowling ball](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bowling_ball) and a [feather](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feather), the bowling ball hits the ground first. Why? Not because gravity pulls it faster, but because of air! **[Air resistance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air_resistance) is a type of [fluid friction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drag_%28physics%29) that opposes the downward motion of falling objects.** The feather catches the air. If you sucked all the air out of the room, creating a [vacuum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vacuum), the bowling ball and the feather would hit the ground at the exact same millisecond. 
> 
> *Teaching Tip:* Remind students that **Earth's gravity pulls objects downward toward the [center of the planet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_core).** "Down" isn't an absolute direction in space; "down" just means "toward the core."

### 2. Magnetism: The Magic of Metals
**Magnetism is a non-contact force.** It acts over a distance, and just like gravity, **the [magnetic force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_field) between objects gets stronger as the distance between the interacting objects decreases.**

Every [magnet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnet) in your classroom kit has two distinct ends:
* **Every magnet has a distinct [north pole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_pole).**
* **Every magnet has a distinct [south pole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_pole).**

When kids play with these, they will quickly discover the golden rule of poles:
* **Opposite magnetic poles attract each other.** (North pulls South).
* **Like magnetic poles repel each other.** (North pushes away North; South pushes away South).

Now, for a crucial diagnostic moment for your teaching:

> 🚨 **Common Student Misconception #4: "Magnets love all metals."**
> **Many elementary students hold the misconception that magnets will attract any object made of [metal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metal).** Hand a student a magnet, and they will try to stick it to an [aluminum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aluminium) soda can, a [copper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copper) [penny](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Penny_%28United_States_coin%29), or a [brass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brass) key, only to be disappointed. You must explicitly teach that **magnets only attract specific types of metals rather than all metallic objects.** 
> 
> What are the "magic" magnetic materials? 
> * **[Iron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron) is an example of a [magnetic material](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ferromagnetism).**
> * **[Nickel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nickel) is an example of a magnetic material.**
> * **[Cobalt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cobalt) is an example of a magnetic material.**

### 3. Electrostatics: Shocks, Sparks, and Sweaters
Finally, let's talk about that moment a student goes down a [plastic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plastic) [slide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Playground_slide) and their hair stands on end. **[Electrostatic force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coulomb%27s_law) is a non-contact force.**

Instead of magnetic poles, [electricity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electricity) deals with charges:
* **Objects can possess a positive [electric charge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_charge).**
* **Objects can possess a negative electric charge.**
* **Objects can possess a neutral electric charge.**

The rules of attraction here perfectly mirror magnetism:
* **Opposite electric charges attract each other.** (+ and -)
* **Like electric charges repel each other.** (+ and +, or - and -)
* And just like the other non-contact forces, **the electrostatic force between charged objects gets stronger as the distance between the objects decreases.**

But how do objects get these charges in a classroom? Through friction! **Rubbing certain materials together can transfer [electrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron).** When a student rubs a [balloon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toy_balloon) on their [wool](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wool) [sweater](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sweater), invisible electrons are being stripped from the wool and deposited onto the balloon. 

This creates an imbalance. **Transferring electrons between materials creates an imbalance of electric charge known as [static electricity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Static_electricity).** That balloon, now loaded with extra negative electrons, will stick to the wall (attracting the positive charges in the wall's surface) without ever needing a drop of [glue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adhesive).

## Conclusion: Empowering the Next Generation

As an elementary educator, you are the gateway to [scientific literacy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_literacy). When your students understand the [Next Generation Science Standards](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Next_Generation_Science_Standards) concepts of balanced and unbalanced forces, they are doing much more than memorizing [vocabulary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vocabulary). They are learning to view the world through a lens of [cause and effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Causality). 

When you see a student staring at a block resting on a desk, you now know how to ask them about the invisible, perfectly balanced [tug-of-war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tug_of_war) happening right in front of their eyes. When they watch a kicked ball slow down in the [grass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grass), you can guide them to thank the contact force of friction, rather than letting them believe the "force inside the ball" simply died out.

You now possess the foundational Content Knowledge for Teaching physics. Go out there, embrace the messy, beautiful reality of your classroom's physical interactions, and help them decode the rules of the game.
