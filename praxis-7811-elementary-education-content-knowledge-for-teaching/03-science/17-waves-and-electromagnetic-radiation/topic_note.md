# The Great Jiggle of the Universe: A Guide to [Waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave), [Sound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sound), and [Light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light)

Welcome, future educators! Pull up a chair. You are about to embark on one of the most magnificent journeys in elementary [science education](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Science_education). We are going to talk about how the [universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe) communicates with itself. 

When you look around your classroom, everything seems pretty still, doesn’t it? The desks are stationary, the whiteboard is flat, the air seems quiet. But underneath it all, the universe is constantly *jiggling*. And when that jiggling moves from one place to another, we call it a [wave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave). 

As a teacher, your job isn't just to tell your students that waves exist. Your job is to help them *see* the jiggle, *feel* the jiggle, and understand how that jiggle brings them everything from the sound of their favorite song to the light of the [sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun), and even the [Wi-Fi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wi-Fi) that powers their [tablets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tablet_computer). 

Let's dive into the profound, deeply interconnected world of waves, [electromagnetic radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation), and [information transfer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_transmission). We will look at this not just as [scientists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientist), but as *teachers*, examining the invisible [mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanics) of the world and the predictable ways your elementary students will misunderstand them.

## 1. The Anatomy of a Wave: Moving [Energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy), Not Stuff

Before we can talk about light or sound, we need to understand what a wave actually is. 

Here is the grand, unifying rule of our topic: **Waves transfer [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy) from one location to another.** 

Notice what I didn't say. I didn't say waves transfer *stuff*. A fundamental concept you must internalize (and soon teach!) is that **waves do not transfer physical [matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matter) from one location to another.** 

> **⚠️ The Great Ocean Misconception**
> Elementary students often hold the misconception that waves transport [water molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water) from the middle of the [ocean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ocean) to the shore. They picture a single drop of water surfing all the way from [Hawaii](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hawaii) to [California](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/California). 

How do we break this misconception? We use a [model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_modelling). Imagine **a [cork](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cork_%28material%29) floating on a [water wave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_wave)**. As the wave passes underneath it, the cork does not go flying forward across the [pond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pond). Instead, the cork floating on a water wave will bob up and down as the wave passes. **The bobbing [motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Motion_%28physics%29) of a floating cork demonstrates that waves do not push matter forward.** The water [molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecule) are just jiggling in place, bumping into their neighbors, passing the *energy* along while the matter stays put. 

![Ripples on a water surface demonstrate how waves transfer energy outward while the water molecules themselves mostly bob in place.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/2006-01-14_Surface_waves.jpg)

### The Geography of a Wave

To help students map out these invisible energy carriers, we develop physical and visual models. When we draw a wave, it looks like a series of rolling hills. We need [vocabulary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vocabulary) to measure these hills:

*   **[Wavelength](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wavelength):** This is the horizontal [distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distance) between one wave peak and the next consecutive wave peak. Think of it as the length of one complete wave cycle.
*   **[Amplitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amplitude):** This is the vertical distance from the resting position of a wave (the flat, middle line if the water were perfectly still) to the highest peak. 

These two measurements are the secret codes that dictate how a wave will look or sound to [human senses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sense). 

![A wave's wavelength is the horizontal distance between consecutive peaks, while its amplitude is the vertical height from the resting center line to a peak.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Sine_wavelength.svg)

## 2. Sound: The Symphony of [Vibration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vibration)

Let’s bring this into the physical world of your classroom. Where does sound come from? 

The golden rule here is a two-way street of [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics): **Vibrating materials produce sound**, and conversely, **sound waves cause [solid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid) materials to vibrate.** 

But kids don't just believe you because you're the teacher; they need [evidence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empirical_evidence)! You must provide tangible evidence of this two-way street. 
*   **Creating sound:** Have a student pluck a stretched [rubber band](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rubber_band). **Plucking a stretched rubber band causes the rubber band to vibrate and emit sound.** The [kinetic energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_energy) of their finger creates a physical vibration in the rubber, which transfers to the air.
*   **Seeing the invisible:** Sound waves in the air are invisible, which makes them hard for kids to trust. But **placing a vibrating [tuning fork](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuning_fork) into a container of water causes the water to splash.** The vibration of the [metal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metal) physically kicks the water out of the bowl!
*   **Feeling the sound:** If you play [music](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Music) in your classroom, **touching a vibrating [speaker cone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Loudspeaker) provides physical evidence of sound vibrations.** You can literally feel the music trying to push your hand away. 

![An exaggerated animation showing how the rapid mechanical vibration of a tuning fork produces invisible sound waves in the surrounding medium.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Mode_Shape_of_a_Tuning_Fork_at_Eigenfrequency_440.09_Hz.gif)

### How Sound Travels: The Need for a [Medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transmission_medium)

Sound is what we call a **[mechanical wave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanical_wave)**. 

> **Blockquote Definition:**
> A **mechanical wave** is a wave that requires a physical medium to travel through. It needs actual [particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_%28physics%29) (solid, [liquid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liquid), or [gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas)) to bump into one another to pass the energy along. 

Because **sound is a mechanical wave**, it demands a medium. This leads us to a classic [science fiction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Science_fiction) problem. 

> **⚠️ The Star Wars Misconception**
> Elementary students often incorrectly believe that sound can travel through [empty space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_space). They’ve seen spaceships explode with massive "BOOMS" in [movies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Film). 

You must excitedly shatter this Hollywood illusion. Space is a [vacuum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vacuum)—it has practically no air molecules. Therefore, **sound cannot travel through a vacuum.** If two [astronauts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronaut) are outside the [space station](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_station) and their [radios](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radio) break, they cannot hear each other shout, no matter how loud they yell, because there are no air molecules to carry the jiggle from one [helmet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helmet) to the other!

### The Shape of Sound: [Longitudinal Waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Longitudinal_wave)

Not all waves jiggle the same way. **Sound waves are [longitudinal waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Longitudinal_wave).** 

What does that mean? **Longitudinal waves vibrate [parallel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_%28geometry%29) to the direction of wave travel.** 
Imagine a stretched-out [Slinky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slinky). If you gather a few coils at one end and let them go, a compression pulse travels straight down the Slinky. The coils bump forward and backward in the *exact same direction* that the energy is moving. The vibration is parallel to the path of the wave. 

![A stretched Slinky toy provides an excellent physical model for longitudinal waves, where the compression travels parallel to the spring itself.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Slinky_rainbow.jpg)

### Decoding Volume and Pitch

How do our wave measurements (amplitude and wavelength/[frequency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frequency)) map onto our human experience of sound?

| Wave Property | Sound Characteristic | Explanation for Students |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **[Amplitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amplitude)** | **[Volume](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Loudness)** | **The volume of a sound is directly related to the amplitude of the sound wave.** Taller waves carry more energy. Therefore, **larger amplitudes produce louder sounds.** |
| **[Frequency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frequency)** | **[Pitch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pitch_%28music%29)** | Frequency is how many wavelengths pass by in a second. **The pitch of a sound is determined by the frequency of the sound wave.** When waves are packed tightly together (shorter wavelength, higher frequency), **higher frequency sound waves produce higher pitched sounds** (like a [mouse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mouse) squeaking). |

![Visualizing frequency and pitch: the closely packed waves at the bottom have a higher frequency and represent a higher-pitched sound.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Sine_waves_different_frequencies.svg)

## 3. Light: The Fastest Thing in the Universe

If sound is the slow, mechanical bumping of air molecules, light is the absolute rebel of the physics world. 

Unlike sound, light is a type of **[electromagnetic wave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation)**. 

Here is the magic of electromagnetic waves: **Electromagnetic waves do not require a physical medium to travel.** Because of this, **light can travel through the vacuum of space.** This is why we can see the light from a [star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star) that is millions of [light-years](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light-year) away, traveling across vast expanses of empty nothingness, yet we couldn't hear it explode. 

![Unlike sound, electromagnetic waves consist of oscillating electric and magnetic fields that can self-propagate through the vacuum of space.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Electromagneticwave3D.gif)

### The Shape of Light: [Transverse Waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transverse_wave)

While sound waves push and pull longitudinally, **light waves are [transverse waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transverse_wave).** 

In a transverse wave, the jiggle is sideways. **Transverse waves vibrate [perpendicular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular) to the direction of wave travel.** If you tie a [jump rope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skipping_rope) to a doorknob and whip your hand up and down, the wave travels *forward* toward the door, but the rope itself moves *up and down*. That [right angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_angle) (perpendicular) movement is the hallmark of a transverse wave.

### The Behavior of Light

Light is remarkably stubborn. **Light travels in [straight lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_%28geometry%29).** It doesn't curve around corners on its own, and it doesn't wander aimlessly. **A [light beam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_beam) will continue in a straight line until the light beam interacts with an object.**

This brings us to a fundamental [biological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology) and physical truth: How do we actually see? 

> **The Rule of Sight:**
> **Objects must [reflect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflection_%28physics%29) or emit light for the [human eye](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_eye) to see those objects.** 

Your students' eyes are not [flashlights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flashlight); they do not shoot beams out to "grab" an image. Sight is purely a receptive process. Light from a source (like the sun or a [lamp](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lamp_%28electrical_component%29)) travels in a straight line, bounces off your classroom desk, and travels in a straight line into the student's [pupil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pupil). 

Because of this, **a dark room contains no visible light.** If you put a student in a room with zero light sources and seal the door, **objects cannot be seen in a completely dark room.** It doesn't matter if their eyes "adjust" for hours; without light to bounce off objects, [vision](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visual_perception) is physically impossible.

## 4. Light Meets Matter: The Great Interaction

So, what happens when that straight-moving beam of light finally hits something? It depends entirely on the material blocking its path. As a teacher, you will frequently set up flashlights and materials to let students discover the following four interactions.

![A visual comparison demonstrating how light interacts with opaque (blocking), translucent (scattering), and transparent (passing) materials.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Opacity_Translucency_Transparency.svg)

### 1. Transparent
**[Transparent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transparency_and_translucency) objects allow almost all [visible light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visible_spectrum) to pass directly through the transparent material.** The light waves march straight through without getting [scattered](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scattering) or absorbed. 
*   *Real-World Example:* **Clear [glass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glass) is a common transparent material.** This is why we use it for [windows](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Window); we want the straight lines of light from the outside world to reach our eyes completely undistorted.

### 2. Translucent
**[Translucent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transparency_and_translucency) objects allow only some light to pass through the translucent material.** But it doesn't just block a little light; it messes with the path. **Translucent objects scatter the light passing through the translucent material.** 
*   *Real-World Example:* **Frosted glass is a common translucent material.** This is why it is used in [bathroom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bathroom) windows—it lets the energy of the light in, but scatters the straight lines so no coherent image can be seen from the outside!

### 3. Opaque & The Nature of Shadows
**[Opaque](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Opacity_%28optics%29) objects completely block light from passing through the opaque material.** The light hits the object and is [absorbed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absorption_%28electromagnetic_radiation%29) or absorbed-and-reflected. It does not pass through.
*   *Real-World Example:* **[Wood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wood) is an example of an opaque material.** 

When an opaque object steps into the path of light, it creates a phenomenon your students interact with every day on the [playground](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Playground): [shadows](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shadow). **Shadows are created when an opaque object blocks a source of light.** 

> **⚠️ The "[Peter Pan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Pan)" Misconception**
> **Elementary students often hold the misconception that shadows are physical objects rather than an absence of light.** They think of shadows as dark "stuff" painted on the ground. 

You must help them realize that a shadow isn't a *thing*. It is a gap. It is the footprint of where light *couldn't* go because an opaque object stood in the way!

### 4. Reflective
Finally, **[reflective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflection_%28physics%29) objects cause light waves to bounce off the surface of the reflective object.** While many opaque objects reflect *some* light (which is why we see them), highly [polished surfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polishing) reflect light back in perfect, straight, organized lines. 
*   *Real-World Example:* **[Mirrors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mirror) are highly reflective objects.**

## 5. Connecting the World: Information Transfer

Why do we care so much about these predictable properties of sound and light? Because once [humans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human) realized that waves travel predictably, we figured out we could manipulate them to talk to each other! 

We know that **information can be transmitted over long distances using patterns of light** and **patterns of sound.** 

### The Low-Tech Telegraphs
Before [cell phones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mobile_phone), we relied on the physical manipulation of the visible and audible world:
*   **Visual Patterns:** **A flashing [lantern](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lantern) communicating [Morse code](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morse_code) is an example of information transfer using light.** By manipulating the opaque shutters on a lantern, [sailors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sailor) could send straight lines of light across the ocean to convey complex messages. 
*   **Audio Patterns:** **Beating a [drum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drum) in a specific pattern is an example of information transfer using sound.** The mechanical waves carry the coded [rhythm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhythm) through the physical medium of the air. 
*   **Physical Medium Transfer:** Have your students build the classic childhood [toy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toy)! **A [paper cup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paper_cup) and [string telephone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tin_can_telephone) uses mechanical vibrations in the string to transmit sound over a distance.** You speak into the cup, the air jiggles the cup, the cup jiggles the string (a solid medium!), the string jiggles the receiving cup, which jiggles the air into your friend's ear!

### The Invisible Magic of Modern Life
Today, we don't have to shout into cups or flash lanterns. But the physics hasn't changed; we just found a different wave to ride. 

**Modern communication devices use invisible electromagnetic waves to transfer [digital information](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_data).** 

When your students play a [video game](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Video_game) online or when you pull up a video on your [smartboard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interactive_whiteboard), you are using the exact same principles as the flashing Morse code lantern. A [router](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Router_%28computing%29) in your school is flashing an invisible "light" ([radio waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radio_wave) or [microwaves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microwave), which are types of electromagnetic waves) in incredibly fast, complex patterns of [1s and 0s](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_number). These waves travel seamlessly through the air (and even through the vacuum of space to [satellites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satellite)), transferring vast amounts of digital information. 

![Modern Wi-Fi routers transmit digital information by manipulating invisible electromagnetic waves, acting like high-speed, invisible lanterns.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Wi-Fi.gif)

## Summary for the Teacher

As you prepare for the Content Knowledge for Teaching exam, remember that you are not just memorizing [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics) trivia. You are learning the structural rules of the universe so you can translate them for a [child](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Child). 

When a child asks why the ocean roars, or why their shadow grows long in the evening, or how their cell phone knows what a [YouTube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/YouTube) video looks like, you have the answers. 
*   You know that **matter stays put while energy moves**. 
*   You know that **sound needs a physical medium to squeeze and stretch (longitudinal)**, while **light can surf through the vacuum of space (transverse)**. 
*   You know how light interacts with **transparent, translucent, opaque, and reflective** materials, and how to prove to a child that **without light, there is no sight**.

Go into your classroom, make some noise, flash some lights, and show your students the beautiful, jiggling [symphony](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symphony) of the world around them!
