Imagine a balanced seesaw on a playground. If you have a mystery box on one side and a bunch of exact weights on the other, the seesaw is perfectly level. Every math move you make on a linear equation is just like adding or taking away weight on that seesaw. If you add weight to the left side, you must add the exact same weight to the right side so it doesn't tip. If you cut the weight in half on one side, you have to cut it in half on the other side. 

The rules of math equations and inequalities are just a way of writing down how this balance works in real life. When we study this, our goal isn't just to find a single missing letter. Our goal is to learn how to read these math sentences like real-world stories—like figuring out exactly how many weeks of allowance it will take to buy a new video game console, or knowing the exact moment your phone battery will drop to zero percent!

## The Architecture of Linear Equations

At its most basic level, **a linear equation in one variable can be written in the form ax + b = c, where a, b, and c are real numbers and a is not equal to zero.** 

Why can't "a" be zero? Because if "a" is zero, the mystery letter "x" gets multiplied by zero and vanishes! We need "x" to stick around so we have something to solve.

To figure out the mystery letter, we use two main rules to keep our seesaw balanced:
1. **The addition property of equality states that adding the same real number to both sides of an equation produces an equivalent equation.** (This works for subtraction too, which is just adding a negative number).
2. **The multiplication property of equality states that multiplying both sides of an equation by the same non-zero real number produces an equivalent equation.** (Dividing works the exact same way, because dividing is really just multiplying by a fraction).

Sometimes you might see an equation packed with fractions that looks super scary. But you don't have to panic! **Multiplying every term in a linear equation by the least common denominator eliminates all fractions from the equation.** 

Here is how that works, step-by-step:
Problem: Find x in the equation (1/3)x + 1/2 = 5/6
Step 1: Find the least common denominator for the bottom numbers 3, 2, and 6. That number is 6.
Step 2: Multiply every single piece of the equation by 6.
6 * (1/3)x + 6 * (1/2) = 6 * (5/6)
Step 3: Simplify the pieces.
2x + 3 = 5
Step 4: Subtract 3 from both sides using the addition property.
2x + 3 - 3 = 5 - 3
2x = 2
Step 5: Divide both sides by 2 using the multiplication property.
2x / 2 = 2 / 2
x = 1

The exact same trick works for decimals! **Multiplying every term in a linear equation by an appropriate power of ten eliminates decimal coefficients from the equation.** 

Here is how to clear decimals:
Problem: 0.25x + 1.50 = 3.75
Step 1: Count the most decimal places used. Here, it goes two places deep (hundredths).
Step 2: Multiply every term by 100 (which is a power of ten) to move the decimal point two spaces to the right.
100 * 0.25x + 100 * 1.50 = 100 * 3.75
Step 3: Write the new, easy-to-read equation.
25x + 150 = 375
Step 4: Subtract 150 from both sides.
25x = 225
Step 5: Divide both sides by 25.
x = 9

What if an equation is almost entirely letters? **Literal equations are solved for a specific target variable by treating all other variables in the equation as constants.** This is like playing a video game where your character has to get to a treasure chest (the target letter) by pushing all the heavy blocks (the other letters) to the other side of the room.

Example: Solve the distance formula d = r * t for the target variable t (time).
Step 1: Identify your target (t) and treat the other letter (r) like a regular number.
Step 2: Since t is multiplied by r, divide both sides by r to push it away.
d / r = (r * t) / r
Step 3: Simplify to get your final answer.
d / r = t

## The Taxonomy of Truth: Conditions, Identities, and Contradictions

A lot of times, we think a math problem will only ever give us one normal answer. But equations are actually just asking a question: "Under what conditions is this true?"

There are three ways this can play out:

1. **A conditional equation is a mathematical statement that is true for exactly one value of the variable.** 
Example: 3x - 1 = 8.
This is only true on the specific condition that x is 3. If x is anything else, the statement is false.

2. **An identity is an equation that is true for all real values of the variable.** 
Think of this like saying "Four quarters equals one dollar." It doesn't matter what number you plug in, the left side and right side are always exactly the same. **Solving an identity analytically yields a true mathematical statement without variables, such as 0 = 0.** 

Example: 2(x + 3) = 2x + 6
Step 1: Multiply the 2 into the parentheses on the left side.
2x + 6 = 2x + 6
Step 2: Subtract 2x from both sides.
6 = 6
Step 3: Subtract 6 from both sides.
0 = 0
The letters vanished! Because 0 = 0 is a completely true fact, the equation works for literally every number in the world.

3. **A contradiction is an equation that has no mathematical solution.** 
This is a broken rule, like saying "I have five more pieces of pizza than I currently have." It's impossible. **Solving a contradiction analytically yields a false mathematical statement without variables, such as 0 = 5.** 

Example: x = x + 5
Step 1: Try to get all the x's on one side by subtracting x from both sides.
x - x = x - x + 5
Step 2: Simplify both sides.
0 = 5
Since 0 never equals 5, this is impossible. The answer is "no solution."

## The Calculus of Boundaries: Linear Inequalities in One Variable

In real life, things aren't always perfectly equal. Roller coasters have a "you must be at least this tall" sign. A video game requires a "high score greater than 10,000" to unlock a secret level.

When writing these rules, **a linear inequality in one variable contains an inequality symbol instead of an equal sign.**

They follow almost the exact same seesaw balance rules as normal equations:
* **Adding or subtracting the same value on both sides of an inequality preserves the direction of the inequality symbol.**
* **Multiplying or dividing both sides of an inequality by a positive number preserves the direction of the inequality symbol.**

But there is one huge, super important rule you must memorize: **Multiplying or dividing both sides of an inequality by a negative number reverses the direction of the inequality symbol.** 

Why? Imagine a number line. We know that 3 is greater than 2 (3 > 2). If we multiply both sides by -1, the numbers flip over the zero to the negative side. Now we are comparing -3 and -2. Because -3 is further to the left and "colder" than -2, it is smaller. We have to flip the symbol so the statement stays true: -3 < -2.

### Notation and Graphing

Inequalities show a range of winning numbers, and they come in two flavors:
* **A strict inequality uses a less than symbol (<) or a greater than symbol (>).** This means the boundary number itself does not count.
* **A non-strict inequality uses a less than or equal to symbol (<=) or a greater than or equal to symbol (>=).** This means the boundary number counts as a winner.

We can draw these rules so they are easy to see:
* **On a number line graph, an open circle represents an excluded boundary value for a strict inequality.** It's like a hollow ring around a number saying, "Start here, but don't count this exact spot."
* **On a number line graph, a closed circle represents an included boundary value for a non-strict inequality.** It is filled in to say, "This exact spot is a winner too."

We can also write this using a shortcut called interval notation. It tells us exactly where the winning numbers start and stop.
* **In interval notation, a parenthesis indicates that an endpoint is excluded from the solution set.** 
* **In interval notation, a square bracket indicates that an endpoint is included in the solution set.** 
* Because you can never actually reach the end of numbers, **the infinity symbol in interval notation is always paired with a parenthesis.**

### Compound Inequalities

Sometimes a game has two rules you have to follow at the same time, like "You must be older than 10 AND younger than 15 to play."
* **A compound inequality joined by the word 'and' requires the solution to satisfy both inequalities simultaneously.** You have to win both tests at the exact same time.
* **The intersection of individual solution sets forms the final solution for an 'and' compound inequality.** Imagine two spotlights shining on a stage. The intersection is the one bright spot where the two lights overlap.

Other times, a rule gives you options, like "You get a ticket discount if you are under 12 OR over 65."
* **A compound inequality joined by the word 'or' requires the solution to satisfy at least one of the inequalities.** You only need to pass one of the tests to win.
* **The union of individual solution sets forms the final solution for an 'or' compound inequality.** This just groups all the winning zones together into one big team of answers.

## Two-Variable Inequalities and Half-Planes

When we level up to inequalities with two letters (like x and y), we move off the simple number line and onto a flat 2D coordinate grid (a piece of graph paper).

**The solution to a linear inequality in two variables is a region on the coordinate plane known as a half-plane.** Imagine drawing a massive line straight across a football field, dividing it into two halves. All the winning solutions live on one entire half of the field!

To draw this, you first need to find the fence.
* **The boundary line of a two-variable inequality is found by replacing the inequality symbol with an equal sign.**

How you draw that boundary fence depends on the inequality symbol:
* **A dashed boundary line is used when graphing a strict linear inequality in two variables.** It's dashed to show you can't stand directly on the fence.
* **A solid boundary line is used when graphing a non-strict linear inequality in two variables.** It's solid to show you are allowed to stand right on the fence.

How do you know which side of the field is the winning side? 
* **A test point not located on the boundary line determines which half-plane contains the solutions to a two-variable inequality.** Usually, people test the point (0, 0) because zero is incredibly easy to do math with!
* **Substituting a test point that yields a true mathematical statement indicates the half-plane containing the test point is the solution region.** (If the math is true, color in that entire side of the field!)
* **Substituting a test point that yields a false mathematical statement indicates the half-plane opposite the test point is the solution region.** (If the math is false, that side is a loser, so color in the other side!)

There is also a handy visual shortcut if your equation is already neatly organized:
* **For a two-variable inequality written in the slope-intercept form y > mx + b, the solution region is shaded above the boundary line.**
* **For a two-variable inequality written in the slope-intercept form y < mx + b, the solution region is shaded below the boundary line.**

## Mathematical Models: Slopes and Intercepts

Math isn't just about drawing lines for fun. We use these graphs to map out real-life models, like how much allowance you save over an entire year.

On our graph paper:
* **The independent variable in a mathematical model is typically represented on the horizontal x-axis.** This is the thing that marches on no matter what, like "Time in weeks."
* **The dependent variable in a mathematical model is typically represented on the vertical y-axis.** This is the thing that reacts and changes, like "Total dollars saved."

Lines eventually cross the heavy grid axes. 
* **The x-intercept of a linear model is the coordinate point where the graph crosses the horizontal x-axis.**
* **The x-intercept is calculated algebraically by substituting zero for the y-variable and solving the equation for the x-variable.**
* **In a modeling context, the x-intercept often represents the specific time or quantity at which a given process reaches a value of zero.** For example, if you are tracking a leaking water tank, the x-intercept is the exact minute the tank is finally completely empty!

* **The y-intercept of a linear model is the coordinate point where the graph crosses the vertical y-axis.**
* **The y-intercept is calculated algebraically by substituting zero for the x-variable and solving the equation for the y-variable.**
* **In a modeling context, the y-intercept typically represents the initial starting value or a fixed baseline quantity before any changes occur.** If you started with \$50 in your piggy bank before earning any new allowance, your y-intercept is simply 50.

Now let's talk about the steepness of your line.
* **The slope of a line represents the ratio of the vertical change to the horizontal change between any two distinct points on the line.** Think of it as "rise over run," like how high a staircase goes up before stepping forward.
* **The slope formula is calculated by dividing the difference in y-values by the difference in corresponding x-values.**

Here is the step-by-step math for finding the slope between two points, (1, 3) and (4, 9):
Step 1: Find the difference in the y-values (the second numbers in the parentheses): 9 - 3 = 6.
Step 2: Find the difference in the x-values (the first numbers in the parentheses) in the exact same order: 4 - 1 = 3.
Step 3: Divide the y-difference by the x-difference: 6 / 3 = 2. The slope is 2.

What does this slope tell us in real life?
* **In a modeling context, the slope represents a constant rate of change between the dependent variable and the independent variable.** This is your "speed," like earning \$10 per week.
* **A positive slope in a linear model indicates that the dependent variable increases as the independent variable increases.** Your line goes uphill, meaning you are gaining money as time passes.
* **A negative slope in a linear model indicates that the dependent variable decreases as the independent variable increases.** Your line goes downhill, meaning your phone battery is losing power as time passes.

Finally, how do we label the slope so people understand it?
* **The measurement units of a linear model's slope are defined as the units of the dependent variable divided by the units of the independent variable.** If your y-axis is "miles" and your x-axis is "hours," the slope's unit is miles divided by hours, which we call "miles per hour"!
