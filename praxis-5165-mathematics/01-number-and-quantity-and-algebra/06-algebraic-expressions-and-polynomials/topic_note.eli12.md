Think of building in a creative video game like Minecraft. You have blocks, items, and crafting recipes. **An algebraic expression** is a mathematical phrase containing numbers, variables, and operational symbols without an equal sign. It is exactly like a crafting recipe waiting to be used! By moving the parts around, we can see what the recipe does. 

If two players build the exact same house using different steps, but the house behaves and looks exactly the same in the end, they have created **equivalent algebraic expressions**. This means they yield identical numerical values for every possible valid substitution of variables in the domain. 

The ultimate way to test if your game works is by playing it! In math, **evaluating a specific algebraic expression** requires substituting concrete numerical values for the present variables and subsequently computing the arithmetic result. 

Let's look at an example of evaluating the expression 2x + 3 when x is 4:
1. Start with the expression: 2x + 3
2. Substitute (swap) the number 4 into the spot where the x is: 2(4) + 3
3. Multiply the 2 by the 4 to get 8.
4. Add the 3 to the 8 to get your final result: 11.

## The Anatomy of Polynomials and Their Ecosystem

To really get good at math, we have to know the rules of the world we are playing in. The most common structures you will see are called polynomials. **A polynomial** is a mathematical expression consisting of variables and coefficients connected using exclusively addition, subtraction, and non-negative integer exponents. That means no negative powers and no fraction powers are allowed! 

Because the powers (exponents) are so normal and friendly, polynomials are smooth and predictable. The "level" of a polynomial—which tells you how big and powerful it gets—is called its degree. **The degree of a single-variable polynomial is determined by the highest exponent of the variable present in the entire expression.** For example, in the polynomial x^3 + x^2, the highest exponent is 3, so its degree is 3!

### The Power of Closure

In math, "closure" is like a VIP club. If two VIP members combine together and make another VIP member, the club is "closed" (meaning nobody from outside the club was created). Because polynomials are built from basic adding and multiplying, they have very strong rules:
*   **The mathematical set of polynomials exhibits strict closure under the operation of addition.** (Add two polynomials, you get a polynomial!)
*   **The mathematical set of polynomials exhibits strict closure under the operation of subtraction.** (Subtract them, still a polynomial!)
*   **The mathematical set of polynomials exhibits strict closure under the operation of multiplication.** (Multiply them, still a polynomial!)

But the VIP club breaks down when we start dividing. **The mathematical set of polynomials does not exhibit closure under the operation of division.** 

Why? Because **dividing one polynomial by another polynomial can produce a rational algebraic expression instead of a polynomial.** **A rational algebraic expression** is formally defined in mathematics as the algebraic ratio of two individual polynomials. Basically, it’s a fraction with a polynomial on the top and a polynomial on the bottom!

Just like you can't divide a pizza among zero friends, division in math has a strict rule against dividing by zero. **The valid domain of a rational algebraic expression strictly excludes any input values causing the denominator polynomial to evaluate to zero.** If the bottom part of the fraction becomes zero, it's game over! Knowing what numbers cause a "game over" is a super important skill.

## The Arithmetic of Polynomials: Expanding the Machine

When we do basic math on polynomials, it's just like sorting your inventory in a game. 

**Adding polynomials requires computing the combined sum of numerical coefficients for terms possessing identical variable parts.** This is known as combining like terms. **Combining like terms simplifies expressions by adding the numerical coefficients of terms with identical variable components.** You just add your apples to your apples, and your wood blocks to your wood blocks!

Let's add the polynomials (2x + 3) and (4x + 1):
1. Group the matching parts together: Put the x's together (2x + 4x) and the plain numbers together (3 + 1).
2. Add the numbers in front of the x's: 2 + 4 = 6, so you have 6x.
3. Add the plain numbers: 3 + 1 = 4.
4. Put your final pieces together: 6x + 4.

**Subtracting polynomials requires distributing a negative one to every individual term in the subtrahend polynomial before combining like terms.** (The "subtrahend" is just a fancy word for the second polynomial you are taking away). People often mess this up by only subtracting the first piece, but you have to subtract everything in that second group!

Let's subtract (5x + 4) - (2x + 1):
1. Distribute (multiply) a -1 to the second group: -1 times 2x is -2x, and -1 times 1 is -1.
2. Group the like terms together with your new signs: (5x - 2x) and (4 - 1).
3. Combine the x's: 5x minus 2x gives you 3x.
4. Combine the plain numbers: 4 minus 1 gives you 3.
5. Put it all together: 3x + 3.

To make things bigger, we multiply. **Multiplying two polynomials requires distributing every individual term in the first polynomial to every individual term in the second polynomial.** This uses the **distributive property**, which **allows multiplying a sum by a single term by multiplying each individual addend by that single term.** 

When we do this completely, we are **expanding an algebraic expression**, which **removes parentheses by completely applying the distributive property across all present terms.** It’s like unpacking everything from your backpack until it is all spread out on the floor.

## Dissecting the Structure: The Art of Factoring

If expanding is unpacking your backpack, factoring is packing it back up into neat, organized bags. **Factoring an algebraic expression** rewrites an expanded sum or difference as a mathematical product of simpler factors. 

Before doing any hard math, a smart student always looks for the biggest piece they can easily pull out of the whole group. **The greatest common factor of a polynomial** is the largest single monomial expression (a single math chunk) that divides evenly into all individual terms of the polynomial. Because of this, **extracting the greatest common algebraic factor is the standard preliminary step in completely factoring any given polynomial expression.**

Let's factor the expression 4x + 8 by extracting the greatest common factor:
1. Find the biggest number that divides perfectly into both 4 and 8. That number is 4!
2. Pull that 4 outside of a set of parentheses: 4( ... )
3. Divide your first piece (4x) by 4. That leaves just an x.
4. Divide your second piece (8) by 4. That leaves a 2.
5. Put the x and the +2 inside the parentheses for your final answer: 4(x + 2).

### Factoring Quadratics and Grouping
When you face a three-part polynomial (a trinomial), it's like a number puzzle. **A quadratic expression taking the mathematical form ax^2 + bx + c can be factored by identifying two numerical values multiplying to ac and summing to b.** You are just looking for two numbers that multiply to make one part, and add together to make the other part!

For polynomials that have exactly four terms, we split them into teams. **Factoring by grouping** is an algorithmic technique involving grouping terms with common factors before factoring out a shared binomial factor. 

### Special Polynomial Forms
Some polynomials have built-in cheat codes that let you skip the long puzzle! Recognizing these forms helps you go way faster on tests.

| Name | Factored Form | Conceptual Note |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Difference of Two Squares** | a^2 - b^2 = (a - b)(a + b) | **The difference of two squares formula states that a^2 - b^2 factors into the product (a - b)(a + b).** |
| **Perfect Square Trinomial (Positive)** | a^2 + 2ab + b^2 = (a + b)^2 | **A perfect square trinomial with a positive middle term factors according to the algebraic formula a^2 + 2ab + b^2 = (a + b)^2.** |
| **Perfect Square Trinomial (Negative)** | a^2 - 2ab + b^2 = (a - b)^2 | **A perfect square trinomial with a negative middle term factors according to the algebraic formula a^2 - 2ab + b^2 = (a - b)^2.** |
| **Difference of Two Cubes** | a^3 - b^3 = (a - b)(a^2 + ab + b^2) | **The difference of two cubes formula states that a^3 - b^3 factors into the product (a - b)(a^2 + ab + b^2).** |
| **Sum of Two Cubes** | a^3 + b^3 = (a + b)(a^2 - ab + b^2) | **The sum of two cubes formula states that a^3 + b^3 factors into the product (a + b)(a^2 - ab + b^2).** |

Eventually, you will find some math expressions that are like a locked treasure chest—they just won't break apart. **Irreducible polynomials fundamentally cannot be factored into non-constant polynomials of lower degree over a specified mathematical number field.**

For example, look at a^2 + b^2. **The sum of two squares algebraic expression a^2 + b^2 cannot be factored into real binomial factors using purely real numbers.** If you are only allowed to use normal, everyday numbers on the number line, it is completely locked!

But math always finds a way to open locked doors. We do this by creating a new kind of number. **The imaginary unit i is mathematically defined as the principal square root of negative one.** Because it is a square root, **the mathematical square of the imaginary unit i is exactly equal to negative one** (so i^2 = -1).

By using this cool imaginary key, the locked door opens: **the sum of two squares formula over complex numbers states that a^2 + b^2 factors into the product (a - bi)(a + bi).** 

## The Division Machinery and Its Theorems

Division is the tool we use to chop polynomials down into smaller pieces. It comes in two different styles.

**Polynomial long division is a systematic algorithm used to divide a dividend polynomial by a divisor polynomial of equal or lower degree.** It works almost exactly like regular long division that you learned in elementary school, but with letters added in!

If the thing you are dividing by is super simple, you can use a great shortcut. **Synthetic division is a condensed computational method strictly utilized for dividing a polynomial by a linear monic binomial** (which is just a simple piece like x - 3, where the x doesn't have a number stuck to the front of it).

No matter which way you chop it up, the final answer always follows one main rule:
> **The polynomial division algorithm states that a dividend polynomial equals the product of the divisor and quotient polynomials added to a remainder polynomial.** 
> Polynomial = (Divisor * Answer) + Remainder

To make sure you didn't stop dividing too early, **in the polynomial division algorithm, the mathematical degree of the remainder polynomial must be strictly less than the degree of the divisor polynomial.** If the remainder was still big enough, you would just keep dividing!

### The Remainder and Factor Theorems
Division gives us two awesome magic tricks. 

Trick #1: **The Remainder Theorem states that dividing a polynomial by a linear binomial x - c yields a constant remainder equal to the polynomial evaluated at c.** 

Let's find the remainder of (x^2 + 5) divided by (x - 2) without doing any long division:
1. Find the "c" number. Since we are dividing by (x - 2), our number is 2.
2. Substitute that 2 in place of the x in the original polynomial: (2)^2 + 5.
3. Calculate 2 squared, which is 4.
4. Add 4 + 5 to get 9. The remainder is exactly 9! 

Trick #2: What if that remainder turns out to be exactly zero? That brings us to our second trick! **The Factor Theorem states that a linear binomial x - c perfectly divides a polynomial if and only if evaluating the polynomial at c yields zero.** So, if you plug a number into the expression and get zero as your final answer, congratulations! You just found a piece that fits perfectly, with no leftovers!

## Unearthing Roots: The Deep Theory

Finding factors is completely tied to finding "roots" (which are the spots where a graph touches the zero line). The biggest rule in all of algebra is about roots. 

**The Fundamental Theorem of Algebra states that every non-zero single-variable polynomial of degree n has exactly n complex roots.** 
This is a total guarantee! If your polynomial's highest exponent is a 5, it has exactly 5 roots. 

Sometimes those roots are normal numbers, but sometimes they are complex numbers using that imaginary 'i' we talked about earlier. When complex roots show up in a normal polynomial, they behave like mirrored twins. **Complex conjugate roots always occur in pairs for any mathematical polynomial possessing exclusively real coefficients.** 

This means that **for any real-coefficient polynomial possessing a complex root a + bi, the complex conjugate a - bi is guaranteed to be a root of the identical polynomial.** It is literally impossible to have 3 + 2i as a root without its twin 3 - 2i coming along for the ride. They balance each other out!

### The Rational Root Theorem
Before students had graphing calculators to magically show them where the roots were, they needed a way to guess the roots without having to test every single number in the universe. We still teach this today to help you understand how numbers connect. 

**The Rational Root Theorem provides a finite list containing all theoretically possible rational roots of a polynomial equation featuring integer coefficients.** 

How do we build this list of guesses? It’s basically just dividing! **Possible rational roots of an integer-coefficient polynomial are generated by dividing a factor of the constant term by a factor of the leading coefficient.** Once you make this short list, you can just test them one by one to see which ones are the winners. 

When you learn all these rules, you are learning the secret codes behind how math works, turning a giant mess of numbers into a puzzle you can easily solve!
