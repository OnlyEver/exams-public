In mathematical terms, a system of equations is a set of two or more equations sharing the same variables. Imagine those variables, $x$ and $y$, are the literal physical hallways of your high school. You’ve got two completely different entities—your equations—running frantic through the exact same space. When we are hunting for an answer, we aren't just looking for an arbitrary number. A solution to a system of two equations in two variables is an ordered pair that satisfies both equations simultaneously. It is the exact locker number and the exact second where these two chaotic elements physically collide in the hallway! 

To see this drama unfold, you have to play the role of the security camera. Graphically representing a system of equations involves plotting each equation on the same coordinate plane. You lay out the floor plan, you map their trajectories, and boom! The graphical solution to a system of equations corresponds to the exact points of intersection between the graphed functions. Where the lines crash, that's your smoking gun.

Now, let's break down the three types of linear hallway drama. If a system has any truth to it at all—if there's even a fraction of a chance these paths cross—we call it consistent. Think of it as the umbrella term for "drama actually occurs." Formally, a consistent system of linear equations is defined as a system possessing at least one valid solution. 

Under this umbrella, you have the lone wolf scenario. An independent system of linear equations possesses exactly one unique solution. Picture two kids sprinting full speed from totally different directions. Because two linear equations exhibiting different slopes will always intersect at exactly one coordinate point, it’s physically impossible for them to cross paths more than once. Graphically, an independent system of two linear equations forms two lines intersecting at exactly one point. Pow. One collision, one incident report.

Then you have the identical twins pulling a heist. Two linear equations sharing identical slopes and identical y-intercepts represent a dependent system. They are the exact same person wearing a fake mustache, running the exact same route! Because they are practically glued together, a dependent system of linear equations possesses infinitely many valid solutions. Every single step they take is a shared point! Graphically, a dependent system of two linear equations forms two coincident lines overlapping entirely. 

Finally, we have the tragedy. The star-crossed lovers. Two linear equations sharing identical slopes and differing y-intercepts represent an inconsistent system. They are running at the exact same pace, frantically trying to catch up to each other, but they started in different wings of the school. They will never, ever touch. Because of this, an inconsistent system of linear equations contains zero valid solutions. Graphically, an inconsistent system of two linear equations forms two parallel lines with no points of intersection. Pure heartbreak.

But you can't always rely on drawing pictures. Sometimes the coordinates are decimal nightmares, and you need to lock these equations in an interrogation room and squeeze the truth out of them algebraically. 

Your first tactic is the Body Snatcher, technically known as substitution. The algebraic substitution method for solving a system begins by isolating one variable in one of the equations. You corner $y$ alone in a room until it sings and confesses it’s actually equal to $(2x + 4)$. Once you have that confession, the trap is set. In the substitution method, the isolated variable expression is substituted into the second equation to create a single-variable equation. You literally rip $y$ out of the second equation and shove $(2x + 4)$ into its place! The $y$ dimension completely collapses, leaving you with an equation entirely in $x$ that you can solve.

Your second tactic is Dodgeball Annihilation, or elimination. Sometimes substitution yields hideous fractions. Instead, you line the equations up vertically and chuck dodgeballs at them. The algebraic elimination method involves adding or subtracting the given equations to eliminate a variable entirely. But what if they don't cancel out right away? What if the top team has a $5x$ and the bottom team has a $2x$? You have to dope them up! In the elimination method, equations can be multiplied by non-zero constants to create opposite coefficients for a specific variable. You multiply the top by $2$ and the bottom by $-5$, creating a $10x$ and a $-10x$. When they collide, the $x$ variable is utterly obliterated from existence, leaving $y$ standing alone to face the music.

Now, lean in, because this is where the Praxis separates the rookies from the veterans. What happens when one of your equations isn't running in a straight line? What if a kid is sprinting straight down the hall, but the principal is pacing in a massive, sweeping U-turn? Welcome to the linear-quadratic system.

Because a parabola curves back on itself like a boomerang, a system comprised of one linear equation and one quadratic equation can yield a maximum of two real coordinate solutions. The kid and the principal can cross paths at most twice before the principal spirals out of the building. 

Let's visualize these three high-stakes scenarios. Scenario one: The double bust. A linear-quadratic system containing two real solutions graphically depicts a straight line intersecting a parabola at two distinct points. The principal intercepts the kid twice. Scenario two: The terrifyingly close shave. A system comprised of one linear equation and one quadratic equation can yield exactly one real coordinate solution. A linear-quadratic system containing exactly one real solution graphically depicts a straight line tangent to a parabola. The kid just barely brushes the principal’s shoulder in a passing graze and keeps running. Scenario three: The clean getaway. A system comprised of one linear equation and one quadratic equation can yield zero real coordinate solutions. A linear-quadratic system containing zero real solutions graphically depicts a straight line that never intersects the parabola. They are in completely different timezones of the school!

How do you catch them analytically? You cannot use dodgeball elimination here! You can’t vertically add an $x^2$ to an $x$ and hope they disappear; it’s a violation of the laws of nature. Solving a linear-quadratic system algebraically typically requires isolating a variable in the linear equation first. Do not try to isolate a variable in the quadratic! That introduces square roots and $\pm$ symbols, and it is a psychological booby trap! Isolate the clean, straight-line variable. 

Once you have it, substituting the isolated linear variable expression into the quadratic equation yields a new single-variable quadratic equation. This new equation is a mutant. It’s the DNA of the straight line and the curved parabola mashed into one singular chimera. 

And here is the ultimate lie detector test: the discriminant. Listen to me carefully, do not test the original parabola! It doesn't care about the line. Instead, the discriminant of the combined single-variable quadratic equation determines the total number of real solutions for the linear-quadratic system. You run $b^2 - 4ac$ on the mutant chimera equation! 

If the lie detector comes back hot? A positive discriminant in the combined quadratic equation confirms that the linear-quadratic system possesses two distinct real solutions. If it comes back flat? A discriminant of zero in the combined quadratic equation confirms that the linear-quadratic system possesses exactly one real solution. And if it goes sub-zero? A negative discriminant in the combined quadratic equation confirms that the linear-quadratic system possesses zero real solutions. The trail is entirely cold.

Let's say you execute the heist perfectly. You do the math, and you find your $x$-values. You're not done! A coordinate is an ordered pair, a partnership! Each single-variable solution obtained in a linear-quadratic system must be substituted back into an original equation to determine the corresponding paired coordinate. You have to put the $x$ into a getaway car to find its $y$.

But which getaway car do you choose? The sleek, predictable straight-line sedan, or the winding, erratic parabolic clown car? Always take the sedan! Substituting a solved variable back into the linear equation rather than the quadratic equation prevents the introduction of extraneous mathematical solutions. Why? Because parabolas are two-timing liars. If you plug your solved $x$ into a quadratic, it might spit out a ghost $y$-value that makes the math work for the curve, but doesn't actually exist on the straight line. The linear equation is strictly one-to-one. It never lies. It will give you the exact, undisputed coordinate where your two worlds collided.
