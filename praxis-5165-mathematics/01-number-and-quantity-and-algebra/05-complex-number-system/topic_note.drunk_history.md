We write it into the curriculum from scratch: the imaginary unit $i$ is defined as the principal square root of -1. You square the ghost, and it drops a tangible, negative-one bomb right into your lap. 

With this ghost in our pocket, we build an entirely new educational paradigm. A complex number is any number that can be expressed in the standard form $a + bi$. Think of this standard form as a co-teaching model for the most chaotic classroom on earth. In the standard form of a complex number $a + bi$, the variables $a$ and $b$ represent real numbers! Let me repeat that because the test loves to trick you: both teachers are totally real, certified human beings. In the complex number $a + bi$, the real number $a$ is called the real part. That’s your tenured teacher who strictly follows the syllabus. In the complex number $a + bi$, the real number $b$ is called the imaginary part. That’s your unhinged student teacher who is legally tethered to our ghost unit, $i$. 

Now, if the tenured teacher calls out sick, they are zero. A pure imaginary number is a complex number $a + bi$ where the real part $a$ is exactly zero and the imaginary part $b$ is non-zero. It’s pure substitute teacher chaos—just $bi$ running the room! But what about the days you teach solo? This is the plot twist of the century: every real number is a complex number possessing an imaginary part of exactly zero. Every normal, boring integer or fraction you’ve taught your entire career was actually a complex number all along, just keeping its imaginary student teacher locked in the closet at a strict zero! 

Because these two parts operate in totally different realities, parent-teacher conferences are incredibly strict. Two complex numbers are equal if and only if the respective real parts are equal and the respective imaginary parts are equal. You cannot negotiate. You cannot say, "Well, my real part is struggling, but my imaginary part is doing great!" No! They must match entirely on both fronts to be equal. 

When the bell rings, we have to move these kids around. To add two complex numbers, a person must add the corresponding real parts together and add the corresponding imaginary parts together. Keep the lunch duty teachers with the lunch duty teachers, and the bus duty ghosts with the bus duty ghosts. Same goes for the end of the day: to subtract two complex numbers, a person must subtract the corresponding real parts and subtract the corresponding imaginary parts. You don't cross the streams! 

But then comes the school-wide assembly: multiplication. Total intermingling. The product of two complex numbers is calculated by applying the distributive property to multiply each part of the first number by each part of the second number. You FOIL it out like a middle school dance! But watch out, because when the two imaginary parts ($bi$ and $di$) dance together, they create an $i$-squared term! Suddenly the fire alarm goes off. What do you do? Remember our founding rule! When multiplying complex numbers, any resulting $i$-squared term must be replaced with -1 to simplify the expression into standard form. The ghosts collide and boom—they materialize as a negative real number, and order is restored.

Even the neutral zones survive! A snow day where nothing happens? The additive identity for the complex number system is $0 + 0i$. They annihilate each other back to zero.

Here is where the Praxis exam writers are going to try to rob you blind: the block schedule of exponents. The powers of the imaginary unit $i$ repeat in a cyclical sequence of four distinct values! It is a relentless, rotating four-period day. First period: the value of the imaginary unit $i$ raised to the power of 1 is $i$. You're just starting, you feel the ghost. Second period: the value of the imaginary unit $i$ raised to the power of 2 is -1. Reality hits hard, morale is negative. Third period: the value of the imaginary unit $i$ raised to the power of 3 is $-i$. You are the anti-ghost, completely drained. Fourth period, the final bell: the value of the imaginary unit $i$ raised to the power of 4 is 1. Sweet release, you are whole and positive again! Because this cycle resets perfectly every four steps, to evaluate any integer power of $i$, a person can divide the exponent by 4 and use the remainder to determine the value within the four-step cycle. If they ask you for $i$ to the 83rd power? Divide 83 by 4. The remainder is 3. Boom, it maps perfectly to $-i$. It's the greatest grading hack in existence.

But what about division? Division is the ultimate HR nightmare. You cannot have an imaginary number in the denominator of a fraction. That’s a massive liability—like having an unlicensed, imaginary chaperone driving the field trip bus. You need to vaporize the ghost in the denominator while keeping the fraction legally equivalent. Enter the district's 'fixer': the conjugate. The complex conjugate of a complex number $a + bi$ is defined as $a - bi$. It’s the exact same numbers, you just flip the mood of the imaginary part to its opposite sign.

Why is this the ultimate weapon? Because multiplying a complex number by the corresponding complex conjugate eliminates the imaginary part completely! It triggers a difference of squares where the middle terms cancel out, and that $i^2$ becomes a -1. The product of a complex number $a + bi$ and the complex conjugate $a - bi$ is the real number $a^2 + b^2$. No ghosts left. Pure, administrative reality. So, to divide two complex numbers, one must multiply both the numerator and the denominator by the complex conjugate of the denominator. It's like multiplying by a very fancy number 1. The top becomes a chaotic mess of distribution, but the bottom becomes a totally safe, real number $a^2 + b^2$, allowing you to split it back into standard form.

Notice there is no $i$ in that calculation! The ghost doesn’t have length; only the real coefficients $a$ and $b$ dictate the distance.
