Imagine building your own video game. If you want a character to jump, run, or earn points, you can't just tell the computer "do stuff." You have to write specific code to make it happen. Math is just like that computer code, but for the real world! Instead of guessing how much pizza to buy for a party or how long a road trip will take, we use math to figure it out exactly. Right now, you are learning how to take real-world stuff—like money, time, and sports—and translate it into the clean, awesome language of algebra. Then, you can translate your math answers right back into real life!

## The Anatomy of Translation: One-Variable Models

Before we solve a puzzle, we have to explain what our pieces mean. **The process of modeling a real-world situation requires defining all variables explicitly along with the corresponding units of measure.** If you just write down the letter "x," nobody knows what that means! But if you write, "Let x be the number of minutes I played my video game," you have connected math to real life. Basically, **translating a scenario into an equation involves identifying the unknown quantity and assigning a specific variable to that quantity.**

At its heart, **an algebraic equation is a mathematical statement asserting the equality of two expressions.** When you put an equals sign between two things, you are making a big promise. You are saying those two sides are exactly the same amount, just wearing different masks! A cool trick to remember when you read a math story: **the word "is" in a word problem typically translates to the equals sign in an algebraic equation.** If a problem says "My allowance is 10 dollars," you just write "a = 10".

### Families of One-Variable Equations

Different kinds of real-world puzzles need different kinds of math equations. Here are the main groups you will use when dealing with just one variable:

*   **Linear Equations:** **Creating a one-variable linear equation requires identifying a constant rate of change and an initial value from a given scenario.** Let's say you join a movie streaming service. 
    1. Identify the starting cost (initial value): a \$10 sign-up fee.
    2. Identify the rate: \$5 times the number of months (let's use the variable m).
    3. Write the equation putting it all together: Cost = 5m + 10.
*   **Quadratic Equations:** When things curve, like throwing a baseball in an arc, we use a different tool. **Quadratic equations in one variable are used to model real-world scenarios involving area, optimization, or projectile motion.** (Projectile motion is just a fancy science way of saying "throwing or shooting stuff through the air").
*   **Exponential Equations:** Sometimes things grow super fast! **Exponential equations in one variable model scenarios involving a constant percentage rate of growth or a constant percentage rate of decay.** Think of a zombie video game where the number of zombies increases by 50% every hour, or a glowing rock losing 10% of its energy every day.
*   **Rational Equations:** **Rational equations in one variable are used to model shared work-rate problems or average cost calculations.** Imagine you and your friend are painting a treehouse together.
    1. You can paint it alone in 3 hours, so your rate is 1/3 of the treehouse per hour.
    2. Your friend can paint it alone in 4 hours, so your friend's rate is 1/4 of the treehouse per hour.
    3. Add your rates together to find your combined rate: 1/3 + 1/4.
    4. Set this equal to the unknown combined rate, which we call 1/t: 1/3 + 1/4 = 1/t.

### Defining Boundaries: Inequalities in One Variable

Sometimes, we aren't looking for one perfect answer. Sometimes we want to stay within a limit, like having a maximum budget for a pizza party. This is where inequalities come in! 

When reading a word problem, remember these hints:
*   **The phrase "at least" in a word problem translates to the greater than or equal to mathematical inequality symbol (>=).** (Example: You need at least 10 points to win a game, so points >= 10).
*   **The phrase "at most" in a word problem translates to the less than or equal to mathematical inequality symbol (<=).** (Example: You can spend at most \$20 at the store, so dollars <= 20).

Another cool tool is absolute value, which helps us stay super close to a target goal. **Absolute value inequalities in one variable model scenarios involving a specified margin of error or tolerance from a target value.** Think of a factory making a skateboard wheel. 
1. Identify the target wheel size: 50 millimeters.
2. Identify the allowed mistake, or tolerance: 1 millimeter.
3. Set up the absolute value of the difference between your wheel (w) and the target: |w - 50|.
4. Make it less than or equal to the allowed error: |w - 50| <= 1. This means your wheel's size must be within 1 millimeter of 50.

> **The Reversal Axiom:** There is a super important rule when solving these boundaries: **multiplying or dividing an algebraic inequality by a negative number requires reversing the direction of the inequality symbol.** Why? Let's use a number line to see why it makes sense.
> 1. Start with a true math statement: 5 is greater than 3 (5 > 3).
> 2. Multiply both sides by -1.
> 3. The 5 becomes -5, and the 3 becomes -3.
> 4. Look at a number line. Because -5 is actually smaller (further to the left) than -3, you must flip the symbol to keep the math true: -5 < -3.

## Dynamic Relationships: Two-Variable Models

Life is full of things that depend on each other. How tired you are depends on how much you ran. How much money you earn depends on how many chores you did! **An equation in two variables defines a relationship where the value of a dependent variable changes based on the value of an independent variable.** 

Because our brains love pictures, we draw these relationships. **The coordinate plane is used to visually represent equations and inequalities containing exactly two variables.** It turns our math into a graph you can actually look at!

### The Architectures of Two-Variable Relationships

When we map these connections out, we look for how the two variables play together:

*   **Linear Relationships:** **A linear equation in two variables is used when the rate of change between the independent and dependent variables is constant.** That means it always goes up or down by the exact same steady amount. There are three ways to write these:
    *   **Standard Form:** **The standard form of a linear equation in two variables is Ax + By = C.** This is great for combining two different things you want to buy. 
        1. Let x be hotdogs, which cost \$2 each.
        2. Let y be sodas, which cost \$1 each.
        3. Set the total money you are allowed to spend: \$10.
        4. Write the standard form equation: 2x + 1y = 10.
    *   **Slope-Intercept Form:** **The slope-intercept form of a linear equation in two variables is y = mx + b.** This form is the absolute best way to graph a straight line!
    *   **Point-Slope Form:** **The point-slope form of a linear equation in two variables is y - y1 = m(x - x1).** This is the fastest way to write an equation if you only know one single point on the graph and the rate of change.

*   **Proportional Variations:** Sometimes variables connect directly. **Direct variation models require a two-variable equation where the dependent variable is a constant multiple of the independent variable.** (Like earning \$10 for every lawn you mow: Money = 10 * Lawns). On the flip side, **inverse variation models require a two-variable equation where the product of the independent and dependent variables is a constant.** Think about cleaning your bedroom. If 1 person takes 60 minutes, 2 people take 30 minutes. More people means less time!

*   **Exponential Relationships:** What if a funny video goes viral? The views don't just add up at a steady rate; they multiply! **An exponential equation in two variables is used when the dependent variable changes by a constant multiplicative factor for each unit change in the independent variable.** The views might double every single hour.

*   **Quadratic Relationships:** **A quadratic equation in two variables models mathematical relationships exhibiting a constant second difference.** This sounds tricky, but it just means the *rate* of the rate of change is steady. If you graph a kicked soccer ball flying through the air over time, the shape makes a U-curve called a parabola.

### Mapping Regions: Two-Variable Inequalities

When we have an inequality with two variables, we don't just draw a single straight line. We color in a whole area! **An inequality in two variables represents a boundary line and a shaded region containing all ordered pairs that satisfy the inequality.** (Ordered pairs are just the specific dots on your graph).

When we draw the fence around this colored area, the type of line matters a lot:
*   **The boundary line of a two-variable inequality is drawn as a solid line for inclusive inequalities** (like >= or <=). A solid line means the dots exactly on the line are allowed to be winning answers.
*   **The boundary line of a two-variable inequality is drawn as a dashed line for strict inequalities** (like > or <). A dashed line acts like a force field—you can get super close to it, but you are never allowed to touch it!

## Intersecting Realities: Systems and Constraints

In the real world, you usually have to follow more than one rule at a time. Maybe you want to buy video games, but you have a rule for how much money you can spend *and* a rule for how much storage space is on your console. **Mathematical constraints are conditions or restrictions placed on the variables within a mathematical model.**

To handle these multiple rules at the same time, we group equations together. **A system of equations consists of two or more equations that share the same set of variables.** When you solve a system, you are finding the exact spot on the graph where both rules agree.

Plus, **constraints in a real-world scenario can be represented algebraically by a system of linear equations or a system of linear inequalities.** When you graph multiple colored inequalities together, the places where their shaded colors overlap create something really neat. **The solution set to a system of linear inequalities forms a feasible region on the coordinate plane.** 

What is that? Well, **a feasible region contains all possible combinations of variable values that satisfy all given mathematical constraints simultaneously.** "Simultaneously" just means "at the exact same time." Any dot inside that shaded area is a winning choice that perfectly follows all your rules!

## The Physics of the Solution: Viability and Practical Domains

Here is a super important lesson: your calculator does not know about real life. It just crunches numbers! **An algebraic solution to a mathematical model must be evaluated to determine whether the solution is a viable option in the real-world context.** ("Viable" means it actually makes sense and works in real life).

A number might perfectly solve the math puzzle on paper but make absolutely zero sense in the real world. **A viable solution must fall within the practical domain of the real-world scenario being modeled.** The math might say any number is allowed, but the rules of reality say otherwise!

### Recognizing Non-Viable Solutions

You always need to double-check your answers. Watch out for these silly situations:

| Type of Non-Viable Solution | The Reality Check | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Negative Solutions** | **Negative algebraic solutions are non-viable in real-world models involving exclusively positive quantities like elapsed time, geometric distance, or physical mass.** | Let's say you solve an equation for how long it takes a dropped water balloon to hit the ground. <br> 1. The math gives you two possible answers: 3 seconds and -3 seconds. <br> 2. You check reality: you cannot travel backward in time! <br> 3. You throw away -3 because it is a non-viable answer. |
| **Fractional Solutions** | **Fractional algebraic solutions are non-viable in models that require discrete whole quantities such as the number of living people or manufactured items.** | Let's say you calculate how many whole pizzas to order for your sports team. <br> 1. The math says you need exactly 4.2 pizzas to feed everyone. <br> 2. You check reality: a restaurant will not sell you 0.2 of a pizza. <br> 3. You must round up and order 5 whole pizzas! |

### The Illusion of Extraneous Solutions

Sometimes, the math actually plays a trick on us. **Extraneous solutions are mathematically correct roots of an algebraic process that do not satisfy the original contextual constraints of the model.** 

This happens when we do totally legal math moves, but accidentally create a "ghost" answer or a fake answer.
1. You solve a math puzzle that has a fraction with the letter "x" on the bottom.
2. You do all the math correctly step-by-step and get the answer x = 0.
3. You check the original equation. If x = 0 makes the bottom of the fraction 0, you are dividing by zero.
4. Because dividing by zero breaks the rules of math, x = 0 is a fake answer (an extraneous solution) and you must throw it away.
