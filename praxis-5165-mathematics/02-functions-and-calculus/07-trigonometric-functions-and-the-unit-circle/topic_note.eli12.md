Imagine you are playing a video game where your character is standing right in the middle of a map, looking straight ahead. If you move your joystick to turn the character, you are creating an angle! In math, instead of a video game map, we use a circle on a graph to measure how things spin or turn. This helps us describe everything from a wheel rolling on the ground to a skateboarder doing tricks. Let's learn how to measure those spins!

## The Geometry of Rotation: Standard Position and Measurement

Before we start doing math, we have to agree on how a character or a line stands at the starting line. 

In math, **an angle is in standard position when its vertex is at the origin and its initial side lies along the positive x-axis.** Think of the "vertex" as your character's feet standing exactly at the center point (the origin) of the map. The "initial side" is the direction they are looking when the game starts, staring straight ahead along the flat, horizontal line to the right.

When you turn the character, the new line they are looking down is called the terminal side. 
*   **Positive angles are generated by counterclockwise rotation of the terminal side from the positive x-axis.** (Like turning left).
*   **Negative angles are generated by clockwise rotation of the terminal side from the positive x-axis.** (Like turning right).

### Degrees vs. Radians

Normally, we measure turns in degrees. Think of dividing a round pizza into 360 tiny slices. **One full revolution around a circle is exactly 360 degrees.** 

But in higher-level math, we use a different measuring stick called "radians." Radians measure the actual distance wrapped around the outside edge of the circle. **One full revolution around a circle is exactly 2pi radians.** 

Since 360 degrees and 2pi radians both describe spinning exactly one time all the way around, we can cut both of them in half to find a super helpful middle point: **an angle of 180 degrees is equivalent to pi radians.**

If we want to switch between degrees and radians, we use conversion tools:
*   **The conversion factor to change degrees to radians is pi radians divided by 180 degrees.** 
*   **The conversion factor to change radians to degrees is 180 degrees divided by pi radians.**

Let's look at a step-by-step example of changing 90 degrees into radians:
1. Start with your angle: 90 degrees.
2. Multiply by the conversion factor: 90 times (pi / 180).
3. Combine the fraction: (90 times pi) / 180.
4. Simplify the numbers: 90 / 180 is the same as 1/2.
5. So, **an angle of 90 degrees is equivalent to pi over 2 radians.** (This is exactly a quarter-turn, like turning to face the left wall).

Using the exact same steps, we can find a few more common angles:
*   **An angle of 60 degrees is equivalent to pi over 3 radians.**
*   **An angle of 45 degrees is equivalent to pi over 4 radians.**
*   **An angle of 30 degrees is equivalent to pi over 6 radians.**

## The Architecture of the Unit Circle

Now let's build the most important tool in this whole topic: the unit circle. **The unit circle is a circle with a radius of exactly one centered at the origin of a Cartesian coordinate plane.** The "radius" is just the distance from the center out to the edge. So, no matter where you go on the circle, you are exactly 1 step away from the center!

Because of how distance works on a graph, **the algebraic equation of the unit circle is x squared plus y squared equals one.**

When you spin your angle from the starting line, the line you are looking down will eventually hit the edge of the unit circle at a specific spot. That spot has an x-coordinate (how far left or right it is) and a y-coordinate (how far up or down it is). 

These coordinates are secretly our trigonometry functions!
*   **For any point (x, y) on the unit circle, the x-coordinate represents the cosine of the corresponding standard-position angle.**
*   **For any point (x, y) on the unit circle, the y-coordinate represents the sine of the corresponding standard-position angle.**

From just the x and y coordinates, we can build four more tools by making fractions:
*   **The tangent of an angle is the ratio of the y-coordinate to the x-coordinate on the unit circle.**
*   **The secant of an angle is the reciprocal of the x-coordinate on the unit circle.** (Reciprocal just means flipping the fraction upside down, so it is 1 divided by x).
*   **The cosecant of an angle is the reciprocal of the y-coordinate on the unit circle.** (This is 1 divided by y).
*   **The cotangent of an angle is the ratio of the x-coordinate to the y-coordinate on the unit circle.**

### Domain Boundaries and Undefined Values

When dealing with fractions, we have one big rule in math: you can never divide by zero! It's like trying to share 5 pizzas with 0 friends; it just doesn't make sense.

If your angle points straight up or straight down (like at 90 degrees), your x-coordinate on the graph is exactly zero. Because we can't divide by zero:
*   **The tangent function is undefined when the x-coordinate on the unit circle is exactly zero.**
*   **The secant function is undefined when the x-coordinate on the unit circle is exactly zero.**

If your angle points straight right or straight left (like at 0 degrees or 180 degrees), your y-coordinate on the graph is exactly zero. So:
*   **The cosecant function is undefined when the y-coordinate on the unit circle is exactly zero.**
*   **The cotangent function is undefined when the y-coordinate on the unit circle is exactly zero.**

## Exact Values and Reference Angles

There are a few special angles that you just have to memorize, kind of like your times tables. These are angles in the first section of our graph (Quadrant I, which goes from 0 to 90 degrees).

Here are the exact facts:
*   **The sine of 30 degrees evaluates to exactly 1/2.**
*   **The cosine of 30 degrees evaluates to the square root of 3 divided by 2.**
*   **The sine of 45 degrees evaluates to the square root of 2 divided by 2.**
*   **The cosine of 45 degrees evaluates to the square root of 2 divided by 2.**
*   **The sine of 60 degrees evaluates to the square root of 3 divided by 2.**
*   **The cosine of 60 degrees evaluates to exactly 1/2.**

### Expanding Beyond Quadrant I

What happens if your angle goes way past 90 degrees? Let's say you spin to 150 degrees. To figure out the sine or cosine, we use a shortcut called a reference angle. **A reference angle is the acute positive angle formed by the terminal side of an angle and the x-axis.** ("Acute" just means it is a small, sharp angle). Because it just measures the quick distance back to the flat horizontal line, **reference angles always have a measure between 0 degrees and 90 degrees inclusive.**

Here is the rule for any big angle: **To evaluate a trigonometric function for any angle, determine the trigonometric value for the reference angle and apply the appropriate sign for the quadrant.**

Let's do this step-by-step for the sine of 150 degrees:
1. First, find how far 150 degrees is from the flat x-axis (180 degrees). Subtract 150 from 180 to get your reference angle: 30 degrees.
2. Next, find the sine of that reference angle. From our memorized facts above, the sine of 30 degrees is 1/2.
3. Now, figure out if the answer should be positive or negative. 150 degrees is in the top-left section of the graph (Quadrant II). In this section, the y-coordinate is positive.
4. Since sine is the y-coordinate, the sine of 150 degrees is positive 1/2!

To know if an answer is positive or negative, follow these quadrant rules:
*   Quadrant I (top-right): **All six standard trigonometric functions yield positive values for angles terminating in Quadrant I.**
*   Quadrant II (top-left): **Only the sine and cosecant functions yield positive values for angles terminating in Quadrant II.**
*   Quadrant III (bottom-left): **Only the tangent and cotangent functions yield positive values for angles terminating in Quadrant III.**
*   Quadrant IV (bottom-right): **Only the cosine and secant functions yield positive values for angles terminating in Quadrant IV.**

## Coterminal Angles and Infinite Rotations

Have you ever watched a skateboarder do a "360"? They spin around exactly one full time and land facing the exact same direction they started. You can spin as many times as you want! 

When you spin more than once and land in the same spot, you are creating coterminal angles. **Coterminal angles are angles drawn in standard position that share the exact same terminal side.** 

Because a full circle is 360 degrees, **coterminal angles can be generated by adding integer multiples of 360 degrees to an original angle.** If you are using radians, **coterminal angles can be generated by adding integer multiples of 2pi radians to an original angle.** ("Integer multiples" just means adding 1 full circle, 2 full circles, 3 full circles, and so on).

Because your character is facing the exact same spot on the unit circle, **trigonometric functions evaluated at coterminal angles always yield identical values.** 

Let's prove this step-by-step:
1. Start with an angle of 30 degrees. The sine of 30 degrees is 1/2.
2. Spin around one full circle by adding 360 degrees: 30 + 360 = 390 degrees.
3. 390 degrees is a coterminal angle with 30 degrees.
4. Therefore, the sine of 390 degrees is also exactly 1/2!

## Symmetry and Periodicity: The Heartbeat of Trigonometry

The unit circle is incredibly perfectly shaped. If you were to fold the graph paper in half, the circle would match up perfectly! 
*   **The unit circle exhibits geometric symmetry across the x-axis.** (Folding top to bottom).
*   **The unit circle exhibits geometric symmetry across the y-axis.** (Folding left to right).
*   **The unit circle exhibits geometric symmetry across the origin.** (Folding diagonally through the center).

### Even and Odd Functions

Because the circle is perfectly balanced, spinning backward (a negative angle) has a neat relationship with spinning forward. 

If you spin down (negative angle), your left/right position (the cosine) doesn't change! Because of this, **the cosine function exhibits even symmetry.** In math talk, **the equation cosine of negative theta equals cosine of theta demonstrates the even symmetry of the cosine function.** ("Theta" is just a math word for a mystery angle).

But if you spin down, your up/down position (the sine) flips to the opposite sign. Because of this, **the sine function exhibits odd symmetry.** In math talk, **the equation sine of negative theta equals negative sine of theta demonstrates the odd symmetry of the sine function.**

Since tangent is made by mixing sine and cosine together, it acts odd, too. **The tangent function exhibits odd symmetry. The equation tangent of negative theta equals negative tangent of theta demonstrates the odd symmetry of the tangent function.**

### Periodicity

A pattern that repeats itself over and over is called "periodic." Think of receiving a \$10 allowance every single week. It's a repeating cycle! In math, **a periodic function satisfies the equation f(x) equals f(x plus P) for some non-zero constant P and all x in the domain.** That sounds complicated, but "P" is just the length of time it takes for the pattern to repeat.

Since the unit circle repeats itself every full spin:
*   **The sine function has a fundamental period of 2pi radians.**
*   **The cosine function has a fundamental period of 2pi radians.**

The tangent function is a little bit special. Because of how the signs divide out, its pattern repeats twice as fast! **The tangent function has a fundamental period of pi radians.**

The most important rule of these repeating cycles is that **evaluating a trigonometric function at an angle theta plus its period yields the exact same value as evaluating the function at theta.** It's like knowing that if today is Monday, 7 days from now (one full period of a week), it will be Monday all over again!
