Slide that coaster out of the way and look at this napkin. Listen to me! You’re taking this Praxis exam to become a high school math teacher, and you’re sitting here looking at me like functions are just cold, dead lines on a piece of grid paper. They aren't! They are a reality TV show of mathematical chaos. They are heists, they are kitchen disasters, they are the literal emotional rollercoaster of the nine-month school year you are about to endure! 

When you get a word problem on that test, you are translating a scenario into a visual dashboard. Translating a verbal description into a graph requires accurately identifying the independent variable for the horizontal axis and the dependent variable for the vertical axis. Who is driving the getaway car, and who is screaming in the trunk? The independent variable is the driver—usually time—marching relentlessly forward across the bottom. The dependent variable is the screamer on the vertical axis, reacting to every bump in the road—like your stress levels, or the cost of classroom supplies. The moment this heist starts, you need an anchor. An initial starting value given in a verbal functional description directly corresponds to the y-intercept on the resulting plotted graph. That is ground zero, the exact point where the graph of the function intersects the vertical axis before the students even walk into the room.

Now, how fast are things going wrong? If your supply of dry-erase markers is disappearing at exactly two per week, a constant rate of change presented in a verbal scenario dictates the drawing of a straight linear segment on the corresponding graph. It is predictable, steady destruction. But if a freshman drops a rumor, the panic doesn't spread at a constant rate. A variable rate of change that continuously increases or decreases in a verbal scenario dictates the drawing of a curved non-linear segment on the corresponding graph. 

Let's start with the easiest kids in your class. The linear ones. The graph of a linear function is a straight line with a constant rate of change known as the slope. It’s like grading standardized quizzes. Three minutes a paper, forever. Unchanging. But most days aren't linear. Most days have movement. 

As the days of the semester—the input values—tick by, you have to read your classroom from left to right. A function is strictly increasing on an interval if the function value increases as the input value increases throughout the entire interval. Every single day, the volume goes up. The curve climbs. It is relentless! On the flip side, a function is strictly decreasing on an interval if the function value decreases as the input value increases throughout the entire interval. That is the miraculous week after state testing when everyone is exhausted and the noise level just plummets. 

Where these increasing and decreasing phases collide, you get the drama—the extrema! A relative maximum is a point on a graph where the function value is greater than or equal to the function values of all immediately adjacent points. That’s the localized peak of the week—the Tuesday before Thanksgiving. A relative minimum is a point on a graph where the function value is less than or equal to the function values of all immediately adjacent points. That’s a sweet, localized valley, like a Friday afternoon movie day. But we need to look at the big picture. An absolute maximum is the highest function value achieved over the entire specified domain of the function. This is Prom Night chaperoning. The single highest spike of sheer terror in the entire year. Conversely, an absolute minimum is the lowest function value achieved over the entire specified domain of the function. That is you, sitting on a beach in mid-July, at absolute zero stress.

Sometimes, the chaos comes in the form of a thrown object. A graphing calculator chucked across the room. The graph of a quadratic function is a symmetric U-shaped curve called a parabola. Because the universe demands poetic justice, that parabola is perfectly balanced. The vertex of a parabola represents the absolute maximum or absolute minimum of the corresponding quadratic function. That’s the peak height of the flying calculator before gravity takes it down. And slicing right down the middle of that flying trajectory is the axis of symmetry of a quadratic function, which is a vertical line passing directly through the vertex of the parabola. If you know the rise, you know the fall!

But what happens when gossip takes over? Enter the exponential function. An exponential function models growth or decay with a constant multiplicative rate of change over equal intervals. It doesn't add; it multiplies. Two kids know, then four, then sixteen! It’s a total meltdown! But eventually, the rumor dies down. This introduces you to the beauty of limits. The graph of a basic exponential function possesses a horizontal asymptote representing a limit to the function's decay. The rumor fades, and fades, but never mathematically hits zero. Specifically, the horizontal asymptote of a basic exponential function of the form y equals a times b to the power of x is the line y equals zero. You can chop a piece of paper in half infinitely, but a microscopic sliver always survives!

Now, let's step up to the high school heavyweights: Polynomials. The turning points of a polynomial graph correspond to the relative extrema of the polynomial function. Every time the class entirely shifts its collective mood, that's a turn! But they only have so much energy. A polynomial function of degree n can have at most n minus 1 turning points. A degree-5 class of hyperactive sophomores can pivot their attention at most 4 times before they completely run out of steam!

When those polynomials crash into the baseline, things get interesting. The x-intercepts of a polynomial graph represent the real roots or zeros of the polynomial function. This is when the lesson actually clicks. But *how* it clicks depends on the algebraic multiplicity. If they truly understand, a polynomial graph completely crosses the x-axis at a root if the corresponding linear factor has an odd multiplicity. You pierced the veil! You crossed over to the other side of comprehension! But if they are just pretending to understand? A polynomial graph touches the x-axis and turns around at a root if the corresponding linear factor has an even multiplicity. They nod, they smile, the graph ricochets right off the axis of understanding, and they bounce back into the negative zone of total confusion!

What about the long game? The end of the year? End-behavior describes the trend of a function's output values as the input values approach positive infinity or negative infinity. And in any classroom, the end-behavior of a polynomial function is determined entirely by the term with the highest degree. The loudest kid dictates the final vibe. If the alpha student has an even degree and a positive leading coefficient, a polynomial function approaches positive infinity as the input approaches both positive and negative infinity. Both arms raised in eternal victory! If they have an even degree and a negative leading coefficient, it approaches negative infinity as the input approaches both positive and negative infinity. Everyone is miserable, hands pointing to the floor. Now, if it's an odd degree and a positive leading coefficient, it approaches positive infinity as the input increases and negative infinity as the input decreases. That’s the classic uphill battle—started low, finished high! Finally, a polynomial function with an odd degree and a negative leading coefficient approaches negative infinity as the input increases and positive infinity as the input decreases. The tragic downhill slide of a teacher who gave up by December.

But wait, it gets worse. What happens when you divide one nightmare by another? You get a rational function, which is defined as the quotient of two polynomial functions. Let’s say the numerator is the student body and the denominator is the administration. Because dividing by zero breaks the laws of physics, the denominator sets strict, immovable boundaries. A vertical asymptote occurs in the graph of a rational function at x-values that make the denominator zero and the numerator non-zero. This is a policy wall! The function hits this rule and legally cannot exist, so the stress violently surges to infinite chaos! 

But what if the students and admin actually agree on a loophole? A hole or removable discontinuity occurs in the graph of a rational function when the numerator and denominator share a common linear factor. The graph looks perfectly continuous, but there is one infinitesimally small missing pixel. A single, easily patchable blind spot in the dress code!

We also have to figure out who wins the long-term turf war at infinity. A horizontal asymptote of a rational function indicates the end-behavior of the function as the input approaches positive or negative infinity. It’s a tug-of-war of degrees! If the administration has more power, a rational function has a horizontal asymptote at y equals zero if the degree of the numerator is strictly less than the degree of the denominator. You are crushed flat into compliance. If it’s a perfectly matched standoff, a rational function has a horizontal asymptote equal to the ratio of the leading coefficients if the numerator degree is exactly equal to the denominator degree. You negotiate a flat, stable compromise. But if the teachers are top-heavy? A slant or oblique asymptote occurs in a rational function graph when the degree of the numerator is exactly one greater than the degree of the denominator. You break the horizontal mold entirely and drift off on a slanted, linear trajectory toward a union strike!

Now, wipe the sweat off your forehead, because we have to talk about the bell schedule. A piecewise function is defined by multiple sub-functions with each sub-function applying to a specific non-overlapping interval of the main function's domain. Period 1 you are teaching Algebra. Period 2 you are teaching Geometry. You cannot be in both rooms at once! Because the rules change instantly, a jump discontinuity frequently occurs in a piecewise function graph at the specific boundary points separating different sub-function domains. The bell rings, and you violently teleport from one reality to the next. The ultimate classic here is the absolute value function, which can be graphically represented as a piecewise function consisting of two linear sub-functions meeting at a single vertex. It is your mood pre-coffee dropping negatively, hitting the vertex of a triple-espresso shot, and sharply rebounding into a positive slope. A perfect, aggressive V-shape!

Finally, the repetitive, soul-crushing, oscillating grind of the week itself. Trigonometric functions. These are the waves of your existence. The amplitude of a sinusoidal trigonometric function is half the vertical distance between the absolute maximum and absolute minimum values. It’s the sheer volume, the intensity of the emotional swing from a manic Monday morning to a crashed-out Wednesday afternoon. The period of a trigonometric function is the horizontal length of one complete non-repeating cycle of the function's graph. Five days. Monday to Friday. Over and over. The midline of a sinusoidal function is the horizontal line situated exactly halfway between the function's maximum and minimum vertical values. That is your baseline, dead-eyed, resting teacher face. And sometimes, Mother Nature intervenes. A phase shift represents a horizontal translation applied to the graph of a trigonometric function. A snow day! The entire weekly wave just slides smoothly to the right along the horizontal axis!

But beware the fractured siblings of sine and cosine. Because they involve division, the graphs of the tangent, cotangent, secant, and cosecant functions contain infinitely many vertical asymptotes. These are the weeks entirely broken by fire drills, assemblies, and pep rallies! Infinite, recurring walls of undefined madness!

Look at me. When you sit down at that computer for the 5165 exam, do not just see formulas. See the story! Track the intervals, respect the asymptotes, and find the roots. You aren't just plotting points—you are predicting the future! Now drink your water, we have to go over geometry next.
