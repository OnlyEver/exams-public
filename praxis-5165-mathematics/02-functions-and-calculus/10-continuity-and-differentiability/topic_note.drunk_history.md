Slide that graphing calculator away for a second, my friend. Have a sip of your drink and look at me. You look like you're trying to memorize a phone book of calculus rules for your Praxis exam, and it is making your eyes glaze over. You’re looking at graphs like they’re just squiggly lines on a pixelated screen, but you have to start looking at them like they are the getaway route for the most high-stakes, multi-million dollar heist in history. If your getaway car is driving along the Cartesian plane, your survival depends entirely on two things: the road being unbroken, and the road being smooth. If you don't understand the rigorous mathematical difference between those two things, your getaway car is going to shatter into a thousand pieces, and you—the aspiring math teacher—are going to fail the exam. 

Let's talk about the pavement first. To pull off this heist, continuity of a function at a point requires exactly three mathematical conditions to be met simultaneously. Not two. All three, at the exact same instant, with zero margin for error. We test the drop point, let’s call it $x = c$. The first condition for a function $f$ to be continuous at a point $c$ is that the function value $f(c)$ must be defined. The safehouse has to actually exist! If you plug in $c$ and you get a division by zero, your safehouse is a mirage, and you crash. The second condition for a function $f$ to be continuous at a point $c$ is that the limit of $f(x)$ as $x$ approaches $c$ must exist. Your crew coming in from the west side and your crew coming in from the east side have to coordinate. Mathematically, the existence of a limit at a point requires both the left-hand limit and the right-hand limit at that point to exist and be equal. If the left crew is aiming for 4th Street and the right crew is aiming for 9th Street, the limit doesn't exist, and the cops get you. Finally, the third condition for a function $f$ to be continuous at a point $c$ is that the limit of $f(x)$ as $x$ approaches $c$ must exactly equal the defined function value $f(c)$. The coordinates your crews are driving toward must actually be the exact location of the safehouse! It is an all-or-nothing game. A function fails to be continuous at a point if any one of the three continuity conditions is not satisfied. One failure, and the bridge collapses.

When the bridge does collapse, the way it breaks tells you exactly how badly you messed up the plan. Let's look at the crime scenes. Sometimes, you have a pothole. A removable discontinuity occurs when the limit of a function exists at a point while the function value at that point remains undefined. The road aims exactly at the right spot, but somebody stole the manhole cover! Alternatively, a removable discontinuity occurs when the limit of a function exists at a point while the limit does not equal the defined function value at that point. The crews arrive at the GPS coordinates, but the safehouse got moved across town. Your graphing calculator might gloss over this missing pixel, but your getaway car will absolutely hit that pothole and blow a tire. 

Then there are the catastrophic failures. A jump discontinuity occurs when the left-hand limit and right-hand limit of a function at a point both exist and are finite while possessing different values. This is a blown-up bridge in an action movie! You drive up to an altitude of 50 feet on the left, but the road on the right abruptly continues at an altitude of 20 feet. You drop like a rock. And if you think that’s bad, an infinite discontinuity occurs when at least one of the one-sided limits of a function at a point approaches positive infinity or negative infinity. This is the ultimate trap. You hit a vertical asymptote, and the road violently shoots your car straight up into the stratosphere or down to the center of the earth.

Now, suppose you planned perfectly. The road is completely unbroken. You’re safe, right? Wrong! This is where students get completely destroyed on the test. Connected pavement means nothing if you hit a hairpin turn at a hundred miles an hour. We have to talk about smoothness. We have to talk about differentiability. A function is differentiable at a point if the derivative of the function exists at that specific point. But what is the derivative in the reality of our heist? It’s your microscopic speedometer and steering column. Specifically, the derivative of a function $f$ at a point $c$ is defined as the limit of the difference quotient $\frac{f(x) - f(c)}{x - c}$ as $x$ approaches $c$. Because the derivative is a limit itself, it means your rate of change approaching from the left must seamlessly, perfectly match your rate of change approaching from the right. 

Here is the golden rule of the getaway, the absolute bedrock of calculus: Differentiability at a point mathematically guarantees continuity at that exact same point. If a function is differentiable at a point, the function must inherently be continuous at that point. If you are smoothly steering through a curve, I can guarantee you there is pavement under your tires! You can logically flip this like a master detective. If a function is not continuous at a point, the function absolutely cannot be differentiable at that point. A lack of continuity at a point precludes the existence of a derivative at that point. You cannot calculate the turning speed of a car that is actively plummeting into a ravine! 

But beware the trap! This relationship is a one-way street! Continuity at a point does not mathematically guarantee differentiability at that point. I will scream this into the void until I lose my voice: A function can be continuous at a point while failing to be differentiable at that exact point! Connectedness does not equal smoothness. If you are continuous but not differentiable, your getaway car is going to survive the gap, but the suspension is going to be ripped apart by the sheer G-force of the geometry. 

Let me show you the rogues' gallery of exceptions you must memorize. A continuous function is not differentiable at a point if the graph of the function features a sharp corner at that point. Take the absolute value function $f(x) = |x|$. The absolute value function $f(x) = |x|$ is perfectly continuous at $x = 0$. You can walk right through the origin. But the absolute value function $f(x) = |x|$ is not differentiable at $x = 0$ due to a sharp corner in the graph. Imagine driving down an alleyway and having to make an instantaneous, zero-radius 90-degree turn. You bounce off the brick wall! Why? Because the left-hand derivative of the absolute value function $f(x) = |x|$ at $x = 0$ is exactly -1, pointing you down. But the right-hand derivative of the absolute value function $f(x) = |x|$ at $x = 0$ is exactly 1, pointing you up. They don't match, the difference quotient limit fails, and your car is wrecked.

Next suspect: the cusp. A continuous function is not differentiable at a point if the graph of the function features a cusp at that point. Look at $f(x) = x^{2/3}$. It looks like a seagull drawn by a kindergartener. The function $f(x) = x^{2/3}$ is continuous for all real numbers. It is connected everywhere. Yet, the function $f(x) = x^{2/3}$ is not differentiable at $x = 0$ because the graph forms a sharp cusp at the origin. The slopes are whipping toward negative infinity on one side and positive infinity on the other. It is a mathematical meat grinder. 

Sometimes, it’s not even a sharp turn; it's gravity betraying you. A continuous function is not differentiable at a point if the graph of the function has a vertical tangent line at that point. Consider the cube root, $f(x) = x^{1/3}$. The function $f(x) = x^{1/3}$ is continuous for all real numbers. It slithers beautifully across the entire plane. However, the function $f(x) = x^{1/3}$ is not differentiable at $x = 0$ because the graph contains a vertical tangent line at the origin. A vertical tangent line occurs at a point $c$ if the function is continuous at $c$ and the absolute value of the derivative approaches infinity as $x$ approaches $c$. Your car is driving forward, and for one brief, horrifying instant, the road goes perfectly, ninety-degrees straight up into the air. The rate of change is infinite. The derivative explodes.

And if you want to know the ultimate villain of calculus, the Moriarty of mathematics, it is the Weierstrass function. The Weierstrass function is a specific mathematical example of a function that is continuous everywhere. It is completely unbroken. But the Weierstrass function is a specific mathematical example of a function that is nowhere differentiable. It is a fractal nightmare made entirely of microscopic razor blades. You zoom in, and there is no local linearity, just more corners and cusps infinitely deep. You try to drive on it, and your tires are instantly shredded at every single coordinate. 

When you get to the Praxis exam, they aren't going to just hand you these simple equations. They are going to hand you the Frankenstein heist maps—piecewise functions—where jurisdiction crosses from the city cops over to the county mounties. A piecewise function must be checked for continuity at the boundary points between different rule definitions. You have to make sure the roads actually connect at the border. But checking the pavement isn't enough to secure the getaway! Differentiability of a piecewise function at a boundary point requires the function to be continuous at that point first, establishing the connection. Then, differentiability of a piecewise function at a boundary point requires the left-hand derivative to equal the right-hand derivative at that point. You are a negotiator demanding \$100,000! You set the algebraic rules equal to force the roads to meet, and you set their derivatives equal to force the slopes to match. You solve the system, you find the constants, the road becomes smooth, and you drive away into the sunset with your teaching license!
