Imagine building a fort out of Popsicle sticks. If you pin four sticks together in a square, pushing on one corner makes it instantly flop over into a slanted shape. But if you pin three sticks together to make a triangle, it locks perfectly in place! It won't wiggle at all. That amazing stiffness is why triangles are the superhero shapes of geometry. In this guide, we are going to learn the simple rules that make polygons (shapes with straight sides) and triangles work. 

## The Triangle: The Unyielding Polygon

You might think you can take any three random sticks and make a triangle. But try building one with a 10-inch stick, a 2-inch stick, and a 3-inch stick. The two short sticks will never be able to reach each other! To actually make a triangle, you must follow the most important rule in geometry: **the sum of the lengths of any two sides of a triangle must be strictly greater than the length of the third side.** 

Because of this rule, another rule is also always true: **the positive difference between the lengths of any two sides of a triangle must be strictly less than the length of the third side.**

Let's figure out what the third side of a triangle could be if we only know two of the sides are 5 and 8. 
1. First, find the maximum size. Add the two sides together: 5 + 8 = 13. The third side MUST be strictly less than 13.
2. Next, find the minimum size. Subtract the smaller side from the larger side: 8 - 5 = 3. The third side MUST be strictly greater than 3.
3. So, your third side has to be anywhere between 3 and 13!

Angles and sides also work together like opening a laptop. If you open a laptop super wide, the top of the screen is very far from the keyboard. Because of how this works, **the longest side of a triangle is located directly opposite the largest interior angle of the triangle.** On the flip side, **the shortest side of a triangle is located directly opposite the smallest interior angle of the triangle.**

What if you draw a line straight past the edge of the triangle to make an angle on the outside? That’s called an exterior angle. Think of it like leveling up in a video game by combining two smaller items. **The measure of an exterior angle of a triangle is equal to the sum of the measures of the two remote interior angles of the triangle.** ("Remote" just means the two inside angles furthest away from it). 

Because that outside angle is literally two inside angles glued together, a powerful rule naturally follows: **the measure of an exterior angle of a triangle is strictly greater than the measure of either remote interior angle of the triangle.**

## Orthogonality: The Right Triangle and Pythagorean Classifications

"Orthogonality" is just a fancy math word for a perfect 90-degree, L-shaped corner. When a triangle has one of these corners, we call it a right triangle. The longest side (across from the L-shape) is called the hypotenuse, and the two shorter sides are the legs. 

Here is the famous rule for them: **In a right triangle, the square of the length of the hypotenuse equals the sum of the squares of the lengths of the two legs.** (You might have seen this written as a^2 + b^2 = c^2).

There are a few "cheat codes" you should memorize so you don't even have to do the math. These are special side lengths that always make a perfect right triangle:
* **A triangle with side lengths of 3, 4, and 5 units forms a right triangle.**
* **A triangle with side lengths of 5, 12, and 13 units forms a right triangle.**
* **A triangle with side lengths of 8, 15, and 17 units forms a right triangle.**

Let's prove the 3, 4, and 5 one together step-by-step:
1. Take the two smaller legs (3 and 4) and square them: 3 * 3 = 9, and 4 * 4 = 16.
2. Add those two squares together: 9 + 16 = 25.
3. Now square the longest side (5): 5 * 5 = 25.
4. Because 25 equals 25, it forms a perfect right triangle!

But what if the numbers don't match up perfectly? Think of the longest side like a pizza box lid. 
* If the lid is too small, it squishes the angle inward. So, **if the square of the longest side of a triangle is strictly less than the sum of the squares of the other two sides, the triangle is an acute triangle.** (Acute means all angles are smaller than 90 degrees).
* If the lid is too big, it stretches the angle wide open. So, **if the square of the longest side of a triangle is strictly greater than the sum of the squares of the other two sides, the triangle is an obtuse triangle.** (Obtuse means one angle is larger than 90 degrees).

### Special Right Triangles

Sometimes, if you slice perfect shapes into pieces, you get special right triangles. 

If you cut a square perfectly in half corner-to-corner, you get a 45-45-90 triangle. 
* **In a 45-45-90 right triangle, the two legs are congruent.** (Congruent just means exactly the same size and shape).
* **In a 45-45-90 right triangle, the length of the hypotenuse is exactly the length of a leg multiplied by the square root of 2.**

If you cut a perfectly balanced triangle in half straight down the middle, you get a 30-60-90 triangle. In this shape, the rules are specific:
* **In a 30-60-90 right triangle, the shorter leg is always positioned opposite the 30-degree angle.**
* **In a 30-60-90 right triangle, the hypotenuse is exactly twice the length of the shorter leg.**
* **In a 30-60-90 right triangle, the longer leg is exactly the length of the shorter leg multiplied by the square root of 3.**

## Symmetry in Triangles: Isosceles and Equilateral Constraints

Symmetry means something looks exactly the same on both sides, like a butterfly. 
**An isosceles triangle has at least two congruent sides.** Because of this perfect balance, **the angles opposite the congruent sides of an isosceles triangle are congruent.**

If you draw a straight line right down the middle (called an altitude), magic happens:
* **The altitude drawn to the base of an isosceles triangle bisects the vertex angle.** (Bisect means it slices the top angle perfectly into two equal halves).
* **The altitude drawn to the base of an isosceles triangle bisects the base.** (It cuts the bottom line perfectly into two equal halves).

When we crank that symmetry up to maximum, we get the equilateral triangle!
* **An equilateral triangle has three congruent sides.**
* Because sides control angles, **an equilateral triangle has three congruent interior angles.**
* Because all inside angles of a triangle add up to 180 degrees, **every interior angle of an equilateral triangle measures exactly 60 degrees.**

Just like the isosceles shape, **the altitude of an equilateral triangle bisects the base of the equilateral triangle**, and **the altitude of an equilateral triangle bisects the vertex angle of the equilateral triangle.**

There is a special trick for finding the space inside (the area) of this shape. **The area of an equilateral triangle with side length s is equal to s squared multiplied by the square root of 3, all divided by 4.** 

Let's try calculating the area for an equilateral triangle with side lengths of 6:
1. Take the side length (s = 6) and square it: 6 * 6 = 36.
2. Multiply that by the square root of 3: 36 * sqrt(3).
3. Divide the whole thing by 4: 36 / 4 = 9.
4. The final area is 9 * sqrt(3)!

## The Quadrilateral Family Tree: Managing Degrees of Freedom

When shapes have four sides, we call them quadrilaterals. They are a bit looser than triangles. Convex just means the shape doesn't cave inward (like a bouncy ball, not a popped tire). **The sum of the interior angles of any convex quadrilateral is exactly 360 degrees.** 

### Parallelograms
The most famous type of four-sided shape is the parallelogram. **A parallelogram is a quadrilateral with two pairs of parallel opposite sides.** (Parallel means the lines run perfectly side-by-side like train tracks and never touch).

Because of those train tracks, lots of cool rules happen automatically:
* **The opposite sides of a parallelogram are congruent.**
* **The opposite interior angles of a parallelogram are congruent.**
* **Any two consecutive interior angles of a parallelogram are supplementary.** (Consecutive means sitting right next to each other, and supplementary means they add up to exactly 180 degrees).
* **The diagonals of a parallelogram bisect each other.** (Diagonals are lines drawn from corner to opposite corner. They cut each other perfectly in half!).

If you are trying to figure out if a shape is a parallelogram, here is a surefire test: **A quadrilateral is guaranteed to be a parallelogram if exactly one pair of opposite sides is both parallel and congruent.**

Special parallelograms have extra superpowers:
* **A rectangle is a parallelogram containing exactly four right angles.** Because it stands perfectly straight up and down, **the diagonals of a rectangle are congruent.**
* **A rhombus is a parallelogram containing exactly four congruent sides.** Think of a diamond shape! Its corner-to-corner lines do amazing things: **the diagonals of a rhombus intersect at perpendicular angles** (meaning they cross to make perfect 90-degree L-shapes), and **the diagonals of a rhombus bisect the interior angles of the rhombus.**
* The absolute coolest one is the square. **A square is a quadrilateral that has exactly four congruent sides**, AND **a square is a quadrilateral that has exactly four right angles.** It gets all the superpowers of the rectangle and the rhombus!

### Trapezoids
If we break the rules and only have one set of train tracks, we get a trapezoid. **A trapezoid is a quadrilateral containing exactly one pair of parallel sides.**
* **The parallel sides of a trapezoid are called the bases of the trapezoid.** (Usually the top and bottom).
* **The non-parallel sides of a trapezoid are called the legs of the trapezoid.** (The slanted side pieces).

If you draw a line right through the middle, it's called a midsegment. **The midsegment of a trapezoid is parallel to the bases of the trapezoid.** Plus, **the length of the midsegment of a trapezoid is exactly half the sum of the lengths of the two bases.**

Let's find a midsegment together:
1. Imagine the top base is 10 inches wide and the bottom base is 20 inches wide. 
2. Add the two bases together: 10 + 20 = 30.
3. Cut that total exactly in half: 30 / 2 = 15. 
4. The midsegment is 15 inches wide!

If the slanted legs on the sides match perfectly, it gets a special name. **An isosceles trapezoid is a trapezoid with congruent non-parallel legs.** Because it is perfectly symmetrical, **the base angles of an isosceles trapezoid are congruent.** Also, **the diagonals of an isosceles trapezoid are congruent.**

### Kites
Imagine the toy you fly in the wind! **A kite is a quadrilateral with exactly two distinct pairs of consecutive congruent sides.** (Consecutive means the matching sides are touching each other).

Because of its classic cross shape, **the diagonals of a kite intersect at perpendicular angles.** However, only the horizontal crossbar gets cut in half, meaning **exactly one diagonal of a kite bisects the other diagonal.** Finally, where the short sides and long sides meet, **a kite contains exactly one pair of opposite congruent interior angles.**

## General Polygons: Scaling the Rules

What about shapes with 5, 10, or 100 sides? Let the letter "n" stand for the number of sides. 

To find out how many degrees fit inside a shape, use this rule: **The sum of the interior angles of an n-sided convex polygon is 180 degrees multiplied by the quantity n minus 2.**
Let's figure out the total inside degrees for a 5-sided shape (a pentagon):
1. Start with the number of sides: n = 5.
2. Subtract 2 from that number: 5 - 2 = 3.
3. Multiply that by 180 degrees: 3 * 180 = 540 degrees. 
4. A pentagon has 540 inside degrees!

Outside angles are super easy. Imagine walking around the edge of a park. Eventually, you turn in a full circle to get back to the start. Because a circle is always the same, **the sum of the exterior angles of any convex polygon is exactly 360 degrees**, no matter how many sides it has!

When a shape is totally fair and perfectly balanced, we call it regular. **A regular polygon is a polygon that is both equilateral and equiangular.** (All sides match, and all angles match).

Think of angles like an allowance of money being shared fairly! Since a regular polygon shares its degrees totally equally:
* **The measure of a single interior angle of an n-sided regular polygon is 180 degrees multiplied by the quantity n minus 2, all divided by n.** (For our pentagon example, divide the \$540 total degrees by 5 perfectly equal angles to get 108 degrees each).
* **The measure of a single exterior angle of an n-sided regular polygon is exactly 360 degrees divided by n.**

Finally, what if you want to draw lines connecting the inside corners? Those are diagonals. **The total number of diagonals in an n-sided polygon is equal to n multiplied by the quantity n minus 3, all divided by 2.**

Let's find out how many diagonals an 8-sided shape (octagon) has!
1. Start with the number of sides: n = 8.
2. Subtract 3 from the sides: 8 - 3 = 5.
3. Multiply those together: 8 * 5 = 40.
4. Divide by 2: 40 / 2 = 20.
5. An octagon has exactly 20 diagonals!
