Listen to me, put down the pint, and look at these pretzel sticks. When you finally stand in front of those teenagers, they’re going to think geometry is just connecting dots. They think you can take any three random stick lengths, glue them together, and magically get a triangle. They are painfully, hilariously wrong. 

Building a triangle is a high-stakes hostage negotiation with the laws of reality. There are strict boundaries to this domain! If you have two pretzel sticks of lengths 2 and 3, they will never, ever bridge across a pretzel stick of length 10. They’ll just flap around like sad little T-Rex arms! That is because the sum of the lengths of any two sides of a triangle must be strictly greater than the length of the third side. If it’s not, the shape collapses. It’s dead on arrival. Conversely, the floor of this negotiation is just as brutal: the positive difference between the lengths of any two sides of a triangle must be strictly less than the length of the third side. Together, those rules give you the exact window of survival for your missing side lengths on the exam. 

Once you lock those three sides together, congratulations, you’ve just created a locked-room reality TV drama. Every angle is fighting for real estate, and the biggest mouth always gets the biggest slice of the pie. The longest side of a triangle is located directly opposite the largest interior angle of the triangle! It demands the space, pushing the walls outward. And the meek? They get squeezed into the corner. The shortest side of a triangle is located directly opposite the smallest interior angle of the triangle. 

But what if someone can’t take the pressure and bursts through the wall? If you extend one side out into the yard, you create an exterior angle. This angle is basically the venting of the drama from the other side of the house. The measure of an exterior angle of a triangle is equal to the sum of the measures of the two remote interior angles of the triangle. And because it is the literal sum of those two distinct voices, a fundamental inequality drops like a gavel: the measure of an exterior angle of a triangle is strictly greater than the measure of either remote interior angle of the triangle. 

Now, let’s talk about the hitmen. The enforcers of Euclidean space. The Right Triangles. 

When one angle snaps to a perfect 90 degrees, you are out of reality TV and into a mob movie. The Pythagorean theorem is the ironclad law of the streets here: in a right triangle, the square of the length of the hypotenuse equals the sum of the squares of the lengths of the two legs. For your exam, you need to know their elite hit-squad configurations so you don't waste time fumbling with your graphing calculator. Memorize their mugshots! A triangle with side lengths of 3, 4, and 5 units forms a right triangle. A triangle with side lengths of 5, 12, and 13 units forms a right triangle. A triangle with side lengths of 8, 15, and 17 units forms a right triangle. 

But what if the hypotenuse gets a big head? Imagine the hypotenuse as a lid over the two legs. If it stretches too far, it pulls the legs apart! If the square of the longest side of a triangle is strictly greater than the sum of the squares of the other two sides, the lid is too big, popping the shape open, and the triangle is an obtuse triangle. But if the lid shrinks? It clamps the legs inward. If the square of the longest side of a triangle is strictly less than the sum of the squares of the other two sides, the triangle is an acute triangle. 

Within the mob, you have specialized strike teams. First, the twins holding down the corners: the 45-45-90 right triangle. Because the angles are identical, in a 45-45-90 right triangle, the two legs are congruent. And the boss connecting them? In a 45-45-90 right triangle, the length of the hypotenuse is exactly the length of a leg multiplied by the square root of 2. 

Then you have the sniper squad, the 30-60-90 right triangle. Size ordering is life or death here. In a 30-60-90 right triangle, the shorter leg is always positioned opposite the 30-degree angle—it's the scout. The commander? In a 30-60-90 right triangle, the hypotenuse is exactly twice the length of the shorter leg. And the heavy artillery? In a 30-60-90 right triangle, the longer leg is exactly the length of the shorter leg multiplied by the square root of 3. 

This brings us to the cults. The obsession with symmetry.

First, the Isosceles cult. An isosceles triangle has at least two congruent sides. Because their physical walls are completely balanced, their ideologies are too: the angles opposite the congruent sides of an isosceles triangle are congruent. If you drop a plumb line from the very top peak straight to the floor, it splits the cult right down the middle with terrifying precision. The altitude drawn to the base of an isosceles triangle bisects the vertex angle, and perfectly symmetrically, the altitude drawn to the base of an isosceles triangle bisects the base. 

Then we go to the ultimate extreme. The Equilateral triangle. Total conformity. An equilateral triangle has three congruent sides. Because the sides dictate the angles, the entire shape is brainwashed into uniformity: an equilateral triangle has three congruent interior angles. And since the total budget for a triangle’s interior is always 180 degrees, every interior angle of an equilateral triangle measures exactly 60 degrees. If you drop a plumb line in this utopia, it behaves just like its isosceles cousin on steroids. The altitude of an equilateral triangle bisects the base of the equilateral triangle, and simultaneously, the altitude of an equilateral triangle bisects the vertex angle of the equilateral triangle! Because this altitude naturally spawns two perfect 30-60-90 sniper squads, we unlock the ultimate shortcut for your test: the area of an equilateral triangle with side length $s$ is equal to $s$ squared multiplied by the square root of 3, all divided by 4.

Alright, wipe the table. Let’s add a fourth side. 

When you move to four sides, you lose the inherent rigidity of the triangle. The structure gets sloppy. There's 360 degrees of sheer chaos buzzing around inside—because the sum of the interior angles of any convex quadrilateral is exactly 360 degrees. To survive the madness, shapes have to form alliances. They have to form... Parallelograms. 

The Parallelogram Dynasty is old money. A parallelogram is a quadrilateral with two pairs of parallel opposite sides. Once you lock in those parallel tracks, a massive inheritance of rules cascades down. The opposite sides of a parallelogram are congruent! The opposite interior angles of a parallelogram are congruent! If you just walk around the perimeter, any two consecutive interior angles of a parallelogram are supplementary, adding up to 180 degrees to keep the peace. And their interior crossing paths? The diagonals of a parallelogram bisect each other, splitting the loot straight down the middle. Now, if you are acting as a bouncer at the club and you need to verify if a shape is secretly in the parallelogram family, here’s your VIP shortcut: a quadrilateral is guaranteed to be a parallelogram if exactly one pair of opposite sides is both parallel and congruent. 

From the Parallelogram Dynasty come the elite nepo babies. 
First, the Rectangle. A rectangle is a parallelogram containing exactly four right angles. Because of this flawless, rigid posture, the diagonals of a rectangle are congruent. 
Next, the eccentric Rhombus. A rhombus is a parallelogram containing exactly four congruent sides. It’s so intensely focused on its outer equality that its inner workings clash beautifully: the diagonals of a rhombus intersect at perpendicular angles, and further, the diagonals of a rhombus bisect the interior angles of the rhombus. 
And then, the absolute golden child, the Square. A square is a quadrilateral that has exactly four congruent sides, AND a square is a quadrilateral that has exactly four right angles. It hoards all the wealth of the rectangle and the rhombus!

But what about the shapes that didn't make the dynasty? The awkward middle management of geometry. The Trapezoids. 
A trapezoid is a quadrilateral containing exactly one pair of parallel sides. They just have one matching set of tracks! The parallel sides of a trapezoid are called the bases of the trapezoid, while the non-parallel sides of a trapezoid are called the legs of the trapezoid. If you want to find the middle ground between the upper and lower management bases, you draw a line straight across the middle. The midsegment of a trapezoid is parallel to the bases of the trapezoid, and its value is perfectly bureaucratic: the length of the midsegment of a trapezoid is exactly half the sum of the lengths of the two bases. 

Now, if middle management decides to wear matching suits, you get an Isosceles Trapezoid. An isosceles trapezoid is a trapezoid with congruent non-parallel legs. Suddenly, because of the symmetry, they get perks! The base angles of an isosceles trapezoid are congruent, and shockingly, the diagonals of an isosceles trapezoid are congruent. 

And out in left field? The bizarre indie-pop duo. The Kite. A kite is a quadrilateral with exactly two distinct pairs of consecutive congruent sides. It’s built like a cross-frame. Because of this cross structure, the diagonals of a kite intersect at perpendicular angles. But only the horizontal crossbar gets perfectly split—exactly one diagonal of a kite bisects the other diagonal. And where those weird mismatched sides finally bump into each other? A kite contains exactly one pair of opposite congruent interior angles. 

Finally, my friend, the district expands. You aren't just teaching four-sided shapes. You’re looking at massive $n$-sided school districts! Pentagons, octagons, dodecagons. 

How do you handle the interior drama of a massive polygon? You just slice it into triangles from one corner! Every shape can be reduced to $(n - 2)$ triangles. Therefore, the sum of the interior angles of an $n$-sided convex polygon is 180 degrees multiplied by the quantity $n$ minus 2. 
But what if you step outside and walk the perimeter fence? Listen to me. No matter how many sides a shape has, walking all the way around is just one giant turn. Three sides or three thousand sides, the physical reality is identical: the sum of the exterior angles of any convex polygon is exactly 360 degrees. 

When the district decides to go full utopian and perfectly divide all resources, you get Regular Polygons. A regular polygon is a polygon that is both equilateral and equiangular. Everyone gets the exact same slice. So, to find one internal slice? The measure of a single interior angle of an $n$-sided regular polygon is 180 degrees multiplied by the quantity $n$ minus 2, all divided by $n$. And the outside corners? The measure of a single exterior angle of an $n$-sided regular polygon is exactly 360 degrees divided by $n$. 

Last thing before the bartender kicks us out! The school gossip network. How many secret lines of communication can you draw between the corners of a massive polygon? From any single corner, you can't text yourself, and you can't text the two people standing right next to you, so that’s $(n - 3)$ lines. To avoid double-counting the text thread from both ends, you multiply by the total number of people and divide by two! The total number of diagonals in an $n$-sided polygon is equal to $n$ multiplied by the quantity $n$ minus 3, all divided by 2.
