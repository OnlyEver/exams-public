Let’s start with the ultimate reality TV drama of the math world: the cross-section. What is a cross-section? It is the two-dimensional figure formed by the intersection of a plane and a three-dimensional solid. Think of it as taking a giant, invisible guillotine and slashing right through a 3D relationship just to look at the flat 2D drama exposed on the inside. 

Take the sphere. The sphere is that perfect, unbothered, straight-A student. No matter how you try to cut them down, the intersection of a sphere and a plane always produces a circle, or, if you just barely graze the surface with a tangent insult, a single point. But if you want maximum emotional damage, you aim right for the heart. A great circle is the cross-section formed when an intersecting plane passes exactly through the center of a sphere. Boom. The equator. The biggest slice possible. 

But what if you’re slicing a right circular cylinder? Picture a towering stack of ungraded standardized tests, perfectly cylindrical. If you slice it with a plane parallel to the base, what do you get? A circular cross-section. Just another circle of grading hell. But what if you bring the blade straight down from the ceiling? A plane perpendicular to the base of a right circular cylinder drops straight through the top, down the height, and creates a rectangular cross-section. But let’s say you’re exhausted, it’s 3 AM, and your grading standards are tilting. You slice it at a weird angle. An oblique plane intersecting only the lateral surface of a cylinder creates an elliptical cross-section. An ellipse! A stretched out, distorted circle of truth!

And the cones! Oh, the right circular cone is the megaphone of the school’s cheerleading squad. Slice it flat with a plane parallel to the base? You get a circular cross-section. But if you take a plane and slice it parallel to the slant edge of the cone? You create a parabolic cross-section! A parabola! It curves out toward the edge and just escapes into infinity, never closing back on itself, exactly like a freshman asking to go to the bathroom and never returning! Now, take your plane and chop perpendicular to the base, right down straight through the apex. You just created an isosceles triangle cross-section, perfectly splitting that megaphone in half. And if you have a double cone—two megaphones stacked point-to-point like an hourglass of doom—and a plane intersects both halves parallel to the vertical axis? You create a hyperbolic cross-section. Two mirrored curves running away from each other like warring co-teachers. 

Now, curved surfaces are smooth, but polyhedrons? Pyramids and cubes? They hide secrets. If you take a plane parallel to the base of any pyramid, it creates a cross-section that is geometrically similar to the base polygon. It’s just a scaled-down clone of the base. 

But the cube is a chaotic trickster god. You think a cube only holds squares? A plane intersecting a cube can actually create a cross-section shaped like an equilateral triangle! You just slice off a single corner, intersecting exactly three faces. Want to pull off a geometric heist? A plane intersecting a cube can create a cross-section shaped like a regular hexagon! It’s the ultimate surgical strike—a regular hexagon cross-section of a cube is formed by a plane passing through the midpoints of exactly six specific edges of the cube. Six edges! Perfectly balanced! 

But don't get greedy. If a student ever tells you they sliced a regular pentagon out of a cube, you fail them on the spot. A plane intersecting a cube absolutely cannot form a regular pentagon as a cross-section. Why? Look at the strict domain boundaries of reality! To get a five-sided cross-section, your plane must intersect five faces of the cube. A cube only has three pairs of parallel faces. If you hit five faces, it is mathematically guaranteed that you will hit at least one pair of parallel faces! And the ironclad law of geometry states that any cross-section intersecting parallel planes must produce parallel lines of intersection! Therefore, any five-sided cross-section of a cube must contain at least one pair of parallel sides! A regular pentagon has zero parallel sides! It’s geometrically illegal! 

Okay, okay, deep breath. We’ve sliced the universe to pieces. Now we have to build it back up. We have to spin it. Enter the solid of revolution! This is a three-dimensional figure obtained by rotating a two-dimensional plane curve around an axis. It’s the rumor mill of the teacher's lounge—you take a flat, 2D whisper, spin it around an axis of gossip fast enough, and suddenly it sweeps through empty space and becomes a massive, 3D object! 

Imagine you’re holding a flat, rigid rectangle. Rotating a rectangle around one of the rectangle's sides generates a right circular cylinder. The mechanics are ruthless and exact. The side of the rectangle used as the axis of rotation becomes the height of the resulting right circular cylinder! The side of the rectangle perpendicular to the axis of rotation sweeps outward and becomes the radius of the resulting right circular cylinder! 

What about a right triangle? Rotating a right triangle around one of the triangle's legs generates a right circular cone! The leg used as the axis of rotation becomes the height of the resulting right circular cone. The leg perpendicular to the axis of rotation becomes the radius of the resulting right circular cone! And that slanted hypotenuse whipping through the air? The hypotenuse of the rotated right triangle becomes the slant height of the resulting right circular cone! 

Isosceles triangles want in on the action too. Rotating an isosceles triangle around the triangle's line of symmetry splits it into two spinning right triangles and generates a right circular cone! 

And if you take a flat semicircle and rotate it around the straight diameter of the semicircle? You generate a perfect sphere. 

But what if you spin a shape around a line it never even touches? The ultimate social distancing! Rotating a rectangle around a line parallel to one of the rectangle's sides without intersecting the rectangle generates a hollow cylindrical tube. Like a cardboard paper towel roll of emptiness. And if you take a full circle and rotate it around a coplanar line that does not intersect the circle? You generate a torus! A massive, 3D doughnut hovering in the void!

Finally, we arrive at the autopsy table. The unpacking. The Nets. A net is a two-dimensional pattern that can be folded along defined edges to form a three-dimensional solid. It’s your flat lesson plan before the kids arrive, waiting to be folded into a 3D classroom experience. 

The absolute golden rule of the universe, the conservation of trauma, is this: The surface area of a three-dimensional solid is exactly equal to the total area of the solid's corresponding two-dimensional net. No area is lost in the fold. None! 

For prisms and pyramids, it’s like packing a lunch. A net folding into a closed rectangular prism must contain exactly three pairs of congruent rectangles. Front and back, left and right, top and bottom. A net of a regular n-sided right prism consists of two congruent n-sided polygons for the lids, and n corresponding rectangles wrapping around the sides. A net of a regular n-sided pyramid consists of one n-sided polygon base and n corresponding isosceles triangles reaching up to form the point. And the spiciest little shape of them all, the regular tetrahedron, can be formed from a net consisting of exactly four congruent equilateral triangles. 

But the cube? The cube is a diva. There are exactly eleven distinct valid nets that can fold into a perfect cube. Eleven different flat patterns that perfectly assemble into a 3D box. 

Curved surfaces, though? Their nets are psychological traps designed to ruin your students' numeric-entry responses. Think about a right circular cylinder. A standard net of a right circular cylinder consists of two congruent circles—the top and bottom lids—and one rectangle. That rectangle is just the label peeled off a soup can! And because that flat label has to wrap perfectly around the edge of the circular lid, the length of the rectangular part of a cylinder's net equals the circumference of the cylinder's circular base! 

But the cone... oh, the cone is the final boss of the Praxis exam. A standard net of a right circular cone consists of one full circle for the base, and one circular sector. It looks like a flat circle sitting next to a giant Pac-Man. Because the curved mouth of that Pac-Man has to perfectly swallow the base, the arc length of the circular sector in a cone's net equals the circumference of the cone's circular base. 

But here is where your future students will fail if you do not physically shake them by the shoulders! They will look at the circular sector in the net and assume its flat radius is the radius of the cone. IT IS NOT. The radius of the circular sector in a cone's net corresponds to the slant height of the folded three-dimensional cone! The slant height! Because when you fold that Pac-Man up into a party hat, that flat radius lifts off the table and becomes the diagonal wall of the cone!
