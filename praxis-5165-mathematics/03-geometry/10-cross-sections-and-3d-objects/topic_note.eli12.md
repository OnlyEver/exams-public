Doctors use a really cool trick when they want to see inside the human body. They use a machine called a CT scanner, which takes a bunch of flat, 2D pictures of a person’s insides. Then, a computer stacks all those flat pictures together to build a 3D model! Even though we live in a 3D world—like in your favorite video games—everything 3D can actually be broken down into flat 2D shapes. 

Learning how 2D shapes and 3D objects work together gives you a sort of x-ray vision. You will learn how slicing an object reveals secret shapes, how spinning a flat drawing in the air creates a 3D solid, and how folding a flat piece of paper can build a perfect box. Let's dive in!

## The Anatomy of the Slice: Cross-Sections

To really understand a 3D shape, we have to imagine cutting it open. **A cross-section is the two-dimensional figure formed by the intersection of a plane and a three-dimensional solid.** Think of a "plane" as a perfectly flat ninja sword slicing straight through a block of cheese. The fresh, flat surface you see after making the cut is your cross-section. 

The shape of this slice changes depending on two things: what 3D object you are cutting, and the angle of your slice.

### Slicing Symmetrical Solids
Let's start with a sphere, which is perfectly round like a basketball. Because it is perfectly smooth and even, **the intersection of a sphere and a plane always produces a circle or a single point** (it makes a single point if your sword just barely grazes the very edge). If you slice straight through the exact middle, you get the biggest circle possible. **A great circle is the cross-section formed when an intersecting plane passes exactly through the center of a sphere.** If you slice the Earth in half, the Equator is a great circle!

Now think about a right circular cylinder, which is shaped exactly like a soup can. The slices here look completely different depending on how you angle your cut:
*   **A plane parallel to the base of a right circular cylinder creates a circular cross-section.** (This is like cutting across the can so the slice matches the flat top).
*   **A plane perpendicular to the base of a right circular cylinder creates a rectangular cross-section.** Imagine chopping a soup can straight down from the top lid to the bottom base. The flat face you reveal inside the can is a perfect rectangle!
*   What if you tilt your slice? **An oblique plane intersecting only the lateral surface of a cylinder creates an elliptical cross-section.** (An oblique plane is a tilted cut, and the lateral surface is the curved side. This makes a stretched-out oval shape, called an ellipse).

### The Conic Sections
When we slice a right circular cone (like an orange traffic cone), we get some famous shapes that scientists use to study how planets orbit the sun!
*   **A plane parallel to the base of a right circular cone creates a circular cross-section.** (Like chopping off the pointy tip).
*   **A plane intersecting a cone parallel to the slant edge of the cone creates a parabolic cross-section.** A parabola is a U-shape. You get this by matching your cutting angle perfectly with the sloped side of the cone.
*   If you chop straight down from the very top point, **a plane perpendicular to the base of a right circular cone and passing through the apex creates an isosceles triangle cross-section.**
*   Finally, imagine two cones stacked tip-to-tip, like an hourglass. This is called a double cone. **A plane intersecting both halves of a double cone parallel to the vertical axis creates a hyperbolic cross-section.** A hyperbola looks like two mirrored U-shapes facing away from each other.

### The Hidden Polygons of Cubes and Pyramids
Curved shapes give us smooth slices. But shapes with flat faces and sharp edges (like cubes and pyramids) give us slices with straight lines and angles.

Let's look at a pyramid. **A plane parallel to the base of any pyramid creates a cross-section that is geometrically similar to the base polygon.** "Geometrically similar" just means it is the exact same shape, only shrunken down. 

A cube (like a 6-sided dice) hides some wild surprises. When you slice a cube, the slice gets one side for every face of the cube you cut through.
*   If you chop off just one pointy corner, you only cut through three faces. So, **a plane intersecting a cube can create a cross-section shaped like an equilateral triangle.**
*   If you are super precise and angle your cut to go right through the middle, you get six sides! **A plane intersecting a cube can create a cross-section shaped like a regular hexagon.** To do this trick, **a regular hexagon cross-section of a cube is formed by a plane passing through the midpoints of six specific edges of the cube.**

Could you cut a cube to make a regular pentagon (a perfect 5-sided shape)? To get 5 sides, you have to cut through 5 faces of the cube. But a cube only has 3 pairs of opposite (parallel) faces. If you cut 5 faces, the rules of math say you are forced to cut through at least one pair of parallel faces, which means your slice will have parallel sides. Because a *regular* pentagon has zero parallel sides, **a plane intersecting a cube cannot form a regular pentagon as a cross-section because any five-sided cross-section of a cube must contain at least one pair of parallel sides.**

## Spinning Space: Solids of Revolution

We just learned how to break a 3D object down into a 2D slice. Now, let's play the game in reverse!

> **Definition:** A **solid of revolution** is a three-dimensional figure obtained by rotating a two-dimensional plane curve around an axis.

Think of holding a coin upright on a table and flicking it so it spins really fast. While it spins, that flat coin looks like a 3D ball! We can create lots of 3D shapes by spinning flat 2D shapes around an invisible straight line called an "axis."

### Generating Prisms and Cylinders
If you hold a rectangular flag on a flagpole and spin the pole as fast as you can between your hands, the flapping flag blurs into a 3D shape. **Rotating a rectangle around one of the rectangle's sides generates a right circular cylinder.** 
Here is how the parts of the rectangle build the cylinder:
*   **The side of the rectangle used as the axis of rotation becomes the height of the resulting right circular cylinder.** (The flagpole itself is the height).
*   **The side of the rectangle perpendicular to the axis of rotation becomes the radius of the resulting right circular cylinder.** (The distance the flag sticks out from the pole is the radius).

What if we move the pole outside the flag? Imagine spinning a rectangle around a pole that is a few inches away from it. Because there is a gap, the middle stays totally empty. **Rotating a rectangle around a line parallel to one of the rectangle's sides without intersecting the rectangle generates a hollow cylindrical tube** (exactly like a paper towel roll!).

### Generating Cones, Spheres, and Tori
We can make lots of shapes just by spinning different flat figures:

| 2D Shape | What We Spin It Around | Resulting 3D Solid | The Details |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Right Triangle** | Around one of its straight legs | **Right Circular Cone** | • **The leg of the right triangle used as the axis of rotation becomes the height of the resulting right circular cone.** <br>• **The leg of the right triangle perpendicular to the axis of rotation becomes the radius of the resulting right circular cone.** <br>• **The hypotenuse of the rotated right triangle becomes the slant height of the resulting right circular cone.** |
| **Isosceles Triangle** | Around the exact middle (its line of symmetry) | **Right Circular Cone** | **Rotating an isosceles triangle around the triangle's line of symmetry generates a right circular cone.** The middle line becomes the height. |
| **Semicircle** | Around its flat, straight bottom (diameter) | **Sphere** | **Rotating a semicircle around the straight diameter of the semicircle generates a sphere.** |
| **Full Circle** | Around a line sitting outside the circle | **Torus** | **Rotating a full circle around a coplanar line that does not intersect the circle generates a torus.** (A torus is just the fancy math word for a doughnut!). |

## Unfolding Dimensions: Two-Dimensional Nets

Another way to build a 3D shape from flat 2D pieces is by folding. Think about how a flat, open cardboard pizza box folds up into a 3D container.

> **Definition:** A **net** is a two-dimensional pattern that can be folded along defined edges to form a three-dimensional solid. 

The coolest trick about nets is how they help you measure the outside of an object. **The surface area of a three-dimensional solid is exactly equal to the total area of the solid's corresponding two-dimensional net.** If you want to know how much wrapping paper it takes to cover a box, just unfold the box flat and measure the area of the flat shape!

### Polyhedron Nets
Shapes with flat sides have nets that are pretty easy to imagine, but there is usually more than one way to unfold them.
*   If you unfold a 6-sided dice, **there are exactly eleven distinct valid nets that can fold into a perfect cube.**
*   If you have a normal box, **a net folding into a closed rectangular prism must contain exactly three pairs of congruent rectangles.** (That means the top matches the bottom, the left matches the right, and the front matches the back).
*   For other shapes with straight sides: **a net of a regular n-sided right prism consists of two congruent n-sided polygons and n corresponding rectangles.** (For example, an octagon-shaped prism has 2 octagons and 8 rectangles).
*   For pyramids: **A net of a regular n-sided pyramid consists of one n-sided polygon and n corresponding isosceles triangles.** (So a pentagon-pyramid has 1 pentagon base and 5 triangle walls).
*   Finally, for a simple triangle-pyramid: **a regular tetrahedron can be formed from a net consisting of exactly four congruent equilateral triangles.**

### Curved Surface Nets (Cylinders and Cones)
Curved shapes are a bit trickier because unfolding them looks surprising! 

If you peel the paper label off a soup can, the label unrolls into a perfect flat rectangle. Because of this, **a standard net of a right circular cylinder consists of two congruent circles and one rectangle.** The circles are the top and bottom lids. But how long does that rectangle need to be? 

Here is how you figure out the exact length of the rectangle in a cylinder's net:
1.  Measure the diameter straight across one of the circular lids (let's say it is 4 inches).
2.  Multiply the diameter by pi (we can use 3.14) to find the circumference. (4 * 3.14 = 12.56 inches).
3.  Draw a rectangle that is exactly 12.56 inches long.

Because you measured carefully, **the length of the rectangular part of a cylinder's net equals the circumference of the cylinder's circular base.** That guarantees the rectangle wraps all the way around the lid perfectly!

A cone is even weirder to unfold. **A standard net of a right circular cone consists of one full circle and one circular sector.** (A circular sector looks exactly like Pac-Man!). Just like with the soup can, the curved edge of that Pac-Man shape has to wrap entirely around the circle base. So, **the arc length of the circular sector in a cone's net equals the circumference of the cone's circular base.**

Be super careful on this next part! It is easy to get confused by the sizes of the circles. **The radius of the circular sector in a cone's net corresponds to the slant height of the folded three-dimensional cone.** Do not mix this up with the radius of the flat base!

### Conclusion

You now have a powerful new way of looking at the world. When you look at a simple soup can, you can picture the flat rectangle spinning through the air to create it. You can picture the rectangle and two circles waiting to be folded into it. And you can picture the perfect, flat rectangle hiding inside if you were to slice it straight down! Geometry is everywhere, and now you know exactly how to spot its hidden secrets.
