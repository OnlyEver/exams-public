Modern [medicine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medicine) relies on a profound [geometric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry) truth: a [three-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_space) [solid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_geometry) can be perfectly understood by analyzing its [two-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two-dimensional_space) slices. A [computed tomography (CT) scan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CT_scan) passes a [plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_%28geometry%29) of [X-rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray) through the human body, capturing a sequence of flat images that [computers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer) reconstruct into a complex [volume](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume). You, as a future [mathematics educator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics_education), are tasked with building this exact [spatial intuition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spatial_ability) in your students. To them, the world is naturally three-dimensional. Your job is to show them how [dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension) interact—how slicing a solid reveals hidden [polygons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon), how spinning a flat [curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curve) carves out a volume in empty [space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_%28mathematics%29), and how folding a flat sheet of paper can entirely enclose a void. 

![A medical CT scan relies on the geometric principle of cross-sections, slicing a three-dimensional human thorax into two-dimensional images to reveal internal structures.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Axial_plane_CT_scan_of_the_thorax_illustrative_image.jpg)

Mastering these spatial translations—[cross-sections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_section_%28geometry%29), [rotations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation_%28mathematics%29), and [nets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Net_%28polyhedron%29)—is not just about memorizing [shapes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shape). It is about understanding the strict [mechanical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanics) constraints that govern our [physical universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe). Let us dissect exactly how these [geometric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry) phenomena behave.

## The Anatomy of the Slice: Cross-Sections

To understand a [solid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_geometry), we must cut into it. By definition, **a [cross-section](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_section_%28geometry%29) is the [two-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two-dimensional_space) figure formed by the [intersection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intersection_%28geometry%29) of a [plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_%28geometry%29) and a [three-dimensional solid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_geometry).** When you slice a block of [cheese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cheese), the exposed flat face is your cross-section. 

The shape of this cross-section depends entirely on two factors: the [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry) of the solid, and the [angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle) of the intersecting plane. 

### Slicing Symmetrical Solids
Let's begin with the [sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere), the universe's most perfectly [symmetrical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry_%28geometry%29) object. Because of this symmetry, **the [intersection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intersection_%28geometry%29) of a sphere and a [plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_%28geometry%29) always produces a [circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle) or a single [point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_%28geometry%29)** (the latter occurring if the plane merely rests [tangent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent) to the [surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_%28mathematics%29)). If you shift that intersecting plane so it passes exactly through the center of the sphere, you create a maximum-sized slice. We call this specific cross-section a **[great circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_circle)**. In [navigation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Navigation), great circles represent the [shortest distance between two points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic) on the [globe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Globe); every [line of longitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Longitude), and the [equator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equator) itself, is a great circle.

![Passing a plane directly through the center of a sphere produces a great circle, which divides the solid into two perfectly equal hemispheres.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Great_circle_hemispheres.png)

Now consider a right circular [cylinder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder)—like a standard soup can. The cross-sections here change dramatically based on your cutting [angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle):
*   **A [plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_%28geometry%29) [parallel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_%28geometry%29) to the [base](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Base_%28geometry%29) of a right circular cylinder creates a [circular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle) cross-section.** 
*   **A plane [perpendicular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular) to the base of a right circular cylinder creates a [rectangular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle) cross-section.** Imagine a [guillotine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guillotine) dropping straight down through the top of the can. The cut runs across a [chord](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chord_%28geometry%29) of the top circle, drops straight down the [height](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Height_%28mathematics%29) of the cylinder, runs across a chord of the bottom circle, and returns straight up. 
*   If you tilt the cut? **An oblique plane intersecting only the [lateral surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lateral_surface_area) of a cylinder creates an [elliptical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse) cross-section.** 

![Depending entirely on the angle of the intersecting plane, slicing a solid right circular cylinder will yield a circular, elliptical, or rectangular cross-section.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Cylindric_section.svg)

### The Conic Sections
When we slice a right circular [cone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cone), we generate the famous [conic sections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conic_section), which govern everything from [satellite orbits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbit) to [satellite dishes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satellite_dish). 
*   **A [plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_%28geometry%29) [parallel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_%28geometry%29) to the [base](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Base_%28geometry%29) of a right circular cone creates a [circular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle) cross-section.**
*   **A plane intersecting a cone parallel to the slant edge of the cone creates a [parabolic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parabola) cross-section.** Because the plane perfectly matches the angle of the cone's growth, the [curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curve) never closes back on itself.
*   If we slice straight down from the top [point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_%28geometry%29), **a plane [perpendicular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular) to the base of a right circular cone and passing through the [apex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apex_%28geometry%29) creates an [isosceles triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isosceles_triangle) cross-section.**
*   To find our last [shape](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shape), we must visualize a double cone (two cones meeting at their apexes, like an [hourglass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hourglass)). **A plane intersecting both halves of a double cone parallel to the vertical [axis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system) creates a [hyperbolic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbola) cross-section**, resulting in two separate, mirrored curves.

![Slicing a double cone at various angles produces the four primary conic sections: the circle, ellipse, parabola, and hyperbola.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Conic_Sections.svg)

### The Hidden Polygons of Cubes and Pyramids
[Curved surfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_%28mathematics%29) yield smooth cross-sections. [Polyhedrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyhedron), possessing flat [faces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Face_%28geometry%29) and sharp [edges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edge_%28geometry%29), yield fascinating [polygonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon) cross-sections.

Consider a [pyramid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyramid_%28geometry%29). Due to the proportional [scaling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scaling_%28geometry%29) of a pyramid from its [base](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Base_%28geometry%29) to its [apex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apex_%28geometry%29), **a [plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_%28geometry%29) [parallel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_%28geometry%29) to the base of any pyramid creates a cross-section that is [geometrically similar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Similarity_%28geometry%29) to the base [polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon)**, simply scaled down depending on how close the cut is to the apex.

The [cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube), however, hides deep counterintuitive beauty. When a [plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_%28geometry%29) intersects a cube, the number of [sides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edge_%28geometry%29) on the resulting cross-section corresponds to the number of [faces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Face_%28geometry%29) the plane slices through. 
*   By slicing off a single corner (intersecting three faces), **a plane intersecting a cube can create a cross-section shaped like an [equilateral triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equilateral_triangle).**
*   If we meticulously orient a plane to slice exactly through the middle of the cube, we find a hidden [symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry_%28geometry%29): **A plane intersecting a cube can create a cross-section shaped like a [regular hexagon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hexagon).** Specifically, **a regular hexagon cross-section of a cube is formed by a plane passing through the [midpoints](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Midpoint) of six specific [edges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edge_%28geometry%29) of the cube.** 

Can a [cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube) produce a [regular pentagon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentagon)? To get a five-sided [polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon), the plane must intersect five [faces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Face_%28geometry%29) of the cube. Because a cube only has three pairs of [parallel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_%28geometry%29) faces, intersecting five faces guarantees that you will hit at least one pair of parallel faces. A rigid rule of [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry) dictates that when a plane intersects parallel planes, the resulting [lines of intersection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intersection_%28geometry%29) must also be parallel. Therefore, **a plane intersecting a cube cannot form a regular pentagon as a cross-section because any five-sided cross-section of a cube must contain at least one pair of parallel sides** (and a *regular* pentagon has zero parallel sides).

## Spinning Space: Solids of Revolution

We have seen how a [plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_%28geometry%29) can cut down a 3D [solid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_geometry) into a 2D [figure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shape). Now, let us reverse the process.

> **Definition:** A **[solid of revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_of_revolution)** is a [three-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_space) figure obtained by [rotating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation_%28mathematics%29) a [two-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two-dimensional_space) plane [curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curve) around an [axis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system).

Think of a [potter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pottery) at a [wheel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potter%27s_wheel). The potter's hands remain completely still in [two-dimensional space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two-dimensional_space), forming a specific profile. As the clay spins around the central [axis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system), that 2D profile sweeps through space, carving out a 3D vase. In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics), we achieve this by rigidly rotating flat [shapes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shape) around an axis.

![A solid of revolution is generated by taking a two-dimensional curve and sweeping it continuously around a central axis, enclosing a three-dimensional volume.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Rotationskoerper_animation.gif)

### Generating Prisms and Cylinders
If you hold a flag perfectly still while the wind blows, you see a [rectangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle). If you take that rectangular flag and twirl its pole rapidly between your hands, the flag sweeps out a [cylinder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder) in the air. 
**Rotating a rectangle around one of the rectangle's [sides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edge_%28geometry%29) generates a right circular cylinder.** 
The [mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanics) of this rotation are strict:
*   **The side of the rectangle used as the [axis of rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation_around_a_fixed_axis) becomes the [height](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Height_%28mathematics%29) of the resulting right circular cylinder.**
*   **The side of the rectangle [perpendicular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular) to the axis of rotation becomes the [radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radius) of the resulting right circular cylinder.**

What if we pull the axis away from the shape entirely? Imagine [rotating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation_%28mathematics%29) a [rectangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle), but this time spinning it around a vertical [line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_%28geometry%29) floating a few inches to its left. Because the axis does not touch the rectangle, the rotation sweeps out an empty center. Thus, **rotating a rectangle around a line [parallel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_%28geometry%29) to one of the rectangle's sides without intersecting the rectangle generates a hollow [cylindrical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder) tube** (much like a roll of [paper towels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paper_towel)).

![When a two-dimensional shape is rotated around an axis that it does not physically touch, the resulting solid of revolution features a hollow center.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Zylinder-rohr-s.svg)

### Generating Cones, Spheres, and Tori
We can map out other common [solids of revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_of_revolution) based on the 2D shapes that generate them:

| 2D Shape | Axis of Rotation | Resulting 3D Solid | Key Anatomical Correspondences |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **[Right Triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_triangle)** | Around one of its [legs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cathetus) | **[Right Circular Cone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cone)** | • **The leg used as the axis becomes the [height](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Height_%28mathematics%29).** <br>• **The leg [perpendicular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular) to the axis becomes the [radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radius).** <br>• **The [hypotenuse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypotenuse) becomes the [slant height](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slant_height).** |
| **[Isosceles Triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isosceles_triangle)** | Around its [line of symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflection_symmetry) | **Right Circular Cone** | The line of symmetry serves as the height; half the base serves as the radius. |
| **[Semicircle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semicircle)** | Around its straight [diameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diameter) | **[Sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere)** | The [radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radius) of the semicircle remains the radius of the sphere. |
| **[Full Circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle)** | Around a [coplanar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coplanarity) line that does not intersect it | **[Torus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torus)** | Generates a doughnut shape; the [distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distance) from the line to the circle determines the size of the "hole." |

Whenever your students encounter a [solid of revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_of_revolution) problem, advise them to draw a [cross-section](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_section_%28geometry%29) that includes the [axis of rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation_around_a_fixed_axis). The 2D shape resting on one side of that axis is exactly what was spun to create the solid.

## Unfolding Dimensions: Two-Dimensional Nets

While a [solid of revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_of_revolution) is generated by motion, we can also build 3D [solids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_geometry) through folding. When packaging [engineers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering) design [cardboard boxes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardboard_box), they don't manufacture them in three dimensions. They cut flat, perfectly patterned 2D cardboard and fold the edges up. 

> **Definition:** A **[net](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Net_%28polyhedron%29)** is a [two-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two-dimensional_space) pattern that can be folded along defined [edges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edge_%28geometry%29) to form a [three-dimensional solid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_geometry). 

The most important [physical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics) truth regarding nets is one of [conservation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservation_law): **The [surface area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_area) of a three-dimensional solid is exactly equal to the total [area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area) of the solid's corresponding two-dimensional net.** If your students are struggling with a surface area [formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formula), have them draw the net. The formula will suddenly reveal itself as nothing more than the sum of basic 2D areas.

### Polyhedron Nets
For shapes with flat [faces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Face_%28geometry%29), [nets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Net_%28polyhedron%29) are highly [intuitive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intuition), though multiple valid configurations can exist for a single solid. 
*   **There are exactly eleven distinct valid nets that can fold into a perfect [cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube).** 
*   More broadly, **a net folding into a closed [rectangular prism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cuboid) must contain exactly three pairs of [congruent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congruence_%28geometry%29) [rectangles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle)**, corresponding to the front/back, top/bottom, and left/right faces of the box.
*   **A net of a regular n-sided right [prism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prism_%28geometry%29) consists of two congruent n-sided [polygons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon) and n corresponding rectangles.** (For example, a [hexagonal prism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hexagonal_prism) net requires two regular [hexagons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hexagon) and six rectangles).
*   **A net of a regular n-sided [pyramid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyramid_%28geometry%29) consists of one n-sided polygon and n corresponding [isosceles triangles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isosceles_triangle).** 
*   For the most elemental of the [Platonic solids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platonic_solid), **a regular [tetrahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron) can be formed from a net consisting of exactly four congruent [equilateral triangles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equilateral_triangle).**

![There are exactly eleven geometrically distinct two-dimensional nets that can be folded along their edges to form a closed three-dimensional cube.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/The_11_cubic_nets.svg)

### Curved Surface Nets (Cylinders and Cones)
[Curved surfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_%28mathematics%29) present a conceptual hurdle because their nets do not perfectly resemble their 3D counterparts. 

Consider the right circular [cylinder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder). If you peel the label off a soup can, the label unfurls into a perfect [rectangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle). Therefore, **a standard net of a right circular cylinder consists of two [congruent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congruence_%28geometry%29) [circles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle)** (the top and bottom lids) **and one rectangle** (the [lateral surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lateral_surface_area)). The width of that rectangle is the cylinder's [height](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Height_%28mathematics%29), but what is its length? For the rectangle to wrap perfectly around the circular base without overlapping or leaving a gap, **the [length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Length) of the rectangular part of a cylinder's net equals the [circumference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumference) of the cylinder's circular [base](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Base_%28geometry%29).**

The right circular [cone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cone) is even trickier. 
**A standard net of a right circular cone consists of one full [circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle)** (the base) **and one [circular sector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circular_sector)** (a "[Pac-Man](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pac-Man)" shape representing the lateral surface). 
Just as with the cylinder, the curved edge of this lateral sector must wrap perfectly around the base. Thus, **the [arc length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arc_length) of the circular sector in a cone's net equals the [circumference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumference) of the cone's circular base.**

> **Teacher's Warning:** Students frequently conflate the [radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radius) of the [circular sector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circular_sector) in the net with the radius of the cone's base. This is a fatal [geometric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry) error. **The radius of the circular sector in a cone's net corresponds to the [slant height](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slant_height) of the folded three-dimensional cone.** 

![A cone's geometry is defined by its base radius and its slant height. When unfolded into a 2D net, the cone's slant height strictly becomes the radius of the net's circular sector.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Cone_surface_area.svg)

### Conclusion

Your ability to fluently translate between [two and three dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension) will dictate your success on the [Praxis Mathematics (5165)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Praxis_test) exam, as well as your effectiveness in the [classroom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classroom). When you look at a [cylinder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder), you must simultaneously see the [rectangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle) spinning to create it, the rectangle and two [circles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle) waiting to be folded into it, and the [ellipse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse) waiting to be sliced out of it. This is not mere [abstraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abstraction_%28mathematics%29)—this is the hidden [mechanical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanics) language of the [physical objects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_object) we interact with every single day. Teach your students to see the invisible [planes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_%28geometry%29) intersecting their world, and you will teach them to see [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry) everywhere.
