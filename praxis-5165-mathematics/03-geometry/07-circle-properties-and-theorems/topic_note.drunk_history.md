Slide it over here. Let’s start with the velvet rope keeping the riffraff out. The circumference of a circle is the total distance around the perimeter of the circle. Imagine unrolling that rope and stretching it out straight. How long is it? It’s ruled by the ultimate bouncer: Pi. Pi is defined as the ratio of a circle's circumference to the diameter of that same circle. It is the universal exchange rate of geometry. No matter if this club is the size of a coaster or Jupiter, that ratio holds. That means you can always calculate the perimeter if you know the width across. The circumference of a circle equals pi multiplied by the diameter of the circle. Or, since a diameter is just two radii standing shoulder to shoulder, the circumference of a circle equals two times pi multiplied by the radius of the circle. And what about the dance floor inside? The actual square footage? The area of a circle equals pi multiplied by the square of the radius of the circle. 

But you’ve got to measure the angles of this room, and this is where you warn your future students about their graphing calculators! They have to know what reality they’re operating in. If they are in the old-school reality-TV mode, the degree measure of a full circle is exactly 360 degrees. A full pan of the camera. But if they’re dealing with the raw, natural mechanics of the universe, they need radians. The radian measure of a full circle is exactly two times pi radians. Radians are just taking the radius and wrapping it around the velvet rope. 

Now, the club isn’t one big homogeneous space. It gets sectioned off. An arc is a continuous portion of the circumference of a circle. It’s just a stretch of the velvet rope. If you’ve got an indie band in the corner, they only need a small slice. A minor arc has a degree measure strictly less than 180 degrees. But if you’ve got a massive, egotistical diva taking up the whole VIP section? A major arc has a degree measure strictly greater than 180 degrees. And if it perfectly cuts the club in two? A semicircle is an arc that measures exactly 180 degrees. 

When you block off an arc and run walls straight to the center, you create a private booth. A sector of a circle is the geometric region enclosed by two radii and their intercepted arc. How much velvet rope did that VIP booth get? How much floor space? It depends entirely on whether your calculator is sipping degrees or radians! If you’re in degrees, you’re just paying a percentage of the total club bill. The length of an arc equals the central angle in degrees divided by 360, multiplied by the full circumference of the circle. The same logic applies to the floor space: the area of a circle sector equals the central angle in degrees divided by 360, multiplied by the full area of the circle. But radians? Radians don’t mess with percentages. They are a direct, geometric transaction! In radians, the length of an arc equals the circle radius multiplied by the central angle measured in radians. Bam! And the area? The area of a circle sector equals one-half multiplied by the central angle in radians multiplied by the square of the circle radius. Absolute perfection.

Are you tracking with me? Good, because the perspectives are about to shift, and the exam loves this. Where you stand in the club dictates what you see! Let’s say you are the DJ. You are dead center. A central angle is an angle with its vertex located exactly at the center point of the circle. Because you’re at the center of power, your reality is undistorted. The measure of a central angle is exactly equal to the degree measure of its intercepted arc. 

But what if you’re a wallflower? You are shoved all the way to the back wall of the club. An inscribed angle is an angle with its vertex positioned exactly on the circumference of the circle. You’re looking across the dance floor. The sides of an inscribed angle contain chords of the circle. Because you’ve been pushed back so far, the visual field opens up like a wide-angle lens, meaning the angle itself pinches down! It’s the Inscribed Angle Theorem! The measure of an inscribed angle is exactly half the degree measure of its intercepted arc! 

This is where the drama happens. Think about it! If you and I are both wallflowers standing in different spots on the back wall, but we are both staring jealously at the exact same VIP booth—the exact same arc—our visual angles are identical! Two inscribed angles that intercept the exact same arc are congruent to each other! If you stare at a diameter—the ultimate divider of the room—something terrifyingly beautiful happens. An angle inscribed in a semicircle is always a 90-degree right angle! Every single time! Thales figured that out thousands of years ago, probably while drinking something stronger than what we have. And what if four wallflowers stand on the edge, looking at each other, forming a cyclic quadrilateral? Their sightlines perfectly swallow the entire room. Therefore, the opposite angles of any quadrilateral inscribed in a circle are supplementary! They perfectly add up to 180 degrees to cover their halves of the 360-degree room. 

Okay, let’s talk about the laser tripwires holding this place together. A chord is a straight line segment whose two endpoints both lie on the circumference of the circle. The granddaddy of all lasers, the one the DJ controls? A diameter is a chord that passes directly through the center point of the circle. The center holds ultimate authority over the lasers. If a circle radius that is perpendicular to a chord shoots out, it plays peacemaker. It perfectly bisects that chord. It cuts it exactly in half! And it doesn’t stop there! That exact same circle radius that is perpendicular to a chord perfectly bisects the arc intercepted by that chord! It chops the velvet rope in half, too! 

You can reverse-engineer this to find the DJ! If you just have a random laser wire, the perpendicular bisector of any chord always passes through the center point of the circle! Draw two, see where they cross, and you’ve raided the DJ booth! And if two lasers are perfectly parallel and never cross? They trap the exact same amount of space. Parallel chords within a single circle intercept congruent arcs between the chords. 

But what if the lasers cross *inside* the club? Turf war! They chop each other into pieces! But there is an economic balance to the chaos: for two chords intersecting inside a circle, the product of the segment lengths of one chord equals the product of the segment lengths of the second chord. The math stays perfectly balanced. And the angle they hit each other with? It’s a compromise. The measure of an angle formed by two chords intersecting inside a circle equals half the sum of the measures of the two intercepted arcs. They literally average out their drama! 

Now, finish your beer, because we are going outside to the paparazzi and the getaway drivers. Lines that don’t play by the rules. 

A secant line intersects a circle at exactly two distinct points. This is a paparazzi who crashes through the front door, runs across the dance floor, and busts out the back door. But a tangent line intersects a circle at exactly one distinct point. This is the getaway driver. He just grazes the outside of the building without ever coming inside. 

The spot where the getaway car touches the building is highly unstable. A tangent line is perfectly perpendicular to the circle radius drawn to the point of tangency. That’s a 90-degree angle on the street corner, setting up right triangles for days! And if two getaway drivers start from the exact same dark alleyway to pick up the VIPs? Two tangent line segments drawn to a circle from the exact same exterior point have identical lengths. Perfectly symmetrical escape routes.

When these guys meet in the alleyway outside the club, their angles are bitter. Inside, the chords averaged out their arcs by adding them. Outside? It’s a rivalry. It’s subtraction! It doesn’t matter who is meeting up out there—the rule is mercilessly the same. The measure of an angle formed by two secants intersecting outside a circle equals half the absolute difference of the measures of the intercepted arcs. Got a crasher and a driver? The measure of an angle formed by a secant and a tangent intersecting outside a circle equals half the absolute difference of the measures of the intercepted arcs. Two drivers? The measure of an angle formed by two tangents intersecting outside a circle equals half the absolute difference of the measures of the intercepted arcs! Far arc minus near arc, cut in half. Period. 

There is one freak-accident exception. What if a getaway driver (tangent) and a laser (chord) meet exactly on the velvet rope, right at the door? The measure of an angle formed by a tangent and a chord intersecting at the point of tangency equals half the degree measure of the intercepted arc. It acts exactly like an inscribed angle! Half the arc! 

Finally, the grand heist of the exterior segments! The Power of a Point! Just like the chunks of the chords multiplied perfectly inside the club, the lines multiply perfectly outside, but the rule is "Whole times Outside"! For two secant segments drawn from an exterior point, the product of one entire secant length and its external part equals the product of the second entire secant length and its external part! Whole times Outside equals Whole times Outside! 

But what if one of those lines is the getaway driver? The tangent never goes inside! The entire length *is* the external part! So, for a secant and tangent drawn from an exterior point, the square of the tangent length equals the product of the entire secant length and its external part! Tangent squared equals the Whole times the Outside!
