Right now, you’re staring at adolescent chaos. We tame chaos by trapping it in a mathematical cage called a random variable. Listen to me: a random variable is a variable whose possible values are numerical outcomes of a random phenomenon. It is literally just you, the teacher, pointing at a completely unpredictable event—like how many cell phones you'll confiscate during third period—and attaching a cold, hard number to it. Now, depending on the breed of chaos you are dealing with, this variable takes one of two forms. If you’re counting those confiscated cell phones—0, 1, 2, 3—you are dealing with a discrete random variable, because a discrete random variable has a countable number of possible values. But suppose you are timing how long it takes the class clown to derail your meticulously planned lesson. Time doesn't hop in neat little blocks; it flows. A continuous random variable takes all values in an interval of numbers. It’s an uncountable continuum, like the exact fluid ounce volume of coffee you’ll need to survive until Friday. 

But for the games of chance, the multiple-choice tests, and the survival strategies we’re talking about tonight, we live in the discrete world. Now, to truly map your enemy, you need a blueprint. A probability distribution of a discrete random variable provides the probability of each possible value. Think of it as the ultimate seating chart of destiny. But this chart has ironclad rules, my friend. It’s not the Wild West. Rule number one: every probability assigned to a specific value in a discrete probability distribution must be a number between zero and one, inclusive. You cannot have a negative probability of a student eating a graphing calculator, and you cannot have a 120% chance of it either. You are bound by 0 and 1. Rule number two: the sum of the probabilities of all possible values in a discrete probability distribution must exactly equal one. You have to map the entire universe of possibilities. 

If you want to draw this universe, grab your graphing calculator or just picture stacking sugar cubes right here on this bar. A probability histogram visually represents a discrete probability distribution with the total area of all bars summing exactly to one. Every bar's width and height represents the space that specific outcome occupies in reality. But you can't just slap bars together willy-nilly! Expected value calculations require mutually exclusive and exhaustive outcomes to accurately represent a complete decision scenario. If you’re figuring out the odds of how a kid got a black eye, "playing dodgeball" and "sitting in the library" must be mutually exclusive—they cannot happen at the same exact time. And your list of causes must be exhaustive; every single possible hiding spot in that school must be accounted for! 

Once your distribution is locked in, you can find the center of gravity. You can find the expected value. The expected value of a discrete random variable is the long-run average outcome of a random process repeated infinitely many times. It is the Groundhog Day of teaching! If you teach the exact same lesson to infinite universes of students, what happens on average? The expected value of a discrete random variable is calculated by multiplying each possible value by its probability and adding these products together. It’s a weighted recipe. You take the outcome, multiply it by its chance of happening, and sum the whole glorious mess. 

When you write this down on the board, the symbol used to represent the expected value of a random variable X is E(X). Keep that etched in your skull. And theoretically? The expected value of a random variable is mathematically equivalent to the mean of the random variable. Because they are the same cosmic entity, the Greek letter mu ($\mu$) is conventionally used to denote the expected value or mean of a probability distribution. It is the sacred anchor of statistics. 

Now, here is where your future students will lose their minds, so you better be ready to preach. An expected value of a random variable is not required to be an actual possible outcome of the random variable! If you calculate that the expected number of students who will forget their pencils tomorrow is 2.4, you are never going to see two full students and a disembodied torso begging for a Ticonderoga. You will never observe the outcome of 2.4. But the Law of Large Numbers dictates that the empirical average of independent trials approaches the expected value as the number of trials increases. You might have 1 kid forget today, 4 tomorrow, 2 the next day, but if you teach for thirty years, the average of all those days will lock onto 2.4 with terrifying precision. Nature is noisy today, but she is flawlessly obedient over a lifetime. 

This brings us to the actual witchcraft: the algebra of expectation. Because E(X) is just an average, it behaves beautifully when you manipulate it. Imagine you give a brutal midterm. The expected score is a dismal 40 points. You panic. You decide to curve it by doubling every score and adding 5 bonus points. What happens to the expectation? Listen: for any random variable X and constants a and b, the expected value of aX plus b equals a times the expected value of X, plus b. So $E(aX + b) = aE(X) + b$. You just double the 40 and add 5. Your new expected average is an 85! You don't have to recalculate a single individual probability! It gets better! What if you want to combine the chaos of your 2nd period class with your 3rd period class? For any two random variables X and Y, the expected value of X plus Y equals the expected value of X plus the expected value of Y. $E(X + Y) = E(X) + E(Y)$. Period! Even if the classes secretly plot together, their combined expected mayhem is just the sum of their individual expectations. 

Once you have this power, you transcend teaching. You become a risk-management overlord making decisions under uncertainty. When the principal gives you three different curriculum options, you don’t guess. You build a decision matrix! A decision matrix uses expected values to compare the average long-term payoffs of different available actions under uncertainty. You calculate the EV for Option A, Option B, and Option C, and you pick the winner. 

You’ll use this same logic when the Math Club forces you to design a fundraiser carnival game. Your students will ask if the game is "fair." In the real world, fair means everyone gets a turn. In your world, a mathematically fair game is a game in which the expected net winnings for a player equal exactly zero. Nobody makes money, nobody loses money on average. But you're raising funds for a trip to the state competition! You can't run a fair game! You have to charge them! If a game costs money to play, the expected net profit is calculated as the expected gross payout minus the initial cost of playing. You rig it so the expected gross payout is \$2, but you charge them \$5 to play. The expected net profit is negative \$3 for them, which means a positive \$3 for the Math Club every single time that wheel spins! 

Do you think you’re the only one doing this? Why do you think the district buys liability insurance? Insurance companies determine base policy premiums by calculating the expected value of the policy payout for a given risk class. If there’s a 1% chance a chemistry teacher blows up the lab costing \$100,000, the expected payout is \$1,000. So the insurance company charges the school a \$1,200 premium. They don’t sweat the variance. They don't wake up in cold sweats wondering if the lab will explode tomorrow. They practice risk-neutral decision making, which involves consistently choosing the option with the highest expected value regardless of outcome variance. They are dead inside to the emotional rollercoaster of a single claim because they know the Law of Large Numbers is their silent partner.
