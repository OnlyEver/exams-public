Let’s talk about the teacher’s lounge. The lounge isn't just a room; it’s a living matrix of gossip and survival mechanics. We are going to map it. When you track two categorical variables—characteristics that place individuals into specific groups—collected from the exact same set of subjects, you don't just make a list. You build a cage. A two-way frequency table displays the frequencies of those two categorical variables collected from that same set of subjects. 

Imagine we are categorizing fifty teachers. Variable one: Subject Taught (Math versus English). Variable two: Caffeine Source (Energy Drinks versus Black Coffee). You draw the grid. Look at those inner body cells of the table. Those entries are called joint frequencies! They are the specific intersections of madness. Joint frequencies represent the exact number of subjects that share a specific characteristic from *each* of the two categorical variables simultaneously. If you have eight individuals trapped in the cell for "Math" and "Energy Drinks," that is your joint frequency. Those eight poor souls share both traits. 

But what about the outer edges of your table? The sums of the individual rows and individual columns are called marginal frequencies. They live in the margins! They strip away the messy overlap and just tell you the total number of Math teachers, or the total number of Energy Drink chuggers, regardless of any other factor. And anchored in the bottom right corner is the grand total. The grand total is the sum of all joint frequencies—it’s the entire population of the lounge, all fifty teachers, trapped in your study.

But raw numbers are garbage for comparing things, especially if the drama is unevenly split. We need proportions! We need relative frequency, which is just the ratio of a specific frequency to the grand total. If you want a joint relative frequency, you calculate it by dividing a joint frequency by the grand total. Eight Math/Energy Drinkers divided by fifty total teachers? Boom, 16%. If you want a marginal relative frequency, you calculate it by dividing a marginal frequency by the grand total. Twenty-five total Math teachers divided by fifty? 50%.

But rookie, here is where the heist movie twist happens. You don’t always want to look at the whole school. Sometimes, you want to interrogate a specific crew. You want a conditional relative frequency, which is calculated by dividing a joint frequency by a marginal frequency! You completely ignore the grand total. You lock the doors to the Math department. You restrict your denominator solely to the twenty-five Math teachers (the marginal frequency) and look at the eight who drink Energy drinks (the joint frequency). Eight divided by twenty-five! Conditional relative frequencies show the distribution of one categorical variable among a specific subgroup of another categorical variable! You are asking, "Under the *condition* that a teacher teaches Math, what is their caffeine distribution?"

Why do we do this? To uncover conspiracy! An association exists between two categorical variables if the conditional relative frequencies differ significantly across categories. If 80% of Math teachers drink energy drinks, but only 10% of English teachers do, those variables are associated! The department you work in heavily influences your beverage! But what if the conditional relative frequencies are approximately equal across categories? What if it's 40% for Math and 41% for English? That means independence! Independence between two categorical variables means knowing their department gives you absolutely zero predictive power about their drink. They operate totally independently.

Now, finish that drink. We are leaving the categorical tables behind and entering the realm of hard, numerical measurements. We are talking about quantitative bivariate data, and to map that, we use a scatterplot. A scatterplot displays quantitative bivariate data as individual points plotted on a Cartesian coordinate plane. Every dot is a student holding two numerical secrets.

Axis placement is everything, it is the setup and the punchline! The explanatory variable is typically plotted on the horizontal axis of a scatterplot. This is the instigator. The thing doing the explaining. The response variable is typically plotted on the vertical axis. This is the fallout. If we plot "Hours Spent Playing Video Games" on the horizontal, we plot the response, "Score on the Midterm," on the vertical.

When you stare at a scatterplot, you are looking for the macro-structure in the constellation of dots. First, look at the direction. A positive association in a scatterplot occurs when the response variable increases as the explanatory variable increases. The dots climb upward from left to right! The more hours you study, the higher your grade. Simple! A negative association occurs when the response variable decreases as the explanatory variable increases. The dots slide downward. The more video games played, the lower the grade! 

Next, look at the shape. The form of an association in a scatterplot can be described as linear or nonlinear. A linear association is indicated when the plotted points cluster around a straight line. They march together like a coordinated army. A nonlinear association is indicated when the plotted points cluster around a curve—like a parabola, mirroring the trajectory of a dodgeball hitting the bleachers. 

And how tight is that army marching? That’s your strength. The strength of an association in a scatterplot is determined by how closely the plotted points follow a specific mathematical form. If they form a razor-sharp, unmistakable line, the strength is massive. If they look like someone sneezed pepper onto a piece of grid paper, the association is weak. 

Watch out for the anomalies, though! An outlier in a scatterplot is a rogue data point that falls substantially outside the overall pattern of the other data points. It’s the kid who played forty hours of video games and still scored a 100%. They defy the trend! And keep an eye out for gangs. A cluster in a scatterplot is a distinct grouping of data points that are located close together. It usually means some unmeasured third variable—like a secret study group—is skewing your results in one specific pocket of the graph.

Visuals are great, but you are a mathematician! You need to quantify the chaos! Correlation measures the strength and direction of a relationship between two quantitative variables. And the king of these measurements is the correlation coefficient, usually called `$r$`. The correlation coefficient measures the strength and direction of a *linear* relationship between two quantitative variables. 

This number is trapped in a mathematical cage. The correlation coefficient is always a value between negative one and positive one inclusive. It cannot escape those bounds! 

A correlation coefficient of exactly positive one indicates a perfect positive linear relationship. Every single point falls flawlessly on an upward-sloping straight line. A correlation coefficient of exactly negative one indicates a perfect negative linear relationship. A flawless, downward-sloping straight line! And a correlation coefficient of zero? A correlation coefficient of zero indicates that there is no linear relationship between the two quantitative variables whatsoever. It is statistical static. 

But here is the ultimate rule, rookie. The thing you will scream from the rooftops until your voice gives out. A strong association between two variables in a scatterplot does NOT prove that one variable causes changes in the other! You might find a massive positive correlation between the number of band-aids sold at the school nurse's office and the number of melted popsicles on the sidewalk. Does buying a band-aid cause a popsicle to melt? NO! Causation indicates that an event or change in one variable directly produces a corresponding change in another variable. In this case, a hidden third variable—a 100-degree heatwave causing kids to trip on the blacktop and their popsicles to melt—is causing both!
