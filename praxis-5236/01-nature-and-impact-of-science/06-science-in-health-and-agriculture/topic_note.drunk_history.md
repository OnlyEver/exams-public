You’re about to walk into a high school biology classroom to teach, which means you are about to step onto the front lines of literal, unhinged biological warfare. You think managing a bunch of feral teenagers is just pedagogy? No! It is population genetics, it is virology, it is a heist movie happening at the cellular level! 

Let’s start with the rumor mill. Tracking a nasty rumor in a high school is exactly how we track diseases, and that brings us to the ultimate hallway monitor of history. You remember John Snow, right? Not the guy with the sword—the physician. John Snow mapped cholera cases in London in 1854. Back then, everyone thought diseases were caused by "miasma" or bad air, basically the Victorian equivalent of blaming a bad vibe. But by tracking the exact locations of the victims, John Snow's 1854 cholera mapping demonstrated that cholera is a waterborne disease. He found the contaminated Broad Street pump, took the handle off, and saved the neighborhood. 

What Snow did was lay the foundation for a whole new field. Epidemiology is the study of the distribution and determinants of health-related states or events in specified populations. It’s the science of who is getting caught passing notes, where they are sitting, and why. Now, if you’re tracking this chaos, you need metrics. Disease incidence measures the number of new disease cases in a specific population over a defined time period. That’s how many *new* kids got sent to detention this Tuesday. But disease prevalence measures the total number of existing disease cases in a specific population at a given point in time. That is the absolute, teeming mass of sweaty teenagers currently sitting in the detention hall right now, regardless of when they got there. 

You also have to categorize the scale of the drama. An endemic disease is constantly present at a baseline level within a specific geographic area or population—like the low-level, permanent cloud of freshmen body spray in the gym hallway. You expect it. But an epidemic occurs when there is a sudden increase in the number of disease cases above what is normally expected in a population. Suddenly, half the sophomore class has mono! Chaos! And if they take that mono on a band trip overseas? A pandemic is an epidemic that has spread over several countries or continents.

To know if you’re going to survive the school year, you calculate the math of the outbreak. The basic reproduction number, R0, represents the average number of secondary infections produced by one infected individual in a fully susceptible population. If that one kid with a TikTok dance obsession infects three more kids, your R0 is 3. An R0 greater than 1 indicates that an infectious disease outbreak is actively spreading and growing in a population. You are losing control of the classroom! But if you lock it down, an R0 less than 1 indicates that an infectious disease outbreak is declining within a population. The trend is dying.

How do you get that number down without locking everyone in their lockers? Herd immunity! Herd immunity occurs when a sufficiently high proportion of a population becomes immune to an infectious disease. It’s like when enough kids in the group project actually understand the assignment, so the whole group doesn't fail. Because the strong carry the weak, herd immunity provides indirect protection to vulnerable individuals who are not immune to the infectious disease.

But how do we make them immune before the test? Pop quizzes! Practice runs! Vaccines! Vaccines stimulate the adaptive immune system to produce memory B cells and memory T cells. These are your upperclassmen. They’ve seen the trick questions before. Once generated, memory B cells and memory T cells enable a rapid secondary immune response upon future exposure to a previously encountered pathogen. The body just looks at the invader and says, "Oh, I remember you from 2019, get out."

We have different ways to prep the kids. Live-attenuated vaccines contain a weakened form of the living pathogen. You give the immune system a slightly easier, dizzy version of the real threat. Inactivated vaccines contain a pathogen that has been killed using chemicals, heat, or radiation. Handing out a dead, canceled exam from last year. Subunit vaccines include only specific, isolated antigens from a pathogen to stimulate an immune response. You just hand them the hardest essay prompt and say, "Study this one shape." Toxoid vaccines use an inactivated toxin produced by a pathogen to elicit an immune response. You aren't studying the bacteria; you're studying the poison it makes—like preparing for the stress sweat of the exam, not the exam itself. 

Then you have the ultimate biological heist: the mRNA platform. Messenger RNA (mRNA) vaccines deliver a synthetic mRNA transcript enveloped in a lipid nanoparticle into host cells. Picture slipping a highly classified, fake cheat sheet tucked inside a slick, greasy manila envelope directly into a student's desk. Once that envelope is opened inside the cell, host cells use vaccine-delivered mRNA to translate and display a specific viral antigen on the cell surface. The host cell reads the cheat sheet and builds a dummy target, literally training the immune system using the body's own 3D printers!

But what if you want to completely rewrite the school curriculum from scratch? Welcome to agriculture and biotechnology! Recombinant DNA technology involves combining DNA molecules from different species into a single hybrid molecule. You’re taking the permanent record of a theater kid and taping it to the file of a mathlete. When you do this, you create transgenic organisms, which contain genetic material into which DNA from an unrelated organism has been artificially introduced. 

Plants are stubborn, though. They have thick cell walls. You need a manipulative guidance counselor to sneak the new files in. That’s where our friend *Agro* comes in. Agrobacterium tumefaciens is a soil bacterium naturally capable of transferring genetic material into plant cells. It evolved to do this! So scientists hijack it. Agrobacterium tumefaciens transfers a portion of its Tumor-inducing (Ti) plasmid into the plant cell genome. We just swap the tumor instructions for something useful. And if the subtle approach fails? We grab the megaphone! A gene gun uses microparticles of gold or tungsten coated with DNA to physically shoot genetic material into plant cells. Literal microscopic golden bullets of knowledge!

And what are we teaching these plants? Self-defense! Bacillus thuringiensis (Bt) is a soil bacterium that naturally produces crystalline proteins toxic to certain insect larvae. Scientists ripped that chapter out and made Bt crops, which are genetically modified agricultural plants engineered to express the Bacillus thuringiensis toxin. When the school bully—the caterpillar—tries to take a bite of the leaf, the expressed Bacillus thuringiensis toxin causes pore formation and cell lysis in the insect midgut. His stomach literally explodes! It's brutal! It's beautiful! 

We also teach them to ignore the dress code. Roundup Ready crops are genetically modified to express resistance to the broad-spectrum herbicide glyphosate. Normally, glyphosate inhibits an enzyme essential for the synthesis of aromatic amino acids in plants. It starves them of the building blocks of life. But Roundup Ready crops have a mutant enzyme. You can spray the whole field, nuke the weeds, and the crops just stand there wearing sunglasses. 

We can even make plants into multivitamin gummy bears. Golden Rice is a genetically modified rice variety engineered to produce beta-carotene. Why rice? Because beta-carotene is a metabolic precursor to Vitamin A. By slipping that trait into a staple crop, we can cure nutritional blindness in kids around the world!

But listen—the kids always adapt. If you keep using the same punishments, they find loopholes. The continuous use of specific herbicides on genetically modified crops acts as a selective pressure leading to herbicide-resistant weeds. You've bred super-weeds! Furthermore, if every kid in the school thinks exactly the same way, one weird rumor destroys the whole student body. Widespread planting of identical genetically modified crops reduces overall agricultural genetic diversity. And in biology, uniformity is a death sentence, because reduced agricultural genetic diversity increases crop vulnerability to novel pathogens and changing environmental conditions. 

Now, let's say you do find the perfect, flawless student. You want a whole classroom of them. Let's talk cloning! Reproductive cloning aims to produce an entire genetically identical living organism. The rockstar of this was a sheep. Dolly the sheep was the first mammal successfully cloned from an adult somatic cell. Dolly the sheep was cloned in 1996 using Somatic Cell Nuclear Transfer. 

How do you do that? Somatic Cell Nuclear Transfer (SCNT) is a laboratory technique for creating an ovum with a donor nucleus. Picture this absolute coup d'etat: in Somatic Cell Nuclear Transfer, the nucleus of a somatic cell is injected into an enucleated egg cell. You suck the brain out of the gym teacher's classroom and inject the fully-formed brain of the valedictorian into that empty space! 

But usually, we don't want a whole clone; we just want the raw potential to fix broken parts. Therapeutic cloning aims to produce embryonic stem cells for medical treatments and physiological research. And stem cells are categorized by their potential, just like students. Embryonic stem cells are pluripotent. They are the incoming freshmen. They can be anything! Pluripotent stem cells can differentiate into any cell type belonging to the three embryonic germ layers. But adult stem cells are multipotent. They are the seniors. They've already picked a major. Multipotent stem cells can only differentiate into a limited number of cell types within a specific tissue or organ system. A blood stem cell can only become blood; it can't suddenly decide to be a brain cell.

Here is the massive reality TV drama of the science world, though: deriving human embryonic stem cells traditionally requires the destruction of a human blastocyst. That is a massive PR and moral issue. The destruction of human embryos for stem cell derivation presents a major ethical controversy in medical research. 

So, scientists found a cheat code to rewind time. Induced pluripotent stem cells (iPSCs) are adult somatic cells genetically reprogrammed to an embryonic stem cell-like state. You take a burned-out senior and biologically brainwash them until they think they are a freshman again! And the best part? Induced pluripotent stem cells bypass the primary ethical concerns associated with destroying human embryos. 

Finally, let's talk about the ultimate red pen: CRISPR. Gene therapy involves the introduction, removal, or alteration of a person's genetic code to treat or cure a disease. The heavy artillery here is bacterial. CRISPR-Cas9 is a genome-editing tool adapted from a naturally occurring bacterial immune system. The Cas9 enzyme acts as molecular scissors to cut DNA at a specific location guided by a synthetic RNA molecule. It finds the typo and snips it out. 

But what if the teacher grading the paper is exhausted and makes a mistake? CRISPR-Cas9 off-target effects occur when the Cas9 enzyme introduces DNA cuts at unintended genomic locations. And a sloppy red pen in DNA is fatal. Off-target genetic edits in human patients can potentially cause cellular toxicity or induce cancer-causing mutations. 

The ethics entirely depend on *whose* paper you are editing. Somatic gene editing introduces specific genetic changes into non-reproductive patient cells. You fix the kid's homework today. Because of this, genetic modifications made through somatic gene editing are not passed on to the patient's offspring. But if you rewrite the district's foundational charter? That's germline editing. Germline gene editing introduces direct genetic changes into human reproductive cells. Meaning, genetic modifications made through germline gene editing are permanently heritable by all future offspring. You change the human lineage forever!

Add IVF to the mix, and the PTA moms go wild. Preimplantation genetic diagnosis (PGD) allows the screening of embryos for specific genetic disorders prior to in vitro fertilization implantation. Originally, it was just to stop terrible diseases. But humans are greedy. The potential use of genetic engineering to select desirable non-medical traits in humans forms the basis of the designer baby ethical dilemma. "Can we make him taller? Can we give him green eyes?" 

With all this DNA reading and rewriting, you’d think the school board would use it against you, right? Fire you because your DNA says you might get sick in ten years? Thankfully, the superintendent stepped in. The Genetic Information Nondiscrimination Act (GINA) is a United States federal law passed in 2008. Listen closely: the Genetic Information Nondiscrimination Act prevents health insurers and employers from discriminating against individuals based on their genetic information.
