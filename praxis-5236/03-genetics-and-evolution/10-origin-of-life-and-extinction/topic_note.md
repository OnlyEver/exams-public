To understand the [history of life on Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_life) is to grapple with a profound [thermodynamic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamics) paradox: how did a chaotic, sterile sphere of cooling rock spontaneously organize itself into a thriving [biosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere)? As educators preparing to guide students through the grand narrative of [biology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology), you must be equipped to explain not just the mechanics of [cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_%28biology%29) and [ecosystems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem), but their ultimate origins and their abrupt, sometimes violent, terminations. The story of life is characterized by two opposing forces: the relentless, creative drive of [biochemical evolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_evolution), and the catastrophic pruning shears of [extinction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction). By tracing the journey from the first [abiotic synthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abiogenesis) of [organic molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_compound) to the human-driven [ecological crises](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecological_crisis) of the present day, we can provide students with a cohesive framework for understanding both the resilience and the extreme fragility of life.

## The Chemical Origins of Life

Before we can teach [biology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology), we must briefly teach [prebiotic chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abiogenesis). When your students look at a [living cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_%28biology%29), they see an incredibly complex machine. The question they will inevitably ask is, *how did the machine build itself?* 

The foundational concept here is **[abiotic synthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abiogenesis)**, which is the formation of [organic molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_compound) from non-living [inorganic precursors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inorganic_compound). We are looking for the bridge between [geochemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geochemistry) and [biochemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biochemistry).

### The Primordial Soup and the Reducing Atmosphere
In the 1920s, scientists [A.I. Oparin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_Oparin) and [J.B.S. Haldane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J._B._S._Haldane) independently proposed what we now call the **[Oparin-Haldane hypothesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primordial_soup)**. This model proposes that life arose gradually from [inorganic molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inorganic_compound) in a "[primordial soup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primordial_soup)." But this soup required a very specific kind of kitchen.

The **hypothesized [early Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early_Earth) [atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth) lacked [free oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen)**. This is a critical point to emphasize in your classroom, as students often assume [oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen) is a prerequisite for life. In reality, early [free oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen) would have destroyed the fragile [chemical bonds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_bond) of developing [organic molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_compound). Instead, scientists hypothesize that the [early Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early_Earth) possessed a **[reducing atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reducing_atmosphere)**, which facilitates the [abiotic synthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abiogenesis) of [organic molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_compound) by providing high-energy [electrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron). 

[Geochemical modeling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geochemical_modeling) suggests that **hypothesized [early Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early_Earth) [atmospheric gases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth) included [water vapor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water_vapor), [methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane), [ammonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia), and [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen)**. It was a volatile, [toxic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toxicity) environment by modern standards, but a perfect incubator for early chemistry.

### The Miller-Urey Chemical Time Machine
In science, [hypotheses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypothesis) demand [empirical testing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empirical_evidence). Enter the **1953 [Miller-Urey experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Miller%E2%80%93Urey_experiment)**, a landmark study that simulated hypothesized [early Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early_Earth) chemical conditions. [Stanley Miller](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stanley_Miller) and [Harold Urey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harold_Urey) constructed a closed glass apparatus containing the hypothesized gases. To provide [activation energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Activation_energy), the **[Miller-Urey experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Miller%E2%80%93Urey_experiment) utilized electrical sparks to simulate [early Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early_Earth) [lightning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lightning)**.

The results were [paradigm-shifting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paradigm_shift): the **[Miller-Urey experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Miller%E2%80%93Urey_experiment) successfully produced [amino acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amino_acid) from [inorganic precursors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inorganic_compound)**. Within days, the water in their apparatus turned a murky brown, rich with the building blocks of [proteins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein). This experiment gives your students a tangible anchor: complex [organic chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_chemistry) can occur spontaneously under the right abiotic conditions.

![Diagram of the Miller-Urey experiment, which simulated early Earth conditions by applying electrical sparks to a mixture of water, methane, ammonia, and hydrogen to spontaneously synthesize amino acids.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Miller-Urey_experiment-en.svg)

### Alternative Origins: The Deep Sea and Deep Space
While the [primordial soup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primordial_soup) model is canonical, modern [biology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology) recognizes other plausible origins that you should introduce to your students:
1. **The [Hydrothermal Vent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrothermal_vent) Hypothesis:** This model posits that life originated deep in the [ocean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ocean) at alkaline vents. The natural thermal and chemical gradients found at these vents could have provided the sustained energy required to drive early [metabolic reactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metabolism), far away from the chaotic surface of the [early Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early_Earth).

![White smoker hydrothermal vents provide a plausible deep-sea origin for life, offering sustained chemical and thermal gradients far from the chaotic conditions of the early Earth's surface.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Champagne_vent_white_smokers.jpg)

2. **[Extraterrestrial Origins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Panspermia):** We must also look to the stars. The **[Murchison meteorite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Murchison_meteorite)**, which fell in [Australia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australia) in 1969, contains **over eighty different naturally occurring [amino acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amino_acid)**. This remarkable fact demonstrates that the [abiotic synthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abiogenesis) of [organic compounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_compound) is not unique to Earth; it is a universal chemical phenomenon happening right now in the freezing [vacuum of space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vacuum).

![A sample of the Murchison meteorite, which contains over eighty naturally occurring amino acids, providing physical evidence that the abiotic synthesis of organic compounds occurs in space.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Murchison_crop.jpg)

## The RNA World: The First Swiss Army Knife of Life

Having organic building blocks is like having a pile of bricks; you still need a blueprint and a bricklayer. In modern [cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_%28biology%29), [DNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNA) stores the blueprint ([genetic information](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genetics)), and [proteins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein) act as the bricklayers ([catalysts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalysis)/[enzymes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enzyme)). But which came first? [DNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNA) needs [proteins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein) to replicate, and [proteins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein) need [DNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNA) for their assembly instructions.

This [chicken-and-egg paradox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chicken_or_the_egg) is neatly solved by the **[RNA world hypothesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RNA_world)**, which proposes that [ribonucleic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RNA) was the first genetic material on Earth. 

Unlike [DNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNA), [RNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RNA) is highly versatile. We see evidence of this today in **[ribozymes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ribozyme)**, which are [RNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RNA) molecules capable of catalyzing specific biochemical reactions. Therefore, **[RNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RNA) possesses the dual ability to store [genetic information](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genetics) and catalyze biochemical reactions**. 

> **Key Pedagogical Framing:** Teach [RNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RNA) as the ultimate biological "[Swiss Army knife](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swiss_Army_knife)" of the [early Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early_Earth). Because it could both hold the [genetic code](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genetic_code) and perform the catalytic work to replicate that code, **the dual functionality of [RNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RNA) supports the [RNA world hypothesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RNA_world)**.

![The RNA world hypothesis posits that early life relied on RNA for both storing genetic information and catalyzing biochemical reactions, prior to the evolutionary shift toward DNA and proteins.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Etls-2019-0024c.01.png)

Eventually, this highly versatile molecule specialized. As life evolved, a [division of labor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_of_labour) proved far more efficient:
* **[DNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNA) later replaced [RNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RNA) as the primary genetic material due to the greater [chemical stability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_stability) of [DNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNA).** (Its [double-helix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleic_acid_double_helix) structure protects the [nucleotide bases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleobase)).
* **[Proteins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein) later replaced [RNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RNA) as primary biological [catalysts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalysis) due to the greater [structural diversity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein_structure) of [proteins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein).** (Twenty [amino acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amino_acid) fold into vastly more complex shapes than four [RNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RNA) [nucleotides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleotide)).

| Function | [Early Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early_Earth) ([RNA World](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RNA_world)) | Modern Life | Evolutionary Reason for Shift |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Information Storage** | [RNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RNA) | [DNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNA) | [DNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNA) offers **greater [chemical stability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_stability)**. |
| **Catalysis / Machinery** | [RNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RNA) ([Ribozymes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ribozyme)) | [Proteins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein) | [Proteins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein) offer **greater [structural diversity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein_structure)**. |

## Biological Terraforming: The Oxygen Crisis

Once life was established, it began to change the planet. **Earth's [earliest life forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms) were [anaerobic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anaerobic_organism) [prokaryotes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prokaryote)**, existing solely in environments without [oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen). 

The most profound environmental shift in Earth's history was biologically driven. **[Cyanobacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyanobacteria) were the first organisms to evolve the ability to perform [oxygenic photosynthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photosynthesis)**. By splitting [water molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Properties_of_water) to capture [electrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron), they released [oxygen gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen) as a waste product. 

Over millions of years, the **proliferation of early [cyanobacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyanobacteria) caused a massive global increase in [atmospheric oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geological_history_of_oxygen)**, an episode known as the **[Great Oxygenation Event](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Oxidation_Event), which occurred approximately 2.4 billion years ago**. 

![The progressive build-up of atmospheric oxygen was driven by the proliferation of photosynthetic cyanobacteria during the Great Oxygenation Event.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Oxygenation-atm.svg)

How do we know this? We can read the rocks. **[Banded iron formations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Banded_iron_formation) in [sedimentary rocks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sedimentary_rock) provide geological evidence of early biological [oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen) production.** As [oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen) dissolved in the [oceans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ocean), it reacted with dissolved iron, causing it to precipitate out as [iron oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron_oxide) ([rust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rust)) and settle on the [seafloor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seabed). The Earth essentially rusted.

![Alternating layers of iron oxide in banded iron formations provide the geological record of Earth's early oceans "rusting" as cyanobacteria produced the first free oxygen.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Banded_Iron_Formation_Barberton.jpg)

### The Consequences of Oxygen
[Oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen) is highly [reactive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reactivity_%28chemistry%29). For the organisms of the time, the **rapid accumulation of [atmospheric oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth) was [toxic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toxicity) to many early [anaerobic organisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anaerobic_organism)**, causing what was likely Earth's first [mass extinction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_event).

However, this [oxygen crisis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Oxidation_Event) had a silver lining that made our existence possible. As [oxygen gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen) (O₂) floated into the [upper atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stratosphere), [UV light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultraviolet) split it, allowing it to recombine as [ozone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ozone) (O₃). Therefore, the **accumulation of [atmospheric oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth) directly led to the formation of the [stratospheric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stratosphere) [ozone layer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ozone_layer)**.

Before the [ozone layer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ozone_layer), the land was blasted by lethal [radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiation). The **newly formed [ozone layer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ozone_layer) protected Earth's surface from lethal [ultraviolet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultraviolet) [solar radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunlight).** Ultimately, the **protection provided by the [ozone layer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ozone_layer) allowed [early life forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms) to safely colonize terrestrial environments.** 

![The formation of the stratospheric ozone layer from atmospheric oxygen blocked lethal ultraviolet radiation, enabling life to emerge from the oceans and colonize land.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Ozone_altitude_UV_graph.svg)

## The Fragility of Life: Drivers of Extinction

Having explored how life took hold, we must explore how it is lost. In [biology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology), **[extinction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction) is the complete permanent disappearance of a biological [species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Species) from Earth.** It is a certainty for most [lineages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lineage_%28evolution%29); it is estimated that 99% of all [species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Species) that have ever lived are now [extinct](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction).

When teaching this, it is crucial to distinguish between the two tempos of [extinction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction):
1. **[Background extinction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Background_extinction_rate) is the standard ongoing rate of [extinction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction) that occurs gradually over [geological time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geologic_time_scale)** as [species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Species) fail to [adapt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adaptation) to normal environmental changes.
2. **A [mass extinction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_event) is a rapid widespread decrease in the [biodiversity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity) on Earth.** 

### Natural Drivers and The "Big Five"
The **[geological record](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geologic_record) identifies [five major natural mass extinction events](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_event) in Earth's history.** These events are the great reset buttons of [ecology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecology), clearing away dominant [lineages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lineage_%28evolution%29) and opening [ecological niches](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecological_niche) for new [evolutionary radiations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evolutionary_radiation).

![The geological record tracks changes in biodiversity over time, clearly showing the "Big Five" mass extinction events as sharp declines in the number of surviving taxonomic groups.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Sepkoski_1997_extinction_graphs.png)

When probing the causes, **sudden global [climate change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change) is a primary natural driver of [mass extinction events](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_event).** When the climate shifts faster than populations can [adapt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adaptation) or [migrate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Animal_migration), [food webs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food_web) collapse. Additionally, geological shifts causing **extreme [sea-level](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sea_level) drops can lead to the [extinction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction) of shallow [marine species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marine_life) by eliminating [coastal habitats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marine_habitat)**—the highly productive [continental shelves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continental_shelf).

Two of the "Big Five" are essential case studies for your students:

#### 1. The Permian Mass Extinction (The Great Dying)
 Occurring roughly 252 million years ago, this was life's closest brush with total annihilation. The **[Permian mass extinction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permian%E2%80%93Triassic_extinction_event) eliminated approximately 96 percent of all [marine species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marine_life) on Earth**. 
* **The Cause:** **Massive [volcanic eruptions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volcanism) in the [Siberian Traps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siberian_Traps) are the leading hypothesized cause of the [Permian mass extinction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permian%E2%80%93Triassic_extinction_event).** These eruptions pumped monumental quantities of [carbon dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_dioxide) into the [atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth), leading to extreme [global warming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_warming) and [ocean acidification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ocean_acidification).

#### 2. The Cretaceous-Paleogene (K-Pg) Extinction Event
Occurring roughly 66 million years ago, the **[Cretaceous-Paleogene extinction event](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cretaceous%E2%80%93Paleogene_extinction_event) resulted in the complete loss of all non-avian [dinosaurs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dinosaur).** 
* **The Cause:** A **massive [asteroid impact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Impact_event) is the widely accepted primary cause of the [Cretaceous-Paleogene extinction event](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cretaceous%E2%80%93Paleogene_extinction_event)**. 
* **The Evidence:** How do we prove a rock hit the Earth 66 million years ago? **An anomalous global geological layer of [iridium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iridium) provides physical evidence for the [Cretaceous-Paleogene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cretaceous%E2%80%93Paleogene_extinction_event) [asteroid impact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Impact_event).** [Iridium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iridium) is rare on [Earth's crust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_crust) but common in [meteorites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteorite). The "[iridium anomaly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iridium_anomaly)" in the [K-Pg boundary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cretaceous%E2%80%93Paleogene_boundary) layer is the unmistakable signature of extraterrestrial impact dust settling over the globe.

![The Cretaceous-Paleogene (K-Pg) boundary layer, indicated by the red arrow, contains a global iridium anomaly serving as the physical fingerprint of the asteroid impact that wiped out non-avian dinosaurs.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/K-T_boundary.jpg)

## The Sixth Extinction: The Human Era

[Biology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology) is not just the study of the deep past; it is the study of the urgent present. The **currently elevated rate of global [species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Species) [extinction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction) is scientifically referred to as the [Holocene extinction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holocene_extinction)**. Unlike the previous five, which were driven by [volcanism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volcanism) or [asteroids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asteroid), the sixth [mass extinction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_event) is being driven by a single biological [species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Species): *[Homo sapiens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human)*.

![The global biomass of wild land mammals has plummeted by an estimated 85% since the emergence of humans, highlighting the severe anthropogenic impact defining the Holocene extinction.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Decline-of-the-worlds-wild-mammals.png)

To help students conceptualize [anthropogenic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment) [extinction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction) drivers, frame them around specific ecological disruptions:

* **[Habitat Destruction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habitat_destruction):** This is the most critical factor. **[Habitat destruction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habitat_destruction) is the primary human-caused driver of modern [species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Species) [extinctions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction).** [Deforestation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deforestation), [urbanization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urbanization), and [agricultural expansion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agricultural_expansion) physically erase the [ecosystems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem) [species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Species) rely upon.
* **[Invasive Species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invasive_species):** When human [global trade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_trade) transports organisms across oceans, **[invasive species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invasive_species) drive native [extinctions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction) by outcompeting [endemic species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endemism) for limited biological resources**, or by introducing novel [predators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Predation) and [diseases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disease) to naive populations.
* **[Overharvesting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Overexploitation):** Whether it is [industrial fishing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commercial_fishing), [poaching](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poaching), or [logging](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logging), **[overharvesting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Overexploitation) directly reduces [species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Species) [population sizes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Population_size) to unsustainably low levels**, pushing them below the [minimum viable population](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minimum_viable_population) needed to recover.
* **Rapid Global [Climate Change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change):** Unlike natural climate fluctuations, **human-induced global [climate change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change) causes rapid habitat shifts that outpace the [adaptation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adaptation) capabilities of many [species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Species).** Animals cannot evolve new thermal tolerances or [migrate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Animal_migration) to higher [latitudes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latitude) fast enough to survive the warming trend.
* **[Pollution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pollution):** From [microplastics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microplastics) to [agricultural runoff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_runoff) creating oceanic [dead zones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dead_zone_%28ecology%29), **[anthropogenic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment) [pollution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pollution) introduces [toxic substances](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toxicity) that directly reduce the survivability and [reproductive success](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reproductive_success) of wildlife populations.** 

### Synthesizing Origin and Extinction for the Classroom

As you prepare to lead your [biology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology) classrooms, remember that the [origin of life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abiogenesis) and the [extinction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction) of life are fundamentally connected through the environment. Early [microbes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism) engineered our [atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth), creating the [ozone shield](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ozone_layer) that allowed us to exist. Today, [humans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human) are rapidly engineering the [atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth) and the [biosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere) in ways that are driving unprecedented [ecological collapse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecological_collapse). By teaching the deep history of [abiotic synthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abiogenesis), the [RNA world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RNA_world), and [mass extinctions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_event), you give your students the exact perspective they need to understand their own place in Earth's ongoing [biological history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_life).
