Drop a handful of [elemental](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_element) [carbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon), [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen), [oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen), and [nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen) onto a laboratory bench, and you have nothing but a quiet, inert pile of dust and [gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas). Yet, if you arrange those exact same [atoms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atom) according to a specific set of rules governing [electron sharing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covalent_bond) and [electrostatic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_geometry), you construct a system capable of [metabolizing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metabolism), [dividing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_division), and eventually looking back at itself to investigate how it works. The leap from dead [chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemistry) to living [biology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology) is entirely a function of [molecular architecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_structure). For an aspiring [biology teacher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology_education) preparing to illuminate this subject for [secondary students](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Secondary_education), mastering these [atomic relationships](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_bond) is not about memorizing trivia; it is about grasping the [engineering principles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering) of [life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life). If you cannot explain the [polarity of water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_polarity) or the [folding of a protein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein_folding), you cannot explain [cellular respiration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cellular_respiration), [genetics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genetics), or [ecology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecology). Every [biological process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biological_process) you will ever teach is, at its core, a [chemical event](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_reaction).

## The Atomic Rules of Engagement: Bonds and Carbon

Before we can build a [cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_%28biology%29), we must understand how atoms stick together. The behavior of an atom is dictated by its [electrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron), specifically those in its [outermost shell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Valence_electron). 

> **[Covalent bonds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covalent_bond)** form when two atoms share pairs of electrons to achieve a [stable electron configuration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_configuration). 

![Lewis structure of methane ($CH_4$) illustrating nonpolar covalent bonds, where a central carbon atom equally shares its four valence electrons with four surrounding hydrogen atoms.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Covalent.svg)

When your future students ask why some [molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecule) are [stable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_stability) while others are [reactive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reactivity_%28chemistry%29), point them to the [symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry) of this sharing. **[Nonpolar covalent bonds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covalent_bond)** involve the equal sharing of electrons between atoms. However, atoms are not all equally "greedy" for electrons. When atoms have different [electronegativities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronegativity)—different inherent pulls on electrons—they form **[polar covalent bonds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_polarity)**, which involve the unequal sharing of electrons due to differences in atomic electronegativity. 

If the electronegativity difference is so severe that sharing is completely abandoned, one atom steals an electron entirely. The resulting **[ionic bonds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ionic_bonding)** result from the [electrostatic attraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrostatics) between oppositely charged [ions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ion).

![The crystal lattice structure of sodium chloride (table salt). Ionic bonds form rigid inorganic structures through the electrostatic attraction between positively charged sodium ions and negatively charged chloride ions.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/NaCl_octahedra.svg)

But the most important chemical division you must teach is the line between the [organic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_chemistry) and the [inorganic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inorganic_chemistry). **[Inorganic molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inorganic_compound) generally lack [carbon-hydrogen bonds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon%E2%80%93hydrogen_bond).** [Water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water), [carbon dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_dioxide), and [sodium chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride) fall into this category. Conversely, **[organic molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_compound) are defined by the presence of carbon-hydrogen bonds**. 

Why [carbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon)? Why is life [carbon-based](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon-based_life) rather than [silicon-based](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypothetical_types_of_biochemistry)? Because a single carbon atom has four [valence electrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Valence_electron). Consequently, carbon atoms can form up to four covalent bonds with other atoms. This allows carbon to act as the ultimate molecular intersection, forming [long chains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polymer), [branched structures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Branching_%28polymer_chemistry%29), and complex [rings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclic_compound). It is the [Lego](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lego) brick of the [universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe), offering infinite [geometric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry) possibilities.

![A Lewis dot diagram of a carbon atom highlighting its four valence electrons. This unique configuration allows carbon to form up to four simultaneous covalent bonds, acting as the highly versatile primary building block of organic life.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Carbone_lewis.svg)

## The Asymmetry of Water: The Matrix of Life

When you look at a photograph of [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth) from [space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_space), you are looking at a watery world. Life evolved in water, and every living cell is essentially a microscopic bag of water. To understand biology, you must understand the strange, counterintuitive [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics) of [$H_2O$](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Properties_of_water).

A [water molecule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water_model) consists of two hydrogen atoms covalently bonded to one oxygen atom. However, oxygen is more electronegative than hydrogen. It hoards the shared electrons. This unequal sharing of electrons in a water molecule creates a [polar molecule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_polarity). Because electrons spend more of their time buzzing around the oxygen [nucleus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_nucleus), the oxygen atom in a water molecule carries a [partial negative charge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_charge), while the hydrogen atoms in a water molecule carry partial positive charges.

![An electrostatic potential map of a water molecule. Oxygen's high electronegativity draws shared electrons closer, creating a partial negative charge (red) near the oxygen atom and partial positive charges (blue) near the hydrogen atoms.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Water-elpot-transparent-3D-balls.png)

This polarity sets the stage for a critical phenomenon: the [hydrogen bond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_bond). 

> **[Hydrogen bonds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_bond)** are weak attractions between a highly electronegative atom and a hydrogen atom covalently bonded to another electronegative atom. 

In liquid water, hydrogen bonds form between the partially positive hydrogen of one water molecule and the partially negative oxygen of an adjacent water molecule. Though individually weak, in their trillions, these bonds give water its almost magical [emergent properties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emergence).

![Hydrogen bonding between water molecules. The partially positive hydrogen atoms of one molecule are attracted to the partially negative oxygen atoms of adjacent molecules, establishing a vast network of weak bonds.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/3D_model_hydrogen_bonds_in_water.svg)

### The Emergent Properties of Water

When you are at the [whiteboard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whiteboard), you can group the consequences of water's hydrogen bonding into functional categories for your students:

**1. Sticking Together (Cohesion and Adhesion)**
*   **[Cohesion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cohesion_%28chemistry%29)** is the attraction between individual water molecules due to hydrogen bonding. This is why water forms [droplets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drop_%28liquid%29) rather than spreading into an infinitely thin film.
*   **[Surface tension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_tension)** in water is caused by the cohesive forces of hydrogen bonds at the liquid surface, creating a "skin" strong enough to support a [water strider](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water_strider) insect.
*   **[Adhesion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adhesion)** is the attraction between water molecules and other polar or charged surfaces.
*   **[Capillary action](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capillary_action)** occurs when the adhesive forces between water and a surface are stronger than the cohesive forces between water molecules. This is the exact mechanism that allows 300-foot-tall [redwood trees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sequoia_sempervirens) to pull water from their [roots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Root) to their highest [leaves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leaf) without a mechanical [pump](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pump).

**2. Temperature Moderation**
*   Water has a remarkably high [specific heat capacity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Specific_heat_capacity) compared to most other liquids. This high specific heat capacity of water allows [aquatic environments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aquatic_ecosystem) to absorb significant heat energy without large changes in [temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperature). It is why coastal cities have milder climates and why ocean temperatures remain stable enough to sustain sensitive [marine ecosystems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marine_ecosystem).
*   Water requires a large amount of energy to change from a liquid to a [gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas). When we [sweat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perspiration), we rely on **[evaporative cooling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evaporative_cooling)**, which occurs because the highest-[kinetic-energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_energy) water molecules transition to gas and leave the lower-energy molecules behind, lowering the overall temperature of the surface.

**3. The Density Anomaly**
Almost every substance in the universe becomes denser as it [freezes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freezing). Water is the exception. Solid water is less [dense](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Density) than liquid water. Why? Because as kinetic energy drops, hydrogen bonding arranges frozen water molecules into a rigid [crystalline lattice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystal_structure) that keeps them further apart than in liquid water. If [ice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ice) sank, lakes and oceans would freeze from the bottom up, effectively killing all aquatic life on Earth. 

![The crystalline lattice of solid ice. As temperature decreases, hydrogen bonds lock water molecules into a rigid, spacious hexagonal structure, expanding the molecules and making ice less dense than liquid water.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Ice_Ih_Crystal_Lattice.png)

**4. The Universal Solvent**
Because of its polarity, water is an excellent [solvent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solvent) for [ionic compounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ionic_compound) and [polar molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_polarity). 
*   **[Hydrophilic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrophile)** substances are polar or ionic molecules that interact readily with water (they [dissolve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dissolution_%28chemistry%29)). 
*   **[Hydrophobic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrophobe)** substances are nonpolar molecules that do not readily dissolve in water (like [oil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oil)). This hydrophobic repulsion is exactly what forces [lipid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lipid) molecules to form [cell membranes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_membrane), literally defining the boundaries of life.

## The Polymer Principle: Building Macromolecules

Cells build their massive structural and functional molecules ([polymers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polymer)) out of simpler, repeating subunits ([monomers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monomer)). To transition between these states, cells use two opposing [chemical reactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_reaction). Teach these to your students as the universal construction and demolition crews of the cell:

1.  **[Dehydration synthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dehydration_reaction)** reactions link monomers together into polymers by removing a water molecule.
2.  **[Hydrolysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrolysis)** reactions break polymers apart into individual monomers through the addition of a water molecule. (Think: *[hydro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water)* = water, *[lysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lysis)* = to split).

## Carbohydrates: Energy and Architecture

[Carbohydrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbohydrate) are [macromolecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macromolecule) typically composed of carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen atoms in a 1:2:1 ratio. They are the primary biological fuels and vital structural components.

The simplest carbohydrate monomers are **[monosaccharides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monosaccharide)**. The most famous of these is [glucose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glucose), a six-carbon monosaccharide used as a primary energy source by cells. Through dehydration synthesis, monosaccharides are linked together by covalent bonds known as **[glycosidic linkages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycosidic_bond)**.

![The ring structure of glucose, a six-carbon monosaccharide that serves as the primary fuel for cellular respiration and the fundamental monomer for complex carbohydrates.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Glucose_Haworth.png)

When you link exactly two monosaccharides joined by a glycosidic bond, you create a **[disaccharide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disaccharide)** (like [sucrose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sucrose) or [lactose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lactose)). But when cells need to store energy for the long term or build rigid structures, they build **[polysaccharides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polysaccharide)**, which are long polymer chains of monosaccharides linked by glycosidic bonds.

| Polysaccharide | Organism | Function / Structure |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **[Starch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Starch)** | [Plants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant) | Used for energy storage in plants. |
| **[Glycogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycogen)** | [Animals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Animal) | A highly branched polysaccharide used for energy storage in animals (stored primarily in the [liver](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liver) and [muscles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muscle)). |
| **[Cellulose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cellulose)** | Plants | An unbranched structural polysaccharide found in [plant cell walls](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_wall). (The tough fiber humans cannot digest). |
| **[Chitin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chitin)** | [Fungi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fungus) & Animals | A structural polysaccharide found in [fungal cell walls](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fungal_cell_wall) and [arthropod](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arthropod) [exoskeletons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exoskeleton). |

## Lipids: Boundaries and Reserves

Unlike carbohydrates, [lipids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lipid) are not united by a single common monomer. Instead, lipids are a diverse group of nonpolar, hydrophobic macromolecules. Their unifying feature is that they fear water. 

**[Triglycerides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triglyceride)** are lipids composed of one [glycerol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycerol) molecule bound to three [fatty acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fatty_acid) chains. The fatty acids are joined to a glycerol molecule via covalent **[ester linkages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ester)**. This is the fat stored in our [adipose tissue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adipose_tissue). The physical state of a triglyceride depends on the geometry of its fatty acid tails:
*   **[Saturated fatty acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saturated_fat)** contain exclusively single bonds between the carbon atoms in their hydrocarbon chains. Because their tails are straight, they pack tightly. Therefore, triglycerides containing mostly saturated fatty acids are typically solid at [room temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Room_temperature) (like [butter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Butter)).
*   **[Unsaturated fatty acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unsaturated_fat)** contain at least one double bond between carbon atoms in their hydrocarbon chains. This double bond creates a rigid "kink" in the chain, preventing tight packing. Consequently, triglycerides containing mostly unsaturated fatty acids are typically liquid at room temperature (like [olive oil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Olive_oil)).

**[Phospholipids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phospholipid)** are the foundation of all cellular biology. They consist of a glycerol attached to two fatty acid chains and one phosphate-containing group. This structure makes phospholipids [amphipathic molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amphiphile) containing both a hydrophilic head (the phosphate group) and a hydrophobic tail (the fatty acids). Because of this split personality, phospholipids spontaneously arrange into [bilayers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lipid_bilayer) in aqueous environments, with heads facing the water and tails hiding inside. This is the structural basis of every [cell membrane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_membrane).

![Because phospholipids are amphipathic—containing both hydrophilic heads and hydrophobic tails—they spontaneously self-assemble in water to form structures like micelles and the lipid bilayers that comprise all cell membranes.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Phospholipids_aqueous_solution_structures.svg)

**[Steroids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steroid)** are a class of lipids characterized by a carbon skeleton consisting of four fused rings. **[Cholesterol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cholesterol) is a steroid molecule.** While the media demonizes cholesterol, it is absolutely essential to life; cholesterol regulates the [membrane fluidity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Membrane_fluidity) of animal cells across a range of temperatures, acting as a buffer that prevents membranes from freezing solid in the cold or melting in the heat.

![The chemical structure of cholesterol, characterized by its core of four fused carbon rings. It embeds itself within cell membranes to act as a crucial structural buffer against temperature fluctuations.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Cholesterol.svg)

## Proteins: The Molecular Machines

If [DNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNA) is the blueprint, [proteins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein) are the bricklayers, the scaffolding, the engines, and the doors. **Proteins are polymers composed of [amino acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amino_acid) monomers.**

An amino acid contains a central carbon atom bonded to an [amino group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amine), a [carboxyl group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carboxyl_group), a hydrogen atom, and a variable [R group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Side_chain). There are 20 standard amino acids used by living organisms to build functional proteins. Because the main backbone of every amino acid is identical, the specific chemical properties of each amino acid are determined solely by its unique R group. Some R groups are bulky, some are small, some are acidic, some are basic, some love water, and some hate it.

Amino acids are linked together into polypeptide chains by covalent **[peptide bonds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peptide_bond)**. A peptide bond forms between the carboxyl group of one amino acid and the amino group of an adjacent amino acid.

### The Four Levels of Protein Structure

A protein is not just a straight chain of amino acids; its function is entirely dependent on its three-dimensional origami [folding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein_folding). 

1.  **[Primary Structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein_primary_structure):** The primary structure of a protein refers to its exact linear sequence of amino acids. 
2.  **[Secondary Structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein_secondary_structure):** The secondary structure of a protein consists of localized folding patterns such as [alpha-helices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_helix) and [beta-pleated sheets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beta_sheet). Crucially, protein secondary structure is stabilized exclusively by hydrogen bonds between the backbone components of amino acids (ignoring the R groups entirely).
3.  **[Tertiary Structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein_tertiary_structure):** The tertiary structure of a protein refers to its overall three-dimensional functional shape. Protein tertiary structure is determined by complex chemical interactions among the diverse R groups of the amino acids (hydrophobic interactions, [disulfide bridges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disulfide), ionic bonds).
4.  **[Quaternary Structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein_quaternary_structure):** Not all proteins have this level. The quaternary structure of a protein arises from the assembly of multiple individual polypeptide chains into a single functional complex (like [hemoglobin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hemoglobin), which requires four separate chains to carry oxygen).

![The four levels of protein architecture: from the primary linear sequence of amino acids (a), to localized secondary folding (b), complete 3D tertiary structure (c), and the assembled quaternary functional complex of multiple polypeptide chains (d).](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/225_Peptide_Bond-01.jpg)

Because a protein's function relies on its exact shape, **[denaturation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Denaturation_%28biochemistry%29)** is a catastrophic event. Denaturation is the process by which a protein loses its native three-dimensional structure. Changes in environmental [pH](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PH) or temperature can disrupt stabilizing bonds and cause protein denaturation. When you explain to your students why a high fever of [$106^{\circ}\text{F}$](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fahrenheit) is deadly, tell them this: heat shakes the molecular bonds apart. Protein denaturation typically results in the total loss of the protein's biological function. 

![The denaturation of the protein albumin in an egg white caused by heat. Environmental stress disrupts the fragile bonds maintaining the protein's 3D structure, resulting in a permanent loss of biological function.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Protein_Denaturation.png)

## Nucleic Acids: The Information Archive

Living systems must possess a way to store instructions and pass them to the next generation. This is the domain of **[nucleic acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleic_acid)**, which are complex polymers composed of individual [nucleotide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleotide) monomers. 

A single nucleotide consists of three parts: a [nitrogenous base](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogenous_base), a [pentose sugar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentose), and one or more [phosphate groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphate). Nucleotides are linked together into nucleic acid strands by covalent **[phosphodiester bonds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphodiester_bond)**. A phosphodiester bond forms between the phosphate group of one nucleotide and the pentose sugar of the next nucleotide, creating a strong sugar-phosphate backbone.

There are two primary nucleic acids: [Deoxyribonucleic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNA) (DNA) and [Ribonucleic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RNA) (RNA). They differ structurally in three distinct ways:

1.  **The Sugar:** Deoxyribonucleic acid contains the specific pentose sugar [deoxyribose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deoxyribose). Ribonucleic acid contains the specific pentose sugar [ribose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ribose). 
2.  **The Shape:** Deoxyribonucleic acid exists primarily in cells as a [double-stranded helix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleic_acid_double_helix). Ribonucleic acid exists primarily in cells as a [single-stranded](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RNA) molecule.
3.  **The Bases:** Both utilize nitrogenous bases, which are split into two geometric families:
    *   The nitrogenous bases [adenine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adenine) and [guanine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guanine) are classified as **[purines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Purine)**. Purines feature a double-ring chemical structure.
    *   The nitrogenous bases [cytosine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cytosine), [thymine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thymine), and [uracil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uracil) are classified as **[pyrimidines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrimidine)**. Pyrimidines feature a single-ring chemical structure.

| Feature | DNA | RNA |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Sugar** | [Deoxyribose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deoxyribose) | [Ribose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ribose) |
| **Strands** | [Double-stranded](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double-stranded_DNA) | [Single-stranded](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RNA) |
| **Purines** | [Adenine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adenine) (A), [Guanine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guanine) (G) | [Adenine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adenine) (A), [Guanine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guanine) (G) |
| **Pyrimidines** | [Cytosine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cytosine) (C), [Thymine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thymine) (T) | [Cytosine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cytosine) (C), [Uracil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uracil) (U) |

![A structural comparison between RNA (single-stranded) and DNA (double-stranded). Note the differing nitrogenous bases, with RNA utilizing uracil (U) in place of DNA's thymine (T).](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Difference_DNA_RNA-EN.svg)

Notice the difference in pyrimidines: ribonucleic acid completely lacks thymine and instead utilizes the nitrogenous base uracil. 

When DNA forms its [double helix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleic_acid_double_helix), the two strands are held together by hydrogen bonds between the nitrogenous bases, following strict [complementary pairing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Base_pair) rules based on width and bonding geometry. A purine must always pair with a pyrimidine to keep the helix a uniform width. 
*   In a DNA double helix, adenine pairs exclusively with thymine via two hydrogen bonds.
*   In a DNA double helix, cytosine pairs exclusively with guanine via three hydrogen bonds.

![The chemical structure of a DNA double helix. The strong sugar-phosphate backbones form the outer edges, while the predictable geometry of hydrogen bonds between complementary purine and pyrimidine bases holds the two strands together.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/DNA_chemical_structure.svg)

This exactness—this predictable geometry of hydrogen bonding—is the molecular basis for [genetic replication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNA_replication). Because A only bonds with T, and C only bonds with G, one strand of DNA can serve as a perfect [template](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Template_strand) to build the other.

As you prepare to pass this knowledge onward, remember that biology is not a collection of arbitrary facts. It is a continuous, logical narrative. The covalent bonds established in carbon dictate the shape of glucose. The hydrogen bonding of water dictates the folding of a protein's secondary structure. The hydrophobic interactions of lipids build the arena in which all this chemistry occurs. Understand the atoms, and the organism will make sense.
