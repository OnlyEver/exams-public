Look at this coaster. Look at it. It’s dead, limp cardboard soaking up the sweat of my pint glass, and do you know why it has no life? Because of its chemical allegiance. Inorganic molecules generally lack carbon-hydrogen bonds. It’s just sad, inert matter. But you? Me? The horrifyingly energetic freshmen you are going to be teaching next week? We are the organic elite. Organic molecules are defined by the presence of carbon-hydrogen bonds. That’s the VIP list of biology, my friend, and the bouncer at the door of life is carbon. 

Why is carbon the ultimate social butterfly of the atomic world? Because a single carbon atom has four valence electrons. It sits down at the cafeteria table of the universe, and it has exactly four empty seats next to it, which means carbon atoms can form up to four covalent bonds with other atoms. 

And what is a covalent bond? It’s a custody agreement. Covalent bonds form when two atoms share pairs of electrons. Now, in a utopian middle school, everyone shares perfectly. Nonpolar covalent bonds involve the equal sharing of electrons between atoms. It’s Kumbaya, everyone is happy. But you know teenagers, and you know chemistry. Some atoms are absolute bullies. They have higher electronegativity—a stronger pull. So, polar covalent bonds involve the unequal sharing of electrons due to differences in atomic electronegativity. One atom hoards the electron like a kid refusing to pass the communal lab tablet. And if the bully just straight-up steals the electron’s lunch money, ripping it away entirely? You get a full-blown electrostatic stand-off. Ionic bonds result from the electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions.

But the most dramatic, reality-TV-level tension in the molecular world is the hydrogen bond. Hydrogen bonds are weak attractions between a highly electronegative atom and a hydrogen atom covalently bonded to another electronegative atom. They are the fleeting hallway crushes of chemistry—not a permanent ring-on-the-finger covalent bond, but a magnetic "I'm going to stare at you across the lockers" energy. 

Which brings us to the ultimate drama queen: Water. 

A water molecule consists of two hydrogen atoms covalently bonded to one oxygen atom. Sounds simple, right? Wrong. Oxygen is more electronegative than hydrogen. It’s a total diva, hoarding all the shared electrons. This unequal sharing of electrons in a water molecule creates a polar molecule. Because the electrons spend all their time gossiping around the oxygen nucleus, the oxygen atom in a water molecule carries a partial negative charge. Meanwhile, the hydrogen atoms in a water molecule carry partial positive charges, standing out in the cold. 

Because of this intense polarity, water molecules start looking at each other. Hydrogen bonds form between the partially positive hydrogen of one water molecule and the partially negative oxygen of an adjacent water molecule. Trillions of these hallway crushes happen every microsecond. They hold the biological world together! 

Because of this relentless bonding, water exhibits cohesion. Cohesion is the attraction between individual water molecules due to hydrogen bonding. They form an impenetrable clique! In fact, this clique is so tight at the boundary with the air that surface tension in water is caused by the cohesive forces of hydrogen bonds at the liquid surface. It’s a literal skin—a water strider can do backflips on it. But water doesn’t just love water. Adhesion is the attraction between water molecules and other polar or charged surfaces. Water will cling to the inside of a plant stem like a desperate student clinging to an A-minus. When that clinging beats the clique, magic happens: capillary action occurs when the adhesive forces between water and a surface are stronger than the cohesive forces between water molecules. That’s how a three-hundred-foot redwood drinks!

And water is practically unflappable when it comes to drama. Water has a remarkably high specific heat capacity compared to most other liquids. Just like a veteran teacher absorbing twenty different teenage meltdowns without blinking, the high specific heat capacity of water allows aquatic environments to absorb significant heat energy without large changes in temperature. Try to boil it, and it fights back. Water requires a large amount of energy to change from a liquid to a gas. When you sweat during your first principal evaluation, you are relying on the fact that evaporative cooling occurs because the highest-kinetic-energy water molecules transition to gas and leave the lower-energy molecules behind. The wildest, hottest molecules get expelled, and the surface calms down. 

Even freezing makes water weird! Every other substance on Earth shrinks and sinks when it freezes, but solid water is less dense than liquid water. Why? Because as the temperature drops, hydrogen bonding arranges frozen water molecules into a rigid crystalline lattice that keeps them further apart than in liquid water. They literally T-pose to assert dominance and take up more volume! If they didn't, lakes would freeze bottom-up and every fish on Earth would be a popsicle. 

Because water is such a polarized diva, it’s the ultimate host. Water is an excellent solvent for ionic compounds and polar molecules. If you like the drama, you’re in the club. Hydrophilic substances are polar or ionic molecules that interact readily with water. But the misanthropes? The goths sitting under the bleachers? Hydrophobic substances are nonpolar molecules that do not readily dissolve in water. 

Now, how does a cell actually build things out of this watery mess? It has a construction crew and a demolition crew. If the cell wants to build a massive polymer out of tiny monomers, it forces them to link up by taking away their water. Dehydration synthesis reactions link monomers together into polymers by removing a water molecule. But if you need to tear the building down? Throw a water balloon into the crowd! Hydrolysis reactions break polymers apart into individual monomers through the addition of a water molecule.

Let's look at the bricks. Carbohydrates are macromolecules typically composed of carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen atoms in a 1:2:1 ratio. The single bricks are monosaccharides—monosaccharides are the simplest carbohydrate monomers. The homecoming king of these is glucose. Glucose is a six-carbon monosaccharide used as a primary energy source by cells. When you want to hook two monosaccharides together, you form a bridge. Monosaccharides are linked together by covalent bonds known as glycosidic linkages. Do it once, and disaccharides consist of exactly two monosaccharides joined by a glycosidic bond. Keep going until you have a massive chain, and polysaccharides are long polymer chains of monosaccharides linked by glycosidic bonds. 

Everyone uses these chains differently! Plants hoard them for a rainy day, meaning starch is a polysaccharide used for energy storage in plants. You and I? We pack it into our livers. Glycogen is a highly branched polysaccharide used for energy storage in animals. If you want to build a literal wall instead of storing energy, you lay the bricks flat. Cellulose is an unbranched structural polysaccharide found in plant cell walls—it’s the celery string you get stuck in your teeth. And if you're a fungus or a spider, you wear armor. Chitin is a structural polysaccharide found in fungal cell walls and arthropod exoskeletons.

But what about the kids who hate water? The bouncers of the cell! Lipids are a diverse group of nonpolar, hydrophobic macromolecules. Take the fat in the cafeteria burgers: triglycerides are lipids composed of one glycerol molecule bound to three fatty acid chains. They are bolted together because fatty acids are joined to a glycerol molecule via covalent ester linkages. If those chains are straight as an arrow, saturated fatty acids contain exclusively single bonds between the carbon atoms in their hydrocarbon chains. Because they are straight, they pack together in a dense, solid mob. Therefore, triglycerides containing mostly saturated fatty acids are typically solid at room temperature. Butter! Lard! But if just one carbon atom decides to kick a leg out? Unsaturated fatty acids contain at least one double bond between carbon atoms in their hydrocarbon chains. That double bond makes a massive kink in the chain. They can't pack tightly anymore, which is why triglycerides containing mostly unsaturated fatty acids are typically liquid at room temperature. Olive oil!

The most important lipids in the universe, though, are the security guards forming the cell membrane. Phospholipids consist of a glycerol attached to two fatty acid chains and one phosphate-containing group. They have a total split personality. Phospholipids are amphipathic molecules containing both a hydrophilic head and a hydrophobic tail. Throw them in a watery cell, and they panic, protecting their tails. Phospholipids spontaneously arrange into bilayers in aqueous environments, tails pointing inward, heads facing out. Boom. You have a cell membrane. To keep this fence from freezing or melting, you shove steroids into it. Steroids are a class of lipids characterized by a carbon skeleton consisting of four fused rings. The most famous one? Cholesterol is a steroid molecule. Forget the diet blogs—cholesterol regulates the membrane fluidity of animal cells across a range of temperatures. It’s the cellular antifreeze!

Now, who actually does the work in this school? The overworked faculty: Proteins. Proteins are polymers composed of amino acid monomers. An amino acid contains a central carbon atom bonded to an amino group, a carboxyl group, a hydrogen atom, and a variable R group. Here’s the crazy part: there are exactly 20 standard amino acids used by living organisms to build functional proteins. They all wear the exact same uniform except for that R group. The specific chemical properties of each amino acid are determined solely by its unique R group. Some love water, some hate it, some are acid, some are base. They hook up into a conga line because amino acids are linked together into polypeptide chains by covalent peptide bonds. Specifically, a peptide bond forms between the carboxyl group of one amino acid and the amino group of an adjacent amino acid.

But a straight line of teachers is useless. They have to fold into an exact origami shape to do their jobs! The primary structure of a protein refers to its exact linear sequence of amino acids. Boring. Next level up: the secondary structure of a protein consists of localized folding patterns such as alpha-helices and beta-pleated sheets. And listen to me, this is vital for the exam: protein secondary structure is stabilized exclusively by hydrogen bonds between the backbone components of amino acids. The R groups are totally ignored here! But then the whole thing scrunches into a 3D blob. The tertiary structure of a protein refers to its overall three-dimensional functional shape. Now the R groups get involved: protein tertiary structure is determined by complex chemical interactions among the diverse R groups of the amino acids. Sometimes, one protein isn't enough, and they form a committee. The quaternary structure of a protein arises from the assembly of multiple individual polypeptide chains into a single functional complex, like hemoglobin.

But if you turn up the heat, or dump acid on a teacher? They crack. Denaturation is the process by which a protein loses its native three-dimensional structure. Changes in environmental pH or temperature can disrupt stabilizing bonds and cause protein denaturation. The hydrogen bonds snap, the shape collapses, and the classroom descends into anarchy, because protein denaturation typically results in the total loss of the protein's biological function. 

So who wrote the rulebook that built these teachers? The administration's permanent record: Nucleic Acids. Nucleic acids are complex polymers composed of individual nucleotide monomers. A single nucleotide consists of a nitrogenous base, a pentose sugar, and one or more phosphate groups. They link up into a massive spine because nucleotides are linked together into nucleic acid strands by covalent phosphodiester bonds. Think of it as a heavy-duty stapler: a phosphodiester bond forms between the phosphate group of one nucleotide and the pentose sugar of the next nucleotide.

We have two versions of the master file. The original copy is DNA, and the disposable sticky-note copy is RNA. Deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA) contains the specific pentose sugar deoxyribose. Ribonucleic acid (RNA) contains the specific pentose sugar ribose. DNA is a heavily guarded vault—deoxyribonucleic acid exists primarily in cells as a double-stranded helix. RNA is the frantic messenger running down the hall—ribonucleic acid exists primarily in cells as a single-stranded molecule.

The actual letters of this code are the nitrogenous bases. We have the big heavyweights: the nitrogenous bases adenine and guanine are classified as purines. They are massive because purines feature a double-ring chemical structure. Then we have the lighter ones: the nitrogenous bases cytosine, thymine, and uracil are classified as pyrimidines. They are smaller because pyrimidines feature a single-ring chemical structure. 

When DNA zips up into its double helix, it demands perfect architectural spacing. A double-ring must always hold hands with a single-ring. No exceptions! In a DNA double helix, adenine pairs exclusively with thymine via two hydrogen bonds. It’s a very specific handshake. Meanwhile, in a DNA double helix, cytosine pairs exclusively with guanine via three hydrogen bonds. It’s a tighter, three-finger grip! But what about RNA, the single-stranded rebel? It refuses to play entirely by the administration's rules. Ribonucleic acid completely lacks thymine and instead utilizes the nitrogenous base uracil. 

Breathe. Finish your drink. If you can walk into that exam and see the molecules not as dots on a page, but as a chaotic, high-stakes high school drama of sharing, stealing, folding, and freezing... you're going to ace this thing. Now, who's paying the tab?
