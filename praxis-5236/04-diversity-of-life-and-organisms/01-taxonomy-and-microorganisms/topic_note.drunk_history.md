Listen to me, rookie. Slide that pint glass over and pay attention, because before you step foot in that high school biology classroom tomorrow, you need to understand that the entire history of life on Earth is exactly like the social hierarchy of the teenagers you're about to teach. It’s a chaotic, three-and-a-half-billion-year-old reality show of alliances, toxic breakups, and identity theft, and you are the poor producer tasked with keeping the cast list straight. 

To map this mess, we have three jobs. First, there’s taxonomy. Taxonomy is the scientific discipline of describing, naming, and classifying organisms—it’s writing the school directory. But you can't just group kids by who is wearing a blue shirt. You need phylogeny, which describes the evolutionary history and evolutionary relationships among a species or a group of species. That’s the raw, unfiltered gossip of who is related to whom. Finally, you have systematics, which is the biological field that organizes and classifies organisms based on evolutionary relationships. Systematics is the guidance counselor furiously rearranging the seating chart so it accurately reflects the phylogenetic gossip. 

Before the 1700s, naming these biological delinquents was a nightmare. Then Carl Linnaeus came along, the ultimate strict hall monitor. Carl Linnaeus developed the binomial nomenclature system for formally naming organisms. Binomial nomenclature assigns each distinct organism a unique two-part scientific name. It’s a strict dress code. In binomial nomenclature, the first word represents the genus name, and standard biological naming conventions require the genus name to be capitalized. The second word represents the specific epithet or species name, and standard biological naming conventions require the species name to be written entirely in lowercase letters. If you print it, scientific names formatted under binomial nomenclature must be italicized in print, or underlined when handwritten. Period. No exceptions, or Linnaeus gives you detention.

Once they’re named, we shove them into a district-wide tier system. The modern taxonomic classification system uses a hierarchical model. The eight primary taxonomic levels from most inclusive to least inclusive are domain, kingdom, phylum, class, order, family, genus, and species. Anytime you point to a specific group of organisms at any given level within the taxonomic classification system, you are pointing to a taxon. 

For the longest time, science teachers taught the traditional six-kingdom biological classification system, which includes Eubacteria, Archaebacteria, Protista, Fungi, Plantae, and Animalia. Throw that out. The school district got completely rezoned at the top level. Now, the big boss categories are the three domains of life: Bacteria, Archaea, and Eukarya. 

To figure out who goes into what taxon, we use cladistics. Cladistics classifies organisms into monophyletic groups based on shared derived characteristics—like grouping all the kids who inherited the exact same ugly marching band jacket. A monophyletic clade consists of a single evolutionary ancestor and all the descendants of that ancestor. It’s the perfect, unbroken family tree. But sometimes, taxonomists mess up and create a paraphyletic taxonomic group, which includes a common ancestor and only a subset of the descendants of that ancestor. It’s like throwing a family reunion but deliberately not inviting your weird cousins. 

And you have to watch out for posers. Homologous structures share a similar embryonic origin resulting from descent from a common evolutionary ancestor. That’s generational wealth; they actually inherited the trait. Analogous structures, on the other hand, perform similar functions due to convergent evolution rather than common ancestry. That’s just two unrelated kids buying the same knockoff Rolex because they both want to look rich.

Now, let’s talk about the biological workforce: the cell. Unicellular microorganisms contain a single cell that executes all physiological functions required for survival. They are the solo entrepreneurs of the biological world. Multicellular microorganisms rely on differentiated, specialized cells cooperating to maintain the life of the entire organism, like a massive corporate conglomerate. All known species of Bacteria and Archaea exist exclusively as unicellular organisms. 

But wait, what about the criminals? The entities that don’t even have a business license? Viruses are acellular entities. They aren't cells. Viruses lack an independent cellular metabolism. Because they can't do anything on their own, viruses are obligate intracellular parasites requiring a host cell's machinery to synthesize proteins and replicate. They are microscopic squatters taking over a cellular factory to print their own bootleg merch. 

A viral particle is just a genetic threat wrapped in a getaway vehicle. It consists of a nucleic acid core enclosed within a protein coat called a capsid. The genetic material of a virus consists of either DNA or RNA, and get this, viral genomes can be single-stranded or double-stranded. That capsid coat? It’s built from capsomeres, which are the individual protein subunits that assemble to form a viral capsid. Some viruses are even sneakier. Enveloped viruses possess an outer lipid envelope derived from the plasma membrane of a host cell. They literally skin their victims and wear the plasma membrane as camouflage. To pick the locks on their next victim, viral surface glycoproteins bind to specific receptor molecules on the exterior of a host cell. 

If a virus specifically targets bacterial cells, it’s called a bacteriophage. When a virus breaks in, it has two options. The lytic viral replication cycle is a smash-and-grab; it results in the immediate rupture and death of the infected host cell. But the lysogenic viral replication cycle is a deep-cover sleeper agent operation; it involves the integration of the viral genome into the host cell's chromosome, just hiding there quietly while the cell divides. Oh, and keep an eye on retroviruses. Retroviruses are RNA viruses, but they pull a total wild card move: retroviruses utilize the enzyme reverse transcriptase to synthesize a DNA copy from a viral RNA template. They read the genetic instructions backward! 

If you think viruses are weird, meet the subviral agents, which are basically just biological junk mail that destroys everything. Prions are infectious pathogen particles composed entirely of misfolded proteins. Prions contain no detectable nucleic acids! They’re just crumpled-up proteins that bump into healthy proteins and convince them to crumple up too, like a toxic rumor mill. Then you have viroids, which are small infectious plant pathogens consisting solely of circular, single-stranded RNA without a protein coat. Just naked, rogue code.

Let’s get back to the true heavyweights of the microscopic world: Domain Bacteria. Bacteria are unicellular prokaryotic organisms. Their defining outfit is that bacterial cell walls are primarily composed of a polymer called peptidoglycan. Inside, the primary genetic material of a bacterium is typically a single circular chromosome. Since they don't have a VIP lounge (a nucleus), the bacterial chromosome is located in an irregularly shaped cytoplasmic region called the nucleoid. But bacteria also carry around cheat codes in their pockets: plasmids. Plasmids are small, circular DNA molecules in bacteria existing independently of the main chromosomal DNA, and plasmids frequently carry accessory genes conferring advantageous traits such as antibiotic resistance.

Under the microscope, bacteria look like scattered pills and pasta. Cocci are spherically shaped bacterial cells. Bacilli are rod-shaped bacterial cells. Spirilla are spiral-shaped bacterial cells. To tell them apart, we run a dress-code check called a Gram stain. Gram-positive bacteria possess a remarkably thick cell wall composed of many overlapping layers of peptidoglycan. Because of that thick sweater, Gram-positive bacteria retain crystal violet dye and appear purple after a standard Gram stain. Gram-negative bacteria possess a thin layer of peptidoglycan located between the inner plasma membrane and an outer lipid membrane. Because their sweater is so thin, Gram-negative bacteria lose the crystal violet dye and appear pink or red after counterstaining in a Gram stain procedure. 

When it comes to making more of themselves, the predominant mode of asexual cellular reproduction shared by both Bacteria and Archaea is binary fission. Binary fission results in the division of a single parent cell into two genetically identical daughter cells. But they also steal genetic secrets from each other! Bacterial transformation occurs when a live bacterium absorbs fragments of naked DNA from the surrounding extracellular environment—literally picking up dropped notes off the floor. Bacterial transduction occurs when a bacteriophage accidentally transfers genetic material from a previously infected bacterium to a newly infected bacterium, like a clueless mailman delivering the wrong package. And bacterial conjugation involves the direct physical transfer of genetic material from a donor bacterium to a recipient bacterium via a specialized structure called a pilus. 

Some bacteria changed the whole planet. Cyanobacteria are photoautotrophic bacteria, and they are the reason you're breathing right now because cyanobacteria generate oxygen gas as a direct metabolic byproduct of photosynthesis. And if the apocalypse hits? Bacteria can pack up and wait it out. Endospores are highly durable, dormant bacterial structures capable of surviving extreme environmental stressors. 

Now, let's look at Domain Archaea. Archaea are unicellular prokaryotic organisms, but don't confuse them with bacteria! The cell walls of Archaea completely lack peptidoglycan. Instead, many archaeal species possess cell walls constructed from a polymer known as pseudopeptidoglycan. Their lipid construction is totally bizarre, too. The plasma membranes of Archaea contain ether linkages between the glycerol molecule and the lipid chains, and archaeal lipid chains frequently exhibit branched molecular structures. Meanwhile, the plasma membranes of Bacteria contain ester linkages between the glycerol molecule and the lipid chains, and the plasma membranes of Eukarya contain ester linkages between the glycerol molecule and the lipid chains as well. Despite looking like bacteria, the transcription and translation molecular machinery in Archaea more closely resembles the machinery in Eukarya than the machinery in Bacteria! 

Archaea are the adrenaline junkies of life. Many are extremophiles, organisms that achieve optimal growth in physically or geochemically extreme environmental conditions. Halophiles are extreme archaea that require highly saline environments for survival. Thermophiles are extreme archaea that thrive at exceptionally high temperatures. Methanogens are archaea that generate methane gas as a byproduct of cellular metabolism. Methanogens are obligate anaerobes, and obligate anaerobes are organisms that undergo lethal cellular damage in the presence of oxygen. Oxygen literally melts their faces off.

Let’s move up to the big leagues: Domain Eukarya. First up, the Island of Misfit Toys: the Protists. Protists are organisms classified within the domain Eukarya, meaning protist cells contain a distinct membrane-bound nucleus protecting the cellular DNA. Because taxonomists basically threw every weird eukaryote that wasn't a plant, animal, or fungus into this bin, the kingdom Protista is widely recognized by modern taxonomists as a paraphyletic group. While the majority of protist species are unicellular organisms, some go massive—giant kelps are an example of large multicellular protists. 

Protists hustle for their food in every way possible. Photoautotrophic protists synthesize organic compounds utilizing chloroplasts. Heterotrophic protists obtain organic carbon exclusively by consuming other organisms or organic matter. And the ultimate opportunists, mixotrophic protists can alternate between photoautotrophic and heterotrophic nutritional modes based on current environmental conditions. Many heterotrophs eat via phagocytosis, an active transport process involving the cell membrane engulfing a solid particle to form an intracellular food vacuole. 

To get to that food, they need to move. Certain protist species exhibit motility propelled by short, hair-like cellular appendages called cilia. Certain protist species exhibit motility propelled by long, whip-like cellular structures called flagella. And amoeboid protists move by temporarily extending portions of cytoplasm called pseudopodia. 

You’ll meet all kinds of protists. Diatoms are unicellular photosynthetic protists encased in protective distinct cell walls composed of silica—they literally live in glass houses! Dinoflagellates are marine and freshwater protists structurally characterized by the presence of two flagella. Oh, and when paramecia want to mix things up genetically, sexual reproduction in paramecia occurs through conjugation involving the direct exchange of haploid micronuclei between two temporarily joined protist cells. 

Finally, let me tell you about the absolute weirdos of Eukarya: Fungi. Fungi are non-photosynthetic organisms classified within the domain Eukarya. They might look like plants, but they are apex recyclers. All fungi are obligate heterotrophic organisms. But they don't have stomachs! Instead, fungi acquire nutrients by secreting digestive exoenzymes into their external environment and subsequently absorbing the broken-down organic molecules. They puke their digestion outside their bodies! Saprobic fungi derive essential nutrients from decaying, dead organic matter. 

Unlike plants, fungal cell walls are structurally reinforced by a complex polysaccharide named chitin. The majority of identified fungal species are multicellular organisms, and the vegetative body of a multicellular fungus consists of long, thread-like filament structures called hyphae. When those hyphae tangle together in a massive underground mat, a dense, interconnected network of fungal hyphae is formally termed a mycelium. 

These hyphae have two architectural styles. Septate hyphae are physically divided into separate cellular compartments by internal cross-walls called septa. Coenocytic hyphae completely lack internal septa, meaning coenocytic hyphae form a single, continuous cytoplasmic mass containing hundreds or thousands of nuclei. It’s just a giant tube of nuclear soup! 

Not all are multicellular, though. Yeasts constitute a specialized group of unicellular fungi. And some fungi have a dual identity—dimorphic fungi possess the unique capability to alternate between a unicellular yeast morphology and a multicellular mold morphology depending on the environment. 

When fungi reproduce asexually, unicellular yeast fungi typically reproduce asexually through an unequal cellular division process known as budding. Multicellular fungi frequently reproduce asexually by generating and releasing massive quantities of microscopic spores, and these fungal spores are environmentally dispersed by forces such as wind, water currents, or animal vectors. 

But their sex lives? Absolutely unhinged. The sexual reproduction cycle in fungi requires a distinct phase called plasmogamy, where plasmogamy represents the physical fusion of the cytoplasm from two compatible fungal mycelia. But they don't merge their DNA right away! They just live as roommates for a while. Eventually, the sexual reproduction cycle in fungi requires a distinct phase called karyogamy, and karyogamy represents the physical fusion of two haploid nuclei resulting in the formation of a single diploid nucleus. 

Sometimes, fungi team up for world domination. Mycorrhizae characterize a mutually beneficial symbiotic association between actively growing plant roots and a network of fungal mycelia. The fungus acts like a giant root extension for the plant. And if they want to conquer bare rock? Lichens constitute a composite organism emerging from a mutualistic symbiotic relationship between a fungal host and a photosynthetic partner. 

So drink up, kid. Tomorrow you’re standing in front of thirty teenagers, but just remember: compared to the genetic theft, shape-shifting, external digestion, and evolutionary drama happening microscopically inside that classroom, high school is a breeze.
