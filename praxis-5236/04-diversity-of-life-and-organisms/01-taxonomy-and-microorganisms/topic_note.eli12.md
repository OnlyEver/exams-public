Imagine you have a giant, messy room with millions of different toys. To find anything, you need to sort them out! In biology, we use three main tools to sort all the living things on Earth:
1. **Taxonomy** is the scientific discipline of describing, naming, and classifying organisms. Think of this as putting the right labels on your toy bins.
2. **Phylogeny** describes the evolutionary history and evolutionary relationships among a species or a group of species. Think of this as drawing a giant family tree to see how your toys are related.
3. **Systematics** is the biological field that organizes and classifies organisms based on evolutionary relationships. This is the big project of making sure your toy bin labels perfectly match the family tree!

## The Architecture of Biological Classification

Before we had a good system, people named animals using long, confusing descriptions. 

### Linnaeus and the Rules of Naming
A scientist named Carl Linnaeus changed the game. Carl Linnaeus developed the **binomial nomenclature** system for formally naming organisms. 

**Binomial nomenclature** assigns each distinct organism a unique two-part scientific name. It is exactly like giving an animal a first and last name! 

In binomial nomenclature, the first word represents the **genus** name. In binomial nomenclature, the second word represents the **specific epithet or species** name. To keep things neat across the whole world, standard biological naming conventions require the genus name to be capitalized. Furthermore, standard biological naming conventions require the species name to be written entirely in lowercase letters. Finally, scientific names formatted under binomial nomenclature must be italicized in print or underlined when handwritten. 

### The Taxonomic Hierarchy
The modern taxonomic classification system uses a hierarchical model. This is like zooming in on a map from the universe down to your house. 

The eight primary taxonomic levels from most inclusive to least inclusive are **domain, kingdom, phylum, class, order, family, genus, and species**. As you move from domain down to species, the groups get smaller and more specific. A **taxon** is a specific group of organisms at any given level within the taxonomic classification system (like pointing to "dogs" at the species level).

## Mapping the Tree of Life

In the old days, scientists used the traditional six-kingdom biological classification system, which includes Eubacteria, Archaebacteria, Protista, Fungi, Plantae, and Animalia. However, as we learned more, we realized the biggest splits happened way earlier! Today, we use an even bigger category system: the three domains of life are **Bacteria, Archaea, and Eukarya**.

To figure out how things are related, scientists use **cladistics**. **Cladistics** classifies organisms into monophyletic groups based on shared derived characteristics (like grouping video game characters together because they all share a special "double-jump" power they got from the exact same older game). 

When looking at these family trees, you'll see different types of groups:
* A **monophyletic clade** consists of a single evolutionary ancestor and all the descendants of that ancestor. (This is a perfect, complete family tree).
* A **paraphyletic taxonomic group** includes a common ancestor and only a subset of the descendants of that ancestor. (This is like having a family reunion but accidentally forgetting to invite a few cousins).

Sometimes animals look similar but aren't related at all! We have to be careful to spot the difference:
* **Homologous structures** share a similar embryonic origin resulting from descent from a common evolutionary ancestor (like your arm and a bat's wing—you both got those bones from the same ancient grandparent).
* **Analogous structures** perform similar functions due to convergent evolution rather than common ancestry (like a bird's wing and a bug's wing—they both fly, but they developed completely on their own).

## Cellular Organization: The Basis of Life
Before we talk about tiny microbes, we need to know how cells work. 
* **Unicellular microorganisms** contain a single cell that executes all physiological functions required for survival. (Like a solo player in a survival game doing all the work).
* **Multicellular microorganisms** rely on differentiated, specialized cells cooperating to maintain the life of the entire organism. (Like a huge team playing different roles to win).

All known species of **Bacteria** and **Archaea** exist exclusively as unicellular organisms.

## The Acellular Fringe: Viruses, Prions, and Viroids

Viruses are like tiny biological hackers. **Viruses are acellular entities**, meaning they aren't even made of cells! Because they aren't cells, viruses lack an independent cellular metabolism (they don't eat or make their own energy). Therefore, **viruses are obligate intracellular parasites requiring a host cell's machinery to synthesize proteins and replicate**. They literally break into a cell and force its factory to build more viruses!

### Viral Anatomy
A viral particle is built very simply. A viral particle consists of a **nucleic acid core** enclosed within a protein coat called a **capsid**. The genetic material of a virus consists of either **DNA or RNA**. And unlike you and me, viral genomes can be **single-stranded or double-stranded**. 

The capsid is built like Lego bricks; **capsomeres** are the individual protein subunits that assemble to form a viral capsid. Some viruses wear a disguise! **Enveloped viruses** possess an outer lipid envelope derived from the plasma membrane of a host cell (like wearing a stolen jacket). On the outside of this jacket, **viral surface glycoproteins** bind to specific receptor molecules on the exterior of a host cell, acting like a fake key to unlock the door.

### Viral Replication Cycles
**Bacteriophages** are specialized viruses that infect bacterial cells. When a virus attacks, it usually uses one of two sneaky plans:
1. **The lytic viral replication cycle** results in the immediate rupture and death of the infected host cell (the cell pops like a balloon, releasing tons of new viruses).
2. **The lysogenic viral replication cycle** involves the integration of the viral genome into the host cell's chromosome (it hides quietly in the cell's code while the cell copies itself).

Some viruses play by completely backward rules. **Retroviruses are RNA viruses**. Unlike normal cells that read DNA to make RNA, **retroviruses utilize the enzyme reverse transcriptase to synthesize a DNA copy from a viral RNA template**.

### Subviral Agents
There are even smaller troublemakers out there:
* **Prions** are infectious pathogen particles composed entirely of misfolded proteins. They act like bad influencers, but prions contain no detectable nucleic acids (no DNA or RNA at all).
* **Viroids** are small infectious plant pathogens consisting solely of circular, single-stranded RNA without a protein coat.

## Domain Bacteria: The Ubiquitous Survivors

**Bacteria** are unicellular prokaryotic organisms. They wear tough armor: **bacterial cell walls are primarily composed of a polymer called peptidoglycan**. 

Inside, they don't have a safe (a nucleus) for their instructions. Instead, the primary genetic material of a bacterium is typically a **single circular chromosome**. The bacterial chromosome is located in an irregularly shaped cytoplasmic region called the **nucleoid**. 

Bacteria can also carry extra cheat codes. **Plasmids** are small, circular DNA molecules in bacteria existing independently of the main chromosomal DNA. **Plasmids frequently carry accessory genes conferring advantageous traits such as antibiotic resistance**. 

### Bacterial Morphology and Staining
Bacteria come in three main shapes:
* **Cocci** are spherically shaped bacterial cells (like tiny balls).
* **Bacilli** are rod-shaped bacterial cells (like hot dogs).
* **Spirilla** are spiral-shaped bacterial cells (like corkscrews).

Scientists use a test called a Gram stain to check their armor:
* **Gram-positive bacteria** possess a remarkably thick cell wall composed of many overlapping layers of peptidoglycan. Because of this thick armor, Gram-positive bacteria retain crystal violet dye and appear purple after a standard Gram stain.
* **Gram-negative bacteria** possess a thin layer of peptidoglycan located between the inner plasma membrane and an outer lipid membrane. Because it's so thin, Gram-negative bacteria lose the crystal violet dye and appear pink or red after counterstaining in a Gram stain procedure.

### Bacterial Physiology and Reproduction
How do they make babies? **Binary fission** is the predominant mode of asexual cellular reproduction shared by both Bacteria and Archaea. **Binary fission results in the division of a single parent cell into two genetically identical daughter cells.** 

Let's do a quick math breakdown of how binary fission creates huge numbers fast:
1. You start with 1 parent cell.
2. It splits, giving you 2 identical cells.
3. Those 2 cells split, giving you 4 cells.
4. Those 4 cells split, giving you 8 cells... and so on!

Bacteria also trade DNA like trading cards in three ways:
1. **Bacterial transformation** occurs when a live bacterium absorbs fragments of naked DNA from the surrounding extracellular environment (just picking it up off the ground!).
2. **Bacterial transduction** occurs when a bacteriophage accidentally transfers genetic material from a previously infected bacterium to a newly infected bacterium (a mistake by a viral hacker!).
3. **Bacterial conjugation** involves the direct physical transfer of genetic material from a donor bacterium to a recipient bacterium via a specialized structure called a **pilus** (like passing a note through a tube).

Some bacteria help the whole planet. **Cyanobacteria** are photoautotrophic bacteria. They act like plants! Cyanobacteria generate oxygen gas as a direct metabolic byproduct of photosynthesis. 

When conditions get totally awful (too hot, too dry), some bacteria turtle up. **Endospores** are highly durable, dormant bacterial structures capable of surviving extreme environmental stressors.

## Domain Archaea: The Extremophiles

Archaea look like bacteria, but they are the extreme sports athletes of the microbe world. **Archaea are unicellular prokaryotic organisms**. 

First off, their armor is different: the **cell walls of Archaea completely lack peptidoglycan**. Instead, many archaeal species possess cell walls constructed from a polymer known as **pseudopeptidoglycan**.

Their skin is built differently, too. The **plasma membranes of Archaea contain ether linkages** between the glycerol molecule and the lipid chains. On top of that, **archaeal lipid chains frequently exhibit branched molecular structures**. In contrast, the **plasma membranes of Bacteria contain ester linkages** between the glycerol molecule and the lipid chains, and the **plasma membranes of Eukarya contain ester linkages** between the glycerol molecule and the lipid chains. 

Surprisingly, the inner factory parts (the transcription and translation molecular machinery) in Archaea more closely resemble the machinery in Eukarya than the machinery in Bacteria!

### The Edge of Life
Many of these guys are **extremophiles**, which are organisms that achieve optimal growth in physically or geochemically extreme environmental conditions (places that would instantly destroy us!).
* **Halophiles** are extreme archaea that require highly saline (super salty) environments for survival.
* **Thermophiles** are extreme archaea that thrive at exceptionally high temperatures (like boiling underwater volcanoes).
* **Methanogens** are archaea that generate methane gas as a byproduct of cellular metabolism (they literally burp gas). Methanogens are **obligate anaerobes**. **Obligate anaerobes** are organisms that undergo lethal cellular damage in the presence of oxygen (so even a breath of fresh air will destroy them!).

## Domain Eukarya: The Protists

Protists are a wild, mixed-up group. **Protists are organisms classified within the domain Eukarya**. This means protist cells contain a distinct membrane-bound nucleus protecting the cellular DNA (they finally have a safe for their instructions!). 

Because they are such a mixed bag, the kingdom Protista is widely recognized by modern taxonomists as a **paraphyletic group**. 

The majority of protist species are unicellular organisms. But some get huge! **Giant kelps** are an example of large multicellular protists (entire underwater forests made of protists!).

### Protist Nutrition and Motility
Protists get their food in awesome ways:
* **Photoautotrophic protists** synthesize organic compounds utilizing chloroplasts (like solar panels making food).
* **Heterotrophic protists** obtain organic carbon exclusively by consuming other organisms or organic matter (like us eating dinner).
* **Mixotrophic protists** can alternate between photoautotrophic and heterotrophic nutritional modes based on current environmental conditions (acting like a plant in the sun, and an animal in the dark!).

Many eat by **phagocytosis**, which is an active transport process involving the cell membrane engulfing a solid particle to form an intracellular food vacuole (like a blob wrapping around a pizza slice).

To move around, they use special parts:
* Certain protist species exhibit motility propelled by short, hair-like cellular appendages called **cilia** (like tiny oars).
* Certain protist species exhibit motility propelled by long, whip-like cellular structures called **flagella** (like a propeller tail).
* Amoeboid protists move by temporarily extending portions of cytoplasm called **pseudopodia** (stretching out a "false foot" to drag themselves).

### Diversity and Reproduction
There are some beautiful and strange protists:
* **Diatoms** are unicellular photosynthetic protists encased in protective distinct cell walls composed of silica (they live in tiny glass houses!).
* **Dinoflagellates** are marine and freshwater protists structurally characterized by the presence of two flagella.

When it comes to babies, **sexual reproduction in paramecia occurs through conjugation involving the direct exchange of haploid micronuclei** between two temporarily joined protist cells (they lock together and trade half of their genetic cards).

## Domain Eukarya: The Fungi

Fungi might look like plants, but they can't make their own food! **Fungi are non-photosynthetic organisms classified within the domain Eukarya**. 

**All fungi are obligate heterotrophic organisms**. However, they don't have stomachs. Instead, **fungi acquire nutrients by secreting digestive exoenzymes into their external environment and subsequently absorbing the broken-down organic molecules** (imagine spraying stomach juices onto a burger, letting it melt, and then slurping it up!). Many are nature's garbage cleaners; **saprobic fungi** derive essential nutrients from decaying, dead organic matter.

### Fungal Anatomy
Fungi have strong walls, but not like plants. **Fungal cell walls are structurally reinforced by a complex polysaccharide named chitin** (the exact same tough material in a beetle's shell).

The majority of identified fungal species are multicellular organisms. The vegetative body of a multicellular fungus consists of long, thread-like filament structures called **hyphae**. A dense, interconnected network of fungal hyphae is formally termed a **mycelium**. 

These threads come in two styles:
1. **Septate hyphae** are physically divided into separate cellular compartments by internal cross-walls called septa (like a long hallway with doors).
2. **Coenocytic hyphae** completely lack internal septa. Because there are no walls, coenocytic hyphae form a single, continuous cytoplasmic mass containing hundreds or thousands of nuclei.

Some fungi are solo acts. **Yeasts** constitute a specialized group of unicellular fungi. Even cooler, **dimorphic fungi possess the unique capability to alternate between a unicellular yeast morphology and a multicellular mold morphology** (like shapeshifters changing forms!).

### Fungal Reproduction
Fungi can clone themselves or mix it up! 
* Unicellular yeast fungi typically reproduce asexually through an unequal cellular division process known as **budding** (a mini-cell pinches off).
* Multicellular fungi frequently reproduce asexually by generating and releasing massive quantities of microscopic **spores**. These **fungal spores are environmentally dispersed by forces such as wind, water currents, or animal vectors** (like dandelion seeds blowing away).

When fungi reproduce sexually, they do it in two slow steps:
1. **Plasmogamy:** The sexual reproduction cycle in fungi requires a distinct phase called plasmogamy. **Plasmogamy** represents the physical fusion of the cytoplasm from two compatible fungal mycelia (their bodies merge).
2. **Karyogamy:** The sexual reproduction cycle in fungi requires a distinct phase called karyogamy. **Karyogamy** represents the physical fusion of two haploid nuclei resulting in the formation of a single diploid nucleus (their DNA finally merges, sometimes a long time after their bodies did!).

### Symbiotic Masterminds
Fungi love teamwork! 
* **Mycorrhizae** characterize a mutually beneficial symbiotic association between actively growing plant roots and a network of fungal mycelia. The fungus acts like a sponge, fetching water for the plant, and the plant gives the fungus a sugar allowance in return.
* **Lichens** constitute a composite organism emerging from a mutualistic symbiotic relationship between a fungal host and a photosynthetic partner (like algae). Together, they can survive on bare rocks!
