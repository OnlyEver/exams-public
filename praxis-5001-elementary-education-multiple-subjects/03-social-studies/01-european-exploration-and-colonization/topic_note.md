## 1. The Starting Gun: Why Leave Europe?

To understand why European explorers suddenly braved terrifying, uncharted oceans, you just have to remember three powerful motivators: **wealth, the spread of Christianity, and the pursuit of national glory**. (Historians often call these "Gold, God, and Glory"). 

Five major players got in the game early. If you were placing bets in the 15th and 16th centuries, your money would be on these seafaring heavyweights: **Spain, France, England, Portugal, and the Netherlands**. They were the primary European nations involved in the early exploration of the Americas.

### The First Movers
The spark was struck when **Christopher Columbus reached the Caribbean islands in 1492 while sailing for Spain**. He was looking for a shortcut to Asia, but he stumbled into a hemisphere completely unknown to Europe. 

Suddenly, everyone wanted a piece of the pie. To prevent Spain and Portugal from going to war over these discoveries, the Pope actually stepped in and drew a literal line on the map. This resulted in the **1494 Treaty of Tordesillas**, which legally divided the newly discovered lands outside Europe between the Portuguese Empire and the Spanish Empire.

## 2. The Columbian Exchange: The Great Biological Swap

When Columbus arrived, it wasn't just a meeting of people; it was a meeting of ecosystems. This is perhaps the most important concept in early global history:

> **The Columbian Exchange** was the widespread transfer of plants, animals, culture, human populations, and ideas between the Americas and the Old World.

This wasn't a formal trade agreement; it was an accidental, chaotic biological explosion. Let's look at how it reshaped both sides of the Atlantic:

### What went to the Old World (Europe, Africa, Asia)?
The Americas provided Old World societies with incredible new staple crops like **potatoes and maize (corn)**. Why does this matter? Because a potato packs an immense amount of calories and grows in terrible soil. The introduction of potatoes and maize from the Americas **supported significant population growth in Europe**. Basically, European empires could suddenly feed much larger populations.

### What came to the New World (The Americas)?
European colonists introduced Old World crops like **wheat and sugarcane** to the Americas. But they also brought animals. Specifically, European colonists introduced **horses** to North America. The introduction of horses was a technological revolution; it fundamentally altered the hunting and warfare practices of **Plains Native American tribes**, allowing them to hunt bison more effectively and travel vast distances.

### The Invisible Conqueror: Disease
But the Columbian Exchange had a horrific dark side. European explorers introduced new, deadly diseases such as **smallpox and measles** to the Americas. 

Because the populations of the Americas had been isolated for millennia, they had absolutely no biological defenses against these pathogens. Epidemics of European diseases caused a **massive decline in Native American populations due to a lack of natural immunity** among the indigenous peoples. In many ways, microscopic viruses conquered the Americas long before the armies did.

## 3. The Playbooks: How Different Nations Colonized

Not every European nation colonized the same way. It is vital for your Praxis exam to understand the different *styles* of colonization. Here is how the major players operated.

### The Spanish: Conquest and Conversion
Spain hit the mainland running with a strategy of military conquest and religious conversion. 
*   **Juan Ponce de León** led the first known European expedition to the Florida peninsula in **1513**.
*   **Hernán Cortés** led the brutal Spanish conquest of the Aztec Empire beginning in **1519**.
*   **Francisco Pizarro** followed suit, leading the Spanish conquest of the Inca Empire in the **1530s**.

Once they conquered the land, how did they extract the wealth? They used something called the **encomienda system**. 

> **The Spanish Encomienda System:** A legal and labor system that rewarded Spanish conquerors with the forced labor of specific groups of conquered Native American people. It was essentially state-sanctioned enslavement.

Meanwhile, the spiritual side of the Spanish mission was in full swing. **Spanish missionaries** actively sought to convert Native American populations to Catholicism by establishing vast mission settlements throughout their territories.

### France & The Netherlands: Rivers and Furs
The French and the Dutch weren't primarily looking to conquer massive empires of gold; they were looking to trade. 

*   **Jacques Cartier** claimed lands in North America for France during his voyages beginning in **1534**.
*   Later, **Samuel de Champlain** founded the French settlement of Quebec in **1608** to explicitly facilitate the fur trade. 
*   For the Dutch Republic, **Henry Hudson** explored the modern-day New York region in **1609** (which is why we have the Hudson River!).

Because they wanted furs, **French colonization in North America heavily relied on establishing cooperative fur-trading relationships with Native American tribes**. They didn't want to displace the indigenous people; they needed them as business partners to hunt and trap beaver!

### England: Land and Settlement
England came to the game with a completely different mindset. Starting when **John Cabot** explored the North American coastline in **1497** on behalf of England, the English were looking for space to settle. 

They finally found their footing a century later:
*   The English established the **first permanent English North American settlement at Jamestown, Virginia, in 1607**.
*   Shortly after, the English settlement at **Plymouth, Massachusetts, was established by the Pilgrims in 1620**.

Unlike the French, the English wanted to farm. And farming requires land. Because of this, **English colonization frequently led to the violent displacement of Native American populations to acquire land for English agricultural development.**

Furthermore, building farms and towns meant clearing forests. The establishment of permanent European agricultural settlements frequently led to **deforestation and the destruction of local North American habitats.**

## 4. The Economic Engine: Mercantilism and Slavery

So, why were kings and queens funding all these expensive voyages? It was driven by an overarching economic philosophy: **Mercantilism**.

> **Mercantilism:** An economic theory driving European colonization that measured national wealth by the accumulation of precious metals (gold and silver) and maintaining a favorable balance of trade (exporting more than you import).

Think of mercantilism like a giant vacuum cleaner. Under these policies, **colonies existed solely to supply raw materials to the mother country and serve as exclusive markets for manufactured goods**. If you lived in an English colony, you sent your cheap raw cotton to England, and you were legally forced to buy expensive English clothes in return. The mother country gets rich; the colony stays dependent.

### The Triangular Trade and Human Cost
The drive for mercantilist wealth led to a massive demand for raw materials. The Americas were incredibly fertile, but turning that land into wealth required intensive labor. European demand for labor-intensive cash crops like **tobacco and sugar** drove the tragic, large-scale importation of **enslaved African laborers** to the Americas.

This brutal economic loop created what we call the **Triangular Trade**:

> **The Triangular Trade:** A maritime network connecting Europe, Africa, and the Americas that facilitated the exchange of manufactured goods, enslaved people, and raw materials.

1. **Europe to Africa:** European ships brought manufactured goods (guns, cloth, iron) to Africa to trade for enslaved people.
2. **Africa to the Americas (The Middle Passage):** Enslaved African people were brutally transported to the Americas to perform forced labor.
3. **The Americas to Europe:** The products of that forced labor—raw materials like sugar, tobacco, and cotton—were shipped back to Europe to fuel the mercantilist economy.
