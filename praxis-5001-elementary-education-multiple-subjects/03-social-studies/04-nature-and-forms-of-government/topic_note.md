Welcome to one of the most fascinating questions in [human history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_history): *Who is in charge, and why do we let them tell us what to do?*

Imagine you are stranded on an [island](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Island) with three hundred people. The first few days, you might survive on goodwill. But eventually, someone is going to steal someone else’s coconuts. At that moment, you realize a harsh truth: **[anarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anarchy)**—the complete absence of any governing body or established [political authority](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Authority)—might sound like ultimate [freedom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liberty), but it is a terrible way to organize a [society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Society). 

To survive, you need rules. To enforce rules, you need a system. Let's look at the incredible, messy, and brilliant ways [human beings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human) have solved the problem of organizing [power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_power). 

---

## Part I: The Flavor of Power (Comparing [Forms of Government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Form_of_government))

Throughout [history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History), humanity has tried out quite a few "engines" of [government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government). The primary difference between these engines is quite simple: *Where does the [political power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_power) actually rest?* Let's look at the menu.

### The Rule of the Few
Often, power consolidates into the hands of a tiny fraction of the [population](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Population). 

*   **[Monarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monarchy):** This is a form of government ruled by a single [sovereign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sovereignty) leader such as a [king or queen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monarch). 
    *   Historically, we saw the **[absolute monarch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_monarchy)**, a leader who holds completely unrestricted political power over the [state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_%28polity%29) and its people. (Think [Louis XIV of France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_XIV): *"[I am the state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L%27%C3%89tat%2C_c%27est_moi)."*)

![King Louis XIV of France famously embodied the concept of an absolute monarchy, holding unrestricted authority over his state.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Louis_XIV_of_France.jpg)

    *   However, most modern monarchs don't operate this way. Today, we mostly see the **[constitutional monarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitutional_monarchy)**, where a [constitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution) or [parliamentary system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliamentary_system) strictly limits the political power of the sovereign. The monarch becomes a [cultural symbol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_symbol), while the actual governing is done by [elected officials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Representative_democracy).
*   **[Oligarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oligarchy):** What if you don't have a king, but rather a small, [elite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elite) group of individuals? That's an oligarchy. Political power rests entirely with these few—often the [wealthiest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plutocracy) or the [military elite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Military_junta).

![In an oligarchy, political power rests with a small elite, such as the Chilean military junta that seized control of the government in 1973.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Junta_Militar_de_Pinochet.JPG)

*   **[Theocracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theocracy):** If the ruling class claims [divine authority](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divine_right_of_kings), you have a theocracy. This is a form of government where [religious leaders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clergy) rule the state in the name of a specific [deity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deity).
*   **[Dictatorship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dictatorship):** Here is where power gets profoundly ugly. A dictatorship is an [authoritarian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Authoritarianism) form of government where a single leader or small group holds absolute power. [Dictators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dictator) live in constant fear of losing control. Because of this [paranoia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paranoia), dictatorships routinely outlaw organized [political opposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_opposition). Furthermore, to maintain the illusion of absolute [mandate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mandate_%28politics%29) without risking actual defeat, dictatorships generally prohibit [free and fair democratic elections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_and_fair_election). 

![Dictatorships consolidate absolute power into the hands of an autocratic leader or group, routinely relying on fear, military force, and the suppression of political opposition to maintain control.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Historical_dictators.jpg)

### The Rule of the Many: [Democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democracy)
Now, let's look at the radical alternative. What if the engine is powered by the people themselves? 

> A **[democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democracy)** is a form of government where political power rests with the people. 

But *how* the people exercise that power changes everything. 

In a **[direct democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direct_democracy)**, [citizens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Citizenship) vote directly on proposed [laws](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law) and government actions. If the town needs a new bridge, every single citizen drops a stone in a bucket to vote yes or no. This works beautifully in an ancient [Athenian courtyard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Athenian_democracy), but it is a logistical nightmare for a massive [nation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nation). You cannot have 330 million people pausing their day to vote on highway maintenance [budgets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government_budget).

![In ancient Athens, citizens practiced direct democracy by assembling and voting directly on legislation themselves, a system that works for small city-states but becomes logistically impossible for large modern nations.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Discurso_funebre_pericles.PNG)

The brilliant workaround? The **[representative democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Representative_democracy)**. In this system, citizens elect leaders to make laws and govern on behalf of the [populace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Population). 

**The [United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States) operates as a representative democracy.** More specifically, we are a **[republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic)**, which is simply a specific type of representative democracy lacking a monarch as the [head of state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Head_of_state). We don't have a king; we have elected representatives holding the levers of power for a designated time.

### Quick Comparison Matrix
| Form of Government | Where does the power rest? | Defining Characteristic |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **[Anarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anarchy)** | Nowhere | Complete absence of political authority. |
| **[Absolute Monarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_monarchy)**| Single sovereign | Unrestricted power held by a [King/Queen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monarch). |
| **[Dictatorship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dictatorship)** | Single authoritarian leader/group | Prohibits [free elections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_and_fair_election); outlaws opposition. |
| **[Oligarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oligarchy)** | Small elite group | Wealthy/military elite hold the reins. |
| **[Theocracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theocracy)** | Religious leaders | Ruled in the name of a specific deity. |
| **[Direct Democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direct_democracy)** | The populace directly | Citizens vote directly on all laws. |
| **[Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic)** | The populace via representatives | Representative democracy with no monarch. |

## Part II: The Architecture of the U.S. Machine ([Separation of Powers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separation_of_powers))

When the [Framers of the United States Constitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Founding_Fathers_of_the_United_States) sat down to build their new government, they were terrified of creating a new dictatorship or absolute monarchy. They understood a fundamental rule of [human nature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_nature): *anyone given absolute power will eventually abuse it.*

Their solution was a masterstroke of political engineering. The [United States government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federal_government_of_the_United_States) is divided into three branches to prevent any single group from gaining absolute power. 

![The United States government divides power across three co-equal branches—legislative, executive, and judicial—to prevent any single branch from consolidating absolute control.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Separation_of_powers.png)

### The Three Branches
Let's look at the three distinct gears of the federal government.

1.  **The [Legislative Branch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legislature):** This branch is responsible for creating and passing [statutory laws](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statutory_law). 
    *   **The [United States Congress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Congress)** serves as the federal legislative branch.
    *   To keep the legislature itself from getting too powerful, Congress is divided into two separate chambers: the **[Senate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Senate)** and the **[House of Representatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives)**.
    *   *The [Power of the Purse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_of_the_purse):* This branch is incredibly powerful because the legislative branch controls the [federal budget](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_federal_budget) and the allocation of all government funds. You can't run a country without money, and only Congress has the checkbook.

![The United States Capitol building is the seat of the legislative branch, housing both the Senate and the House of Representatives.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/United_States_Capitol_-_west_front.jpg)

2.  **The [Executive Branch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Executive_%28government%29):** You can write all the laws you want, but they are useless if no one enforces them. The executive branch is responsible for enforcing and administering the laws. 
    *   The **[President of the United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_of_the_United_States)** serves as the head of the federal executive branch.
3.  **The [Judicial Branch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Judiciary):** When laws are enforced, arguments will inevitably arise about what the laws actually mean. The judicial branch is responsible for interpreting laws and evaluating their [constitutionality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitutionality). 
    *   The **[United States Supreme Court](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States)** is the highest authority in the federal judicial branch. 

### Checks and Balances: The Great Tug-of-War
Now, here is the real genius of the system. You don't just put these three branches in separate rooms and let them work. You pit them against one another. 

> The **[system of checks and balances](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separation_of_powers)** allows each government branch to limit the political power of the other branches. 

Think of it as a [rock-paper-scissors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rock_paper_scissors) game built into the Constitution. Let's watch it in action:

*   **Passing a Law:** Congress writes and passes a law. But the President can say, "No, I refuse." The President has the power to [veto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Veto) legislation passed by Congress. 
*   **The Rebound:** But the President isn't a king! Congress can override a presidential veto with a [two-thirds majority vote](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supermajority) in both legislative chambers.
*   **The Judicial Trump Card:** What if Congress and the President agree on a law that violates the [fundamental rights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_rights) of the people? The Supreme Court can declare legislative acts passed by Congress [unconstitutional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unconstitutional). 
    *   > **[Judicial review](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Judicial_review_in_the_United_States)** is the power of the courts to invalidate unconstitutional laws and executive actions.
*   **Staffing the Courts:** So, if the Supreme Court is so powerful, who gets to pick the judges? The President has the specific power to nominate [federal judges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_federal_judge) and [Supreme Court justices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associate_Justice_of_the_Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States). 
*   **The Senate's Filter:** However, the President can't just pick their best friend. The Senate must officially [confirm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Advice_and_consent) presidential nominations for federal judgeships.
*   **The Ultimate Penalty:** What if the President or a judge starts committing [high crimes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High_crimes_and_misdemeanors)? Congress possesses the authority to [impeach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Impeachment_in_the_United_States) and remove the President for severe misconduct. Similarly, Congress possesses the authority to impeach and remove federal judges for severe misconduct. 

Every branch is holding a leash that is attached to the other two branches. It is intentionally slow, intentionally argumentative, and wonderfully designed to prevent [tyranny](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tyrant).

## Part III: The Layer Cake of Authority ([Federalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federalism))

Now we must zoom out. We have a beautifully balanced national government in [Washington D.C.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Washington%2C_D.C.), but we also have states. Who is in charge of what? The answer is a system called federalism.

> **[Federalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federalism_in_the_United_States)** is a system of government dividing political power between a national government and regional state governments. 

![Federalism geographically divides political power. Countries indicated in green operate under federal systems, sharing sovereignty between a central national government and regional state or provincial governments.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Map_of_unitary_and_federal_states.svg)

Imagine political power as a giant set of tools. The U.S. Constitution divides these tools into three specific toolboxes: [Enumerated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enumerated_powers_%28United_States%29), [Reserved](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reserved_powers), and [Concurrent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concurrent_powers).

### 1. Enumerated Powers (Federal Only)
The United States Constitution delegates specific powers exclusively to the national federal government. We call these **[enumerated powers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enumerated_powers_%28United_States%29)** because they are specifically listed, or *enumerated*, in the text. 

Why give them only to the federal government? Because some things require national uniformity. 
*   The federal government holds the exclusive power to [coin and print money](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Money_creation). (Imagine the chaos if [Ohio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ohio) printed "Ohio-bucks" and [Nevada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nevada) printed "Nevada-dollars").

![The power to print and coin currency is an enumerated power delegated exclusively to the federal government to ensure a functional, uniform national economy.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/US_one_dollar_bill%2C_obverse%2C_series_2009.jpg)

*   The federal government holds the exclusive power to [declare war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Declaration_of_war_by_the_United_States).
*   The federal government holds the exclusive power to regulate [interstate and foreign commerce](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commerce_Clause) ([trade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trade) that crosses state lines or international borders).

### 2. Reserved Powers (State Only)
If a power isn't specifically given to the federal government in the Constitution, who gets it? The **[Tenth Amendment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tenth_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution)** answers this clearly: it reserves all non-enumerated powers to the [state governments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_governments_of_the_United_States) or the citizens. 

These are known as **[reserved powers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reserved_powers)**, which are the political authorities held exclusively by individual state governments. These powers generally handle the daily, localized safety and morality of the people.
*   State governments possess the authority to establish and regulate [public education](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_education) systems. (This is why high school graduation requirements differ in [New York](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_York_%28state%29) versus [Texas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Texas)).
*   State governments possess the authority to issue [driver licenses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Driver%27s_license_in_the_United_States) and [marriage certificates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marriage_certificate). 
*   State governments possess the authority to regulate commercial activities occurring entirely within state borders.

### 3. Concurrent Powers (Shared)
Sometimes, both layers of government need the exact same tool to function. **[Concurrent powers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concurrent_powers)** are governmental authorities shared simultaneously by both the federal and state governments. 
*   Both the federal and state governments hold the concurrent power to levy and collect financial [taxes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taxation_in_the_United_States). (Look at your pay stub—both the [IRS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internal_Revenue_Service) and your state take a slice!).
*   Both the federal and state governments hold the concurrent power to borrow money.
*   Both the federal and state governments hold the concurrent power to establish judicial [court systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Judiciary) (which is why we have both [Federal courts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federal_judiciary_of_the_United_States) and [State courts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_court_%28United_States%29)).

### The Supremacy Clause: Who Wins a Fight?
What happens if the state government writes a law that directly contradicts a federal law? The Constitution provides a very clear referee. 

> The **[Supremacy Clause](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supremacy_Clause)** of the United States Constitution establishes federal laws as the highest law of the land. 

Because of this clause, **[federal law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federal_law_of_the_United_States) permanently overrides [state law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_law_%28United_States%29) in the event of a direct legal conflict between the two.** If Congress passes a valid federal law, a state cannot simply pass a law ignoring it. The federal law reigns supreme.

### The Boots on the Ground: Local Governments
We've talked about the national level and the state level. But what about the people fixing the [potholes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pothole) in your neighborhood? 

**[Local governments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_government_in_the_United_States)** include [city councils](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/City_council), [county commissions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/County_commission), and [municipal authorities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Municipality). 

Here is the most crucial thing to understand about them: *The U.S. Constitution doesn't even mention local governments.* Therefore, local governments derive their legal authority directly from their respective state governments. A city only exists legally because the state allows it to exist. 

![Local governments, such as city councils, derive their authority entirely from state governments and are responsible for managing daily municipal issues and community policies.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Fullerton_City_Council.jpg)

While they sit at the "bottom" of the federalist structure, local governments impact your daily life the most. 
*   Local municipal governments are typically responsible for managing local [police](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Police) and [fire protection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fire_department) services.
*   Local municipal governments are typically responsible for providing residential [trash collection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Waste_collection) services.

### In Summary for the [Praxis Exam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Praxis_test):
When you sit down to take the test, remember the mechanics of the engine! 
*   **Who holds the power?** Democracies empower the people; dictatorships empower the authoritarian few. 
*   **How does the US prevent tyranny?** Through three branches checking and balancing each other. 
*   **How do Washington and the States share the country?** Through federalism—splitting the tools into enumerated (federal), reserved (state), and concurrent (shared) boxes, with the Supremacy Clause acting as the ultimate tiebreaker.
