Look, we throw around the word "classical" all the time. [Classical music](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_music), [classical literature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classicism), [classical architecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_architecture). But when [historians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History) talk about **[Classical Civilizations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_antiquity)**—specifically [Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Egypt), [Greece](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greece), and [Rome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Rome)—they aren't just talking about old stuff that looks pretty in a [museum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Museum). They are talking about the *operating systems* of the [modern world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modern_history). 

If you want to understand how [human beings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human) figured out how to live together in massive numbers, how to govern themselves, and how to manipulate the physical world to build things that last millennia, you have to look at these three civilizations. They were the ultimate problem solvers. 

For the [Praxis exam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Praxis_test), you don't just need to memorize a list of vocabulary words; you need to understand *how these societies functioned* and *what they left behind*. Let's break them down.

## [Ancient Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Egypt): The Engine of the [Nile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nile) and the Quest for Eternity

When you think of [Ancient Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Egypt), I want you to think of a [society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Society) obsessed with order, predictability, and cheating death. And all of it—their [politics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics), their [social structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_structure), their [religion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Egyptian_religion)—was built on a foundation of mud.

### The Geography of Life
[Ancient Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Egypt) was located along the **[Nile River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nile) in [northeastern Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Africa)**. In the [ancient world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_history), [geography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography) was destiny. The [Egyptian civilization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Egypt) thrived for one spectacular reason: the **predictable annual [flooding of the Nile River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flooding_of_the_Nile) deposited nutrient-rich [silt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silt) for [agricultural](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agriculture_in_ancient_Egypt) use**. 

Think of the [Nile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nile) as a giant, naturally occurring conveyor belt of [fertilizer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fertilizer). Because this flooding happened like clockwork every single year, the [Egyptians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Egyptians) didn’t have to struggle constantly just to survive. This [agricultural surplus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agricultural_surplus) gave them the free time and resources to build a highly complex [culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Culture). 

![An aerial view of the Nile River in modern-day Luxor, Egypt, demonstrating the stark contrast between the lush, fertile river valley created by silt deposits and the surrounding arid desert.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Vallee_fertile_du_Nil_a_Louxor.jpg)

### Gods, Kings, and Theocrats
So, who was in charge of this well-fed society? 

> **[Theocracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theocracy):** A [political system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_system) where [religious leaders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clergy) or divine figures hold supreme governmental authority.

The ancient Egyptian political system was organized as a **[theocracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theocracy)**. They didn't have a [separation of church and state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separation_of_church_and_state); the church *was* the state. The **ancient Egyptian [pharaohs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pharaoh) acted as both absolute [political monarchs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_monarchy) and divine [religious](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divine_right_of_kings) figures**. To the average citizen, the [pharaoh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pharaoh) wasn't just a king making laws; he was literally a [god](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deity) walking the earth, responsible for making sure the sun rose and the river flooded.

![A statue of the Old Kingdom pharaoh Khafre being embraced by the falcon god Horus, visually reinforcing the theocratic belief that Egyptian pharaohs were divine figures walking the earth.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/%C3%84gyptisches_Museum_Kairo_2016-03-29_Chephren_03.jpg)

### The Human Pyramid: Social Structure
Because the pharaoh was a god, society below him was incredibly rigid. **Ancient Egyptian society operated under a rigid [social hierarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_stratification).** 

> **Rigid Social Hierarchy:** A structured [class system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_class) where [upward mobility](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_mobility) is highly restricted. If you were born a [farmer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Farmer), you died a farmer. 

At the very top were the [pharaohs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pharaoh), followed by [priests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Egyptian_religion) and [nobles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nobility). But the vast majority of people were at the bottom. **The bottom tier of ancient Egyptian society consisted primarily of [peasant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peasant) farmers and [enslaved people](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slavery_in_ancient_Egypt)**. These were the masses who worked the fields and provided the immense physical labor required for Egypt's famous building projects. 

### Writing and the Afterlife
The Egyptians didn't just want to build a great society for today; they wanted to take it with them into eternity. 
* **[Mummification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mummy):** **Ancient Egyptians practiced [mummification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mummy) to preserve human bodies for the [afterlife](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Egyptian_afterlife_beliefs).** They believed the [soul](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soul) needed a physical vessel to return to, so they developed extraordinary chemical and medical techniques to stop the human body from decaying.
* **[Pyramids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egyptian_pyramids):** What good is a preserved body if it doesn't have a secure, magnificent home? **Ancient Egyptians constructed large [pyramid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egyptian_pyramids) structures to serve as monumental [tombs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tomb) for deceased [pharaohs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pharaoh).** These weren't palaces; they were giant, stone immortality machines.

![The Pyramids of Giza were constructed as massive monumental tombs to protect the mummified bodies of pharaohs and ensure their passage into the afterlife.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/All_Gizah_Pyramids.jpg)

* **Communication:** To run a [bureaucracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bureaucracy) this massive, you need a way to keep records. **[Hieroglyphics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egyptian_hieroglyphs) was the formal [writing system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Writing_system) used in ancient Egypt containing complex pictorial symbols.** But carving stone is tedious. So, **ancient Egyptians invented a paper-like writing material derived from the [pith](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pith) of the [papyrus plant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyperus_papyrus).** We literally get the English word "paper" from [papyrus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Papyrus)!

![A preserved sheet of ancient Egyptian papyrus, created from the pith of the cyperus papyrus plant to serve as a lightweight and efficient surface for bureaucratic record-keeping.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Papyrus.jpg)

## [Ancient Greece](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greece): Mountains, Minds, and Marathons

If Egypt was a unified, predictable machine, [Ancient Greece](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greece) was a chaotic, brilliant explosion of competing ideas. Why? Once again, blame geography. 

### The Fragmented World
Unlike the wide-open [river valley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Valley) of Egypt, **the mountainous terrain of ancient Greece prevented political unification into a single [empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empire).** You couldn't just march an [army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Army) across Greece easily. People lived in isolated valleys and on scattered [islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Island).

Because of this, **Ancient Greece was organized geographically into independent [city-states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/City-state) known as [poleis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polis)** (the singular is *[polis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polis)*—which is where we get the word "[politics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics)"). Each polis was like its own miniature country, with its own laws, customs, and [military](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Military). The two most famous were **[Athens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_Athens)** and **[Sparta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sparta)**, and they couldn't have been more different.

![The mountainous geography and scattered islands of the Greek mainland naturally fragmented the region into independent, self-governing city-states rather than a single unified empire.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Ancient_Regions_Mainland_Greece.png)

### A Tale of Two City-States: Athens vs. Sparta

| Feature | Athens | Sparta |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Government Type** | **[Direct Democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direct_democracy)** | **[Oligarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oligarchy)** |
| **Who Had Power?** | **The ancient Greek city-state of [Athens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_Athens) developed the first known [direct democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direct_democracy).** | **The ancient Greek city-state of [Sparta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sparta) functioned politically as a militaristic [oligarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oligarchy).** |
| **Cultural Focus** | [Philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek_philosophy), arts, naval trade, and individual [civic participation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civic_engagement). | Extreme [military discipline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Military_discipline), [warfare](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/War), and state control. |

Let's look closely at these two vital political concepts:

> **[Direct Democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direct_democracy):** A political mechanism where eligible [citizens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Citizenship) vote on [legislation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legislation) themselves rather than through representatives. In Athens, if a law needed passing, thousands of citizens would gather on a hill and physically vote on it. It was messy, loud, and revolutionary.

> **[Oligarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oligarchy):** A governmental power structure where control rests with a small number of privileged individuals. In Sparta, a tiny, elite military class made all the decisions. 

### The Life of the Mind and the Body
The [Greeks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greeks) weren't just fighting each other; they were fundamentally rethinking human existence. 
* **[Philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophy):** Instead of just accepting that "the gods did it," **ancient Greek [philosophers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek_philosophy) like [Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socrates), [Plato](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plato), and [Aristotle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle) introduced formal systems of philosophical logic and [reason](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reason).** They asked *why* things happen and demanded evidence. They literally invented the academic method we use today.

![Detail from Raphael's fresco The School of Athens depicting Plato (left) and Aristotle (right), two foundational Greek philosophers who established systems of logic and reason that profoundly influenced Western academia.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Sanzio_01_Plato_Aristotle.jpg)

* **Athletics:** But they also valued physical perfection. **The ancient Greeks established the original [Olympic Games](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Olympic_Games) as an athletic and religious festival to honor [Zeus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zeus).** It was a way for rival city-states to compete for glory without going to [war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/War).

### Building with Mathematics
When it came to building, the Greeks loved [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry) and proportion. **Ancient Greek [architects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Architecture_of_ancient_Greece) extensively utilized vertical stone support pillars known as [columns](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Column).** 

If you look at government buildings in [Washington D.C.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Washington%2C_D.C.), you are looking at Greek ideas. There are three main styles you need to know: **The [Doric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doric_order), [Ionic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ionic_order), and [Corinthian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corinthian_order) styles represent the three main [architectural orders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_order) of ancient Greek columns.** (Doric is simple and plain, Ionic has scrolls at the top, and Corinthian is highly decorated with carved leaves). 

![The three main classical orders of ancient Greek columns: the plain and sturdy Doric (left), the scrolled Ionic (center), and the highly decorated Corinthian (right).](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Schema_Saeulenordnungen.jpg)

The ultimate masterpiece of this style? **The [Parthenon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parthenon) is a prominent ancient Greek [temple](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek_temple) built in Athens to honor the goddess [Athena](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Athena).** It sits on a hill overlooking the city, a perfect mathematical monument.

![The Parthenon in Athens, a temple dedicated to the goddess Athena, remains the most prominent surviving example of classical Greek proportion and column-based architecture.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Parthenon_(30276156187).jpg)

## [Ancient Rome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Rome): The Engineers of Empire

If Greece was the classroom of the ancient world, Rome was the construction site and the [courthouse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Courthouse). **The civilization of ancient Rome originated on the [Italian Peninsula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italian_Peninsula)**, but it would eventually conquer the entire [Mediterranean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mediterranean_Basin). How? Through incredible organization, unmatched engineering, and the [rule of law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rule_of_law).

### Inventing the Republic
Rome originally had [kings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Kingdom), but they hated the idea of a [monarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monarchy). **Ancient Rome established a [republican form of government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Republic) following the overthrow of its early monarchy.** 

You must understand the difference between Athens and Rome here. Athens had a *[direct democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direct_democracy)*. Rome was a [republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic).

> **[Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic):** A political system where citizens elect [representatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Representative_democracy) to govern and make laws on their behalf. 

You don't vote on the taxes yourself; you vote for a guy who votes on the taxes. **The [Roman Senate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Senate) was the primary legislative and advisory council of the [Roman Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Republic).** 

![A 19th-century fresco depicting a debate in the Roman Senate, the primary legislative and advisory body of the Roman Republic composed of powerful representatives.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Cicero_Denounces_Catiline_in_the_Roman_Senate_by_Cesare_Maccari.png)

However, not all Romans were equal. **Roman society was divided politically into wealthy landowning [patricians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patrician_%28ancient_Rome%29) and working-class [plebeians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plebeians).** The patricians had most of the power, and the plebeians were constantly fighting for a fair shake. 

### The Rule of Law
To keep society functioning, the Romans realized that laws couldn't just be whatever the wealthy patrician [judges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Judge) felt like saying that day. You had to write them down. 

**The [Twelve Tables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Twelve_Tables) constituted the foundational text of [codified](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Codification_%28law%29) ancient [Roman law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_law).** 

> **[Codified Written Law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Codification_%28law%29):** Provides a standardized legal mechanism intended to apply uniformly to all citizens. 

Once the laws were carved into bronze tablets (the Twelve Tables) and posted in the public square, everyone—rich or poor—knew exactly what the rules were. This is the basis of our modern [legal system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legal_system)!

![Roman citizens examining the newly implemented Twelve Tables. By physically carving and displaying these codified laws, the Roman Republic ensured standard legal rules applied to all citizens.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Twelve_Tables_Engraving.jpg)

### Engineering a Continent
The Romans were the greatest [engineers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_engineering) of the ancient world. They didn't just conquer territories; they physically wired them together with [infrastructure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infrastructure).

* **[Aqueducts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_aqueduct):** Cities need water. **Ancient Roman engineers built extensive [masonry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Masonry) [aqueduct](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_aqueduct) networks across their territories.** 
  > **[Aqueduct](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aqueduct_%28water_supply%29):** An engineering mechanism that utilizes a precise downward [gradient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grade_%28slope%29) and [gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity) to transport fresh water into urban centers. 
  Imagine a stone water slide, sometimes dropping only an inch per mile, carrying millions of gallons of fresh water from the mountains straight to [public baths](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermae) and fountains. 

![The Pont du Gard in southern France is a surviving Roman masonry aqueduct that utilized precise downward gradients and gravity to transport fresh water to urban centers.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Pont_du_Gard_BLS.jpg)

* **The Arch and Concrete:** How did they build such massive structures? Two inventions. First, **ancient Roman [architects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_architecture) widely implemented the rounded [arch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arch) to support massive building loads.** An arch directs the weight of a heavy stone ceiling outward and downward, allowing for huge open spaces. Second, **the ancient Romans invented a highly durable structural building material known as [Roman concrete](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_concrete).** It was so good it could even cure underwater!

![The interior of the Pantheon in Rome showcases the massive, unreinforced dome and arches made possible by the Roman invention of durable structural concrete.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Rome-Pantheon-Interieur1.jpg)

* **The [Colosseum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colosseum):** Using arches and concrete, they built monstrous [stadiums](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stadium). **The Colosseum is a massive stone [amphitheater](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_amphitheatre) constructed during the [Roman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Empire) for public entertainment spectacles** like [gladiator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gladiator) fights.
* **The Highway System:** To control their massive empire, **the [Roman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Empire) constructed an extensive network of paved stone [roads](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_roads) across [Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Europe) and the Mediterranean.** 
  > **The [Road Network](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_roads):** Functioned as a logistical mechanism to facilitate rapid military deployment and robust regional [trade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trade). 
  If a rebellion broke out, the [Roman legions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_legion) could march there at terrifying speeds on perfectly paved, drained, straight stone highways. 

![The Appian Way (Via Appia) was one of the earliest and most strategically important Roman roads, engineered with paved stone to facilitate rapid military deployment and trade across the empire.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Appian_Way.jpg)

### The Roman Legacy
Because of this incredible organization and military might, the Romans achieved the **[Pax Romana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pax_Romana)—a two-century period of relative peace and internal stability within the [Roman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Empire).** 

![At its peak under Emperor Trajan in 117 AD, the Roman Empire encompassed the entire Mediterranean basin, enabling the two-century period of relative stability known as the Pax Romana.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/RomanEmpire_117.svg)

And long after the physical empire fell, their culture survived. **The [Latin language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin) spoken in ancient Rome served as the foundational root for modern [Romance languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Romance_languages)** ([Spanish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spanish_language), [French](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_language), [Italian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italian_language), [Portuguese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portuguese_language), and [Romanian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Romanian_language)).

### Professor's Summary for the Praxis:
When you are taking the exam, remember the "vibes" of these three civilizations:
1. **[Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Egypt):** Predictability. [Theocratic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theocracy) god-kings ([pharaohs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pharaoh)) ruling a rigid [social hierarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_stratification), fueled by the flooding [Nile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nile), building [pyramids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egyptian_pyramids) for the [afterlife](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Egyptian_afterlife_beliefs). 
2. **[Greece](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greece):** Fragmented brilliance. Mountainous terrain leading to independent [city-states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/City-state). The birthplace of philosophical reason, [direct democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direct_democracy) ([Athens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_Athens)), and classical [columns](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Column). 
3. **[Rome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Rome):** Law and infrastructure. A [Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Republic) governed by a [Senate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Senate), utilizing codified laws ([Twelve Tables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Twelve_Tables)), and engineering mastery ([arches](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arch), [concrete](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_concrete), [aqueducts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_aqueduct), and [roads](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_roads)) to conquer and stabilize a vast [empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empire).
