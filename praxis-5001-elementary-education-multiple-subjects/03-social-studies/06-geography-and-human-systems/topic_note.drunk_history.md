Now, if you’re planning a woodland heist and need to know exactly how steep the getaway hill is, you grab a topographic map. And if you want the juicy gossip—like where all the money is hidden, or where the weather is the most brutal—you pull out a thematic map. It throws standard geography out the window and focuses on a specific topic such as population density, climate, or economic activity. 

But a map is just a pretty picture of a ransom note unless you have the decoder ring. You absolutely need a map legend, which is the little box that explains the specific symbols, colors, and lines used on a given map. You also need a compass rose, that little starburst icon that indicates the cardinal directions of North, South, East, and West on a map, so you don't run straight into the arms of the cops. And don't ever forget the map scale! It represents the proportional relationship between the distance on a map and the actual distance on Earth. Now, how do you track someone in this chaotic world? Two ways. You can use relative location, which describes the position of a place in relation to other nearby places or landmarks. Like, "The safehouse is two blocks past the greasy diner, right next to the bowling alley." It's casual. It's how normal people talk. But if you’re calling in an emergency helicopter—or just using your phone's navigation app—you need absolute location. This is the exact position of a place on Earth using a mathematical coordinate system. No guesswork. Boom. Pinned. Today, we pull this off using the Global Positioning System, which relies on a network of orbital satellites to calculate the exact absolute location of a receiver on Earth. It’s basically a bunch of robotic space-snitches triangulating exactly where your phone is hiding.

But before we had space-snitches, we had to wrap the Earth in an invisible laser grid to trap absolute locations. First, we have the horizontal lasers, the latitude lines, which run horizontally around the Earth parallel to the Equator. Think of them as the rungs of a ladder you climb to get away from the planet's waistline. Right at the very middle, the bellyband of the globe, is the Equator, a line of latitude located at exactly zero degrees. This massive belt divides the Earth perfectly into the Northern Hemisphere and the Southern Hemisphere. And let me tell you, the real estate around the Equator is cooking. If you climb up a bit, you hit the Tropic of Cancer, a line of latitude located at 23.5 degrees north of the Equator. Climb down instead, and you hit the Tropic of Capricorn, a line of latitude located at 23.5 degrees south of the Equator. Everything squeezed between those two is the VIP sun-tanning lounge of the planet.

Then you have the vertical lasers dropping down like prison bars. These are the longitude lines, which run vertically from the North Pole to the South Pole. The granddaddy of them all is the Prime Meridian, a line of longitude located at exactly zero degrees, running right through England because they called dibs centuries ago. Just like the Equator chops the world top and bottom, the Prime Meridian divides the Earth into the Eastern Hemisphere and the Western Hemisphere. Cross those latitude and longitude lines, and nobody can hide from you.

Now, humans, being the neurotic control freaks we are, can't just leave the Earth alone. We slice it up into psychological factions called regions. First, you have your formal region. This is a geographic area defined by distinct physical or cultural characteristics. It’s hard facts—like the freezing tundra or the state boundaries of Ohio. You cross it, you know it. Then you have your functional region. This is a geographic area organized around a central hub or focal point. Think of a pizza delivery zone or the broadcast reach of a local radio station. It only exists because of the central hub; if the pizza shop burns down, the region vanishes into thin air! And finally, the most dramatic one, the perceptual region. This is a geographic area defined by the subjective feelings and attitudes of people. It's totally in our heads! Like, where exactly does "The American South" end and "The Midwest" begin? We choke the river and make it charge our smartphones! And if the trees are in our way?
