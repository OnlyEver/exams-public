Look at this cocktail napkin, Dave. Just look at it. You think you just pick up a pen one day and write a gripping memoir? No! Getting a human brain to write is like watching a complete amateur stumble their way through the most cutthroat, high-stakes reality TV cooking competition on earth, hosted by the grammar mafia! 

It’s what we in the biz call the developmental continuum of writing. This continuum describes the deeply predictable stages students progress through, evolving from their very first chaotic, initial mark-making all the way up to fluent spelling. It’s a culinary journey from total anarchy to a three-Michelin-star kitchen.

Let’s start in the lawless early days of a child's education. Preschool and Pre-Kindergarten writing instruction typically targets the ground-floor basics: drawing, scribbling, mock letters, and random letter strings. 

First up in this kitchen is the picture writing stage. This is the absolute earliest recognized stage of writing development in early childhood. Imagine a rogue chef who doesn’t even know what a written recipe is, so they just serve you a literal photograph of a chicken. In the picture writing stage, children use illustrations to convey meaning before understanding that letters represent spoken words. They draw a picture of a house and tell you, "This is my epic novel." 

But soon enough, the scribbling stage follows the picture writing stage in early writing development. Our amateur chef is panicking! They are just throwing handfuls of flour and ketchup at the wall! The scribbling stage consists of uncontrolled, random marks on a page. Because it is pure, panicked adrenaline, this scribbling typically lacks directionality—just wild, looping swirls—and trust me, scribbling does not visually resemble conventional print. It’s a glorious kitchen disaster. 

But then, a glimmer of hope! The chef gets a massive reality check called print awareness. Print awareness is the grand foundational understanding that written language actually carries meaning and follows specific spatial rules. The chef realizes the mafia bosses at the judges' table have a system: print awareness includes the understanding that English text is read from top to bottom, and it also includes the understanding that English text is read from left to right. 

Because of this breakthrough, directional scribbling emerges when children begin making marks in a linear, left-to-right pattern! They are still just throwing flour at the wall, but now they are throwing it in neat, horizontal rows! This directional scribbling demonstrates an emerging awareness of the physical spatial conventions of English print. It’s a civilized mess. 

Next, they try to fake it to make it with the mock letters stage. This stage involves the creation of shapes that resemble manuscript alphabet letters. Our desperate chef starts molding mashed potatoes into little abstract shapes that *look* like fancy French cuisine, but they aren't. Mock letters are individual squiggles that are not actual alphabet letters—a triangle with a tail, a circle with five legs. But don’t you dare laugh! The creation of mock letters shows a child's profound understanding that written text consists of distinct, separate symbols. They realize, "Hey, sentences are made of individual chunks, just like a meal is made of separate ingredients!" 

Then comes the final Preschool and Pre-K hurdle: the pre-phonemic stage of writing, which is also frequently referred to as the random letters stage. The chef finally found the pantry with the real ingredients! This stage involves producing strings of actual alphabet letters. But here is the tragic, hilarious irony: in the pre-phonemic stage, children write letter strings without any connection to the spoken sounds those letters make. They are just dumping cinnamon, bleach, and raw onions into a pot and calling it a cake. For example, a child writing the random letters "RzxTQO" to represent the word 'bottle' is demonstrating the pre-phonemic stage of writing. They know the letters exist, but the audio-visual recipe is a total mystery.

Enter Kindergarten! The stakes are raised. Kindergarten writing instruction generally addresses the massive transition from random letters to semi-phonetic and phonetic spelling. It’s boot camp time.

Suddenly, the chef connects the flavor to the ingredient. Boom! The semi-phonetic stage of writing marks the beginning of a child's understanding of sound-symbol relationships! But they are rationing their supplies to survive the competition. In the semi-phonetic stage, children often use only one or two letters to represent entire words. They are taking shortcuts! Children in the semi-phonetic stage typically use initial and final consonants to represent words, completely ignoring the expensive, middle-aisle ingredients. That’s right, children in the semi-phonetic stage frequently omit vowels from written words. A child writing "bt" to represent the word 'bottle' is demonstrating the semi-phonetic stage of writing. They gave you the bread, they gave you the mustard, but they left out the meat!

Now, let me give you a piece of advice as a mentor. When you see this, you must embrace invented spelling. Invented spelling is a developmental process where children attempt to write words based on current phonetic knowledge. You don't scream at them for a misspelled word; encouraging invented spelling allows young writers to focus on expressing ideas rather than getting paralyzed trying to find the exact conventional spelling of every single word. Let them cook! Let them express the flavor!

Soon, their palate expands and they hit the phonetic stage of writing. This involves spelling words strictly according to the sounds the writer hears in the spoken word. In the phonetic stage, children use letters to represent almost all the distinct sounds in a spoken word. They hear every crunch and sizzle! But English is a rigged game, Dave. It’s full of silent letters and weird rules borrowed from Latin and French. Because of this, phonetic stage writers often misspell words due to an exclusive reliance on phonetic sounds rather than standard orthographic rules. A child writing "botl" for the word 'bottle' is demonstrating the phonetic stage of writing. A child writing "kat" for the word 'cat' is demonstrating the phonetic stage of writing. They followed the phonetic recipe perfectly, but they didn’t know the mob boss secretly changed the code!

Now we reach the bridge. First-grade writing instruction typically focuses on moving students from phonetic spelling to transitional spelling. 

The transitional stage of writing bridges phonetic spelling and conventional standard spelling. Our chef is no longer just cooking by ear; in the transitional stage, writers begin to rely on visual memory and common spelling patterns. They remember what the dish is *supposed* to look like! You can spot this stage easily because transitional stage writing typically includes a written vowel in every recorded syllable. They know the mafia's golden rule: every family (syllable) needs a boss (a vowel), even if they pick the wrong one! Furthermore, in the transitional stage, students correctly spell many high-frequency sight words. They've finally memorized the daily specials! However, transitional stage writers still invent spellings for complex words. So, when you look at their paper, transitional stage writing features the application of basic phonetic spellings alongside correctly spelled sight words. A child writing "I hav a pet dog" demonstrates the transitional stage of writing. "I", "a", "pet", and "dog" are perfect sight words, but "hav" is a phonetic invention for a complex word!

Finally, we get to the three-Michelin-star finale. Second-grade and third-grade writing instruction usually focuses on solidifying conventional standard spelling. At this elite level, second-grade and third-grade writing instruction usually focuses on mastering complex orthographic rules. 

This brings us to the standard spelling stage, which represents fluent writing where the vast majority of words are spelled correctly. And just so you know, the standard spelling stage is also commonly known as the conventional spelling stage. At this point, the chef is a master of the house. In the standard spelling stage, writers consistently apply English orthographic rules. They don’t just know the ingredients; they know the history, the chemistry, and the secrets. Standard spelling stage writers correctly utilize prefixes, suffixes, and silent letters. They know exactly where the silent 'e' is buried, and they know exactly how to serve the perfect, grammatically flawless plate! Drink up, Dave, class is dismissed!
