Listen to me, grab that pretzel, and pay attention, because the whole reason anyone ever picks up a pen is basically a tactical strike. You don’t bring a water balloon to a mob war, and you don’t bring a love poem to a health inspection. The golden rule of the written word is that authors choose a specific writing type based on their primary authorial intent. The weapon you choose depends entirely on the damage you want to do to your reader's brain!

Let's say you're going through a spectacularly messy breakup and you want to utterly destroy your ex's reputation in our friend group. You are going to use opinion writing, because opinion writing aims to persuade the reader to adopt a specific viewpoint—specifically, the viewpoint that your ex is absolute garbage. Or maybe you want to escalate things, because opinion writing aims to persuade the reader to take a specific action, like boycotting his favorite coffee shop or keying his car! But how do you launch this attack? You have two flavors of destruction. If you are channeling your inner reality TV star throwing a drink in a screaming match, you use persuasive writing, which frequently incorporates emotional appeals to sway the reader—you use tears, betrayal, and absolute melodrama! But if you are acting like the cold, calculating mob accountant of the relationship, you use argumentative writing, which relies on logical reasoning to support a claim. You don't just cry; argumentative writing requires the presentation of verifiable evidence. You bring the printed text messages! The bank statements! And a good mob lawyer knows the ex will try to defend himself, which is why argumentative essays often address opposing viewpoints through counterarguments. You look right at our friends and say, "I know his mother claims he is a good boy, BUT here is the security footage of him stealing a street sign." 

To build this hit piece, you need an ironclad structure. A key component of opinion writing is a clear statement of the author's claim—your thesis of destruction: "Brad is a menace." But you can't just shout that into the void; opinion writing must include supporting reasons for the stated claim. You tell us he chewed with his mouth open, and he hated puppies. Finally, to lock the jail cell, opinion writing typically includes a concluding statement that reinforces the author's claim, leaving us with a final, ringing, "Therefore, Brad belongs in exile." Notice how I linked that together? Opinion writing frequently utilizes transition words indicating causation. *Because* he chewed loudly, *therefore* he is single, and *consequently* we are ignoring him. Where do we see this carnage in the wild? Subgenres of opinion writing include persuasive essays—like a high schooler's burn book. Subgenres of opinion writing include newspaper editorials, where the town gossips take a public stance on zoning laws. Subgenres of opinion writing include book reviews, where critics ruthlessly tear down a terrible celebrity memoir.

Now, chug your drink, because we are pivoting to a completely different disaster. Imagine a health inspector walks into the kitchen of this very bar right now and finds a catastrophe. The inspector isn't trying to make the chef cry; informative writing aims to educate the reader about a specific topic, like the mold growing in the ice machine. Or, if the inspector needs to force the staff to fix it, explanatory writing aims to clarify a process for the reader, detailing the exact, grueling chemical steps to bleach a deep fryer. The inspector is a robot. Informative writing maintains an objective tone. They don't care if the chef is your buddy; informative writing explicitly avoids the inclusion of the author's personal opinions. It is just a ruthless delivery of reality. 

How do they write the citation? A primary component of informative writing is the clear introduction of a central topic, like "Violation Code 402: Kitchen Infestation." From there, informative writing develops the central topic using facts, like stating there are currently six rats on the counter. Informative writing develops the central topic using formal definitions, clarifying that "a rat is a rodent of the genus Rattus," and informative writing develops the central topic using concrete details, noting the rats are specifically eating the sourdough bread. The inspector doesn't say "yucky bugs." Informative writing often employs domain-specific vocabulary like pathogen, cross-contamination, and foodborne illness. And to pile on the misery, informative writing frequently uses transition words indicating addition: "We found rats; *furthermore*, the fridge is broken; *additionally*, the chef is asleep." 

The inspector organizes this disaster logically to ensure maximum fines. Informative texts can be structured using a compare-and-contrast organizational pattern, looking at the spotless kitchen down the street versus this dumpster fire. Informative texts can be structured using a cause-and-effect organizational pattern, proving the fridge broke and *therefore* the meat spoiled. Informative texts can be structured using a problem-and-solution organizational pattern, declaring we have rats and outlining the exterminator's protocol to fix it. To make the destruction undeniable, informational texts frequently utilize text features like headings to boldly chunk out "Section 4: The Rat King," and informational texts frequently utilize text features like glossaries at the back to define terms like "botulism." They even bring visual proof, because informational texts frequently utilize visual aids like charts—imagine a terrifying pie chart showing exactly what percentage of the kitchen is currently violating federal law! Where else do we find this cold, hard truth? Subgenres of informative writing include research reports on the toxicity of the grease trap, subgenres of informative writing include instructional manuals for the new dishwasher, and subgenres of informative writing include biographies detailing the purely factual, chronological life of the health inspector who ruined our night.

But later... oh, later, when we are safe in our living room, we don't want facts or arguments. We want the drama. We turn to the oldest magic trick in human history. Narrative writing aims to tell a story! Maybe we want to gossip about exactly what just happened with the health inspector, because narrative writing can recount real historical events. Or maybe we are so traumatized by the kitchen that we make up a story about superhero rats taking over the city, because narrative writing can recount imagined fictional experiences. To make me feel like I am back in that doomed kitchen, you have to set the stage. A key component of narrative writing is the establishment of a physical setting, letting us feel the sticky floor of the bar, and just as vital is the establishment of a temporal setting, making us feel the dread of midnight on a Tuesday. Then you have to choose whose eyes we are looking through; narrative writing establishes a specific point of view, like you watching it all go down from your corner stool in the first person. 

But a grimy bar is just an empty room without lunatics to populate it. Narrative writing features the introduction and development of characters—the sweating, panicked chef and the stone-faced inspector. And they can't just stand there! Narrative writing typically follows a structured plot. What drives this chaos forward? Friction! A plot structure in narrative writing usually centers around a conflict—the chef versus the inspector in a battle of wills! And to let us finally breathe again at the end of the night, a plot structure in narrative writing usually includes a resolution of the conflict, ending with the bar getting padlocked and us escaping to a diner. 

How do you bring this wild night to life? Voices! Narrative writing frequently utilizes dialogue to advance the plot—"Drop the spatula!" yelled the inspector, forcing the action forward. And narrative writing frequently utilizes dialogue to reveal character traits—"I... I didn't know the chicken was raw," stuttered the chef, revealing his absolute incompetence. You want me to taste the grossness of that kitchen! Narrative writing relies heavily on sensory details to immerse the reader—the sour stench of the meat, the blinding flash of the police sirens. Narrative writing utilizes descriptive language to paint a mental picture so vivid I can't unsee the grease dripping from the ceiling. And how do we move through the absolute chaos of the evening? Narrative writing sequences events using temporal transition words. *Suddenly*, the rat jumped; *meanwhile*, the bartender ran for the back door; *the next day*, we were all looking for a new place to drink. 

Where do these epic tales live? Subgenres of narrative writing include personal narratives, like the traumatizing story of your night out. Subgenres of narrative writing include short stories, like a quick, punchy fiction about a rat who learns to cook. Subgenres of narrative writing include memoirs, which would be a whole book dedicated to your terrible years working in food service. Subgenres of narrative writing include myths, like the legendary tale of the kitchen god who cursed the deep fryer. And subgenres of narrative writing include fables, a story about a greedy pig who didn't clean his kitchen and learned a harsh moral lesson. 

Now do you see it? Every text is a weapon, my friend. You just have to know if you're trying to win an argument, explain a disaster, or tell the greatest bar story of all time!
