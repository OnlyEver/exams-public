Listen to me, slide those stale bar peanuts over here and look me in the eye, because if you want to pass the Praxis Elementary Education exam, you have to stop reading informational texts like a bored tourist and start reading them like a deeply paranoid detective reverse-engineering a massive, scandalous reality TV mobster kitchen disaster. When an author writes an informational text, they are leaving behind a crime scene. They have a motive, they have a blueprint, and they are trying to manipulate your brain into seeing things exactly their way. 

First things first, you step up to the yellow police tape and you ask, "What are we even looking at here?" You identify the broad, general subject matter being discussed. That is the topic of an informational text. Maybe the topic is just "Chef Tony’s Catastrophic Reality TV Mob Wedding." It’s just the territory, the zip code of the drama. But a central idea differs from a topic by expressing a specific perspective or statement about that general subject matter. It is the primary point the author wants to communicate to the reader in an informational text. The central idea isn't just "the wedding"; it’s "Chef Tony’s wedding collapsed into total chaos because a rival reality star maliciously replaced the fondue fountain with fermented squid ink." Boom. That’s a perspective. That’s a central idea! 

Now, sometimes the author is lazy, or maybe just bold, and they leave a giant neon sign at the crime scene. That’s an explicit central idea, meaning it is directly stated by the author within the informational text. They flat out write, "I, the author, believe fermented squid ink ruined the wedding." But sometimes, you get a sneaky author who plays mind games. They give you an implicit central idea, which is not directly stated and must be inferred by the reader using clues from the informational text. They don't explicitly say the rival ruined it, but they casually mention the rival was seen laughing with a bucket of squid ink near the buffet. You have to put the pieces together yourself!

To build that case, the author has to drop evidence. We call these key details, which are specific pieces of information that support, prove, or develop the central idea of a text. You can’t just claim a wedding was ruined without the receipts! Common types of key details in informational texts include statistics, like "eighty-five percent of the wedding guests required immediate stomach pumping." They include expert quotes, like the local health inspector stating on the record, "This was an act of culinary terrorism." They rely on historical dates, noting the feud started on "October 4th, 2019," and they use specific examples, like describing the exact moment Aunt Carmela slipped on the calamari and took down the ice sculpture.

Before you even read the thick paragraphs of this textual police report, you need to scan the room. Informational texts frequently utilize text features to organize information and highlight key concepts, acting like giant evidence tags on the floor. Headings and subheadings are text features that preview the core content of upcoming text sections. You see a bold line that says, "The Vow Renewal and the Vomit," and you immediately know the core content of that specific upcoming section. But what if the report is full of weird mob-kitchen slang? That’s where you flip to the back. A glossary is a text feature that provides definitions for domain-specific vocabulary used within the text. That’s where you learn that a "béarnaise hit" means a sauce sabotage. And when they attach crime scene photos of the ruined tuxedo, you look at the little text underneath. Captions are text features that explain the context or meaning of accompanying visual aids like photographs and charts. 

Once you are deep into the text, you start seeing the matrix. You realize authors use specific organizational structures to establish relationships between individuals, events, and ideas in informational texts. They don’t just throw facts in a blender; they architect a specific timeline or psychological trap. And they guide you through this architecture using signal words, which act as transitions that help readers identify the structural relationships between ideas in a text.

If the author wants to tell you the history of the mob families, they use a chronological structure, which presents historical events or narratives in the exact order those events occurred in time. But if they are explaining how the rival actually poisoned the food, they use a sequential structure, which explains the specific steps required to complete a process or procedure. Whether they are outlining the history of the feud or the recipe for the poisoned risotto, the signal words "initially," "subsequently," and "finally" indicate a chronological or sequential relationship. "Initially, he minced the garlic, subsequently he added the venom, and finally he served the dish." 

But what if the author wants to explain the physics of the disaster? They deploy a cause-and-effect structure, which explains the reasons why a specific event happened and the resulting consequences of that event. You’ll know you’re in this territory because the signal words "consequently," "therefore," and "as a result" indicate a cause-and-effect relationship. "Tony threw a flaming meatball at the drapery; consequently, the entire banquet hall burned to the ground."

Maybe the author wants to weigh two things against each other, using a compare-and-contrast structure that details the similarities and differences between two or more subjects, ideas, or events. They want to compare the mob boss's reaction to the reality TV producer's reaction. The signal words "similarly," "conversely," and "on the other hand" indicate a compare-and-contrast relationship. "The mob boss wanted blood; conversely, the producer just wanted higher ratings." And when things get totally out of hand, the author might use a problem-and-solution structure, which introduces a specific issue and presents one or more ways to resolve that issue. The problem was the flaming banquet hall; the solution was driving a catering truck through the wall to create an exit.

After you survive reading this chaotic report, the police chief demands a debrief. You have to give a summary, which is a brief, condensed restatement of the most important parts of an informational text. You boil the whole saga down into a potent little shot glass. But a summary has strict rules! An accurate summary must include the central idea and the most critical supporting details. But just as importantly, an accurate summary must omit minor details, redundant information, and secondary examples. The chief does not care what color the bridesmaid dresses were; leave it out! And you must remain utterly cold and calculating, because an objective summary strictly excludes the reader's personal opinions, biases, and judgments. You might think Chef Tony’s mustache is a crime against humanity, but you keep that subjective bias out of your report!

Finally, we hit the interrogation room. The author of the text is sitting across from you, and they make a claim, which is an arguable statement that the author asserts to be true. They look you in the eye and claim, "Chef Tony deserved to be sabotaged because he is a fraud." Because that is an arguable statement, an author must support a claim with relevant and sufficient evidence to persuade the reader. You don’t just believe them! You check the evidence. 

Relevant evidence directly connects to and supports the specific claim being made by the author. If they hand you a receipt showing Tony buys frozen pasta instead of making it fresh, that connects directly to the claim of him being a fraud! That is relevant! But is it enough? Sufficient evidence means the author provides enough factual proof to convince a reasonable reader of the claim's validity. One receipt for frozen ravioli does not prove a lifelong career of fraud. We need more! To lock this case down, valid reasoning requires a logical, coherent connection between the provided evidence and the author's claim. You have to draw a straight, coherent line from the frozen pasta to the ruined wedding without jumping to wild, crazy conclusions.

The ultimate test is distinguishing the concrete from the smoke. A fact is a statement that can be objectively proven true or false through observation or documentation. We can observe the catering bill. We can document the fire damage. But an opinion is a statement of personal belief or subjective judgment that cannot be objectively proven. "Chef Tony makes the worst cannoli in New Jersey" is an opinion. You can't put a cannoli's soul under a microscope! Therefore, evaluating an author's claim involves determining whether the supporting evidence relies on verifiable facts rather than subjective opinions. If the author's entire argument that Tony deserved it rests purely on their personal hatred of his cooking, their claim collapses entirely. You throw the case out, you finish your drink, and you walk out of the bar ready to teach your elementary students how to interrogate a text until it begs for mercy!
