## Part I: The Writer’s Digital Laboratory

Back in the day, people had to use heavy typewriters and messy erasers. Today, you get to work in a high-tech digital lab. **Digital writing tools are software applications or web platforms utilized to create, edit, format, and share written content.** They are your ultimate toolbox for creating awesome school projects and totally change how we write.

### The Evolution of the Page
At the base of your toolbox is **word processing software**. **Word processing software allows writers to draft, revise, and format text digitally.** If you mess up, you don't have to throw away the paper—you just hit the backspace key! 

But what if your tablet dies or the power goes out? You don't want to lose all your hard work! That's why we use **cloud-based word processors**. **Cloud-based word processors enable automatic saving of document changes over an internet connection to prevent data loss.** Instead of saving just on your device, your file lives safely in the "cloud"—a giant computer far away that constantly remembers every single letter you type.

Because your paper is in the cloud, you can team up with friends just like in a multiplayer video game. **Digital collaborative platforms permit multiple users to view and edit the same document simultaneously.** You and a friend can be in completely different houses, watching each other's cursors zip around the screen as you write a story together! 

While you are teaming up, it helps to give each other tips. **Digital commenting features allow peers or teachers to leave targeted feedback directly on specific sections of a writer's digital draft.** This means your teacher can leave a digital sticky note right next to one sentence without actually changing your words.

### The Ecosystem of Digital Interaction
Writing isn't just about finishing homework; it's about sharing your big ideas. We have a few cool places to do this:
*   **Discussion boards:** Think of these like a group text message that doesn't happen all at once. **Discussion boards serve as asynchronous digital spaces where students can respond to writing prompts and reply to peers.** "Asynchronous" just means you don't have to be online at the exact same time. You can post your answer after soccer practice, and your friend can reply the next morning before school.
*   **Educational blogs:** Want to be the star of the show? **Educational blogs provide a digital platform for students to publish writing for a broader online audience.** It's like having your own online magazine where people from all over can read what you wrote!
*   **Wikis:** This is like building a giant Lego castle with your whole class. **Wikis are collaborative web pages that allow multiple users to add, modify, or delete content and structure.** Everyone pitches in to build the page together.

### The Automated Assistants
Even your favorite authors need help fixing mistakes. In our digital lab, **digital spelling and grammar checkers assist writers in identifying and correcting mechanical errors during the editing phase.** They are like friendly little robots pointing out that you accidentally typed "teh" instead of "the."

When you do a research project, you have to list all the books and websites you used at the very end. This list is called a bibliography, and getting all the commas and italics right can be super boring. Luckily, **digital citation generators automate the formatting of bibliographical references according to specific style guides.** You just plug in the book's title, and the tool builds the perfect reference for you!

Using all of these tools the right way is a big part of being a good person online. **Digital citizenship involves navigating digital environments safely, responsibly, and ethically.**

## Part II: The Treasure Hunt (Navigating the Digital Search)

So, you need to find some facts for your project. You open a browser, ask a question, and *zap*—millions of results pop up. It feels like magic, but it’s actually just a fancy math process. **Search engines use proprietary algorithms to index web pages and generate results based on user queries.** This means they have secret math rules to quickly scan the whole internet and match it to your words.

Here is a simple breakdown of how this math algorithm filters out what you don't need:
1. The search engine counts a giant pile of web pages (let's say 100 pages total).
2. You type a keyword. The engine subtracts the pages that *don't* have your word (100 - 80 = 20 pages left).
3. It hands you those best 20 pages!

But computers don't think like human beings. You have to speak their logical language to get exactly what you want. That's where **Boolean operators** come in. 

> **Boolean operators are simple words used as conjunctions to combine or exclude keywords in a digital search.** Think of them as special cheat codes for your search bar.

Let's look at how these cheat codes work:
1.  **AND (The Squeezer):** **Applying the Boolean operator AND narrows digital search results to include only web pages containing all specified search terms.** If you search for `pizza AND tacos`, the computer will instantly throw in the trash any webpage that only talks about pizza. It MUST have both words!
2.  **OR (The Catcher's Mitt):** **Applying the Boolean operator OR broadens digital search results to include web pages containing any of the specified search terms.** Searching for `pizza OR tacos` tells the computer, "I'm hungry! Give me anything that mentions either one of these!"
3.  **NOT (The Blocker):** **Applying the Boolean operator NOT excludes specific terms from digital search results.** If you want to look up facts about the fruit but keep getting the tech company, you can use a blocker and search `apple NOT computer`. 

What if you want to find a very specific phrase? **Placing quotation marks around a multi-word phrase in a search engine query restricts the results to that exact phrase.** Searching for `"ice cream sundae"` makes sure the computer doesn't give you a page with the word "ice" at the top and the word "cream" at the bottom. They have to be right next to each other!

## Part III: Separating the Wheat from the Chaff

Just because a search engine found a webpage doesn't mean it's telling the truth. You have to be a tough detective when you look at information.

### Primary vs. Secondary Sources
First, we have to figure out how close our source is to the real event. Think of it like actually playing in a basketball game versus just watching highlights on TV later. We have two main types:

**Primary Sources**
**A primary source is a firsthand account, original document, or physical object from a specific historical time period or event.** It means the person or thing was actually there when it happened!
*   **Examples of primary sources include diaries, personal letters, original photographs, and interview transcripts.** 
*   **An autobiography is considered a primary source because the author writes about their own life experiences.** If you write a book about your own life, you are the primary source!

**Secondary Sources**
**A secondary source analyzes, interprets, or evaluates primary sources.** This is when someone looks at all the primary clues and explains what they mean, even if they weren't there themselves.
*   **Examples of secondary sources include history textbooks, encyclopedia entries, and scholarly monographs.** (A scholarly monograph is just a fancy word for a really detailed book written by an expert).
*   **A biography written by a historian is considered a secondary source.** The historian wasn't there; they just read the letters and diaries of the person they are writing about.

### The Spectrum of Reliability
Next, we have to figure out if something is true or totally made up. 
*   **Reliable sources provide accurate, unbiased, and verifiable information authored by credible experts or organizations.** (Unbiased means they aren't unfairly picking sides, and verifiable means you can prove it's true).
*   **An unreliable source lacks author credentials, contains unsupported claims, or presents heavily biased information.** This is like a random person on the internet saying aliens ate their homework without showing any proof!

A fast way to check if a website might be trustworthy is to look at the letters at the very end of the website's name:
*   **Websites ending in the .gov or .edu domain extensions are generally considered reliable academic sources.** These are run by the government (.gov) or schools and universities (.edu).
*   **The .org domain extension designates a non-profit organization and requires researcher evaluation for potential ideological bias.** They might not be trying to make money, but they might still really want you to agree with their specific opinions.
*   **The .com domain extension designates a commercial entity and requires researcher evaluation for profit-driven bias.** They are usually trying to sell you something, so you have to wonder if they are telling you the whole truth!

What about spaces where anyone can write? **Websites that permit anonymous public editing are considered unreliable for academic research.** If anyone can change the facts without putting their real name on it, you can't trust it for your school paper. 

The absolute best information is found in special magazines for scientists and experts. **Peer-reviewed journals feature academic articles evaluated by independent experts prior to publication to ensure factual accuracy.** If a scientist writes about discovering a new bug, other scientists have to double-check their math and agree it's real before it gets printed!

### The CRAAP Test
How do you know if a source is good while you are working? We use a funny-sounding rule created by smart librarians. 

**The CRAAP test is a widely used evaluation framework measuring a source's Currency, Relevance, Authority, Accuracy, and Purpose.**

Let's look at two super important parts of this test:
1.  **Currency:** **Currency in source evaluation refers to the timeliness of the published information.** Does it matter what year it is? Yes! If you find an article about the newest video game consoles from 2005, it lacks currency for today!
2.  **Authority:** **Authority in source evaluation refers to the professional credentials, education, and expertise of the author or publisher.** If your dentist writes a blog post about how to fix a car engine, they lack authority. You wouldn't trust a dentist to fix your car!

## Part IV: The Ethics of Knowledge

Once we find our facts, we have a big responsibility. How do we use someone else's great ideas without stealing them?

### The Crime of Plagiarism
**Plagiarism is the unethical act of presenting another person's words, ideas, or data as one's own original work.** Basically, it's copying. It's pretending you scored the winning goal when someone else actually kicked the ball.

Plagiarism happens in a few sneaky ways:
*   **Direct Copying:** **Copying text directly from a source without utilizing quotation marks constitutes plagiarism.** If you just copy and paste from a website, you are breaking the rules!
*   **Stealing the Idea:** Even if you use your own words, **failing to include an in-text citation when summarizing another author's concept constitutes plagiarism.** An "in-text citation" is just a little note in parentheses telling your teacher where you got the idea. If you don't use it, you stole the idea!
*   **Cheating Yourself:** Can you copy off yourself? Yes! **Self-plagiarism occurs when a student submits their own previously graded work for a new assignment without instructor permission.** If you turn in your 5th-grade science project for a 6th-grade assignment, that is not allowed.

### How to Use Sources Ethically
To stay out of trouble and be a great digital citizen, you have three super moves: paraphrasing, summarizing, and quoting. Let's learn how they work.

**1. Paraphrasing**
**Paraphrasing involves rewriting an author's specific ideas entirely in the researcher's own words and sentence structure.** It means you read something, close the book, and explain it to your friend using your normal voice.

Here is a trap a lot of kids fall into: **changing only a few words from an original text using a thesaurus does not constitute proper paraphrasing.** If the book says "The large dog ran," and you just swap the words to say "The big hound sprinted," you haven't really written your own sentence. You just dressed up someone else's sentence in a silly costume! 

Even when you use your own totally new words, **paraphrased information requires a formal citation to credit the original author.**

**2. Summarizing**
While paraphrasing is usually for one specific fact, **summarizing involves condensing the broad main ideas of a source into a much shorter overview using the researcher's own words.** This is like telling your mom the entire plot of a two-hour movie in just 30 seconds. You are squishing down the big ideas into a tiny package. 

Because those big ideas still belong to the person who made the movie (or book), **summarized information requires a formal citation to credit the original author.**

**3. Direct Quotes**
Sometimes, a writer says something so awesome that you couldn't possibly say it better. **A direct quote reproduces the exact original phrasing of an author and must be enclosed in quotation marks.** It's like taking a perfect picture of their exact sentence. 

Just like the other moves, **direct quotes require a formal citation to credit the original author.** 

### The Exception to the Rule: Common Knowledge
Do you have to put a little citation note if you write that a pizza is round? No! You get a free pass for things everybody already knows.

**Common knowledge refers to factual information widely known, undisputed, and easily verified across many general sources.** Everyone agrees that a pizza is round, a week has 7 days, and George Washington was the first US President. 

Because no single person "owns" this basic information, **statements considered common knowledge do not require a formal citation in academic writing.**
