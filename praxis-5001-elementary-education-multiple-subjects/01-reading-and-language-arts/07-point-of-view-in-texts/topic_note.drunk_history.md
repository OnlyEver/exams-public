Listen to me, Jimmy. Put the pretzel down and listen, because you think you know the whole story of what happened at Sarah’s disastrous wedding, but you are completely missing the mechanics of how reality works! When you crack open a book, or listen to a rumor, or read a text message, you aren’t just getting "the truth." You are getting a highly curated, deeply manipulated camera angle. This is the magic of Point of View. The point of view completely determines the type and amount of information available to the reader throughout a text. It is the gatekeeper of reality, my friend! 

Let’s talk about the cameras, the actual grammatical lenses. The type of pronoun used is the flashing neon sign telling you exactly where the author has planted the camera.

Picture a reality TV confessional booth. That’s your First-person point of view. It’s entirely self-obsessed, using pronouns such as 'I', 'me', 'my', 'mine', and 'we'. When someone uses first-person, you know instantly that in this point of view, the narrator is an active participant in the events of the story. They are in the kitchen when the wedding cake catches fire! But here is the fatal flaw of the reality TV lens: a first-person narrative structure aggressively limits the reader's knowledge to the narrator's direct observations. If I am locked in the pantry eating leftover shrimp cocktail, and the groom's mom tackles the DJ in the ballroom, I can't write about it! I only know what I can see through the keyhole!

Now, what if the storyteller suddenly leans over the bar, points a finger right at your chest, and drags *you* into the mess? That is the Second-person point of view, which strictly uses the pronoun 'you'. It’s an aggressive, rare beast because second-person point of view addresses the reader directly. It literally hands you the murder weapon and says, "You walked into the room." You aren’t just reading; you are implicated!

But let’s say you want to detach. You want to fly a drone over the entire chaotic wedding reception. Enter the Third-person point of view, which uses pronouns such as 'he', 'she', 'it', and 'they'. Because we are hovering in the rafters, in third-person point of view, the narrator exists completely outside the events of the story. But Jimmy, pay attention, because this drone has three very distinct settings. 

Sometimes, the drone has a psychic laser beam, but it’s only locked onto the groom. That’s Third-person limited point of view—it reveals the internal thoughts and feelings of only *one* character. We can hover above the dance floor and see everyone, but we only know the crippling panic inside the groom’s mind. 

If you upgrade your drone to the VIP god-mode, you get Third-person omniscient point of view. This reveals the internal thoughts and feelings of *multiple* characters. We are inside the groom's panicked brain, we hear the bride plotting revenge, and we know the caterer is mentally calculating how fast he can flee the country. 

But what if the drone loses its psychic abilities completely? That’s Third-person objective point of view. It acts like a cold, unfeeling security camera strapped to the ceiling. It describes only observable actions and dialogue. He yelled, she threw a glass, the cake exploded. Crucially, third-person objective narrators do not reveal the internal thoughts of any characters. You get the raw footage of the crime scene, nothing more.

So, how do authors overcome the blind spots of these cameras? Editing, baby! Authors may structure a text with alternating chapters to present multiple characters' points of view on the same events. Chapter one: The Bride's perspective. Chapter two: The Caterer's perspective. You bounce between their brains to piece together the absolute disaster. 

And why do their stories sound so different? Because a narrator's background and experiences shape the perspective from which a fictional story is told. If a former mobster narrates a simple neighborhood bake sale, it's going to sound like a turf war! Taking it even further into the real world, an author's cultural background can influence the perspective presented in a literary text. The food they grew up eating, the family dynamics they survived—it all bleeds into the lens they construct for the reader.

But Jimmy, what if the person holding the camera is just straight-up lying? Welcome to the terrifying realm of the Unreliable narrator, a storyteller who presents a completely distorted version of events. Why would they lie? Well, sometimes it’s venomous. An unreliable narrator's inaccurate account can stem from massive personal bias. Of course the jealous bridesmaid is going to say the bride looked like a swamp monster; she’s biased! But other times, it’s entirely innocent. An unreliable narrator's inaccurate account can stem from a profoundly limited understanding of events. Imagine a five-year-old ringbearer describing the cops arresting the wedding singer as a "fun game of tag." He’s not lying; he just has no idea how the world works.

This naturally breeds my favorite thing in the universe: Dramatic Irony. This is the exquisite joy of knowing a secret. Dramatic irony occurs when the reader possesses knowledge that a character lacks. Simply put, contrasting points of view between the reader and a character can create dramatic irony. We, the omniscient readers, saw the caterer accidentally put laxatives in the chocolate fondue. The clueless best man, however, is excitedly dipping a massive strawberry. The collision between our knowledge and his impending doom is the sheer driving force of the plot!

Now, you might think you can escape all this drama by switching to non-fiction. You think informational texts are just safe, sterile facts? Don't be naive! Even in non-fiction, an author's point of view in an informational text refers to the author's personal opinion or perspective on a subject. It’s just an argument wearing a necktie!

We divide these into two piles. First, the screamers. Informational texts with a subjective point of view enthusiastically include personal feelings and opinions. It’s an op-ed ranting about the mayor's terrible parking meters. Second, the robots. Informational texts with an objective point of view present strictly verifiable facts. To maintain this illusion, informational texts with an objective point of view must completely avoid emotional or biased language. It’s just dates, times, and parking ticket data.

But here is the trick! Even when they pretend to be objective, authors are still manipulating the narrative. Authors express a specific point of view in non-fiction through the deliberate selection of facts. I can write a totally factual review of a restaurant and list every single time they passed a health inspection. See? Good facts! But wait, authors also express a specific point of view in non-fiction through the deliberate omission of certain details. I simply *forgot* to mention the colony of feral raccoons living in their walk-in freezer! I didn't lie; I just moved the camera away from the raccoons!

How do you catch them doing this? Look for the fingerprints in the ink! First, word choice serves as textual evidence to identify an author's attitude toward a topic. Calling those raccoons "rustic kitchen companions" instead of "a terrifying infestation" tells you exactly where the author's allegiances lie. Second, a text's overall tone serves as textual evidence to identify an author's point of view. If the whole article reads like a sarcastic, mocking joke, you know exactly what the author really thinks about the restaurant, regardless of the data. 

To really see the matrix, you have to put two cameras side-by-side. Comparing multiple accounts of the same event reveals how different authors emphasize different details. Look at the restaurant owner’s press release versus the health inspector’s report of the same exact evening. One emphasizes the artisanal cheese selection; the other emphasizes the hazmat team. 

And finally, if you want to win an argument, you don't hide from the opposition—you invite them in! Authors often structure persuasive texts by directly addressing opposing points of view. They literally drag the enemy's camera into their own essay! "Now, some critics might argue that raccoons in a kitchen are a health code violation..." Why give them screen time? Because acknowledging an opposing point of view can structurally strengthen an author's primary argument. You pop their balloon before they can even blow it up! You predict their attack, dismantle it, and look like an absolute genius. 

So next time someone tries to tell you "what really happened," Jimmy, you look them dead in the eye and ask: Who is holding the camera, what are their pronouns, and what are they deliberately leaving out of the frame?!
