# The Architecture of Perspective: Mastering Point of View in Texts

Hello there! Welcome. If we are going to talk about **Point of View (POV)**, we have to start by acknowledging a fundamental truth about [reading](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reading): when you open a [book](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Book), you are entirely at the mercy of the person holding the [camera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camera). 

Point of view isn't just some dusty grammatical category you memorize for a [test](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Test_%28assessment%29). It’s the architectural foundation of the entire text! It dictates the rules of the [universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fictional_universe) you are stepping into. It decides what you see, what you feel, and—perhaps most importantly—what you are completely blind to. 

To master the [Praxis Elementary Education exam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Praxis_test), we need to understand how authors build these lenses, both in the imaginary realms of fiction and in the supposedly "factual" world of non-fiction. Let’s look at the mechanics of it.

---

## Part I: The Fictional Camera Angles (Identifying Types of POV)

Let’s categorize our lenses. The type of [pronoun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pronoun) the author uses tells you exactly where the camera is stationed.

### 1. First-Person: The [GoPro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GoPro) Camera
Imagine a camera strapped directly to a [character's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Character_%28arts%29) forehead. **First-person point of view uses pronouns such as 'I', 'me', 'my', 'mine', and 'we'.** 

Because we are inside the character's head, **in first-person point of view, the narrator is a participant in the events of the story.** You feel their [heartbeat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heart_rate), you see exactly what they see, but you are trapped within their physical limitations.

### 2. Second-Person: The [VR Headset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virtual_reality_headset)
This one is rare in [literature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Literature), but brilliant when used. **[Second-person point of view](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second-person_narrative) uses the pronoun 'you'.** 

Think of those old *[Choose Your Own Adventure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Choose_Your_Own_Adventure)* books. **Second-person point of view addresses the [reader](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reading) directly**, essentially making *you*, the reader, a participant in the story. 

### 3. Third-Person: The Drone Camera
Now, let's untether the camera and fly it around the room. **Third-person point of view uses pronouns such as 'he', 'she', 'it', and 'they'.** 

By definition, **in third-person point of view, the narrator exists outside the events of the story.** But here is the trick—this "drone" has three very different settings you need to know:

| Type of Third-Person | How it Works (The Camera Setting) |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Limited** | The drone has a [mind-reading](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telepathy) [laser](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laser), but it's locked onto just one person. **Third-person limited point of view reveals the internal thoughts and [feelings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emotion) of only one character.** We see the world from outside, but we only know the secrets of one individual. |
| **Omniscient** | This is the "God-mode" drone. **Third-person omniscient point of view reveals the internal thoughts and feelings of multiple characters.** The narrator knows everything—what the [hero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hero) is fearing, what the [villain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Villain) is plotting, and what the [bartender](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bartender) is daydreaming about. |
| **Objective** | Think of this like a [security camera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed-circuit_television) in a bank. **Third-person objective point of view describes only observable actions and [dialogue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dialogue).** Crucially, **third-person objective narrators do not reveal the internal thoughts of any characters.** You just get the raw, physical [facts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fact) of the scene. |

## Part II: The Architect of the Story (How POV Impacts Structure)

Now, why does any of this matter? Because **point of view determines the type and amount of [information](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information) available to the reader throughout a text.** It is the ultimate gatekeeper of knowledge.

### The Limits of Perspective
Think about the restrictions of that "GoPro" camera. Because we only see through the narrator's eyes, **a first-person narrative structure limits the reader's knowledge to the narrator's direct [observations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observation).** If the narrator isn't in the room when the bank is robbed, we don't get to see the bank being robbed! 

To solve this structural limitation, **authors may structure a text with alternating chapters to present multiple characters' points of view on the same events.** You see this all the time in modern literature. Chapter 1 is from the hero's perspective; Chapter 2 is from the villain's. It's a fantastic structural tool used to build a complete, 360-degree mosaic of the [plot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plot_%28narrative%29).

But a perspective isn't just a camera; it's a human lens. **A narrator's background and [experiences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Experience) shape the perspective from which a fictional story is told.** Pulling back even further to the real world, **an author's cultural background can influence the perspective presented in a [literary text](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Literature).** The lived reality of the writer bleeds into the lens they construct for the reader.

### The Warped Lens: The [Unreliable Narrator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unreliable_narrator)
What if the person telling the story is... well, wrong? 

> **Definition:** **An [unreliable narrator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unreliable_narrator) presents a distorted version of events.**

Why would they do that? Sometimes it's entirely malicious. **An unreliable narrator's inaccurate account can stem from [personal bias](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bias)** (think of a jealous rival narrating a story where they cast themselves as the innocent victim). 

Other times, it's completely innocent! **An unreliable narrator's inaccurate account can stem from a limited understanding of events.** A classic example is a young [child](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Child) narrating an [adult](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adult) conflict. They tell us exactly what they see, but because they don't understand adult [relationships](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interpersonal_relationship), the conclusions they draw are wonderfully, tragically wrong.

### The Joy of the Secret: Dramatic Irony
Let's talk about the absolute thrill of knowing a secret. What happens when the reader knows something that the narrator *doesn't*? 

> **Definition:** **Dramatic irony occurs when the reader possesses knowledge that a character lacks.** 

**Contrasting points of view between the reader and a character can create dramatic irony.** Think of the movie *Toy Story*. Buzz Lightyear genuinely believes he is a real space ranger. His point of view is serious and heroic. But *our* point of view as the [audience](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Audience)? We know he's just a piece of [plastic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plastic). The collision between our knowledge and his ignorance generates all the [humor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Humour) and [tension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suspense) in the story!

## Part III: The Hidden Hand in Non-Fiction (Informational Texts)

Let's step out of fiction and into the real world. You might think non-fiction is just pure, unadulterated facts. Nonsense! 

Even in non-fiction, **an author's point of view in an informational text refers to the author's personal [opinion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Opinion) or perspective on a subject.** We generally divide informational texts into two buckets:

1.  **[Subjective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subjectivity):** **Informational texts with a subjective point of view include personal feelings and opinions.** (Think of a fiery [newspaper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newspaper) [op-ed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Op-ed) or a personal [memoir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Memoir)).
2.  **[Objective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Objectivity_%28philosophy%29):** **Informational texts with an objective point of view present verifiable facts.** Furthermore, **informational texts with an objective point of view avoid emotional or biased language.** (Think of a scientific encyclopedia).

### The Illusion of [Objectivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Objectivity_%28philosophy%29)
Here is where authors get beautifully sneaky. Even when pretending to be perfectly objective, authors are still manipulating the lens. How?

*   **Authors express a specific point of view in non-fiction through the deliberate [selection of facts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cherry_picking).** If I want you to think a historical figure was a hero, I will give you a verifiable list of their greatest accomplishments. 
*   Conversely, **authors express a specific point of view in non-fiction through the deliberate omission of certain details.** I simply *leave out* the fact that the same historical figure was a terrible person in their private life! I didn't lie; I just moved the camera away from the bad stuff.

How do you, as an astute reader, catch them doing this? You look for the textual [fingerprints](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fingerprint). 
*   **Word choice serves as textual evidence to identify an author's attitude toward a topic.** Calling a crowd "passionate citizens" versus "a dangerous mob" describes the exact same group of people, but the word choice reveals exactly how the author feels about them.
*   Similarly, **a text's overall tone serves as textual evidence to identify an author's point of view.** Is the tone mocking? Somber? Urgent? Enthusiastic? Tone is the emotional [shadow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shadow) cast by the author's true perspective.

## Part IV: The Collision of Perspectives

To truly test your understanding of point of view, you need to set two different cameras next to each other and see how they record the exact same event. 

**Comparing multiple accounts of the same event reveals how different authors emphasize different details.** Let's look at a concrete [historical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History) example: The Boston Massacre of 1770. 
*   If you look at Paul Revere's famous engraving and [Patriot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patriot_%28American_Revolution%29) [pamphlets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pamphlet), the visual and textual emphasis is entirely on innocent, unarmed colonists being slaughtered by merciless soldiers. 
*   But if you read the primary source testimony of the British commanding officer, Captain Thomas Preston, the emphasis is heavily placed on the "great number of heavy [clubs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Club_%28weapon%29) and snowballs" being hurled by a terrifying, unruly mob. 

Same exact event. Wildly different points of view. Wildly different [realities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reality).

### Structuring an Argument
Finally, how do authors use point of view to win an argument? If you are trying to persuade someone, you might think you should ignore anyone who disagrees with you. Actually, the opposite is true. 

**Authors often structure persuasive texts by directly addressing opposing points of view.** They literally invite the enemy's camera into their essay! Why? Because **acknowledging an opposing point of view can structurally strengthen an author's primary argument.** 

By saying, *"Now, some critics might argue that this policy is too expensive, but here is exactly why they are wrong..."* the author disarms the counterargument before the reader can even think of it themselves. It is a masterful structural [defense mechanism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Defence_mechanism).

### Final Thoughts
When you sit down for the [Praxis exam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Praxis_test), remember this: Point of View is not just about memorizing pronouns. It is about understanding the flow of information. It's about asking yourself: *Who is holding the camera? What are they forcing me to look at? And what are they trying to hide?* 

Master that, and you'll never read a text the same way again. Happy studying!
