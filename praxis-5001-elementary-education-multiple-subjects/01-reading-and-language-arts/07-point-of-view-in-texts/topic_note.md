Imagine a house with several windows, each facing a different direction. A person looking out the east window describes a brilliant [sunrise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunrise) over a calm lake, while someone at the west window reports a dense, shadowy [forest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forest). Neither is lying; their reality is entirely dictated by where they stand. In [literature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Literature), this vantage point is known as [point of view](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_of_view_%28literature%29). It is not merely a [grammatical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grammar) choice of [pronouns](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pronoun), but the fundamental architecture of a [text](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Text_%28literary_theory%29). Point of view determines what information is illuminated, what is cast in shadow, and ultimately, how the [reader](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reading) experiences the [narrative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Narrative) or [argument](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Argument).

Understanding point of view requires looking past the surface of the words to see the structural skeleton of the text. Whether evaluating a [fictional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiction) narrator’s [reliability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unreliable_narrator) or dissecting an [essayist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Essay)’s hidden [biases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bias), identifying the point of view unlocks the mechanics of how a story is told or an argument is won.

## The Mechanics of Perception: Fictional Point of View

In fiction, the point of view establishes the [narrator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Narrator)'s proximity to the story. We can categorize these perspectives by looking at the specific pronouns the [author](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Author) employs and the narrator's relationship to the events. 

### First-Person and Second-Person 

When an author uses the **[first-person point of view](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First-person_narrative)**, the narrator is a participant in the events of the story. This perspective uses pronouns such as **"I", "me", "my", "mine", and "we."** Because we reside entirely inside this character’s [mind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mind), the point of view determines the type and amount of information available to the reader throughout a text. A first-person [narrative structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Narrative_structure) limits the reader's knowledge exclusively to the narrator's direct observations. We only know what they see, hear, or [deduce](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deductive_reasoning).

Less common, but highly distinct, is the **[second-person point of view](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second-person_narrative)**, which addresses the reader directly. It pulls the [audience](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Audience) into the text, making them a [character](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Character_%28arts%29) in the narrative. This perspective relies on the pronoun **"you"** (e.g., "You walk into the dark room and feel a sudden chill").

### The Variations of Third-Person

In **[third-person point of view](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Third-person_narrative)**, the narrator exists outside the events of the story, observing the characters from a distance. This perspective uses pronouns such as **"he", "she", "it", and "they."** However, not all third-person narrators possess the same level of insight. We must differentiate them by how deeply they can penetrate the characters' minds:

| Point of View Type | Depth of Internal Knowledge | Characteristic |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Third-Person Objective** | None | The narrator describes only observable actions and [dialogue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dialogue). Third-person objective narrators do not reveal the internal thoughts of any characters. Think of this as a "fly on the wall" or a [security camera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed-circuit_television). |
| **Third-Person Limited** | Singular | The narrative reveals the internal thoughts and [feelings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emotion) of only one character. We see the world through their emotional lens, but everyone else remains a [mystery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mystery). |
| **Third-Person Omniscient** | Multiple / All | This "[all-knowing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Omniscience)" narrator reveals the internal thoughts and feelings of multiple characters, drifting freely from one mind to another. |

![A surveillance camera serves as a visual analogy for the third-person objective point of view, which records observable actions and dialogue without accessing internal thoughts.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/SurveillanceCamera4.jpg)

## The Filter of the Narrator: Reliability, Irony, and Structure

A story is only as trustworthy as the person telling it. A narrator's background and experiences naturally shape the perspective from which a fictional story is told. Similarly, an author's cultural background can deeply influence the perspective presented in a literary text, weaving specific [values](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Value_%28ethics%29), [traditions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tradition), and [worldviews](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Worldview) into the fabric of the narrative.

Because first-person and third-person limited narrators are filtered through human imperfection, authors can play with the concept of [truth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Truth). Sometimes, an **[unreliable narrator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unreliable_narrator)** presents a distorted version of events. This distortion is rarely accidental; it is a deliberate structural choice by the author. An unreliable narrator's inaccurate account can stem from a limited understanding of events (such as a child narrating an adult [conflict](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conflict_%28narrative%29) they do not fully comprehend) or it can stem from intense personal bias (such as a jealous rival recounting a [competition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Competition)). 

![Baron Munchausen, depicted here being swallowed by a whale, is a classic example of an unreliable narrator whose intense personal bias and exaggeration shape the narrative.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Gustave_Dor%C3%A9_-_Baron_von_M%C3%BCnchhausen_-_067.jpg)

This limitation of knowledge leads us to one of literature's most powerful tools:

> **[Dramatic Irony](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dramatic_irony)**  
> Dramatic irony occurs when the reader possesses knowledge that a character lacks. Contrasting points of view between the reader (who sees the broader picture) and a character (who operates in the dark) create this intense, suspenseful [irony](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irony). Think of watching a [horror movie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horror_film) where you, the viewer, know the [monster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monster) is behind the door, but the character happily reaches for the doorknob.

![In horror films, monsters like Frankenstein's creation are often used to generate dramatic irony when the audience anticipates a threat before the characters do.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Frankenstein's_monster_(Boris_Karloff).jpg)

Authors frequently manipulate point of view to impact the overall structure of a text. For instance, rather than relying on a single, potentially biased narrator, authors may structure a text with alternating [chapters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chapter_%28books%29) to present multiple characters' points of view on the same events. This creates a multi-faceted narrative where the reader must piece together the [objective truth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Objectivity_%28philosophy%29) from several [subjective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subjectivity) fragments.

## The Architecture of Argument: Point of View in Informational Texts

![Informational texts rely on structured arguments, where the author's point of view is built upon premises and inferences that lead to a specific conclusion.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Argument_terminology_used_in_logic_(en).svg)

When transitioning from fiction to [non-fiction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-fiction), the definition of point of view shifts. In an informational text, an author's point of view refers not to a fictional narrator, but to the author's personal [opinion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Opinion) or perspective on a subject. 

We categorize informational texts heavily by their proximity to bias:
*   **[Objective Point of View](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Objectivity_%28journalism%29):** Informational texts with an objective point of view present verifiable [facts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fact). To maintain [credibility](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Credibility), they avoid emotional or biased language. 
*   **[Subjective Point of View](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subjectivity):** Informational texts with a subjective point of view include personal feelings and opinions. The author’s personal stance is visible on the page.

### Textual Evidence: Unmasking the Author's Perspective

Even in texts that strive for objectivity, an author's perspective often leaks through. How do we spot it? We look for **[textual evidence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evidence)**. 

**[Word choice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diction)** serves as primary textual evidence to identify an author's [attitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Attitude_%28psychology%29) toward a topic. Calling a [public policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_policy) an "initiative" versus a "scheme" reveals drastically different perspectives, even if the underlying facts remain identical. Broadening our view, a text's overall **[tone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tone_%28literature%29)**—whether it is mocking, urgent, reverent, or clinical—serves as textual evidence to identify an author's point of view.

Furthermore, authors express a specific point of view in non-fiction through the deliberate selection of facts. By highlighting [data](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data) that supports their worldview, they guide the reader toward a specific conclusion. Equally important is what is *missing*. Authors express a specific point of view in non-fiction through the [deliberate omission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_of_omission) of certain details that might undermine their argument. 

Because of this manipulation of facts, [historians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Historian) and [researchers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Researcher) must always look at more than one [source](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Source_document). Comparing multiple accounts of the same event reveals how different authors emphasize different details. If two [reporters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Journalist) cover the same [protest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protest), one might focus entirely on [property damage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Property_damage), while the other emphasizes the size and peaceful [chanting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chant) of the crowd. Comparing these accounts exposes the unique point of view of each reporter.

![A single event, such as a protest, can be framed from drastically different points of view depending on whether a journalist emphasizes peaceful assembly or property damage.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/M2109_Iraq_War_Protest_(Black_Bloc_Element).jpg)

### The Persuasive Defense

When authors write explicitly [persuasive texts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persuasion), point of view becomes a structural weapon. A novice [writer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Writer) might only present their own side of an argument, fearing that acknowledging the opposition will weaken their case. An expert writer does the exact opposite. 

Authors often structure persuasive texts by directly addressing opposing points of view. By bringing the [counterargument](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Counterargument) into the light, the author controls how it is presented and subsequently dismantled. Acknowledging an opposing point of view can structurally strengthen an author's primary argument because it demonstrates comprehensive knowledge of the topic, builds [trust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trust_%28social_sciences%29) with the reader, and systematically neutralizes [objections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Objection_%28argument%29) before the reader can even articulate them.
