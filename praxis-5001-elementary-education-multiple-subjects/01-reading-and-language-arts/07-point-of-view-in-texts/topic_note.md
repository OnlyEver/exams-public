# The Architecture of Perspective: Mastering [Point of View](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_of_view_%28literature%29) in Texts

Hello there! Welcome. If we are going to talk about **[Point of View](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_of_view_%28literature%29) (POV)**, we have to start by acknowledging a fundamental truth about [reading](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reading): when you open a [book](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Book), you are entirely at the mercy of the person holding the [camera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camera). 

[Point of view](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_of_view_%28literature%29) isn't just some dusty [grammatical category](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grammatical_category) you memorize for a [test](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Test_%28assessment%29). It’s the architectural foundation of the entire text! It dictates the rules of the [universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fictional_universe) you are stepping into. It decides what you see, what you feel, and—perhaps most importantly—what you are completely blind to. 

To master the [Praxis Elementary Education exam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Praxis_test), we need to understand how [authors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Author) build these lenses, both in the imaginary realms of [fiction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiction) and in the supposedly "factual" world of [non-fiction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-fiction). Let’s look at the mechanics of it.

---

## Part I: The Fictional Camera Angles (Identifying Types of [POV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_of_view_%28literature%29))

Let’s categorize our lenses. The type of [pronoun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pronoun) the author uses tells you exactly where the camera is stationed.

### 1. First-Person: The [GoPro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GoPro) Camera
Imagine a camera strapped directly to a [character's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Character_%28arts%29) forehead. **[First-person point of view](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First-person_narrative) uses pronouns such as 'I', 'me', 'my', 'mine', and 'we'.** 

Because we are inside the character's head, **in first-person point of view, the [narrator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Narrator) is a participant in the events of the [story](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Narrative).** You feel their [heartbeat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heart_rate), you see exactly what they see, but you are trapped within their physical limitations.

![A first-person point of view operates much like a wearable action camera, immersing the reader directly in the narrator's immediate experiences and physical limitations.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Goprohero5actioncamerapoweredon.jpg)

### 2. Second-Person: The [VR Headset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virtual_reality_headset)
This one is rare in [literature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Literature), but brilliant when used. **[Second-person point of view](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second-person_narrative) uses the pronoun 'you'.** 

Think of those old *[Choose Your Own Adventure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Choose_Your_Own_Adventure)* books. **Second-person point of view addresses the [reader](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reading) directly**, essentially making *you*, the reader, a participant in the story. 

![Second-person narration places the reader directly into the story, functioning similarly to a virtual reality headset that casts "you" as the active participant.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Collision_2024_-_VR5_3671_(53804364983).jpg)

### 3. Third-Person: The [Drone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unmanned_aerial_vehicle) Camera
Now, let's untether the camera and fly it around the room. **[Third-person point of view](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Third-person_narrative) uses pronouns such as 'he', 'she', 'it', and 'they'.** 

By definition, **in third-person point of view, the narrator exists outside the events of the story.** But here is the trick—this "[drone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unmanned_aerial_vehicle)" has three very different settings you need to know:

![A third-person narrator observes from the outside. Depending on whether it is limited, omniscient, or objective, this literary "drone" either zeroes in on specific minds or merely records external events.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/DJI_Phantom_2_Vision%2B_V3_hovering_over_Weissfluhjoch_(cropped).jpg)

| Type of Third-Person | How it Works (The Camera Setting) |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Limited** | The drone has a [mind-reading](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telepathy) [laser](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laser), but it's locked onto just one person. **[Third-person limited point of view](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Third-person_narrative) reveals the internal thoughts and [feelings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emotion) of only one character.** We see the world from outside, but we only know the [secrets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Secrecy) of one individual. |
| **Omniscient** | This is the "[God-mode](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/God_mode)" drone. **[Third-person omniscient point of view](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Omniscient_narrator) reveals the internal thoughts and feelings of multiple characters.** The narrator knows everything—what the [hero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hero) is fearing, what the [villain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Villain) is plotting, and what the [bartender](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bartender) is [daydreaming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Daydream) about. |
| **Objective** | Think of this like a [security camera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed-circuit_television) in a [bank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bank). **[Third-person objective point of view](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Third-person_narrative) describes only observable [actions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_%28philosophy%29) and [dialogue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dialogue).** Crucially, **third-person objective narrators do not reveal the internal thoughts of any characters.** You just get the raw, physical [facts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fact) of the [scene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scene_%28drama%29). |

## Part II: The Architect of the Story (How POV Impacts [Structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Narrative_structure))

Now, why does any of this matter? Because **point of view determines the type and amount of [information](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information) available to the reader throughout a text.** It is the ultimate [gatekeeper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gatekeeping_%28communication%29) of [knowledge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge).

### The Limits of Perspective
Think about the restrictions of that "GoPro" camera. Because we only see through the narrator's eyes, **a first-person narrative structure limits the reader's knowledge to the narrator's direct [observations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observation).** If the narrator isn't in the room when the bank is [robbed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bank_robbery), we don't get to see the bank being robbed! 

To solve this structural limitation, **authors may structure a text with alternating [chapters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chapter_%28books%29) to present multiple characters' points of view on the same events.** You see this all the time in [modern literature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modern_literature). Chapter 1 is from the hero's perspective; Chapter 2 is from the villain's. It's a fantastic structural tool used to build a complete, 360-degree [mosaic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mosaic) of the [plot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plot_%28narrative%29).

But a perspective isn't just a camera; it's a human [lens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lens_%28optics%29). **A narrator's background and [experiences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Experience) shape the perspective from which a fictional story is told.** Pulling back even further to the real world, **an author's [cultural background](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Culture) can influence the perspective presented in a [literary text](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Literature).** The lived reality of the [writer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Writer) bleeds into the lens they construct for the reader.

### The Warped Lens: The [Unreliable Narrator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unreliable_narrator)
What if the person telling the story is... well, wrong? 

> **Definition:** **An [unreliable narrator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unreliable_narrator) presents a distorted version of events.**

Why would they do that? Sometimes it's entirely malicious. **An unreliable narrator's inaccurate account can stem from [personal bias](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bias)** (think of a jealous rival narrating a story where they cast themselves as the innocent [victim](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Victimisation)). 

Other times, it's completely innocent! **An unreliable narrator's inaccurate account can stem from a limited understanding of events.** A classic example is a young [child](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Child) narrating an [adult](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adult) conflict. They tell us exactly what they see, but because they don't understand adult [relationships](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interpersonal_relationship), the conclusions they draw are wonderfully, tragically wrong.

![Famous literary figures like Baron Munchausen deliberately distort reality through tall tales, serving as classic examples of an intentionally unreliable narrator.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Gustave_Dor%C3%A9_-_Baron_von_M%C3%BCnchhausen_-_067.jpg)

### The Joy of the Secret: [Dramatic Irony](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irony)
Let's talk about the absolute thrill of knowing a [secret](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Secrecy). What happens when the reader knows something that the narrator *doesn't*? 

> **Definition:** **[Dramatic irony](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irony) occurs when the reader possesses knowledge that a character lacks.** 

**Contrasting points of view between the reader and a character can create dramatic irony.** Think of the movie *[Toy Story](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toy_Story)*. [Buzz Lightyear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buzz_Lightyear) genuinely believes he is a real space ranger. His point of view is serious and heroic. But *our* point of view as the [audience](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Audience)? We know he's just a piece of [plastic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plastic). The collision between our knowledge and his [ignorance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ignorance) generates all the [humor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Humour) and [tension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suspense) in the story!

## Part III: The Hidden Hand in [Non-Fiction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-fiction) (Informational Texts)

Let's step out of fiction and into the real world. You might think non-fiction is just pure, unadulterated facts. [Nonsense](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonsense)! 

Even in non-fiction, **an author's point of view in an informational text refers to the author's personal [opinion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Opinion) or perspective on a subject.** We generally divide informational texts into two buckets:

1.  **[Subjective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subjectivity):** **Informational texts with a subjective point of view include personal feelings and opinions.** (Think of a fiery [newspaper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newspaper) [op-ed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Op-ed) or a personal [memoir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Memoir)).
2.  **[Objective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Objectivity_%28philosophy%29):** **Informational texts with an objective point of view present verifiable facts.** Furthermore, **informational texts with an objective point of view avoid emotional or [biased language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Loaded_language).** (Think of a scientific [encyclopedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Encyclopedia)).

### The Illusion of [Objectivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Objectivity_%28philosophy%29)
Here is where authors get beautifully sneaky. Even when pretending to be perfectly objective, authors are still manipulating the lens. How?

*   **Authors express a specific point of view in non-fiction through the deliberate [selection of facts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cherry_picking).** If I want you to think a historical figure was a hero, I will give you a verifiable list of their greatest accomplishments. 
*   Conversely, **authors express a specific point of view in non-fiction through the deliberate [omission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_of_omission) of certain details.** I simply *leave out* the fact that the same historical figure was a terrible person in their private life! I didn't [lie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie); I just moved the camera away from the bad stuff.

![Authors can manipulate supposedly "objective" non-fiction by cherry-picking data—such as isolating a specific time frame to artificially conceal a broader trend.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Global_warming_hiatus.gif)

How do you, as an astute reader, catch them doing this? You look for the textual [fingerprints](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fingerprint). 
*   **[Word choice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diction) serves as textual evidence to identify an author's [attitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Attitude_%28psychology%29) toward a topic.** Calling a crowd "passionate [citizens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Citizenship)" versus "a dangerous [mob](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crowd)" describes the exact same group of people, but the word choice reveals exactly how the author feels about them.
*   Similarly, **a text's overall [tone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tone_%28literature%29) serves as textual evidence to identify an author's point of view.** Is the tone mocking? Somber? Urgent? Enthusiastic? Tone is the emotional [shadow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shadow) cast by the author's true perspective.

## Part IV: The Collision of Perspectives

To truly test your understanding of point of view, you need to set two different cameras next to each other and see how they record the exact same event. 

**Comparing multiple accounts of the same event reveals how different authors emphasize different details.** Let's look at a concrete [historical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History) example: The [Boston Massacre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boston_Massacre) of 1770. 
*   If you look at [Paul Revere's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Revere) famous [engraving](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engraving) and [Patriot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patriot_%28American_Revolution%29) [pamphlets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pamphlet), the visual and textual emphasis is entirely on innocent, unarmed [colonists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colonial_history_of_the_United_States) being slaughtered by merciless [soldiers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Army_during_the_American_Revolutionary_War). 
*   But if you read the [primary source](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primary_source) [testimony](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Testimony) of the British commanding officer, [Captain Thomas Preston](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Preston_%28British_Army_officer%29), the emphasis is heavily placed on the "great number of heavy [clubs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Club_%28weapon%29) and [snowballs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snowball)" being hurled by a terrifying, unruly mob. 

Same exact event. Wildly different points of view. Wildly different [realities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reality).

![Paul Revere's famous engraving of the Boston Massacre illustrates how point of view can be weaponized; he emphasized innocent, unarmed colonists to rally Patriot sentiment while omitting the mob violence described in British accounts.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Boston_Massacre_high-res.jpg)

### Structuring an [Argument](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logical_argument)
Finally, how do authors use point of view to win an [argument](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logical_argument)? If you are trying to [persuade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persuasion) someone, you might think you should ignore anyone who disagrees with you. Actually, the opposite is true. 

**Authors often structure [persuasive texts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persuasive_writing) by directly addressing opposing points of view.** They literally invite the enemy's camera into their [essay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Essay)! Why? Because **acknowledging an opposing point of view can structurally strengthen an author's primary argument.** 

By saying, *"Now, some [critics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Critic) might argue that this [policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_policy) is too expensive, but here is exactly why they are wrong..."* the author disarms the [counterargument](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Counterargument) before the reader can even think of it themselves. It is a masterful structural [defense mechanism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Defence_mechanism).

### Final Thoughts
When you sit down for the [Praxis exam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Praxis_test), remember this: Point of View is not just about memorizing pronouns. It is about understanding the flow of information. It's about asking yourself: *Who is holding the camera? What are they forcing me to look at? And what are they trying to hide?* 

Master that, and you'll never read a text the same way again. Happy studying!
