# The Engine and the Economy of Nature
**A [Praxis Elementary Education](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Praxis_test) Study Guide – [Ecology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecology) and Adaptation**

Look out your window. What do you see? A tree? A [squirrel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Squirrel)? Maybe a [bird](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bird) hopping around looking for lunch? They look so peaceful, don't they? But don't let the stillness fool you. You are looking at a grand, multi-million-year experiment in survival. Every [organism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism) you see is a winner in the most ruthless, magnificent game ever played: the game of life. 

Today, we are going to explore how this game is played. We're going to break down the rules of [ecology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecology) and adaptation so clearly that you'll not only ace your [Praxis exam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Praxis_test), but you'll never look at a walk in the park the same way again. 

## Part 1: The Engine of Change
How did all this magnificent variety of life get here? To answer that, we have to travel back in time to meet a fellow who fundamentally changed our understanding of the [biological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology) world. 

**[Charles Darwin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Darwin) formally introduced the theory of natural selection in his 1859 book On the Origin of Species.** He didn’t have modern [microscopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microscope) or a map of the human genome. He just looked closely at [nature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nature) and noticed a pattern. 

He realized that **natural selection is the process by which [organisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism) better adapted to their [environment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_environment) tend to survive and produce more offspring.** 

Now, you've probably heard the famous catchphrase, "[survival of the fittest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Survival_of_the_fittest)." It conjures up images of the biggest, meanest [animals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Animal) dominating the [forest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forest). But that’s a deep misunderstanding! In [biology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology), **the phrase [survival of the fittest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Survival_of_the_fittest) refers to an [organism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism)'s [reproductive success](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reproductive_success) within an [environment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_environment) rather than pure physical strength.** A tiny, perfectly camouflaged [moth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moth) that successfully lays hundreds of eggs is far "fitter" than a massive, muscular [lion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lion) that fails to find a mate.

### The Raw Material of Evolution
But wait—where does this [fitness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fitness_%28biology%29) come from? How do [organisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism) become "better adapted"? 

For natural selection to work, you need options. Think of it like a [deck of cards](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_52-card_deck); if every card is the Seven of Clubs, you can't play [poker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poker). Therefore, **[genetic variation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genetic_variation) within a population is an absolute requirement for natural selection to occur.** 

Where does this variation come from? It's not magic; it's a [molecular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecule) accident. **Mutations are random changes in [DNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNA) that introduce new [genetic variations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genetic_variation) into a biological population.** Most mutations do nothing. Some are bad. But occasionally, a random [genetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genetics) typo gives an [organism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism) a slight edge. 

When the world changes, the rules of the game change. **Environmental changes can shift which physical or [behavioral traits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phenotypic_trait) are most advantageous for an [organism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism)'s survival.** If an ice age rolls in, suddenly that random mutation for thicker fur isn't just a quirky [trait](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phenotypic_trait)—it's the difference between [freezing to death](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypothermia) and surviving to [reproduce](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reproduction).

## Part 2: The Survival Toolkit
When a [trait](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phenotypic_trait) proves so useful that it gets passed down and becomes common in a population, we give it a special name.

> **An adaptation is a physical or behavioral [trait](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phenotypic_trait) that helps an [organism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism) survive and [reproduce](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reproduction) in its specific [environment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_environment).**

We can broadly split these survival tools into two categories:

| Type of Adaptation | Definition | Classic Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Structural** | **Structural adaptations are physical features of an [organism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism)'s anatomy that enhance survival.** | **Camouflage is a structural adaptation allowing an [organism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism) to visually blend in with the surrounding [environment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_environment).** |
| **Behavioral** | **Behavioral adaptations are specific actions an [organism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism) takes to survive in a particular [environment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_environment).** | **Migration is a behavioral adaptation involving the seasonal movement of animal populations from one region to another.** |

Think about that! Camouflage isn't something an [animal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Animal) *decides* to do. It’s built into their [anatomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anatomy) over millions of [generations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generation). Migration, on the other hand, is an action—a programmed [behavior](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Behavior) to escape [winter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Winter) or find breeding grounds.

## Part 3: The Economy of the Wild
Now, let's zoom out. [Organisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism) don't live in a [vacuum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vacuum). 

**An [ecosystem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem) consists of all the living [organisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism) in a specific area interacting with the non-living [environment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abiotic_component).** This includes the [plants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant), [animals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Animal), [bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacteria), as well as the rocks, [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water), and climate. 

To understand [ecosystems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem), you must follow the money. And in [biology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology), money is *[energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy)*.

### Where Does the [Energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy) Come From?
Look up! **The [sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun) acts as the primary source of [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy) for almost all terrestrial and shallow [marine ecosystems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marine_ecosystem) on [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth).** 

But you and I can't eat sunshine. We need a biological middleman. 
*   **Producers convert [solar energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_energy) into [chemical energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_energy) through the process of photosynthesis.** These are your [plants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant), [algae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algae), and certain [bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacteria). They are the foundation of the [ecological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecology) economy.
*   Everyone else is a consumer. **Consumers must obtain [chemical energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_energy) by eating other living [organisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism).**

### The Hierarchy of Eaters
Let's map out who eats whom:
1.  **Primary consumers are [herbivores](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herbivore) that obtain [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy) strictly by eating producers.** (e.g., a grasshopper eating a leaf).
2.  **Secondary consumers are carnivores or omnivores that eat primary consumers to obtain [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy).** (e.g., a [frog](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frog) eating the grasshopper).
3.  **[Tertiary consumers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apex_predator) are [apex predators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apex_predator) that obtain [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy) by eating secondary consumers.** (e.g., a hawk swooping down to eat the [frog](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frog)).

When we draw a straight line from the [sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun) to the grass to the grasshopper to the [frog](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frog) to the hawk, we are looking at a [food chain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food_chain). **A [food chain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food_chain) traces the linear transfer of [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy) from one [organism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism) to another within an [ecosystem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem).** 

But [nature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nature) is rarely that simple. The [frog](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frog) might also eat a [fly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fly); the hawk might eat a [mouse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mouse). In reality, **a food web is a complex network of multiple interconnected [food chains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food_chain) within a single [ecosystem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem).**

At the end of the line, we have nature's recycling crew. **Decomposers break down dead [organic matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_matter) to recycle essential [nutrients](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nutrient) back into the soil.** Fungi and [bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacteria) ensure that the building blocks of life are never wasted.

### The Cosmic Rules of [Thermodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamics) in [Biology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology)
Here is where things get brilliantly fascinating. When the grasshopper eats the grass, it doesn't get to keep all that [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy). It has to hop around, breathe, and stay warm! 

> **[Organisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism) lose approximately ninety percent of consumed [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy) as heat during normal [metabolic processes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metabolism).**

Because so much is lost, there is a strict thermodynamic budget. **The ten percent rule states that only about ten percent of the [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy) at one [trophic level](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trophic_level) is passed on to the next higher [trophic level](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trophic_level).** This is why we have billions of blades of grass, thousands of grasshoppers, hundreds of [frogs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frog), but only a few hawks! There simply isn't enough [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy) at the top to support millions of [apex predators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apex_predator).

Notice the difference in how [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy) and [matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matter) move through the system:
*   **[Energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy) flows through an [ecosystem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem) in a single direction from producers upward to various levels of consumers.** It comes from the [sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun), moves up the web, and radiates out into space as heat.
*   However, [matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matter) stays here. **[Nutrients](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nutrient) are continuously recycled through an [ecosystem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem) by the chemical action of decomposers.** [Energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy) flows, but [nutrients](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nutrient) cycle!

## Part 4: The Delicate Balance
Because all these creatures are locked in this grand economic web, they rely deeply on one another. **[Interdependence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biological_interaction) describes the [biological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology) reliance of [organisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism) on other [organisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism) and the [environment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_environment) for survival.**

Some species hold the whole puzzle together. 
**A [keystone species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Keystone_species) is an [organism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism) that has a disproportionately large stabilizing impact on an entire [ecosystem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem).** Think of the [sea otter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sea_otter) in the Pacific kelp forests. They are a classic [keystone species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Keystone_species). If you remove them, the [sea urchins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sea_urchin) they normally eat will experience explosive [population growth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Population_growth), chewing away the base of the [kelp](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelp) and destroying the entire [habitat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habitat). Therefore, **the removal of a [keystone species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Keystone_species) frequently leads to the collapse or drastic alteration of an entire [ecosystem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem).**

What happens when we artificially mess with this balance? Let's say human [hunters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hunting) wipe out the local [wolves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolf) in a national park.

1.  **Removing a [top predator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apex_predator) from an [ecosystem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem) causes an immediate overpopulation of the predator's primary prey species.** (Without [wolves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolf), the local [elk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elk) populations immediately explode).
2.  **Overpopulation of a [herbivorous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herbivore) prey species routinely leads to the severe depletion of local producer populations.** (The millions of [elk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elk) eat all the young [willow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Willow) and [aspen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aspen) trees, devastating the [riverbanks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bank_%28geography%29) and [starving](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Starvation) out other species).

There are physical limits to how many [elk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elk)—or any [organism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism)—a [habitat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habitat) can hold. **[Carrying capacity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carrying_capacity) is the maximum population size of a specific species that a given [environment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_environment) can sustain indefinitely.** When a population exceeds the [carrying capacity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carrying_capacity), [starvation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Starvation) and [disease](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disease) will violently crash their numbers back down to sustainable limits.

### Uninvited Guests
Sometimes, the disruption comes from the outside. 
**An invasive species is a [non-native](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Introduced_species) [organism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism) that causes severe [ecological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecology) or economic harm to a new [environment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_environment).** 

Why do invasive species cause so much trouble? Why do they spread like [wildfire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wildfire)? Because they didn't evolve in this specific game. **Invasive species typically experience rapid [population growth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Population_growth) due to a total lack of natural predators in the new [environment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_environment).** 

With no predators to keep them in check, they multiply endlessly. The tragic result is that **the introduction of an invasive species often results in native species being outcompeted for limited survival resources.**

When a native species loses its food, loses its [habitat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habitat), and cannot genetically adapt fast enough to the changing rules of the game, it faces the absolute finality of [nature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nature). **[Extinction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction) occurs when every individual member of a biological species dies.**
