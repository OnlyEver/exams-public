# The Engine and the Economy of Nature
**A [Praxis Elementary Education](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Praxis_test) Study Guide – [Ecology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecology) and [Adaptation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adaptation)**

Look out your [window](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Window). What do you see? A [tree](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tree)? A [squirrel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Squirrel)? Maybe a [bird](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bird) hopping around looking for lunch? They look so peaceful, don't they? But don't let the stillness fool you. You are looking at a grand, multi-million-year experiment in survival. Every [organism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism) you see is a winner in the most ruthless, magnificent game ever played: the game of [life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life). 

Today, we are going to explore how this game is played. We're going to break down the rules of [ecology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecology) and [adaptation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adaptation) so clearly that you'll not only ace your [Praxis exam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Praxis_test), but you'll never look at a walk in the park the same way again. 

## Part 1: The Engine of Change
How did all this magnificent variety of life get here? To answer that, we have to travel back in time to meet a fellow who fundamentally changed our understanding of the [biological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology) world. 

**[Charles Darwin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Darwin) formally introduced the theory of [natural selection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_selection) in his [1859](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1859_in_science) book [On the Origin of Species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/On_the_Origin_of_Species).** He didn’t have modern [microscopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microscope) or a map of the [human genome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_genome). He just looked closely at [nature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nature) and noticed a pattern. 

![Charles Darwin in 1859, the year he formalized the theory of natural selection by publishing "On the Origin of Species."](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Charles_Darwin_aged_51.jpg)

He realized that **[natural selection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_selection) is the process by which [organisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism) better [adapted](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adaptation) to their [environment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_environment) tend to [survive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Survival) and produce more [offspring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Offspring).** 

Now, you've probably heard the famous catchphrase, "[survival of the fittest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Survival_of_the_fittest)." It conjures up images of the biggest, meanest [animals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Animal) dominating the [forest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forest). But that’s a deep misunderstanding! In [biology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology), **the phrase [survival of the fittest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Survival_of_the_fittest) refers to an [organism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism)'s [reproductive success](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reproductive_success) within an [environment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_environment) rather than pure [physical strength](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_strength).** A tiny, perfectly [camouflaged](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camouflage) [moth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moth) that successfully lays hundreds of [eggs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egg_%28biology%29) is far "fitter" than a massive, muscular [lion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lion) that fails to find a [mate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mating).

![The peppered moth demonstrates "fitness" in action: morphological variations in color provide camouflage that drastically improves an individual's chances of surviving predation to reproduce.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Lichte_en_zwarte_versie_berkenspanner_crop.jpg)

### The Raw Material of [Evolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evolution)
But wait—where does this [fitness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fitness_%28biology%29) come from? How do [organisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism) become "better [adapted](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adaptation)"? 

For [natural selection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_selection) to work, you need options. Think of it like a [deck of cards](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_52-card_deck); if every card is the Seven of Clubs, you can't play [poker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poker). Therefore, **[genetic variation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genetic_variation) within a [population](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Population_genetics) is an absolute requirement for [natural selection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_selection) to occur.** 

Where does this variation come from? It's not magic; it's a [molecular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecule) accident. **[Mutations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mutation) are random changes in [DNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNA) that introduce new [genetic variations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genetic_variation) into a [biological population](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Population_biology).** Most [mutations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mutation) do nothing. Some are bad. But occasionally, a random [genetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genetics) typo gives an [organism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism) a slight edge. 

![Natural selection acts upon the genetic variation introduced by mutations. Here, a random mutation for darker coloration provides an advantage against predators, allowing the darker trait to spread through the population.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Mutation_and_selection_diagram.svg)

When the world changes, the rules of the game change. **[Environmental changes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Environmental_change) can shift which physical or [behavioral traits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phenotypic_trait) are most advantageous for an [organism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism)'s [survival](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Survival).** If an [ice age](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ice_age) rolls in, suddenly that random [mutation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mutation) for thicker [fur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fur) isn't just a quirky [trait](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phenotypic_trait)—it's the difference between [freezing to death](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypothermia) and surviving to [reproduce](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reproduction).

## Part 2: The Survival Toolkit
When a [trait](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phenotypic_trait) proves so useful that it gets passed down and becomes common in a [population](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Population_biology), we give it a special name.

> **An [adaptation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adaptation) is a physical or behavioral [trait](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phenotypic_trait) that helps an [organism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism) survive and [reproduce](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reproduction) in its specific [environment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_environment).**

We can broadly split these survival tools into two categories:

| Type of Adaptation | Definition | Classic Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Structural** | **Structural [adaptations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adaptation) are physical features of an [organism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism)'s anatomy that enhance [survival](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Survival).** | **[Camouflage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camouflage) is a structural [adaptation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adaptation) allowing an [organism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism) to visually blend in with the surrounding [environment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_environment).** |
| **Behavioral** | **Behavioral [adaptations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethology) are specific actions an [organism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism) takes to survive in a particular [environment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_environment).** | **[Migration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Animal_migration) is a behavioral [adaptation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adaptation) involving the seasonal movement of animal populations from one region to another.** |

![Satellite tracking reveals the massive geographical scope of avian migration. This programmed behavior allows animals to seasonally relocate to environments with more favorable survival and reproductive conditions.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Bar-tailed_Godwit_migration.jpg)

Think about that! [Camouflage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camouflage) isn't something an [animal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Animal) *decides* to do. It’s built into their [anatomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anatomy) over millions of [generations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generation). [Migration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Animal_migration), on the other hand, is an action—a programmed [behavior](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Behavior) to escape [winter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Winter) or find breeding grounds.

## Part 3: The Economy of the Wild
Now, let's zoom out. [Organisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism) don't live in a [vacuum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vacuum). 

**An [ecosystem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem) consists of all the living [organisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism) in a specific area interacting with the non-living [environment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abiotic_component).** This includes the [plants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant), [animals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Animal), [bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacteria), as well as the [rocks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rock_%28geology%29), [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water), and [climate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate). 

To understand [ecosystems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem), you must follow the money. And in [biology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology), money is *[energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy)*.

### Where Does the [Energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy) Come From?
Look up! **The [sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun) acts as the primary source of [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy) for almost all [terrestrial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terrestrial_ecosystem) and shallow [marine ecosystems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marine_ecosystem) on [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth).** 

But you and I can't eat sunshine. We need a biological middleman. 
*   **[Producers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primary_producer) convert [solar energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_energy) into [chemical energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_energy) through the process of [photosynthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photosynthesis).** These are your [plants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant), [algae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algae), and certain [bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacteria). They are the foundation of the [ecological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecology) economy.

![Primary producers act as the foundation of the ecological economy by transforming solar energy, water, and carbon dioxide into usable chemical energy through photosynthesis.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Simple_photosynthesis_overview.svg)

*   Everyone else is a consumer. **[Consumers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heterotroph) must obtain [chemical energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_energy) by eating other living [organisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism).**

### The Hierarchy of Eaters
Let's map out who eats whom:
1.  **[Primary consumers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primary_consumer) are [herbivores](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herbivore) that obtain [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy) strictly by eating [producers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primary_producer).** (e.g., a [grasshopper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grasshopper) eating a [leaf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leaf)).
2.  **[Secondary consumers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consumer_%28food_chain%29) are [carnivores](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carnivore) or [omnivores](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Omnivore) that eat [primary consumers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primary_consumer) to obtain [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy).** (e.g., a [frog](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frog) eating the [grasshopper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grasshopper)).
3.  **[Tertiary consumers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apex_predator) are [apex predators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apex_predator) that obtain [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy) by eating [secondary consumers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consumer_%28food_chain%29).** (e.g., a [hawk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hawk) swooping down to eat the [frog](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frog)).

When we draw a straight line from the [sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun) to the [grass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grass) to the [grasshopper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grasshopper) to the [frog](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frog) to the [hawk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hawk), we are looking at a [food chain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food_chain). **A [food chain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food_chain) traces the linear transfer of [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy) from one [organism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism) to another within an [ecosystem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem).** 

But [nature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nature) is rarely that simple. The [frog](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frog) might also eat a [fly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fly); the [hawk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hawk) might eat a [mouse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mouse). In reality, **a [food web](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food_web) is a complex network of multiple interconnected [food chains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food_chain) within a single [ecosystem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem).**

At the end of the line, we have nature's recycling crew. **[Decomposers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decomposer) break down dead [organic matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_matter) to recycle essential [nutrients](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nutrient) back into the [soil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soil).** [Fungi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fungus) and [bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacteria) ensure that the building blocks of [life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life) are never wasted.

### The Cosmic Rules of [Thermodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamics) in [Biology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology)
Here is where things get brilliantly fascinating. When the [grasshopper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grasshopper) eats the [grass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grass), it doesn't get to keep all that [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy). It has to hop around, [breathe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Respiration_%28physiology%29), and stay warm! 

> **[Organisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism) lose approximately ninety percent of consumed [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy) as [heat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat) during normal [metabolic processes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metabolism).**

Because so much is lost, there is a strict thermodynamic budget. **The [ten percent rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ten_percent_law) states that only about ten percent of the [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy) at one [trophic level](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trophic_level) is passed on to the next higher [trophic level](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trophic_level).** This is why we have billions of blades of [grass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grass), thousands of [grasshoppers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grasshopper), hundreds of [frogs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frog), but only a few [hawks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hawk)! There simply isn't enough [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy) at the top to support millions of [apex predators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apex_predator).

![A trophic pyramid visualizes the ten percent rule: energy is lost at each ascending step, resulting in drastically lower total biomass at higher trophic levels. The complex interconnections form the food web.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/TrophicWeb.jpg)

Notice the difference in how [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy) and [matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matter) move through the system:
*   **[Energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy) flows through an [ecosystem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem) in a single direction from [producers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primary_producer) upward to various levels of [consumers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heterotroph).** It comes from the [sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun), moves up the [web](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food_web), and radiates out into [space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_space) as [heat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat).
*   However, [matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matter) stays here. **[Nutrients](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nutrient) are continuously [recycled](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biogeochemical_cycle) through an [ecosystem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem) by the chemical action of [decomposers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decomposer).** [Energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy) flows, but [nutrients](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nutrient) cycle!

## Part 4: The Delicate Balance
Because all these creatures are locked in this grand economic [web](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food_web), they rely deeply on one another. **[Interdependence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biological_interaction) describes the [biological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology) reliance of [organisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism) on other [organisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism) and the [environment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_environment) for [survival](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Survival).**

Some [species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Species) hold the whole puzzle together. 
**A [keystone species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Keystone_species) is an [organism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism) that has a disproportionately large stabilizing impact on an entire [ecosystem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem).** Think of the [sea otter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sea_otter) in the [Pacific](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pacific_Ocean) [kelp forests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelp_forest). They are a classic [keystone species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Keystone_species). If you remove them, the [sea urchins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sea_urchin) they normally eat will experience explosive [population growth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Population_growth), chewing away the base of the [kelp](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelp) and destroying the entire [habitat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habitat). Therefore, **the removal of a [keystone species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Keystone_species) frequently leads to the collapse or drastic alteration of an entire [ecosystem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem).**

![Sea otters are keystone species in kelp forest ecosystems. By preying heavily on herbivores like sea urchins, they actively prevent the urchins from clear-cutting and destroying the foundational kelp.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Sea_otter_with_sea_urchin.jpg)

What happens when we artificially mess with this balance? Let's say human [hunters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hunting) wipe out the local [wolves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolf) in a [national park](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_park).

1.  **Removing a [top predator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apex_predator) from an [ecosystem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem) causes an immediate [overpopulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Overpopulation_in_wild_animals) of the predator's primary [prey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Predation) [species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Species).** (Without [wolves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolf), the local [elk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elk) populations immediately explode).
2.  **[Overpopulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Overpopulation_in_wild_animals) of a [herbivorous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herbivore) [prey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Predation) [species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Species) routinely leads to the severe depletion of local [producer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primary_producer) populations.** (The millions of [elk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elk) eat all the young [willow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Willow) and [aspen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aspen) trees, devastating the [riverbanks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bank_%28geography%29) and [starving](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Starvation) out other [species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Species)).

![Photographic evidence of ecological interdependence: Riparian willow growth in Yellowstone was severely stunted by elk overpopulation (left). After wolves were reintroduced to control the elk, the producers successfully recovered (right).](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Fig._1_-Riparian_willow_recovery_(26485120926)_horiz.jpg)

There are physical limits to how many [elk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elk)—or any [organism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism)—a [habitat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habitat) can hold. **[Carrying capacity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carrying_capacity) is the maximum population size of a specific [species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Species) that a given [environment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_environment) can sustain indefinitely.** When a [population](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Population_biology) exceeds the [carrying capacity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carrying_capacity), [starvation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Starvation) and [disease](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disease) will violently crash their numbers back down to sustainable limits.

![The logistic growth curve demonstrates how environmental constraints naturally cap an accelerating population once it hits the habitat's maximum carrying capacity.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Logistic_Carrying_Capacity.svg)

### Uninvited Guests
Sometimes, the disruption comes from the outside. 
**An [invasive species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invasive_species) is a [non-native](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Introduced_species) [organism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism) that causes severe [ecological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecology) or economic harm to a new [environment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_environment).** 

Why do [invasive species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invasive_species) cause so much trouble? Why do they spread like [wildfire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wildfire)? Because they didn't [evolve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evolution) in this specific game. **[Invasive species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invasive_species) typically experience rapid [population growth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Population_growth) due to a total lack of [natural predators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Predation) in the new [environment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_environment).** 

![An invasive species, Kudzu grows unchecked due to an absence of its natural predators. It rapidly blankets and smothers native tree populations, aggressively outcompeting them for sunlight.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Kudzu_on_trees_in_Atlanta%2C_Georgia.jpg)

With no [predators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Predation) to keep them in check, they multiply endlessly. The tragic result is that **the introduction of an [invasive species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invasive_species) often results in [native species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indigenous_%28ecology%29) being [outcompeted](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Competition_%28biology%29) for limited [survival](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Survival) resources.**

When a [native species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indigenous_%28ecology%29) loses its food, loses its [habitat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habitat), and cannot genetically adapt fast enough to the changing rules of the game, it faces the absolute finality of [nature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nature). **[Extinction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction) occurs when every individual member of a biological [species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Species) dies.**

The story of [ecology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecology) is the story of profound connection. From Darwin's revolutionary insight into [natural selection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_selection) to the relentless, one-way flow of [solar energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_energy) through [food webs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food_web), every concept you've just read is a necessary gear in the engine of [life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life). Remember that [mutations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mutation) provide the [genetic variation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genetic_variation), [adaptations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adaptation) provide the survival toolkit, and available [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy) dictates the hard limits of the [ecosystem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem). 

Understand these mechanisms, see how beautifully they lock together, and you won't just pass your test—you’ll deeply understand the very machinery of the [natural world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nature). Good luck!
