Welcome to the greatest machine you’ll ever study: **[Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth)**. 

When we look down at the ground or up at the sky, we tend to think of our planet as a static, unchanging backdrop. But the reality is far more thrilling! Earth is a massive, dynamic [heat engine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat_engine). It’s breathing, shifting, recycling, and reshaping itself constantly. 

To ace the [Praxis Elementary Education](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Praxis_test) exam, we aren't just going to memorize rock types and [clouds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cloud). We are going to understand *how the machine works*. Let’s tear the planet apart, layer by layer, and see what makes it tick.

## 1. The Anatomy of Earth: What’s on the Inside?

If you were to slice the Earth right down the middle, you’d find that **the Earth is composed of four main compositional layers**. These four main compositional layers of the Earth are the **crust**, the **[mantle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_mantle)**, the **[outer core](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_outer_core)**, and the **[inner core](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_inner_core)**. 

But wait, nature loves variety! The outermost layer, **the Earth's crust, is the outermost solid layer of the planet**, but it isn't uniform. The Earth's crust is divided into **oceanic crust** and **continental crust**. 

Think of the crust like boats floating in water. 
*   **Continental crust** is significantly thicker than oceanic crust, but it is primarily composed of *less dense* granitic rocks. It rides high like a thick wooden raft.
*   **Oceanic crust**, on the other hand, is primarily composed of *denser* [basaltic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basalt) rocks. It is thinner, but heavy, riding lower like a dense sheet of iron.

Directly beneath the Earth's crust sits the **mantle**. The mantle is the thickest compositional layer of the Earth. Now, here is a concept that stumps a lot of people: **the mantle consists of solid rock that slowly deforms and flows over [geological time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geologic_time_scale)**. It is *not* a molten ocean of magma! Imagine [silly putty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silly_Putty) or warm [wax](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wax); it is solid, but if you leave it alone for millions of years, it flows.

Plunging deeper, we hit the core. 
*   The **outer core** is a liquid layer of the Earth composed predominantly of iron and nickel. Because the Earth is spinning and the core is extremely hot, the liquid iron roils and churns. These **[convection currents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convection) within the liquid outer core generate the Earth's magnetic field**—the invisible shield that protects our atmosphere from being blown away by [solar wind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_wind)!
*   Finally, at the very center, the **inner core** is the innermost layer of the Earth. It is a solid ball composed primarily of iron and nickel. How can it be solid when it’s hotter than the surface of the sun? **Extreme [pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pressure) maintains the inner core in a solid state despite extremely high temperatures.** The [atoms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atom) are simply squeezed too tightly together to melt.

### The Mechanical Layers: How the Rock *Behaves*

[Geologists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geologist) also categorize the outer layers not by what they are made of, but by how they act. 

> **Crucial Definition:** The **[lithosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lithosphere)** comprises the crust and the uppermost rigid portion of the mantle. It is the brittle, solid shell of the planet. 

This lithosphere isn't one solid eggshell; **the lithosphere is broken into numerous distinct tectonic plates**. 

Beneath the lithosphere is the **asthenosphere**. The asthenosphere is located directly below the lithosphere and is a highly viscous and mechanically weak layer of the upper mantle. Because it is weak and deformable, the rigid **tectonic plates float and move on top of the ductile asthenosphere**.

## 2. The Envelope of Gas: Earth’s Atmosphere

Earth isn't just rock; it’s wrapped in a blanket of [gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas). **The Earth's [atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth) is divided into five primary layers.** But how do we know where one layer ends and another begins? It’s not arbitrary! **The atmospheric layers are defined by distinct changes in temperature gradients**. Every time the temperature stops falling and starts rising (or vice versa), you’ve hit a new layer boundary.

Let’s travel upwards from the ground:

| Layer | Key Characteristics & Phenomena |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Troposphere** | The lowest atmospheric layer of the Earth. This is where we live! **Nearly all Earth [weather](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weather) phenomena occur within the troposphere.** As you climb a [mountain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mountain) here, it gets harder to breathe because **[atmospheric pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure) decreases as altitude increases in the troposphere**. |
| **[Stratosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stratosphere)** | The atmospheric layer situated directly above the troposphere. Crucially, **the Earth's [ozone layer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ozone_layer) is located primarily within the stratosphere**. Why do we care? **The ozone layer absorbs the majority of the Sun's harmful ultraviolet radiation**, acting as Earth's sunscreen. |
| **[Mesosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mesosphere)** | Situated directly above the stratosphere. It is bitterly cold here. This is our physical shield against space debris—**most incoming meteors burn up upon entering the mesosphere**. |
| **[Thermosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermosphere)** | Situated directly above the mesosphere. It gets incredibly hot here due to solar radiation interacting with stray molecules. Those beautiful glowing lights at the poles? **[Auroras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aurora) primarily occur within the thermosphere.** |
| **Exosphere** | The outermost layer of the Earth's atmosphere. The **exosphere gradually transitions into the vacuum of outer space**. |

## 3. The Hydrosphere: Water, Water Everywhere

Before we look at how the Earth's surface moves, we have to look at what covers it. **The [hydrosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrosphere) encompasses all [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water) found on, under, and above the surface of the Earth.**

Here is the sobering math of our planet's water supply:
*   **[Oceans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ocean) contain approximately 97 percent of the Earth's total water supply.** (It is all [saltwater](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seawater)).
*   **[Freshwater](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fresh_water) accounts for less than 3 percent of the Earth's total water supply.** 
*   If you're thirsty, good luck getting to most of that 3%, because **most of the Earth's freshwater is trapped in glaciers and ice caps!**

## 4. The Great Planetary Dance: Plate Tectonics & Volcanoes

Now we get to the driving force of [geology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geology). **The theory of plate tectonics describes the large-scale motion of the Earth's lithosphere.** 

Why do [continents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent) move? Because the Earth is trying to cool down. **[Convection currents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convection_%28heat_transfer%29) within the Earth's mantle drive the lateral movement of tectonic plates.** Hot rock rises, cools near the crust, and sinks back down, dragging the plates along for the ride like a conveyor belt.

Where these plates meet—the **plate boundaries**—is where the real magic (and destruction) happens. There are three types of boundaries:

1.  **[Divergent Boundaries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divergent_boundary):** These exist where two tectonic plates move *away* from each other. As they separate, mantle material wells up to fill the gap. Consequently, **[mid-ocean ridges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mid-ocean_ridge) form exclusively at divergent plate boundaries**.
2.  **Convergent Boundaries:** These exist where two tectonic plates move *toward* each other. When plates crash, something has to give. 
    *   **Subduction** occurs when one tectonic plate is forced underneath another tectonic plate at a convergent boundary. As the plate is shoved deep into the mantle, a massive trench is dug. Therefore, **deep ocean trenches are formed by the subduction of tectonic plates**.
    *   What if both plates are thick, [buoyant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buoyancy) continental crust? Neither wants to subduct! So they crumple upwards. **Continental mountain ranges form when two continental plates collide at a convergent boundary** (this is how the Himalayas are being formed right now!).
3.  **Transform Boundaries:** These exist where two tectonic plates slide *past* one another horizontally. They get stuck, pressure builds, and *SNAP!* **[Earthquakes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earthquake) frequently occur along transform plate boundaries.** The most famous example? **The [San Andreas Fault](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Andreas_Fault) in [California](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/California) represents a transform plate boundary.**

### Blowing Off Steam: Volcanism
All this cracking and colliding brings extreme heat to the surface. **A volcano is a geological rupture that allows hot [lava](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lava), ash, and gases to escape from a [magma chamber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magma_chamber).** 

> **Magma vs. Lava:** Know the difference! **Magma is molten rock located beneath the surface of the Earth.** Once it breaks out, **[lava](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lava) is molten rock that has erupted onto the surface of the Earth.**

Because boundaries are weak spots in the crust, **volcanoes frequently form near convergent plate boundaries** (as subducting plates melt) and **volcanoes frequently form near [divergent plate boundaries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divergent_boundary)** (as the crust splits apart).

But wait, what about [Hawaii](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hawaii)? Hawaii sits right in the middle of the massive Pacific Plate, nowhere near a boundary! 
Nature always provides exceptions. **[Volcanic hotspots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hotspot_%28geology%29) are regions fed by underlying mantle that is anomalously hot.** These deep mantle plumes punch right through the moving tectonic plate. Thus, **the [Hawaiian Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hawaiian_Islands) were formed by volcanic activity over a stationary mantle hotspot.** As the plate drifted over the hotspot, it left a trail of [island](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Island) volcanoes!

## 5. Sculpting the Surface: Weathering, Erosion, & Deposition

Tectonic plates and volcanoes build the Earth *up*, but weather and [gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity) tear the Earth *down*. This happens in three sequential steps:

### Step A: Weathering (Breaking it down)
**Weathering is the in-place breakdown of rocks, soil, and minerals at the surface of the Earth.** The rock doesn't move yet; it just breaks. It comes in two flavors:
*   **Mechanical Weathering:** This process physically breaks rocks into smaller pieces, but **mechanical weathering does not alter the chemical composition of the rock.** If you smash a granite boulder, it's just smaller pieces of granite. A prime example is **[frost wedging](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frost_weathering)**, a mechanical weathering process caused by the freezing and expanding of water in rock cracks. Water seeps in, freezes, expands, and cracks the rock open!
*   **Chemical Weathering:** This process breaks down rocks through [chemical reactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_reaction). Crucially, **chemical weathering alters the molecular structure of the original rock.** 
    *   For example, **[oxidation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redox) is a chemical weathering process that produces rust on iron-bearing rocks.** 
    *   Furthermore, **[acid rain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acid_rain) accelerates the chemical weathering of carbonate rocks such as limestone**, effectively dissolving them over time.

### Step B: Erosion (Moving it out)
Once the rock is broken into fragments, it goes for a ride. **[Erosion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erosion) is the transport of weathered Earth materials by natural forces.**
*   **Moving liquid water is the primary agent of soil and rock erosion on Earth.** [Rivers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/River) carve massive canyons over deep time.
*   **[Wind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wind) causes erosion by blowing loose sediment away from dry land surfaces.**
*   **Glaciers cause erosion by scraping the underlying rock surface as massive ice sheets move**, essentially acting like planetary bulldozers. 

### Step C: Deposition (Dropping it off)
Gravity always wins, and eventually, the moving rock has to stop. **Deposition is the geological process of adding transported sediments and soil to a landform.**
*   Where a river meets the sea, it slows down and drops its sediment payload. Therefore, **river deltas form through the deposition of sediment at the mouth of a river.**
*   In [deserts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Desert), **sand dunes form through the deposition of wind-blown sand.**

## 6. The Ultimate Recycling Program: The Rock Cycle

Because Earth operates as a [closed system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_system) (it doesn't gain or lose much matter), it has to recycle. **The [rock cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rock_cycle) describes the continuous geological transformation of rocks from one type to another.** 

Any rock on Earth is classified by *how it was formed*, and it falls into one of three categories:

1.  **Igneous Rocks:** These rocks **form from the cooling and solidification of magma or lava**. If a volcano erupts and the lava cools, you get an igneous rock (like [basalt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basalt) or obsidian).
2.  **Sedimentary Rocks:** Over time, weathering and erosion break that igneous rock into [sand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sand). That sand washes into the ocean, builds up layer by layer, and gets cemented together. **Sedimentary rocks form from the accumulation, compaction, and cementation of mineral particles.** Because life lives on the surface and gets buried in [mud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mud), **fossils are found almost exclusively within sedimentary rocks.**
3.  **Metamorphic Rocks:** Now take that sedimentary rock and let plate tectonics shove it deep underground where it gets incredibly hot and squeezed. **Metamorphic rocks form when pre-existing rocks are subjected to high heat and extreme pressure.** But remember: **the formation of metamorphic rock occurs without the complete melting of the original rock.** It bakes and twists like clay in a kiln. If it completely melted, it would become magma again, and eventually cool back into... an igneous rock! The cycle begins anew.
