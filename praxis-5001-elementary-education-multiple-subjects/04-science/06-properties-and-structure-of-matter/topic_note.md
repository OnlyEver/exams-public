Look at a glass of [ice water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ice_water) resting on a [solid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid) [oak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oak) table. To the [naked eye](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naked_eye), this scene presents a tranquil, continuous reality. Yet, beneath the threshold of our vision, it is a chaotic theater of tiny, invisible entities vibrating, colliding, and binding together. **[Matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matter) is defined as anything that has [mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass) and takes up [space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space).** And the profound revelation of modern [science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Science) is that **all matter is composed of tiny fundamental particles called [atoms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atom)**. To understand the physical [universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe)—and to confidently teach its foundational principles—we must understand the rules that govern these particles, how they arrange themselves into the [macroscopic states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macroscopic_scale) we experience, and the precise ways they transform when they interact.

## The Anatomy of the [Atom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atom)

If we were to magnify a single drop of [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water) until it was the size of the [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth), the atoms within it would be roughly the size of [baseballs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baseball_%28ball%29). **An atom is the smallest unit of an [element](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_element) that retains the [chemical properties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_property) of that element.** Despite being the fundamental building block of matter, the atom itself has a highly organized internal structure consisting of three primary [subatomic particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subatomic_particle): [protons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton), [neutrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron), and [electrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron).

### The [Nucleus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_nucleus) and [Electron Cloud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_orbital)
At the very core of the atom lies the **nucleus**. **The nucleus is the small, dense, [positively charged](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_charge) center of an atom.** It is home to two types of particles:
*   **Protons are located inside the nucleus of an atom.** **A proton is a subatomic particle with a positive [electrical charge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_charge).**
*   **Neutrons are located inside the nucleus of an atom.** **A neutron is a subatomic particle with no electrical charge.**

Both of these heavy nuclear particles dictate the atom's mass. **Protons have a mass of approximately one [atomic mass unit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dalton_%28unit%29)** (amu), and similarly, **neutrons have a mass of approximately one atomic mass unit**. Because they are bound together at the center, **most of the mass of an atom is concentrated in the nucleus.**

Swarming around this dense center are the electrons. **An electron is a subatomic particle with a negative electrical charge.** These particles do not sit still; rather, **electrons [orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_orbital) the nucleus of an atom in regions called [electron clouds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_orbital) or [shells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_shell).** They are astonishingly light. **The mass of an electron is roughly [1/1836th](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton-to-electron_mass_ratio) the mass of a proton.** Because these tiny electrons orbit at immense distances relative to the size of the nucleus, a shocking physical truth emerges: **most of the [volume](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume) of an atom consists of [empty space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vacuum) in the electron cloud.**

![A simplified diagram of an atom showing the dense central nucleus composed of protons and neutrons, surrounded by orbiting electrons.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Atom_Diagram.svg)

### Identity and Variation
What distinguishes [carbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon) from [gold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gold), or [oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen) from [iron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron)? Simply the number of protons. **The number of protons determines the elemental identity of an atom.** 
*   **The [atomic number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_number) of an element is equal to the number of protons in the atoms of that element.** 
*   Meanwhile, **the [mass number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass_number) of an atom is the sum of the protons and neutrons in the atom.**

While all atoms of a specific element must have the exact same number of protons, their neutron and electron counts can vary:
*   **[Isotopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotope) are atoms of the same element that have different numbers of neutrons.** 
*   **[Ions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ion) are atoms that have gained or lost electrons to acquire a net electrical charge.**

![The three naturally-occurring isotopes of hydrogen: protium (zero neutrons), deuterium (one neutron), and tritium (two neutrons). All three share the same atomic number but have different mass numbers.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Blausen_0530_HydrogenIsotopes.png)

## Organizing Matter: Elements, [Compounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_compound), and [Mixtures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mixture)

With our atomic building blocks established, we can examine how matter organizes itself on a macroscopic scale.

### [Pure Substances](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_substance)
**An element is a pure substance consisting of only one type of atom.** Because it is perfectly uniform at the atomic level, it cannot be broken down into simpler substances. **Gold is an example of an element.**

However, elements rarely exist in isolation. When different elements chemically bond, they form a new pure substance. **A [compound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_compound) is a pure substance formed by the [chemical combination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_bond) of two or more different elements in fixed proportions.** For example, **water is a compound made of [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen) and oxygen** (always in a fixed 2:1 ratio). One of the most fascinating phenomena in [chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemistry) is that **compounds have different [physical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_property) and chemical properties than their constituent elements.** Hydrogen is a highly explosive [gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas), and oxygen aggressively fuels [combustion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Combustion), yet when bonded together into water, they form a [liquid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liquid) that extinguishes [fire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fire).

![A water molecule is a chemical compound consisting of two hydrogen atoms bonded to a single oxygen atom in a fixed 2:1 ratio.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Water_molecule_(1).svg)

### Mixtures and [Solutions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solution_%28chemistry%29)
Often, substances intermingle without undergoing a [chemical bond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_bond). **A mixture is a combination of two or more substances that are not chemically combined.** Because no [chemical reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_reaction) has taken place, **each substance in a mixture retains its own individual chemical properties.** Mixtures fall into two distinct categories:

1.  **[Heterogeneous Mixtures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mixture):** **A [heterogeneous mixture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mixture) contains substances that are not evenly distributed throughout.** If you take two scoops of it, they might look different. **A bowl of [trail mix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trail_mix) is an example of a heterogeneous mixture.** Within this category, a **[suspension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suspension_%28chemistry%29) is a heterogeneous mixture containing large solid particles that will settle upon standing** (like muddy water).

![Trail mix is a heterogeneous mixture where the individual components—such as nuts, seeds, and raisins—remain distinct and unevenly distributed.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Planters-Trail-Mix.jpg)

2.  **[Homogeneous Mixtures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mixture):** **A [homogeneous mixture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mixture) contains substances that are evenly distributed.** Because of this perfect distribution, **a homogeneous mixture appears uniform throughout.** In chemistry, **a [solution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solution_%28chemistry%29) is another name for a homogeneous mixture.** 

In a liquid solution, we identify two parts. **A [solute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solute) is the substance that is dissolved in a solution**, while **a [solvent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solvent) is the substance that does the dissolving in a solution.** For instance, **in a [saltwater](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saline_water) solution, [salt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salt_%28chemistry%29) acts as the solute** and **water acts as the solvent.** Water is so incredibly effective at dissolving various solutes that **water is commonly referred to as the [universal solvent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solvent).** Solutions are not limited to liquids; **an [alloy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alloy) is a [solid solution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_solution) composed of two or more [metals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metal).** **[Steel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steel) is an example of an alloy**, consisting of iron evenly mixed with carbon and other [trace elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trace_element).

![Making a homogeneous saline solution by dissolving table salt (the solute) into water (the solvent). Because the salt is evenly distributed, the resulting mixture is perfectly uniform.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/SaltInWaterSolutionLiquid.jpg)

Because mixtures are not chemically bonded, **mixtures can be separated by [physical means](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separation_process).**
*   **[Filtration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Filtration) is a separation method used to remove solid particles from a liquid in a heterogeneous mixture** (like using a [paper filter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Filter_paper) for [coffee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coffee)).

![A diagram of simple filtration, a physical separation method where fluid passes through a filter while larger solid particles are blocked.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/_FilterDiagram.svg)

*   **[Distillation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distillation) is a separation method that separates homogeneous mixtures based on differences in [boiling points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boiling_point).**

## Properties of Matter

To categorize the material universe, [scientists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientist) measure specific properties. We divide these into two distinct classes based on how they are observed.

### [Physical Properties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_property)
**A [physical property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_property) is a characteristic of a substance that can be observed without changing the chemical identity of the substance.** Measuring these does not destroy or alter the matter.
*   **Mass is a physical property measuring the amount of matter in an object.**
*   **Volume is a physical property measuring the amount of three-dimensional space an object occupies.**
*   **[Density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Density) is a physical property defined as the mass per unit volume of a substance.**
*   Visual characteristics, such as **[color](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Color) is classified as a physical property.**
*   **[Electrical conductivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrical_resistivity_and_conductivity) is a physical property describing how well a substance allows [electricity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electricity) to flow through the substance.**
*   Thermal benchmarks are also purely physical. **The [melting point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Melting_point) is the specific [temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperature) at which a solid becomes a liquid**, and **the boiling point is the specific temperature at which a liquid becomes a gas.**

### [Chemical Properties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_property)
In contrast, observing a chemical property requires the substance to potentially change its fundamental identity. **A chemical property describes the ability of a substance to undergo a specific [chemical change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_change) to form a new substance.**
*   **[Flammability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flammability) is a chemical property indicating how easily a substance can catch fire.**
*   **[Reactivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reactivity_%28chemistry%29) is a chemical property describing how readily a substance undergoes a chemical reaction with another substance.**
*   **[Toxicity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toxicity) is a chemical property describing the degree to which a substance can harm living [organisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism).**

## [States of Matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_of_matter) and [Thermal Energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermal_energy)

Matter exists in different [phases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase_%28matter%29) depending on the [kinetic energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_energy) of its particles. **The four fundamental states of matter are solid, liquid, gas, and [plasma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasma_%28physics%29).**

![Particle arrangement in the three most common states of matter: tightly packed in a solid, loosely arranged in a liquid, and widely spaced in a gas.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/_States_of_Matter.svg)

| State | Shape & Volume | Particle Behavior |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **[Solid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid)** | **A solid has a definite [shape](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shape)** and **a solid has a definite volume**. | **The particles in a solid are tightly packed together in fixed positions.** They vibrate but do not roam. |
| **[Liquid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liquid)** | **A liquid has a definite volume**, but **a liquid takes the shape of the container holding the liquid.** | **The particles in a liquid are close together**, yet **the particles in a liquid can slide past one another freely.** |
| **[Gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas)** | **A gas does not have a definite shape** and **a gas does not have a definite volume.** | **The particles in a gas are widely spaced** and **the particles in a gas move rapidly in all directions.** |
| **[Plasma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasma_%28physics%29)** | Definite shape/volume absent (like a gas), but electrically conductive. | **A plasma is a highly energized state of matter consisting of a gas of ions and free electrons.** |

While plasmas are rare on [Earth's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth) surface, they dominate the [cosmos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmos). **[Lightning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lightning) consists largely of plasma**, and **[stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star) consist largely of plasma.**

![Lightning is a relatively rare terrestrial example of plasma, a highly energized state of matter consisting of free electrons and ions.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Bliksem_in_Assen.jpg)

[Phase transitions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase_transition) are driven by [heat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat). **Adding thermal energy to a substance increases the kinetic energy of the particles in the substance**, causing them to move faster and spread apart. Conversely, **removing thermal energy from a substance decreases the kinetic energy of the particles in the substance**, causing them to slow down and condense. 

![A diagram illustrating the nomenclature for various phase transitions between solids, liquids, and gases.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Phase_change_-_en.svg)

*   **[Melting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Melting) is the phase transition from a solid to a liquid.**
*   **[Freezing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freezing) is the phase transition from a liquid to a solid.**
*   **[Vaporization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vaporization) is the phase transition from a liquid to a gas.** This occurs in two distinct ways: **[evaporation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evaporation) is a type of vaporization that occurs only on the surface of a liquid**, whereas **[boiling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boiling) is a type of vaporization that occurs throughout the entire volume of a liquid.**
*   **[Condensation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensation) is the phase transition from a gas to a liquid.**

Occasionally, matter skips the liquid phase entirely. **[Sublimation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sublimation_%28phase_transition%29) is the phase transition directly from a solid to a gas without passing through the liquid phase.** For example, solid [carbon dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_dioxide), known as **[dry ice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dry_ice) undergoes sublimation at [room temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Room_temperature).** The reverse process is also possible: **[deposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deposition_%28phase_transition%29) is the phase transition directly from a gas to a solid without passing through the liquid phase.** **The formation of [frost](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frost) on a cold window is an example of deposition.**

![Solid carbon dioxide, commonly known as dry ice, sublimates directly from a solid into a gas at room temperature, skipping the liquid phase entirely.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Dry_Ice_Pellets_Subliming.jpg)

## Transformations: Physical vs. Chemical Changes

The universe is dynamic; matter is constantly changing. We categorize these transformations into two types.

### [Physical Changes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_change)
**A physical change alters the form or appearance of a substance without creating a new substance.** The [molecular structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_structure) remains entirely intact. 
*   **Tearing a piece of [paper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paper) is a physical change.**
*   **Dissolving salt into water is a physical change.** (The salt and water remain salt and water, which is why they can be separated by distillation).
*   **Melting ice into liquid water is a physical change.** By extension, **[changes of state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase_transition) are always classified as physical changes.**

### Chemical Changes
**A chemical change results in the formation of one or more new substances with different properties.** Chemical bonds are broken and new ones are formed.
*   **The [rusting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rust) of iron is a chemical change.** (Iron and oxygen form [iron oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron_oxide)).

![Rusting is a chemical change where iron reacts with oxygen and moisture to form a completely new substance, iron oxide.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/RustyChainEdit1.jpg)

*   **Burning [wood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wood) is a chemical change.**
*   **Baking a [cake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cake) is a chemical change.** 

Because we cannot see atoms rearranging with our naked eyes, we rely on macroscopic evidence. Four primary indicators tell us a chemical reaction is occurring:
1.  **The production of a new gas during a reaction often indicates a chemical change** (e.g., bubbling or fizzing).
2.  **A sudden unexpected change in color during a reaction can indicate a chemical change.**
3.  **The formation of a solid [precipitate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Precipitation_%28chemistry%29) from two liquid solutions indicates a chemical change.**
4.  **The release or absorption of heat without an external source indicates a chemical change.**

### The [Law of Conservation of Mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservation_of_mass)
Regardless of whether a physical or chemical change occurs, the universe keeps a strict ledger. 

> **The Law of Conservation of Mass states that matter is neither created nor destroyed during an ordinary chemical reaction.**

When a log burns, it may seem like matter has vanished into thin air. However, if you were to trap and weigh all the [soot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soot), [ash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ash), and invisible gases released, you would find a perfect equivalence. **The total mass of the [reactants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reagent) in a chemical reaction exactly equals the total mass of the [products](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Product_%28chemistry%29).** Matter simply changes its atomic arrangement, proving that from the invisible electron cloud to a blazing star, the physical universe is an endless, elegant cycle of transformation.

![A balanced chemical equation for the combustion of methane. The total number of each type of atom—and therefore the total mass—remains identical before and after the reaction.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Combustion_reaction_of_methane.jpg)
