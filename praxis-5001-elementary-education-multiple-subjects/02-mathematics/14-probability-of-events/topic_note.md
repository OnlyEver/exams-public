# The [Mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics) of Chance: Interpreting the Probability of Events

Hello, friends! Let’s talk about the [future](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Future).

Can you [predict the future](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prediction)? Of course not! The [universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe) is a beautifully messy, unpredictable place. You don’t know if it’s going to rain next Tuesday. You don’t know who will win the next [World Series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Series). And if I toss a shiny quarter into the air, neither you nor I know precisely how it’s going to land. 

But here is the wonderful, magical secret of mathematics: **even when we cannot predict *what* will happen, we can perfectly measure the *likelihood* of what will happen.** 

At its heart, **probability is a measure of the likelihood that a specific [event](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Event_%28probability_theory%29) will occur.** It is the language we use to tame [uncertainty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uncertainty). It’s how [scientists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientist) predict [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics), how insurance companies stay in business, and how you are going to ace the [Praxis Elementary Education exam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Praxis_test)!

Let’s roll up our sleeves and look at the machinery of chance.

## The Scale of Certainty

How do we talk about this likelihood? We don’t use vague words like "maybe" or "probably." We use [numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number). Nature loves precision! 

**The probability of an event is always a numerical value between [0](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/0) and [1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1) inclusive.** Think of this as a ruler that measures how sure we are about something. 

*   **The Bottom of the Scale (0):** Imagine I ask you the probability of a human jumping to the [moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moon) using only their legs. It can't happen. In mathematics, **a probability of 0 indicates that a specific event is entirely impossible.**
*   **The Top of the Scale (1):** What is the probability that the sun will rise tomorrow? It’s a guarantee. **A probability of 1 indicates that a specific event is absolutely certain to happen.**
*   **The Perfect Balance (0.5):** What if something is a pure [coin toss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coin_flipping)? **A probability of 0.5 indicates that an event is equally likely to occur or not occur.** It's the ultimate fence-sitter.

Once you see this scale, the rule is marvelously intuitive: **an event with a probability closer to 1 is more likely to occur than an event with a probability closer to 0.** 

### Speaking the Language: Fractions, Decimals, and Percents
Now, how do we write these numbers down? We have options, and they all mean the exact same thing. 
1.  **Probabilities can be expressed using mathematical [fractions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fraction).** (Example: $\frac{1}{2}$)
2.  **Probabilities can be expressed using [decimal numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal).** (Example: $0.5$)
3.  **Probabilities can be expressed using [percentages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Percentage).** (Example: $50\%$)

If the [weather forecaster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weather_forecasting) says there is a 75% chance of rain, they could just as easily say there is a $0.75$ probability, or a $\frac{3}{4}$ probability. It’s the same amount of uncertainty, just dressed in different outfits.

## The Vocabulary of the Game

To build our probability machine, we need to define its parts. Whenever we run a probability experiment—whether that's drawing names from a hat or spinning a [roulette wheel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roulette)—we use three vital words: *Outcome*, *Sample Space*, and *[Event](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Event_%28probability_theory%29)*. 

*   **An outcome is a single possible result of a probability experiment.** If you roll a [die](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dice) and get a '4', that '4' is the outcome.
*   **A sample space is the complete set of all possible outcomes for a given probability experiment.** It’s the universe of everything that *could* possibly happen. 
*   **An event is a specific set of outcomes within a larger sample space.** It’s what you are hoping for, or what you are measuring. "Rolling an [even number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parity_%28mathematics%29)" is an event that contains the outcomes 2, 4, and 6.

Here is a beautiful universal truth about the sample space: because *something* in the universe of possibilities absolutely must happen, **the [sum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Summation) of the probabilities of all distinct possible outcomes in a complete sample space is exactly 1.**

## Calculating the Future: Theoretical Probability

Now for the fun part. How do we actually calculate these probabilities? We start by looking at things perfectly, purely, in the mind's eye. 

**Theoretical probability predicts expected outcomes in a probability experiment under ideal conditions.** Notice the word *ideal*. This is how things *should* happen in a perfect world. 

> **The Golden Formula:**
> **Theoretical probability is calculated by [dividing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_%28mathematics%29) the number of favorable outcomes by the total number of possible outcomes.**

Let's test this formula on the classic tools of probability: coins, dice, and cards.

### 1. The Coin Toss
A humble coin is a brilliant teacher. **A standard coin toss has a sample space of exactly two possible outcomes.** What are they? **The two possible outcomes of a standard coin toss are [heads and tails](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coin_flipping).** 

If you want heads, you have 1 favorable outcome. You have 2 total possible outcomes. Therefore, **the theoretical probability of a fair coin landing on heads is exactly one divided by two** ($\frac{1}{2}$). The universe is perfectly [symmetrical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry) here, so **the theoretical probability of a fair coin landing on tails is exactly one divided by two** ($\frac{1}{2}$) as well. 

### 2. The Six-Sided Die
Now, let's step up the [complexity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complexity). **A standard fair [six-sided die](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dice) has a sample space consisting of the numbers 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, and 6.** That's six total possible outcomes. 

If you want to roll a '5', that is just one favorable outcome. Thus, **the theoretical probability of rolling any single specific number on a fair six-sided die is exactly one divided by six** ($\frac{1}{6}$). 

### 3. A Deck of Cards
Finally, let’s look at the granddaddy of all probability generators. **A [standard deck of playing cards](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_52-card_deck) contains exactly 52 cards.** This is our total sample space. 

Within that sample space, **a standard deck of playing cards contains exactly four suits.** What are they? **The four suits in a standard deck of playing cards are hearts, diamonds, [clubs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clubs_%28suit%29), and [spades](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spades_%28suit%29).** 

If I ask you for the theoretical probability of drawing a diamond, you think: "Ah! There are 52 cards total. Since there are four equal suits, there must be 13 diamonds." So, you divide the favorable outcomes (13) by the possible outcomes (52). 
$\frac{13}{52} = \frac{1}{4} = 0.25 = 25\%$

## The Other Side of the Coin: Complements

Sometimes, it is easier to calculate the probability of something *not* happening. Let’s say I ask you for the probability of rolling a die and *not* getting a 6. 

Enter the "complement." **The complement of an event consists of all outcomes in the sample space that are not part of the designated event.** If the event is rolling a 6, the complement is rolling a 1, 2, 3, 4, or 5. 

Because we know that the sum of all probabilities in a sample space equals 1, we can use a clever shortcut: **the probability of the complement of an event is calculated by [subtracting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subtraction) the probability of the event from 1.**

Let's do the math:
1. The probability of rolling a 6 is $\frac{1}{6}$.
2. The complement (NOT rolling a 6) is $1 - \frac{1}{6}$.
3. The probability of the complement is $\frac{5}{6}$. 

Brilliant, right? Whenever finding the direct probability is too complicated, just find the probability of it *not* happening and subtract it from 1!

## Theory vs. Reality: Experimental Probability

So far, we have been floating in the perfect, ideal world of theoretical probability. But what happens when we step into the messy, real world and actually flip a coin or roll a die? We enter the realm of the experiment.

**Experimental probability describes the actual measured results that occurred during a physical probability experiment.** 

Imagine you flip a coin 10 times. Under perfect ideal conditions (theoretical probability), you *should* get 5 heads. But in [reality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reality), you might get 7 heads and 3 tails! Does this mean mathematics is broken? No! It just means you are measuring what *actually* happened.

> **The Real-World Formula:**
> **Experimental probability is calculated by dividing the number of times an event actually occurred by the total number of trials conducted.**

In your coin flipping experiment, your experimental probability of getting heads was 7 (times it occurred) divided by 10 (total trials), or 70%. 

### A Quick Comparison

| Feature | Theoretical Probability | Experimental Probability |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **What it represents** | What *should* happen in a perfect world. | What *actually* happened in reality. |
| **How it's found** | Logical deduction (favorable / possible). | Physical [data](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data) (occurrences / trials). |
| **The Formula** | Favorable Outcomes $\div$ Total Possible Outcomes | Actual Occurrences $\div$ Total Trials |

## Bridging the Gap: The Law of Large Numbers

You might be thinking: *"Professor, if experimental probability can be so different from theoretical probability, why do we bother with the theory at all?"*

Ah! This is where the magic happens. This is the grand finale of our probability lesson. 

If you flip a coin 10 times, you might get 70% heads. It’s completely unpredictable. But what if you flip it 1,000 times? Or 10,000 times? Or a [million](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1%2C000%2C000) times? 

A beautiful mathematical rule known as the Law of Large Numbers takes over. **Increasing the number of trials in a probability experiment causes the experimental probability to move closer to the theoretical probability.** 

If you flip a coin 10 times, 70% heads is entirely possible. But if you flip it a million times, I promise you, with absolute mathematical certainty, that your experimental probability will crawl inexorably closer and closer to 50%. The chaos of the short term smoothes out into the perfect [predictability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Predictability) of the long term.
