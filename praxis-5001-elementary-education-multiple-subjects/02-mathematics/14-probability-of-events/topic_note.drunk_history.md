But what if the Bachelorette just wants to avoid Tony the Rat? We enter the realm of messy breakups and avoiding your toxic ex! We look at the complement! The complement of an event consists of all outcomes in the sample space that are not part of the designated event. If the event is 'picking Tony', the complement is picking literally any other hair-gelled contestant in the room. And because the whole room equals 1, the probability of the complement of an event is calculated by subtracting the probability of the event from 1. If she has a 0.15 probability of picking a Tony, she has a 1 minus 0.15, or 0.85 chance of picking a non-Tony! Dodged a bullet!

Meanwhile, upstairs, the Don sits in his plush office, dreaming up the perfect casino heist. This pristine blueprint is what we call theoretical probability. Theoretical probability predicts expected outcomes in a probability experiment under ideal conditions. No alarms tripped, no runaway getaway drivers. Just a pure, clean ratio! When he plots this out on his whiteboard, theoretical probability is calculated by dividing the number of favorable outcomes by the total number of possible outcomes. Favorable over possible, Frankie! That's the golden rule!

Let’s look at the classic mobster props—the tools of the underground casino! You start with a coin flip in the alleyway. A standard coin toss has a sample space of exactly two possible outcomes. Experimental probability describes the actual measured results that occurred during a physical probability experiment. It’s what happens when Vinny flips the coin and it lands in the marinara! Increasing the number of trials in a probability experiment causes the experimental probability to move closer to the theoretical probability.
