## Part 1: The Blueprint of Space (Zero and One Dimensions)

To build a universe, we have to start with a location. 

### The Anchor of Reality: Points
Imagine the smallest speck of dust you can. Now shrink it until it is completely invisible and has no size at all. What you are left with is a **point**. 
> A point specifies an exact location in geometric space. 

Think of it like a pin dropped on a digital map. It just tells you "here." Because it is only a location, a point has **zero dimensions, meaning zero length, zero width, and zero depth.** 

### Moving in One Dimension: Lines, Rays, and Segments
If you take a point and drag it across your screen, you create a path. This path gives us our very first dimension.

*   **Lines:** A **line** is a straight one-dimensional geometric figure. Imagine a laser beam shooting out endlessly into space. A line **extends infinitely in two opposite directions**. Because it only moves in one dimension (length), a line has **zero thickness**. 
*   **Line Segments:** What if we take out a pair of scissors and snip that endless line? We get a **line segment**, which is a bounded portion of a line. Unlike an endless line, a line segment has **exactly two distinct endpoints**. Just like a bridge, a line segment contains **every point on the line located between its two endpoints**. 
*   **Rays:** What if we only snip the line once? We get a **ray**. A ray is a portion of a line that begins at a single endpoint. However, a ray **extends infinitely in only one direction**. Think of a flashlight: the bulb is the single endpoint, and the beam shoots forever into the dark.

### The Dance of the Lines
If we put multiple lines onto the same flat screen (which we call a plane), they have to interact. How they interact creates the shapes around us.

1.  **Parallel lines** are straight lines located in the **same flat plane**. These lines **never intersect**. Why? Because parallel lines remain a **constant distance apart at all points**. Think of the rails of a train track; if they ever got closer or further apart, the train would crash!
2.  **Intersecting lines** cross each other at **exactly one point**. Think of two roads making an X on a map.
3.  **Perpendicular lines** are a very special type of intersecting lines. They are intersecting lines that meet to form right angles. To be exact, perpendicular lines intersect at an **angle of exactly 90 degrees**. The corners of your TV, your windows, and your math book are all built using perpendicular lines!

## Part 2: The Art of the Turn (Angles)

When two lines or rays crash into each other, they create a corner. In geometry, we don't just look at a corner; we measure how "open" it is. 

> An **angle** is a geometric figure formed by **two rays sharing a common endpoint**. The common endpoint of two rays forming an angle is called the **vertex**.

Think of an angle like a laptop hinge. We classify angles by how far you open your laptop screen. 

### Classifying Angles by Measure

| Classification | The Rule | The Real-World Visual |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Acute angle** | An acute angle is an angle that measures **strictly less than 90 degrees**. | A laptop that is barely open. It looks "sharp," like a slice of pizza. |
| **Right angle** | A right angle measures **exactly 90 degrees**. | The perfect corner of a square room. |
| **Obtuse angle** | An obtuse angle is an angle that measures **strictly greater than 90 degrees and strictly less than 180 degrees**. | A reclining gaming chair leaning back. |
| **Straight angle** | A straight angle measures **exactly 180 degrees**. A straight angle forms a **perfectly straight line**. | Opening your laptop so far back that it lies totally flat on the desk. |
| **Reflex angle** | A reflex angle measures **strictly greater than 180 degrees and strictly less than 360 degrees**. | Pushing a laptop screen so far backwards that you snap the hinges! |
| **Full rotation angle** | A full rotation angle measures **exactly 360 degrees**. | Doing a complete spin on a skateboard. |

### Pairing Angles Up
Sometimes, angles act as partners to build something larger. We can use step-by-step math to figure out a missing partner.

*   **Complementary angles** are two angles whose sum is **exactly 90 degrees**. (They team up to make a perfect corner).
    *   *Step 1:* Imagine you have an angle that is 40 degrees.
    *   *Step 2:* You know complementary angles add up to 90. So, set up your math: 90 - 40.
    *   *Step 3:* Subtract to find the partner: 50 degrees.
*   **Supplementary angles** are two angles whose sum is **exactly 180 degrees**. (They combine to form a perfectly straight line).
    *   *Step 1:* Imagine you have an angle that is 100 degrees.
    *   *Step 2:* You know supplementary angles add up to 180. So, set up your math: 180 - 100.
    *   *Step 3:* Subtract to find the partner: 80 degrees.

## Part 3: Fencing in Space (Two-Dimensional Polygons)

Now we have lines, segments, and angles. Let's take those line segments and build fences to capture some space! 

> A **polygon** is a closed two-dimensional geometric figure. A polygon is formed entirely by **straight line segments**. 

No curves allowed! If it's a closed shape made of straight sticks, it's a polygon. Some polygons are perfectly balanced. A **regular polygon** has **all sides of equal length** AND has **all interior angles of equal measure**. A perfect stop sign is a great example of a regular polygon.

### The Triangle Family
A **triangle** is a polygon with **exactly three sides**. No matter how you stretch or squish a triangle, there is an unbreakable rule: **the sum of the interior angles of a triangle is exactly 180 degrees**. 

We sort triangles in two ways: by looking at their *sides*, or by looking at their *angles*.

**Classifying Triangles by Sides:**
*   **Equilateral triangle:** An equilateral triangle has **three sides of equal length**. Let's use math to find the angles!
    *   *Step 1:* We know the total of the angles is 180 degrees.
    *   *Step 2:* Because all 3 sides are equal, all 3 angles must be equal. Divide the total by 3: 180 / 3.
    *   *Step 3:* The answer is 60. Each interior angle of an equilateral triangle measures **exactly 60 degrees**.
*   **Isosceles triangle:** An isosceles triangle has **at least two sides of equal length**. 
*   **Scalene triangle:** A completely lopsided shape. A scalene triangle has **zero sides of equal length**.

**Classifying Triangles by Angles:**
*   **Acute triangle:** An acute triangle contains **exactly three acute interior angles**. 
*   **Right triangle:** A right triangle contains **exactly one right interior angle**. 
*   **Obtuse triangle:** An obtuse triangle contains **exactly one obtuse interior angle**.

### The Quadrilateral Family Tree
A **quadrilateral** is a polygon with **exactly four sides**. While a triangle's internal angles add up to 180, **the sum of the interior angles of a quadrilateral is exactly 360 degrees** (think of it as two triangles glued together!).

Quadrilaterals act like a family tree, where shapes get stricter and stricter rules.

1.  **Trapezoid:** A trapezoid is a quadrilateral with **at least one pair of parallel opposite sides**. 
2.  **Parallelogram:** A step up! A parallelogram is a quadrilateral with **two pairs of parallel opposite sides**. Because the lines run perfectly alongside each other, math magic happens: the **opposite sides of a parallelogram are equal in length**, and the **opposite interior angles of a parallelogram are equal in measure**.
3.  **Rectangle:** A very strict parallelogram. A rectangle is a parallelogram containing **exactly four right angles**.
4.  **Rhombus:** Another strict parallelogram. A rhombus is a parallelogram containing **exactly four sides of equal length**. (Think of a diamond shape).
5.  **Square:** The ultimate, perfect shape in the family. A square is a rectangle containing **exactly four sides of equal length**. Because it meets all the strict rules—equal sides and equal angles—a square is considered a **regular quadrilateral**.

### Other Notable Polygons
As we add more straight line segments, we get distinct shapes:
*   **Pentagon:** A polygon with **exactly five sides**.
*   **Hexagon:** A polygon with **exactly six sides**.
*   **Octagon:** A polygon with **exactly eight sides**. (The classic stop sign!)
*   **Decagon:** A polygon with **exactly ten sides**.

## Part 4: Stepping into Reality (Three-Dimensional Solid Figures)

Everything we’ve done so far has been flat. Let's pull these shapes out of the screen and into the real world. 

> A **three-dimensional figure** is a solid geometric object that has **length, width, and height**.

To talk about a 3D figure like a Minecraft block, we need three new words:
*   **Face:** A **face** is a flat distinct surface of a solid three-dimensional figure.
*   **Edge:** An **edge** is a line segment where two faces of a solid figure intersect.
*   **Vertex:** A **vertex** of a solid figure is a corner point where three or more edges intersect.

### The Polyhedrons (Straight-Edged Solids)
A **polyhedron** is a three-dimensional solid figure. The rule here is that **every face of a polyhedron is a flat polygon**. No rounded edges, no curves. If you roll a polyhedron across the floor, it will clatter and bounce.

#### 1. Prisms
A **prism** is a polyhedron. A prism has **exactly two polygonal bases**. These two bases act like the floor and the ceiling of a room. The two polygonal bases of a prism are **strictly parallel to each other** and **completely congruent to each other** (meaning they are the exact same shape and size). 

The walls connecting the floor and the ceiling are called lateral faces. The **lateral faces of a prism are all parallelograms or rectangles**. 

*   **Rectangular prism:** Think of a cereal box. A **rectangular prism is a prism**. A rectangular prism has **exactly six rectangular faces**, **exactly 12 edges**, and **exactly 8 vertices**. 
*   **Cube:** A **cube is a specific type of rectangular prism**. It is perfectly balanced. A cube has **exactly six identical square faces**.

#### 2. Pyramids
A **pyramid** is a polyhedron, too. But instead of having a ceiling and a floor, it only has a floor. A pyramid has **exactly one polygonal base**. The **lateral faces of a pyramid are all triangles**. These triangular walls reach upward, and **all lateral faces of a pyramid meet at a single common point called the apex**.

### Curved Solids (Non-Polyhedrons)
The real world isn't just made of sharp edges and flat walls. What happens when we add curves? Because they have curved surfaces, these next shapes are *not* polyhedrons, but they are incredibly important.

#### 1. Cylinders
Think of a can of soda. A **cylinder** is a three-dimensional geometric figure. A cylinder has **exactly two circular bases**. Just like a prism, the two circular bases of a cylinder are **strictly parallel to each other** and **completely congruent to each other**. 

However, instead of flat walls, the two circular bases of a cylinder are **connected by a single curved surface**. Because there are no flat faces crashing together to make sharp corners, a cylinder has **zero vertices**.

#### 2. Cones
Think of a traffic cone or an ice cream cone. A **cone** is a three-dimensional geometric figure. A cone has **exactly one circular base**. From that base, the **curved surface of a cone tapers continuously from the base to a single point**. Just like a pyramid, the single point at the top of a cone is called the **apex**.

#### 3. Spheres
Finally, think of a basketball. A **sphere** is a perfectly round three-dimensional shape. 

How do we know it's perfectly round? **Every point on the surface of a sphere is equidistant from its center point**. This means if you measure from the dead center to the outside, it is exactly the same distance no matter which way you measure!

Because it is perfectly smooth, a sphere lacks the sharp parts of other solids. A sphere has **exactly zero flat faces**, **exactly zero edges**, and **exactly zero vertices**.

### Summary for the Praxis Exam
Look back at what we've just built. We started with a **point**—a location with zero dimensions. We dragged it to make infinite **lines**, snipped them into **segments** and **rays**, and crossed them to make **angles**. We trapped space inside **polygons** like the 180-degree **triangle** and the regular **square**. Finally, we pulled those polygons into 3D space to construct **polyhedrons** like prisms and pyramids, and spun circles into **cylinders**, **cones**, and perfectly smooth **spheres**.
