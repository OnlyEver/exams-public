## Part 1: The Blueprint of Space (Zero and One Dimensions)

To build a universe, we have to start with a location. 

### The Anchor of Reality: Points
Imagine the smallest speck of dust you can. Now shrink it until it has no size at all. What you are left with is a **point**. 
> A point specifies an exact location in geometric space. 

Because it is purely an address—a "here"—a point has **zero dimensions, meaning zero length, zero width, and zero depth.** 

### Moving in One Dimension: Lines, Rays, and Segments
If you take a point and drag it through space, you create a path. This path gives us our first dimension.

*   **Lines:** A **line** is a straight one-dimensional geometric figure. Imagine a perfectly straight laser beam shining endlessly into the cosmos. A line **extends infinitely in two opposite directions** and, because it is only one-dimensional, it has **zero thickness**. 
*   **Line Segments:** What if we take out a pair of geometric scissors and snip that infinite line? We get a **line segment**, which is a bounded portion of a line. Unlike a line, a line segment has **exactly two distinct endpoints**. Furthermore, a line segment contains **every point on the line located between its two endpoints**. It is a complete, solid bridge between "Point A" and "Point B."
*   **Rays:** What if we only snip the line once? We get a **ray**. A ray is a portion of a line that begins at a single endpoint, but unlike a segment, a ray **extends infinitely in only one direction**. Think of a flashlight: the bulb is the endpoint, and the beam shoots infinitely into the dark.

### The Dance of the Lines
If we put multiple lines into the same flat universe (a plane), they have to interact. How they interact defines the structural integrity of the world around us.

1.  **Parallel lines** are straight lines located in the **same flat plane** that **never intersect**. Why? Because parallel lines remain a **constant distance apart at all points**. Think of the rails of a train track; if they ever changed their distance from one another, the train would derail!
2.  **Intersecting lines** cross each other at **exactly one point**.
3.  **Perpendicular lines** are a very special type of intersecting lines. They are intersecting lines that meet to form right angles. To be exact, perpendicular lines intersect at an **angle of exactly 90 degrees**. The corners of your walls, your windows, your books—civilization is built on perpendicular lines!

***

## Part 2: The Art of the Turn (Angles)

When two lines or rays collide, they create a corner. In geometry, we don't just point at a corner; we measure how "open" it is. 

> An **angle** is a geometric figure formed by **two rays sharing a common endpoint**. The common endpoint of two rays forming an angle is called the **vertex**.

Think of an angle as a hinge on a door. We classify angles by how far you push the door open. 

### Classifying Angles by Measure

| Classification | The Rule | The Real-World Visual |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Acute angle** | Measures **strictly less than 90 degrees**. | A pair of tweezers or a slightly open door. It's "sharp." |
| **Right angle** | Measures **exactly 90 degrees**. | The perfect corner of a square room. |
| **Obtuse angle** | Measures **strictly greater than 90 degrees and strictly less than 180 degrees**. | A recliner chair leaning back. |
| **Straight angle** | Measures **exactly 180 degrees**. | The door is pushed flat against the wall. A straight angle forms a **perfectly straight line**. |
| **Reflex angle** | Measures **strictly greater than 180 degrees and strictly less than 360 degrees**. | Pushing a door so far backwards that you break the hinges! |
| **Full rotation angle** | Measures **exactly 360 degrees**. | Doing a complete spin on a skateboard. |

### Pairing Angles Up
Sometimes, angles act as partners to build something larger:
*   **Complementary angles** are two angles whose sum is **exactly 90 degrees**. (They "complement" each other to make a perfect corner).
*   **Supplementary angles** are two angles whose sum is **exactly 180 degrees**. (They combine to form a perfectly straight line).

## Part 3: Fencing in Space (Two-Dimensional Polygons)

Now we have lines, segments, and angles. Let's take those line segments and build fences to capture some two-dimensional space! 

> A **polygon** is a closed two-dimensional geometric figure. It is formed entirely by **straight line segments**. 

No curves allowed! If it's a closed shape made of straight, connected sticks, it's a polygon. Some polygons, however, achieve absolute perfection. A **regular polygon** has **all sides of equal length** AND has **all interior angles of equal measure**. Nature loves regular polygons—think of the cells of a honeycomb!

### The Triangle Family
A **triangle** is a polygon with **exactly three sides**. No matter how you stretch, squish, or distort a triangle, there is an unbreakable law of the universe governing it: **the sum of the interior angles of a triangle is exactly 180 degrees**. 

We classify triangles in two ways: by looking at their *sides*, or by looking at their *angles*.

**Classifying Triangles by Sides:**
*   **Equilateral triangle:** Has **three sides of equal length**. Because the sides are equal, the angles must be equally divided too ($180 \div 3$). Therefore, each interior angle of an equilateral triangle measures **exactly 60 degrees**.
*   **Isosceles triangle:** Has **at least two sides of equal length**. 
*   **Scalene triangle:** A completely asymmetrical shape. It has **zero sides of equal length**.

**Classifying Triangles by Angles:**
*   **Acute triangle:** Contains **exactly three acute interior angles**. 
*   **Right triangle:** Contains **exactly one right interior angle**. (You can't have two 90-degree angles in a triangle, or you'd run out of degrees!)
*   **Obtuse triangle:** Contains **exactly one obtuse interior angle**.

### The Quadrilateral Family Tree
A **quadrilateral** is a polygon with **exactly four sides**. While a triangle's internal angles sum to 180 degrees, the **sum of the interior angles of a quadrilateral is exactly 360 degrees** (think of it as two triangles glued together!).

Quadrilaterals act like a family tree, where shapes inherit traits from their "parents."

1.  **Trapezoid:** A quadrilateral with **at least one pair of parallel opposite sides**. 
2.  **Parallelogram:** A step up in order! A quadrilateral with **two pairs of parallel opposite sides**. Because of this perfect parallelism, mathematical magic happens: the **opposite sides of a parallelogram are equal in length**, and the **opposite interior angles of a parallelogram are equal in measure**.
3.  **Rectangle:** A very disciplined parallelogram. It is a parallelogram containing **exactly four right angles**.
4.  **Rhombus:** A different type of disciplined parallelogram. It is a parallelogram containing **exactly four sides of equal length**. (Think of a diamond shape).
5.  **Square:** The ultimate, perfect child of the rectangle and the rhombus. A square is a rectangle containing **exactly four sides of equal length**. Because it meets all the strict requirements—equal sides and equal angles—a square is considered a **regular quadrilateral**.

### Other Notable Polygons
As we add more straight line segments, we get distinct, familiar shapes:
*   **Pentagon:** A polygon with **exactly five sides**.
*   **Hexagon:** A polygon with **exactly six sides**.
*   **Octagon:** A polygon with **exactly eight sides**. (The classic stop sign!)
*   **Decagon:** A polygon with **exactly ten sides**.

## Part 4: Stepping into Reality (Three-Dimensional Solid Figures)

Everything we’ve done so far has been flat. Let's pull these shapes out of the page and into the real world. 

> A **three-dimensional figure** is a solid geometric object that has **length, width, and height**.

To describe the anatomy of a solid figure, we need three new terms:
*   **Face:** A flat distinct surface of a solid three-dimensional figure.
*   **Edge:** A line segment where two faces of a solid figure intersect.
*   **Vertex:** A corner point where three or more edges intersect.

### The Polyhedrons (Straight-Edged Solids)
A **polyhedron** is a three-dimensional solid figure where **every face is a flat polygon**. No rounded edges, no curved surfaces. If you roll a polyhedron down a hill, it will clatter and bounce on its flat faces.

#### 1. Prisms
A **prism** is a specific type of polyhedron. It is defined by having **exactly two polygonal bases**. These two bases act like the floor and the ceiling of a building. They are **strictly parallel to each other** and **completely congruent to each other** (meaning they are the exact same shape and size). 

The walls connecting the floor and the ceiling are called lateral faces. The **lateral faces of a prism are all parallelograms or rectangles**. 

*   **Rectangular prism:** A familiar prism (think of a cereal box or a brick). A rectangular prism has exactly **six rectangular faces**, exactly **12 edges**, and exactly **8 vertices**. 
*   **Cube:** A **cube is a specific type of rectangular prism**. It represents ultimate symmetry. A cube has exactly **six identical square faces**.

#### 2. Pyramids
A **pyramid** is also a polyhedron, but instead of having a ceiling and a floor, it only has a floor. A pyramid has **exactly one polygonal base**. The **lateral faces of a pyramid are all triangles**. These triangular walls reach upward and **all lateral faces of a pyramid meet at a single common point called the apex**.

### Curved Solids (Non-Polyhedrons)
Nature is not just made of jagged edges and flat planes. What happens when we introduce curves into 3D space? Because they have curved surfaces, these are *not* polyhedrons, but they are incredibly important three-dimensional geometric figures.

#### 1. Cylinders
Think of a soup can. A **cylinder** is a three-dimensional geometric figure that has **exactly two circular bases**. Just like a prism, these two circular bases are **strictly parallel to each other** and **completely congruent to each other**. 

However, instead of flat rectangular walls, the two circular bases of a cylinder are **connected by a single curved surface**. Because there are no flat polygonal faces intersecting to create sharp corners, a cylinder has **zero vertices**.

#### 2. Cones
Think of a traffic cone or an ice cream cone. A **cone** is a three-dimensional geometric figure that has **exactly one circular base**. From that base, the **curved surface of a cone tapers continuously from the base to a single point**. Just like in a pyramid, the single point at the top of a cone is called the **apex**.

#### 3. Spheres
Finally, the most mathematically perfect shape in the cosmos. A **sphere** is a perfectly round three-dimensional shape. 

How do we define perfect roundness? **Every point on the surface of a sphere is equidistant from its center point**. 

Because of this supreme, infinite symmetry, it lacks the jagged anatomy of other solids. A sphere has **exactly zero flat faces**, **exactly zero edges**, and **exactly zero vertices**.

### Summary for the Praxis Exam
Look back at what we've just built. We started with a **point**—a location with zero dimensions. We dragged it to make infinite **lines**, snipped them into **segments** and **rays**, and crossed them to make **angles**. We trapped space inside **polygons** like the 180-degree **triangle** and the perfectly regular **square**. Finally, we pulled those polygons out of the plane to construct **polyhedrons** like prisms and pyramids, and spun circles into **cylinders**, **cones**, and perfectly smooth **spheres**.

Geometry is not a random list of facts to memorize. It is a logical, interconnected architecture. Keep this blueprint in your mind on test day, trust the definitions, and you will see right through the exam questions just as clearly as a physicist sees the gears of the universe. Good luck!
