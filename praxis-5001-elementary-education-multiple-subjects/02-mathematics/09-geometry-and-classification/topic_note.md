# [Geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry) and Classification: Building the [Universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe) from Scratch

Welcome! If you're preparing for the [Praxis Elementary Education exam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Praxis_test), you might be staring at a mountain of [vocabulary words](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vocabulary)—[prisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prism_%28geometry%29), [scalene triangles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalene_triangle), [reflex angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle)—and wondering how you're supposed to [memorize](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Memorization) it all. 

I want you to stop right now and throw away the [flashcards](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flashcard). 

We are not going to [memorize](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Memorization) a list of disconnected words. The beauty of [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics) isn't in [rote memorization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rote_learning); it's in seeing the magnificent, logical structure of reality. [Geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry) is the language of [space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space). It is how we describe the [shape](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shape) of everything in the [universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe), from the screen you are reading this on to the [Great Pyramids of Giza](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Pyramid_of_Giza). 

We are going to build [geometric space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_%28mathematics%29) from [absolute nothingness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nothing), step by step, [dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension) by dimension. Once you understand the rules of the game, the [vocabulary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vocabulary) becomes completely intuitive. Let's begin.

## Part 1: The Blueprint of Space ([Zero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero-dimensional_space) and [One Dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One-dimensional_space))

To build a [universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe), we have to start with a location. 

### The Anchor of Reality: [Points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_%28geometry%29)
Imagine the smallest speck of dust you can. Now shrink it until it has no size at all. What you are left with is a **[point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_%28geometry%29)**. 
> A [point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_%28geometry%29) specifies an exact location in [geometric space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_%28mathematics%29). 

Because it is purely an address—a "here"—a [point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_%28geometry%29) has **[zero dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero-dimensional_space), meaning zero [length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Length), zero [width](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Width), and zero [depth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Depth).** 

### Moving in [One Dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One-dimensional_space): [Lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_%28geometry%29), [Rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ray_%28geometry%29), and [Segments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_segment)
If you take a [point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_%28geometry%29) and drag it through [space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_%28mathematics%29), you create a path. This path gives us our first [dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension).

*   **Lines:** A **[line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_%28geometry%29)** is a straight [one-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One-dimensional_space) [geometric figure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shape). Imagine a perfectly straight laser beam shining endlessly into the cosmos. A [line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_%28geometry%29) **extends infinitely in two opposite directions** and, because it is only [one-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One-dimensional_space), it has **zero thickness**. 
*   **Line Segments:** What if we take out a pair of geometric scissors and snip that infinite [line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_%28geometry%29)? We get a **[line segment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_segment)**, which is a bounded portion of a [line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_%28geometry%29). Unlike a [line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_%28geometry%29), a [line segment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_segment) has **exactly two distinct endpoints**. Furthermore, a [line segment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_segment) contains **every [point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_%28geometry%29) on the [line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_%28geometry%29) located between its two endpoints**. It is a complete, solid bridge between "Point A" and "Point B."
*   **Rays:** What if we only snip the [line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_%28geometry%29) once? We get a **[ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ray_%28geometry%29)**. A [ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ray_%28geometry%29) is a portion of a [line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_%28geometry%29) that begins at a single endpoint, but unlike a [segment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_segment), a [ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ray_%28geometry%29) **extends infinitely in only one direction**. Think of a flashlight: the bulb is the endpoint, and the beam shoots infinitely into the dark.

![A geometric ray begins at a single defining endpoint (A) and extends infinitely in one direction through other points.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Ray_(A%2C_B%2C_C).svg)

### The Dance of the Lines
If we put multiple [lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_%28geometry%29) into the same flat [universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe) (a [plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_%28geometry%29)), they have to interact. How they interact defines the structural integrity of the world around us.

1.  **[Parallel lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_%28geometry%29)** are straight [lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_%28geometry%29) located in the **same flat [plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_%28geometry%29)** that **never intersect**. Why? Because [parallel lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_%28geometry%29) remain a **constant distance apart at all [points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_%28geometry%29)**. Think of the rails of a train track; if they ever changed their distance from one another, the train would derail!
2.  **[Intersecting lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line%E2%80%93line_intersection)** cross each other at **exactly one [point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_%28geometry%29)**.
3.  **[Perpendicular lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular)** are a very special type of [intersecting lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line%E2%80%93line_intersection). They are intersecting lines that meet to form [right angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_angle). To be exact, [perpendicular lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular) intersect at an **[angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle) of exactly 90 [degrees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_%28angle%29)**. The corners of your walls, your windows, your books—civilization is built on [perpendicular lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular)!

![Perpendicular lines are a specific type of intersecting lines that meet to form exactly 90-degree right angles, often denoted by a small square at the intersection.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Perpendicular-coloured.svg)

## Part 2: The Art of the Turn ([Angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle))

When two [lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_%28geometry%29) or [rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ray_%28geometry%29) collide, they create a corner. In [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry), we don't just point at a corner; we measure how "open" it is. 

> An **[angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle)** is a [geometric figure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shape) formed by **two [rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ray_%28geometry%29) sharing a common endpoint**. The common endpoint of two [rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ray_%28geometry%29) forming an [angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle) is called the **[vertex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_%28geometry%29)**.

Think of an [angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle) as a hinge on a door. We classify [angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle) by how far you push the door open. 

### Classifying Angles by Measure

| Classification | The Rule | The Real-World Visual |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **[Acute angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle)** | Measures **strictly less than 90 [degrees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_%28angle%29)**. | A pair of tweezers or a slightly open door. It's "sharp." |
| **[Right angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_angle)** | Measures **exactly 90 [degrees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_%28angle%29)**. | The perfect corner of a square room. |
| **[Obtuse angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle)** | Measures **strictly greater than 90 [degrees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_%28angle%29) and strictly less than 180 [degrees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_%28angle%29)**. | A recliner chair leaning back. |
| **[Straight angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle)** | Measures **exactly 180 [degrees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_%28angle%29)**. | The door is pushed flat against the wall. A [straight angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle) forms a **perfectly straight [line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_%28geometry%29)**. |
| **[Reflex angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle)** | Measures **strictly greater than 180 [degrees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_%28angle%29) and strictly less than 360 [degrees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_%28angle%29)**. | Pushing a door so far backwards that you break the hinges! |
| **[Full rotation angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turn_%28angle%29)** | Measures **exactly 360 [degrees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_%28angle%29)**. | Doing a complete spin on a skateboard. |

![A visual guide illustrating the varying openness of different angle classifications, from acute all the way to a full rotation.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Angle_types.svg)

### Pairing Angles Up
Sometimes, [angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle) act as partners to build something larger:
*   **[Complementary angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complementary_angles)** are two [angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle) whose sum is **exactly 90 [degrees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_%28angle%29)**. (They "complement" each other to make a perfect corner).
*   **[Supplementary angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supplementary_angles)** are two [angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle) whose sum is **exactly 180 [degrees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_%28angle%29)**. (They combine to form a perfectly straight [line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_%28geometry%29)).

![Complementary angles are two distinct angles (a and b) that perfectly combine to form a 90-degree right angle.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Complement_angle.svg)

## Part 3: Fencing in Space ([Two-Dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two-dimensional_space) [Polygons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon))

Now we have [lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_%28geometry%29), [segments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_segment), and [angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle). Let's take those [line segments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_segment) and build fences to capture some [two-dimensional space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two-dimensional_space)! 

> A **[polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon)** is a closed [two-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two-dimensional_space) [geometric figure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shape). It is formed entirely by **straight [line segments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_segment)**. 

No curves allowed! If it's a closed [shape](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shape) made of straight, connected sticks, it's a [polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon). Some [polygons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon), however, achieve absolute perfection. A **[regular polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polygon)** has **all sides of equal [length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Length)** AND has **all [interior angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internal_angle) of equal measure**. Nature loves [regular polygons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polygon)—think of the cells of a [honeycomb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Honeycomb)!

![Polygons are closed flat shapes composed entirely of straight segments. They can range from familiar convex shapes to complex self-intersecting figures.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Polygon_types.svg)

### The Triangle Family
A **[triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle)** is a [polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon) with **exactly three sides**. No matter how you stretch, squish, or distort a [triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle), there is an unbreakable law of the [universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe) governing it: **the [sum of the interior angles of a triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sum_of_angles_of_a_triangle) is exactly 180 [degrees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_%28angle%29)**. 

![An illustration demonstrating that the three interior angles of any triangle can be rearranged to form a perfect straight line, proving their sum is always 180 degrees.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Triangle_sommeangles.svg)

We classify [triangles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle) in two ways: by looking at their *sides*, or by looking at their *[angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle)*.

**Classifying [Triangles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle) by Sides:**
*   **[Equilateral triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equilateral_triangle):** Has **three sides of equal [length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Length)**. Because the sides are equal, the [angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle) must be equally divided too ($180 \div 3$). Therefore, each [interior angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internal_angle) of an [equilateral triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equilateral_triangle) measures **exactly 60 [degrees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_%28angle%29)**.
*   **[Isosceles triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isosceles_triangle):** Has **at least two sides of equal [length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Length)**. 
*   **[Scalene triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalene_triangle):** A completely asymmetrical [shape](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shape). It has **zero sides of equal [length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Length)**.

**Classifying [Triangles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle) by Angles:**
*   **[Acute triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acute_and_obtuse_triangles):** Contains **exactly three [acute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle) [interior angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internal_angle)**. 
*   **[Right triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_triangle):** Contains **exactly one [right](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_angle) [interior angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internal_angle)**. (You can't have two 90-[degree](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_%28angle%29) [angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle) in a [triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle), or you'd run out of degrees!)
*   **[Obtuse triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acute_and_obtuse_triangles):** Contains **exactly one [obtuse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle) [interior angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internal_angle)**.

### The Quadrilateral Family Tree
A **[quadrilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral)** is a [polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon) with **exactly four sides**. While a [triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle)'s [internal angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internal_angle) sum to 180 [degrees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_%28angle%29), the **sum of the [interior angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internal_angle) of a [quadrilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral) is exactly 360 [degrees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_%28angle%29)** (think of it as two [triangles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle) glued together!).

[Quadrilaterals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral) act like a family tree, where [shapes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shape) inherit traits from their "parents."

1.  **[Trapezoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid):** A [quadrilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral) with **at least one pair of [parallel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_%28geometry%29) opposite sides**. 
2.  **[Parallelogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram):** A step up in order! A [quadrilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral) with **two pairs of [parallel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_%28geometry%29) opposite sides**. Because of this perfect parallelism, mathematical magic happens: the **opposite sides of a [parallelogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram) are equal in [length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Length)**, and the **opposite [interior angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internal_angle) of a [parallelogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram) are equal in measure**.
3.  **[Rectangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle):** A very disciplined [parallelogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram). It is a [parallelogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram) containing **exactly four [right angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_angle)**.
4.  **[Rhombus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhombus):** A different type of disciplined [parallelogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram). It is a [parallelogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram) containing **exactly four sides of equal [length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Length)**. (Think of a diamond [shape](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shape)).
5.  **[Square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square):** The ultimate, perfect child of the [rectangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle) and the [rhombus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhombus). A [square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square) is a [rectangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle) containing **exactly four sides of equal [length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Length)**. Because it meets all the strict requirements—equal sides and equal [angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle)—a [square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square) is considered a **[regular quadrilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square)**.

![An Euler diagram mapping the taxonomy of quadrilaterals. Notice how the square resides in the very center, inheriting all the properties of parallelograms, rectangles, and rhombuses.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Euler_diagram_of_quadrilateral_types.svg)

### Other Notable [Polygons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon)
As we add more straight [line segments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_segment), we get distinct, familiar [shapes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shape):
*   **[Pentagon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentagon):** A [polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon) with **exactly five sides**.
*   **[Hexagon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hexagon):** A [polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon) with **exactly six sides**.
*   **[Octagon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octagon):** A [polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon) with **exactly eight sides**. (The classic [stop sign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stop_sign)!)
*   **[Decagon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decagon):** A [polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon) with **exactly ten sides**.

## Part 4: Stepping into Reality ([Three-Dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_space) [Solid Figures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_geometry))

Everything we’ve done so far has been flat. Let's pull these [shapes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shape) out of the page and into the real world. 

> A **[three-dimensional figure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_space)** is a solid geometric object that has **[length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Length), [width](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Width), and [height](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Height)**.

To describe the anatomy of a [solid figure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_geometry), we need three new terms:
*   **[Face](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Face_%28geometry%29):** A flat distinct surface of a solid [three-dimensional figure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_space).
*   **[Edge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edge_%28geometry%29):** A [line segment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_segment) where two [faces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Face_%28geometry%29) of a [solid figure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_geometry) intersect.
*   **[Vertex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_%28geometry%29):** A corner [point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_%28geometry%29) where three or more [edges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edge_%28geometry%29) intersect.

### The [Polyhedrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyhedron) (Straight-Edged Solids)
A **[polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyhedron)** is a [three-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_space) [solid figure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_geometry) where **every [face](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Face_%28geometry%29) is a flat [polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon)**. No rounded [edges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edge_%28geometry%29), no curved surfaces. If you roll a [polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyhedron) down a hill, it will clatter and bounce on its flat [faces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Face_%28geometry%29).

#### 1. [Prisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prism_%28geometry%29)
A **[prism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prism_%28geometry%29)** is a specific type of [polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyhedron). It is defined by having **exactly two polygonal [bases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Base_%28geometry%29)**. These two [bases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Base_%28geometry%29) act like the floor and the ceiling of a building. They are **strictly [parallel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_%28geometry%29) to each other** and **completely [congruent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congruence_%28geometry%29) to each other** (meaning they are the exact same [shape](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shape) and size). 

The walls connecting the floor and the ceiling are called lateral [faces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Face_%28geometry%29). The **lateral [faces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Face_%28geometry%29) of a [prism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prism_%28geometry%29) are all [parallelograms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram) or [rectangles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle)**. 

*   **[Rectangular prism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cuboid):** A familiar [prism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prism_%28geometry%29) (think of a cereal box or a brick). A [rectangular prism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cuboid) has exactly **six rectangular [faces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Face_%28geometry%29)**, exactly **12 [edges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edge_%28geometry%29)**, and exactly **8 [vertices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_%28geometry%29)**. 
*   **[Cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube):** A **[cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube) is a specific type of [rectangular prism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cuboid)**. It represents ultimate [symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry_%28geometry%29). A [cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube) has exactly **six identical [square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square) [faces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Face_%28geometry%29)**.

![A cube is a highly regular polyhedron consisting of 6 square faces, 12 straight edges, and 8 vertices.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Hexahedron.svg)

#### 2. [Pyramids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyramid_%28geometry%29)
A **[pyramid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyramid_%28geometry%29)** is also a [polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyhedron), but instead of having a ceiling and a floor, it only has a floor. A [pyramid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyramid_%28geometry%29) has **exactly one polygonal [base](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Base_%28geometry%29)**. The **lateral [faces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Face_%28geometry%29) of a [pyramid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyramid_%28geometry%29) are all [triangles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle)**. These triangular walls reach upward and **all lateral [faces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Face_%28geometry%29) of a [pyramid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyramid_%28geometry%29) meet at a single common [point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_%28geometry%29) called the [apex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apex_%28geometry%29)**.

![The structural anatomy of a square pyramid, highlighting its single square base and the top vertex point known as the apex.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Pyramid.svg)

### Curved Solids (Non-[Polyhedrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyhedron))
Nature is not just made of jagged [edges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edge_%28geometry%29) and flat [planes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_%28geometry%29). What happens when we introduce curves into 3D [space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_%28mathematics%29)? Because they have curved surfaces, these are *not* [polyhedrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyhedron), but they are incredibly important [three-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_space) [geometric figures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shape).

#### 1. [Cylinders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder)
Think of a soup can. A **[cylinder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder)** is a [three-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_space) [geometric figure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shape) that has **exactly two [circular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle) [bases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Base_%28geometry%29)**. Just like a [prism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prism_%28geometry%29), these two [circular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle) [bases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Base_%28geometry%29) are **strictly [parallel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_%28geometry%29) to each other** and **completely [congruent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congruence_%28geometry%29) to each other**. 

However, instead of flat rectangular walls, the two [circular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle) [bases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Base_%28geometry%29) of a [cylinder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder) are **connected by a single curved surface**. Because there are no flat polygonal [faces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Face_%28geometry%29) intersecting to create sharp corners, a [cylinder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder) has **zero [vertices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_%28geometry%29)**.

![A visual comparison of a right cylinder and an oblique cylinder, both featuring two parallel, congruent circular bases.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Cylinders.svg)

#### 2. [Cones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cone)
Think of a [traffic cone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Traffic_cone) or an [ice cream cone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ice_cream_cone). A **[cone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cone)** is a [three-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_space) [geometric figure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shape) that has **exactly one [circular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle) [base](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Base_%28geometry%29)**. From that [base](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Base_%28geometry%29), the **curved surface of a [cone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cone) tapers continuously from the [base](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Base_%28geometry%29) to a single [point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_%28geometry%29)**. Just like in a [pyramid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyramid_%28geometry%29), the single [point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_%28geometry%29) at the top of a [cone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cone) is called the **[apex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apex_%28geometry%29)**.

![A right circular cone and an oblique circular cone. In both cases, the curved surface extends from a single circular base up to an apex.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Cone_3d.png)

#### 3. [Spheres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere)
Finally, the most mathematically perfect [shape](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shape) in the cosmos. A **[sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere)** is a perfectly round [three-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_space) [shape](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shape). 

How do we define perfect roundness? **Every [point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_%28geometry%29) on the surface of a [sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere) is [equidistant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equidistant) from its [center point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centre_%28geometry%29)**. 

Because of this supreme, infinite [symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry_%28geometry%29), it lacks the jagged anatomy of other solids. A [sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere) has **exactly zero flat [faces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Face_%28geometry%29)**, **exactly zero [edges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edge_%28geometry%29)**, and **exactly zero [vertices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_%28geometry%29)**. 

![A sphere represents perfect three-dimensional symmetry, with every point on its curved surface sitting at an equal distance from the central point.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Sphere_wireframe.svg)

### Summary for the [Praxis Exam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Praxis_test)
Look back at what we've just built. We started with a **[point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_%28geometry%29)**—a location with [zero dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero-dimensional_space). We dragged it to make infinite **[lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_%28geometry%29)**, snipped them into **[segments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_segment)** and **[rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ray_%28geometry%29)**, and crossed them to make **[angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle)**. We trapped [space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_%28mathematics%29) inside **[polygons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon)** like the 180-[degree](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_%28angle%29) **[triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle)** and the perfectly regular **[square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square)**. Finally, we pulled those [polygons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon) out of the [plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_%28geometry%29) to construct **[polyhedrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyhedron)** like [prisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prism_%28geometry%29) and [pyramids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyramid_%28geometry%29), and spun circles into **[cylinders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder)**, **[cones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cone)**, and perfectly smooth **[spheres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere)**.

[Geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry) is not a random list of facts to [memorize](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Memorization). It is a logical, interconnected architecture. Keep this blueprint in your mind on test day, trust the definitions, and you will see right through the exam questions just as clearly as a [physicist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physicist) sees the gears of the [universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe). Good luck!
