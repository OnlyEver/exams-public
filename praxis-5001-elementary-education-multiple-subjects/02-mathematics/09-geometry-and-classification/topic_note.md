Imagine stripping physical reality down to its absolute bare minimum. Before we can measure the [area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area) of a farmland or engineer the architecture of a skyscraper, we must first establish the fabric of [space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space) itself. [Geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry) is precisely this: a rigorous, step-by-step construction of the [universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe) starting from absolute zero. By defining simple rules for how purely theoretical elements interact, we eventually build the complex, [three-dimensional world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_space) we inhabit. To master [elementary geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_geometry) for the [Praxis exam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Praxis_test), you do not need to memorize a disconnected dictionary of [shapes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shape). You simply need to watch how [dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension) stack on top of each other.

## The Foundations: Building Dimensions from Scratch

To build a universe, we must start with a location. In geometry, **a [point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_%28geometry%29) specifies an exact location in [geometric space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_%28mathematics%29).** Because it is purely theoretical, **a point has [zero dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero-dimensional_space), meaning zero [length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Length), zero [width](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Width), and zero [depth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Depth).** It is not a dot drawn by a pencil; it is a [coordinate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_system) of pure existence. 

Once we have a point, we can stretch it. If we take a point and drag it forever in two opposite ways, we create a line. **A [line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_%28geometry%29) is a straight [one-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One-dimensional_space) geometric figure.** Because it captures only the dimension of length, **a line has zero thickness**, yet it **extends infinitely in two opposite directions.** 

[Infinity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinity) is difficult to work with, so we often extract measurable pieces of a line:
*   **A [line segment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_segment) is a bounded portion of a line.** Unlike a line, **a line segment has exactly two distinct endpoints.** It represents a complete path; thus, **a line segment contains every point on the line located between its two endpoints.**
*   If we bound a line on only one side, we create a ray. **A [ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ray_%28geometry%29) is a portion of a line that begins at a single endpoint** and **extends infinitely in only one direction.** Rays are the fundamental building blocks of [angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle) and light [vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_%28mathematics_and_physics%29).

![A geometric ray extending infinitely in one direction from a single starting terminus.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Ray_(A%2C_B%2C_C).svg)

### The Rules of Intersection
When multiple lines exist in the same flat [plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_%28geometry%29), their relationship is defined entirely by whether or not they cross.

> **[Parallel lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_%28geometry%29) are straight lines located in the same flat plane.** By definition, **parallel lines never [intersect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line%E2%80%93line_intersection)**, no matter how far they extend. Because they are perfectly aligned, **parallel lines remain a constant [distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distance) apart at all points.**

If lines are not parallel, they must eventually cross. **[Intersecting lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line%E2%80%93line_intersection) cross each other at exactly one point.** The most structurally important intersecting lines are those that cross perfectly squarely. **[Perpendicular lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular) are intersecting lines that meet to form [right angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_angle)**, meaning that **perpendicular lines intersect at an angle of exactly 90 [degrees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_%28angle%29).** 

![Perpendicular lines intersecting to form four 90-degree right angles.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Perpendicular-coloured.svg)

## The Mechanics of Turning: Angles

Lines and segments give us distance, but to enclose spaces and build shapes, lines must change direction. We measure this deviation using angles. **An [angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle) is a geometric figure formed by two rays sharing a common endpoint.** This pivot point is crucial: **the common endpoint of two rays forming an angle is called the [vertex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_%28geometry%29).** 

Angles measure [rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation). We categorize them strictly by their degree of openness:

| Classification | Definition |
| :--- | :--- |
| **[Acute Angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acute_angle)** | An angle that measures strictly less than 90 degrees. |
| **[Right Angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_angle)** | An angle that measures exactly 90 degrees. |
| **[Obtuse Angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle)** | An angle that measures strictly greater than 90 degrees and strictly less than 180 degrees. |
| **[Straight Angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Straight_angle)** | An angle that measures exactly 180 degrees. Visually, **a straight angle forms a perfectly straight line.** |
| **[Reflex Angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflex_angle)** | An angle that measures strictly greater than 180 degrees and strictly less than 360 degrees. |
| **[Full Rotation Angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turn_%28angle%29)**| An angle that measures exactly 360 degrees, completing a perfect [circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle). |

![Common classifications of angles based on their degree of openness.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Angle_types.svg)

When analyzing geometric figures, angles rarely act alone. We specifically classify pairs of angles based on how they combine to form fundamental limits:
*   **[Complementary angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complementary_angles) are two angles whose sum is exactly 90 degrees.** (Together, they form a right corner).
*   **[Supplementary angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supplementary_angles) are two angles whose sum is exactly 180 degrees.** (Together, they form a straight line).

![Two complementary angles that combine to form a 90-degree right angle.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Complement_angle.svg)

## Flat Worlds: Polygons and the Two-Dimensional Plane

When we take straight line segments and connect them end-to-end until the shape is sealed, we step into the [second dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two-dimensional_space). **A [polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon) is a closed two-dimensional geometric figure** that is **formed entirely by straight line segments.** 

![Various types of polygons, which are closed two-dimensional figures bounded by straight lines.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Polygon_types.svg)

[Nature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nature) and [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics) highly favor [symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry_%28geometry%29). When a polygon is perfectly symmetric, we call it regular. **A [regular polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polygon) has all sides of equal length** and **has all interior angles of equal measure.** 

### Triangles: The Structural Minimum
Three is the absolute minimum number of straight line segments required to close a two-dimensional space. Therefore, **a [triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle) is a polygon with exactly three sides.** Because of the geometric constraints of a flat plane, **the sum of the [interior angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internal_angle) of a triangle is exactly 180 degrees.**

![The interior angles of any flat triangle always sum to exactly 180 degrees.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Triangle_sommeangles.svg)

We classify triangles through two distinct lenses: by their sides and by their angles.

**Classification by Sides:**
*   **[Equilateral triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equilateral_triangle):** Has **three sides of equal length.** Because side lengths force angle measures in a triangle, **each interior angle of an equilateral triangle measures exactly 60 degrees.**
*   **[Isosceles triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isosceles_triangle):** Has **at least two sides of equal length.** 
*   **[Scalene triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalene_triangle):** Has **zero sides of equal length.** Every side and angle is entirely distinct.

**Classification by Angles:**
*   **[Acute triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acute_triangle):** **Contains exactly three acute interior angles.**
*   **[Right triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_triangle):** **Contains exactly one right interior angle.** 
*   **[Obtuse triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_triangle):** **Contains exactly one obtuse interior angle.**

### Quadrilaterals: The Hierarchy of Four
Adding one more side introduces tremendous structural variety. **A [quadrilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral) is a polygon with exactly four sides.** To enclose this shape without crossing lines, **the sum of the interior angles of a quadrilateral is exactly 360 degrees.**

Quadrilaterals are taught not as separate shapes, but as a family tree defined by the addition of increasingly strict attributes:

1.  **[Trapezoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid):** A quadrilateral with **at least one pair of parallel opposite sides.** 
2.  **[Parallelogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram):** A stricter quadrilateral with **two pairs of parallel opposite sides.** This dual-parallelism forces specific internal symmetries: **the opposite sides of a parallelogram are equal in length**, and **the opposite interior angles of a parallelogram are equal in measure.**
3.  **[Rectangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle):** A highly specific parallelogram: **a rectangle is a parallelogram containing exactly four right angles.**
4.  **[Rhombus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhombus):** Another specific parallelogram: **a rhombus is a parallelogram containing exactly four sides of equal length.**
5.  **[Square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square):** The ultimate synthesis. **A square is a rectangle containing exactly four sides of equal length.** Because all four sides and all four interior angles are identical, **a square is considered a regular quadrilateral.**

![An Euler diagram illustrating the strict hierarchy of quadrilaterals, from general polygons down to the highly specific square.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Euler_diagram_of_quadrilateral_types.svg)

### Higher-Order Polygons
Beyond four sides, shapes expand outward into the plane. These are classified purely by the count of their [perimeter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perimeter) segments:
*   **A [pentagon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentagon) is a polygon with exactly five sides.**
*   **A [hexagon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hexagon) is a polygon with exactly six sides.**
*   **An [octagon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octagon) is a polygon with exactly eight sides.**
*   **A [decagon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decagon) is a polygon with exactly ten sides.**

## Stepping into Reality: Three-Dimensional Figures

If two-dimensional geometry is a blueprint, [three-dimensional geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_geometry) is the house itself. **A [three-dimensional figure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_space) is a solid geometric object that has length, width, and height.**

To navigate [solid geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_geometry), we must define the anatomical features of a solid shape:
*   **[Face](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Face_%28geometry%29):** A **flat distinct [surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_%28mathematics%29) of a solid three-dimensional figure.** 
*   **[Edge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edge_%28geometry%29):** A **line segment where two faces of a solid figure intersect.**
*   **[Vertex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_%28geometry%29):** A **corner point where three or more edges intersect.**

![Basic three-dimensional geometric shapes: a pyramid, a sphere, and a cube.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Basic_shapes.svg)

### Polyhedrons: Prisms and Pyramids
When a solid is constructed exclusively from the flat 2D polygons we just studied, it belongs to a specific family. **A [polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyhedron) is a three-dimensional solid figure** where **every face of a polyhedron is a flat polygon.** 

Polyhedrons generally fall into two dominant categories:

**[Prisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prism_%28geometry%29)**
> **A [prism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prism_%28geometry%29) is a polyhedron** defined by uniformity. **A prism has exactly two polygonal [bases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Base_%28geometry%29)** that mirror each other across space: **the two polygonal bases of a prism are strictly parallel to each other** and **are completely [congruent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congruence_%28geometry%29) to each other.** Because these bases are parallel and equal, **the lateral faces of a prism are all parallelograms or rectangles.**

*   **A [rectangular prism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangular_cuboid) is a prism** whose bases and sides are all perpendicular blocks. Specifically, **a rectangular prism has exactly six rectangular faces**, **exactly 12 edges**, and **exactly 8 vertices.**
*   If we force every dimension of a rectangular prism to be perfectly equal, we get a cube. **A [cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube) is a specific type of rectangular prism** that **has exactly six identical square faces.**

![A standard cube is a regular polyhedron composed of six identical square faces.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Hexahedron.svg)

**[Pyramids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyramid_%28geometry%29)**
Unlike a prism, which projects uniformly through space, a pyramid collapses inward. **A [pyramid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyramid_%28geometry%29) is a polyhedron** that **has exactly one polygonal base.** As it rises from this base, **the lateral faces of a pyramid are all triangles.** Eventually, **all lateral faces of a pyramid meet at a single common point called the [apex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apex_%28geometry%29).**

![The anatomy of a square pyramid, highlighting its single polygonal base and the apex where all lateral triangular faces meet.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Pyramid.svg)

### Curved Solids: Mathematics in Motion
Not all three-dimensional reality is built from flat faces and sharp edges. When mathematical [curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curve) are spun into the third dimension, we generate entirely different classes of solids.

**[Cylinders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder)**
**A [cylinder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder) is a three-dimensional geometric figure** that behaves much like a prism, but without the straight-edged polygons. **A cylinder has exactly two circular bases.** Just like a prism, **the two circular bases of a cylinder are strictly parallel to each other** and **are completely congruent to each other.** However, instead of flat rectangular sides, **the two circular bases of a cylinder are connected by a single [curved surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_%28mathematics%29).** Because curves lack corners, **a cylinder has zero vertices.**

![Right and oblique circular cylinders, demonstrating the parallel circular bases connected by a continuous curved surface.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Cylinders.svg)

**[Cones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cone)**
A cone is to a cylinder what a pyramid is to a prism. **A [cone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cone) is a three-dimensional geometric figure** that **has exactly one circular base.** From this foundation, **the curved surface of a cone tapers continuously from the base to a single point.** Just as in a pyramid, **the single point at the top of a cone is called the apex.**

![Right and oblique circular cones, showing the circular base tapering to a single apex.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Cone_3d.png)

**[Spheres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere)**
The ultimate expression of mathematical symmetry is the sphere. **A [sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere) is a perfectly round three-dimensional shape.** Its definition is deceptively simple: **every point on the surface of a sphere is [equidistant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equidistant) from its [center point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centre_%28geometry%29).** Because a sphere exists purely as a continuous equidistant boundary in 3D space, it has no flat geometry whatsoever. **A sphere has exactly zero flat faces**, **exactly zero edges**, and **exactly zero vertices.**

![A sphere is defined by a continuous surface where every point is equidistant from the center.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Sphere_and_Ball.png)
