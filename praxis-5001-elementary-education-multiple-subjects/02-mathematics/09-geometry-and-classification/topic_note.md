## Part 1: The Blueprint of Space (Zero and One Dimensions)

To build a [universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe), we have to start with a location. 

### The Anchor of Reality: Points
Imagine the smallest speck of dust you can. Now shrink it until it has no size at all. What you are left with is a **point**. 
> A point specifies an exact location in geometric space. 

Because it is purely an address—a "here"—a point has **zero dimensions, meaning zero [length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Length), zero width, and zero depth.** 

### Moving in One Dimension: Lines, Rays, and [Segments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_segment)
If you take a point and drag it through space, you create a path. This path gives us our first [dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension).

*   **Lines:** A **line** is a straight one-dimensional geometric figure. Imagine a perfectly straight laser beam shining endlessly into the cosmos. A line **extends infinitely in two opposite directions** and, because it is only one-dimensional, it has **zero thickness**. 
*   **Line Segments:** What if we take out a pair of geometric scissors and snip that infinite line? We get a **[line segment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_segment)**, which is a bounded portion of a line. Unlike a line, a [line segment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_segment) has **exactly two distinct endpoints**. Furthermore, a [line segment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_segment) contains **every point on the line located between its two endpoints**. It is a complete, solid bridge between "Point A" and "Point B."
*   **Rays:** What if we only snip the line once? We get a **ray**. A ray is a portion of a line that begins at a single endpoint, but unlike a [segment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_segment), a ray **extends infinitely in only one direction**. Think of a flashlight: the bulb is the endpoint, and the beam shoots infinitely into the dark.

### The Dance of the Lines
If we put multiple lines into the same flat [universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe) (a plane), they have to interact. How they interact defines the structural integrity of the world around us.

1.  **Parallel lines** are straight lines located in the **same flat plane** that **never intersect**. Why? Because parallel lines remain a **constant distance apart at all points**. Think of the rails of a train track; if they ever changed their distance from one another, the train would derail!
2.  **[Intersecting lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line%E2%80%93line_intersection)** cross each other at **exactly one point**.
3.  **Perpendicular lines** are a very special type of [intersecting lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line%E2%80%93line_intersection). They are intersecting lines that meet to form [right angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_angle). To be exact, perpendicular lines intersect at an **angle of exactly 90 [degrees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_%28angle%29)**. The corners of your walls, your windows, your books—civilization is built on perpendicular lines!

## Part 2: The Art of the Turn (Angles)

When two lines or rays collide, they create a corner. In geometry, we don't just point at a corner; we measure how "open" it is. 

> An **angle** is a geometric figure formed by **two rays sharing a common endpoint**. The common endpoint of two rays forming an angle is called the **[vertex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_%28geometry%29)**.

Think of an angle as a hinge on a door. We classify angles by how far you push the door open. 

### Classifying Angles by Measure

| Classification | The Rule | The Real-World Visual |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Acute angle** | Measures **strictly less than 90 [degrees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_%28angle%29)**. | A pair of tweezers or a slightly open door. It's "sharp." |
| **[Right angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_angle)** | Measures **exactly 90 [degrees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_%28angle%29)**. | The perfect corner of a square room. |
| **Obtuse angle** | Measures **strictly greater than 90 [degrees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_%28angle%29) and strictly less than 180 [degrees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_%28angle%29)**. | A recliner chair leaning back. |
| **Straight angle** | Measures **exactly 180 [degrees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_%28angle%29)**. | The door is pushed flat against the wall. A straight angle forms a **perfectly straight line**. |
| **Reflex angle** | Measures **strictly greater than 180 [degrees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_%28angle%29) and strictly less than 360 [degrees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_%28angle%29)**. | Pushing a door so far backwards that you break the hinges! |
| **Full rotation angle** | Measures **exactly 360 [degrees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_%28angle%29)**. | Doing a complete spin on a skateboard. |

### Pairing Angles Up
Sometimes, angles act as partners to build something larger:
*   **Complementary angles** are two angles whose sum is **exactly 90 [degrees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_%28angle%29)**. (They "complement" each other to make a perfect corner).
*   **Supplementary angles** are two angles whose sum is **exactly 180 [degrees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_%28angle%29)**. (They combine to form a perfectly straight line).

## Part 3: Fencing in Space (Two-Dimensional [Polygons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon))

Now we have lines, [segments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_segment), and angles. Let's take those [line segments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_segment) and build fences to capture some two-dimensional space! 

> A **[polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon)** is a closed two-dimensional geometric figure. It is formed entirely by **straight [line segments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_segment)**. 

No curves allowed! If it's a closed shape made of straight, connected sticks, it's a [polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon). Some [polygons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon), however, achieve absolute perfection. A **regular polygon** has **all sides of equal [length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Length)** AND has **all interior angles of equal measure**. Nature loves regular polygons—think of the cells of a [honeycomb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Honeycomb)!

### The Triangle Family
A **[triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle)** is a [polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon) with **exactly three sides**. No matter how you stretch, squish, or distort a [triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle), there is an unbreakable law of the [universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe) governing it: **the [sum of the interior angles of a triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sum_of_angles_of_a_triangle) is exactly 180 [degrees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_%28angle%29)**. 

We classify [triangles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle) in two ways: by looking at their *sides*, or by looking at their *angles*.

**Classifying [Triangles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle) by Sides:**
*   **Equilateral triangle:** Has **three sides of equal [length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Length)**. Because the sides are equal, the angles must be equally divided too ($180 \div 3$). Therefore, each interior angle of an equilateral triangle measures **exactly 60 [degrees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_%28angle%29)**.
*   **Isosceles triangle:** Has **at least two sides of equal [length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Length)**. 
*   **Scalene triangle:** A completely asymmetrical shape. It has **zero sides of equal [length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Length)**.

**Classifying [Triangles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle) by Angles:**
*   **[Acute triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acute_and_obtuse_triangles):** Contains **exactly three acute interior angles**. 
*   **Right triangle:** Contains **exactly one [right](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_angle) interior angle**. (You can't have two 90-[degree](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_%28angle%29) angles in a [triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle), or you'd run out of degrees!)
*   **[Obtuse triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acute_and_obtuse_triangles):** Contains **exactly one obtuse interior angle**.

### The Quadrilateral Family Tree
A **[quadrilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral)** is a [polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon) with **exactly four sides**. While a [triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle)'s internal angles sum to 180 [degrees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_%28angle%29), the **sum of the interior angles of a [quadrilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral) is exactly 360 [degrees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_%28angle%29)** (think of it as two [triangles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle) glued together!).

[Quadrilaterals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral) act like a family tree, where shapes inherit traits from their "parents."

1.  **Trapezoid:** A [quadrilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral) with **at least one pair of parallel opposite sides**. 
2.  **[Parallelogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram):** A step up in order! A [quadrilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral) with **two pairs of parallel opposite sides**. Because of this perfect parallelism, mathematical magic happens: the **opposite sides of a [parallelogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram) are equal in [length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Length)**, and the **opposite interior angles of a [parallelogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram) are equal in measure**.
3.  **[Rectangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle):** A very disciplined [parallelogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram). It is a [parallelogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram) containing **exactly four [right angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_angle)**.
4.  **[Rhombus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhombus):** A different type of disciplined [parallelogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram). It is a [parallelogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram) containing **exactly four sides of equal [length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Length)**. (Think of a diamond shape).
5.  **Square:** The ultimate, perfect child of the [rectangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle) and the [rhombus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhombus). A square is a [rectangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle) containing **exactly four sides of equal [length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Length)**. Because it meets all the strict requirements—equal sides and equal angles—a square is considered a **regular quadrilateral**.

### Other Notable [Polygons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon)
As we add more straight [line segments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_segment), we get distinct, familiar shapes:
*   **Pentagon:** A [polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon) with **exactly five sides**.
*   **[Hexagon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hexagon):** A [polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon) with **exactly six sides**.
*   **[Octagon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octagon):** A [polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon) with **exactly eight sides**. (The classic [stop sign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stop_sign)!)
*   **Decagon:** A [polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon) with **exactly ten sides**.

## Part 4: Stepping into Reality ([Three-Dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_space) Solid Figures)

Everything we’ve done so far has been flat. Let's pull these shapes out of the page and into the real world. 

> A **[three-dimensional figure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_space)** is a solid geometric object that has **[length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Length), width, and [height](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Height)**.

To describe the anatomy of a solid figure, we need three new terms:
*   **Face:** A flat distinct surface of a solid [three-dimensional figure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_space).
*   **Edge:** A [line segment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_segment) where two faces of a solid figure intersect.
*   **[Vertex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_%28geometry%29):** A corner point where three or more edges intersect.

### The Polyhedrons (Straight-Edged Solids)
A **polyhedron** is a [three-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_space) solid figure where **every face is a flat [polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon)**. No rounded edges, no curved surfaces. If you roll a polyhedron down a hill, it will clatter and bounce on its flat faces.

#### 1. Prisms
A **prism** is a specific type of polyhedron. It is defined by having **exactly two polygonal bases**. These two bases act like the floor and the ceiling of a building. They are **strictly parallel to each other** and **completely [congruent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congruence_%28geometry%29) to each other** (meaning they are the exact same shape and size). 

The walls connecting the floor and the ceiling are called lateral faces. The **lateral faces of a prism are all [parallelograms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram) or [rectangles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle)**. 

*   **Rectangular prism:** A familiar prism (think of a cereal box or a brick). A rectangular prism has exactly **six rectangular faces**, exactly **12 edges**, and exactly **8 [vertices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_%28geometry%29)**. 
*   **[Cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube):** A **[cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube) is a specific type of rectangular prism**. It represents ultimate [symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry_%28geometry%29). A [cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube) has exactly **six identical square faces**.

#### 2. [Pyramids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyramid_%28geometry%29)
A **[pyramid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyramid_%28geometry%29)** is also a polyhedron, but instead of having a ceiling and a floor, it only has a floor. A [pyramid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyramid_%28geometry%29) has **exactly one polygonal base**. The **lateral faces of a [pyramid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyramid_%28geometry%29) are all [triangles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle)**. These triangular walls reach upward and **all lateral faces of a [pyramid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyramid_%28geometry%29) meet at a single common point called the [apex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apex_%28geometry%29)**.

### Curved Solids (Non-Polyhedrons)
Nature is not just made of jagged edges and flat planes. What happens when we introduce curves into 3D space? Because they have curved surfaces, these are *not* polyhedrons, but they are incredibly important [three-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_space) geometric figures.

#### 1. Cylinders
Think of a soup can. A **cylinder** is a [three-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_space) geometric figure that has **exactly two [circular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle) bases**. Just like a prism, these two [circular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle) bases are **strictly parallel to each other** and **completely [congruent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congruence_%28geometry%29) to each other**. 

However, instead of flat rectangular walls, the two [circular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle) bases of a cylinder are **connected by a single curved surface**. Because there are no flat polygonal faces intersecting to create sharp corners, a cylinder has **zero [vertices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_%28geometry%29)**.

#### 2. Cones
Think of a traffic cone or an [ice cream cone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ice_cream_cone). A **cone** is a [three-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_space) geometric figure that has **exactly one [circular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle) base**. From that base, the **curved surface of a cone tapers continuously from the base to a single point**. Just like in a [pyramid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyramid_%28geometry%29), the single point at the top of a cone is called the **[apex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apex_%28geometry%29)**.

#### 3. Spheres
Finally, the most mathematically perfect shape in the cosmos. A **sphere** is a perfectly round [three-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_space) shape. 

How do we define perfect roundness? **Every point on the surface of a sphere is equidistant from its center point**. 

Because of this supreme, infinite [symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry_%28geometry%29), it lacks the jagged anatomy of other solids. A sphere has **exactly zero flat faces**, **exactly zero edges**, and **exactly zero [vertices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_%28geometry%29)**.

### Summary for the [Praxis Exam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Praxis_test)
Look back at what we've just built. We started with a **point**—a location with zero dimensions. We dragged it to make infinite **lines**, snipped them into **[segments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_segment)** and **rays**, and crossed them to make **angles**. We trapped space inside **[polygons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon)** like the 180-[degree](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_%28angle%29) **[triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle)** and the perfectly regular **square**. Finally, we pulled those [polygons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon) out of the plane to construct **polyhedrons** like prisms and [pyramids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyramid_%28geometry%29), and spun circles into **cylinders**, **cones**, and perfectly smooth **spheres**.

Geometry is not a random list of facts to [memorize](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Memorization). It is a logical, interconnected architecture. Keep this blueprint in your mind on test day, trust the definitions, and you will see right through the exam questions just as clearly as a [physicist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physicist) sees the gears of the [universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe). Good luck!
