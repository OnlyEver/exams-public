Imagine a sprawling, absolutely flat concrete jungle stretching out into infinity. This isn't just a map; the coordinate plane is a two-dimensional surface formed by two intersecting perpendicular number lines. These two massive, endless highways are the lifeblood of the city, locked in a perfect ninety-degree death grip. Cutting straight across the landscape from left to right is the East-West superhighway. The horizontal number line in a coordinate plane is named the x-axis. Plunging straight up and down across the landscape is the North-South highway. The vertical number line in a coordinate plane is named the y-axis. 

Now, right where these two colossal concrete behemoths crash into each other is the most vital, highly contested real estate in the whole city. The exact point of intersection between the x-axis and the y-axis is called the origin. This is the neutral zone. Ground zero. At this exact intersection, nobody has made a move left, right, up, or down. It is absolute stillness, which means the x-coordinate of the origin is strictly zero, and the y-coordinate of the origin is strictly zero. 

Because these two massive highways crisscross the entire world, they slice the city into four fiercely guarded territories. The x-axis and y-axis divide the coordinate plane into four distinct regions called quadrants. But the rival families running these turfs are hopelessly pretentious—they consider themselves emperors of their domain, which is why the four quadrants of a coordinate plane are traditionally numbered using Roman numerals. And just to be completely contrarian and dramatic, they don't count clockwise like normal people. No, the quadrants are numbered sequentially in a counterclockwise direction starting from the upper-right quadrant! 

To survive in this city, you can’t just tell someone to "meet you by the deli." You need a foolproof address. You need an ordered pair. An ordered pair consists of two numbers used to define the exact location of a point on a coordinate plane. It’s a strict two-step handshake, and you never, ever mess up the order. 

The first number in an ordered pair represents the x-coordinate. Listen to me, because people get this twisted all the time: the horizontal position of a point on the coordinate plane is determined exclusively by the x-coordinate. It tells you your horizontal distance of a point from the y-axis. Imagine standing on the vertical y-axis looking out. A positive x-coordinate dictates movement to the right of the y-axis. You walk East. A negative x-coordinate dictates movement to the left of the y-axis. You walk West. 

Only after you’ve made your horizontal move do you look at the second number. The second number in an ordered pair represents the y-coordinate. Just like the first number owned the horizon, the vertical position of a point on the coordinate plane is determined exclusively by the y-coordinate. This number measures the vertical distance of a point from the x-axis. You look at the flat horizontal road you just walked along, and you decide your elevation. A positive y-coordinate dictates upward movement from the x-axis. You take the elevator to the penthouse. A negative y-coordinate dictates downward movement from the x-axis. You plunge into the basement. 

Let’s take a grand counterclockwise tour of the families, shall we? We start with the golden children. Quadrant I is the upper-right region of the coordinate plane. To live here, you walk right and take the elevator up. Therefore, every point located in Quadrant I has a positive x-coordinate, and every point located in Quadrant I has a positive y-coordinate. They are living the double-positive dream.

But sweep counterclockwise across the vertical highway, and you hit the fallen angels. Quadrant II is the upper-left region of the coordinate plane. To trespass here, you gotta move left into negative territory, but you still want those high-rise penthouse views. So, every point located in Quadrant II has a negative x-coordinate, but every point located in Quadrant II has a positive y-coordinate. 

Keep moving counterclockwise, diving beneath the horizontal highway, and welcome to the cynical underworld. Quadrant III is the lower-left region of the coordinate plane. You went left, and you went down. Every point located in Quadrant III has a negative x-coordinate, and every point located in Quadrant III has a negative y-coordinate. It is a double-negative wasteland. 

Finally, sweep to the right, staying underground. Quadrant IV is the lower-right region of the coordinate plane. These mobsters took a step to the right, keeping things positive, but they still dwell below ground level. Every point located in Quadrant IV has a positive x-coordinate, but every point located in Quadrant IV has a negative y-coordinate. 

Now, here is the absolute wildest drama of this whole setup—the borderlands. What if you refuse to pick a family? What if you just stand frozen on one of the highways? If you walk left or right on the x-axis but absolutely refuse to go up or down, you have zero vertical elevation. Any point positioned exactly on the x-axis has a y-coordinate equal to zero. What if you stand perfectly still horizontally, but ride the elevator up and down the vertical highway? Any point positioned exactly on the y-axis has an x-coordinate equal to zero. And the mob bosses respect the highways as neutral ground! That means points residing on either the x-axis or the y-axis do not belong to any of the four quadrants. They are stateless!

So, how do these families claim actual, usable territory? They drop safehouses and build walls. A polygon is constructed on a coordinate plane by plotting individual vertices as ordered pairs. You drop your points—your safehouses—all over your turf. But a bunch of scattered dots doesn't secure a neighborhood. To lock it down, the plotted vertices of a polygon must be connected with straight line segments to form a closed geometric shape. Once that shape is closed, that is your turf. 

But you know the bosses. They are greedy. They want to measure exactly how much they own, which means measuring the length of their walls. If two safehouses are on the exact same vertical block, how do you find the distance between them? You can't have a wall that is "negative five miles" long, right? Real estate is strictly positive! So, the distance between two points sharing the same x-coordinate equals the absolute value of the difference between the y-coordinates. And if two safehouses are on the same exact horizontal street? The distance between two points sharing the same y-coordinate equals the absolute value of the difference between the x-coordinates. You just subtract their differing addresses and force the number to be positive. Bam. You have the length of your wall. 

And why does any of this matter? Because in Geometry City, distance is power! Those coordinate plane distances between vertices are used to determine the lengths of polygon sides for calculating perimeter. That's exactly how much barbed wire a family needs to wrap around their borders. And the space inside those borders? Coordinate plane distances between vertices are used to determine the dimensions of polygons for area calculations. You find your base, you find your height, you multiply them, and suddenly you know exactly how many square miles of this magnificent, mathematical madhouse you control.
