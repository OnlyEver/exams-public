## The Anatomy of a Pattern

Before we can solve a puzzle, we need to know what we are looking at. What is a pattern, really? 

A mathematical pattern is an ordered set of numbers, shapes, or objects arranged according to a specific, predictable rule. 

The secret word here is *predictable*. If you just throw down a random handful of playing cards, that is not a pattern. There has to be a solid rule guiding it. 

When you line up your pattern, every single piece inside of it has a special name. 
Each individual number, shape, or object within a mathematical pattern is called a term. Think of a "term" like a single song on your favorite playlist.

### Visualizing the World: Shape Patterns
We don't have to start with tricky numbers. We can start with things we can see, like shapes or emojis! 

There are two main ways shapes act when they are in a line:
1. **Repeating shape patterns:** Imagine the tiles on a bathroom floor. A repeating shape pattern consists of a core group of shapes that duplicates continuously in the exact same sequence. Imagine drawing a square, then a circle, then a triangle, and then doing that over and over. The rule is just to copy and paste!
2. **Growing shape patterns:** This is where things get really cool. A growing shape pattern is a sequence of shapes that increases or decreases in size or quantity according to a specific mathematical rule. Imagine building a staircase out of Lego bricks. Step 1 has 1 brick. Step 2 has 3 bricks. Step 3 has 6 bricks. The shapes aren't just copying themselves; they are growing based on a math rule!

## The Two Great Families of Numerical Sequences

When we switch from shapes to numbers, almost all the basic patterns you will see belong to one of two main families. 

### 1. Arithmetic Sequences (The Allowance)
Imagine getting a weekly allowance. Every single week, you get the exact same amount of money added to your piggy bank. 

An arithmetic sequence is a numerical pattern where each consecutive term is found by adding a constant value to the previous term. 

But what if you are spending your allowance on snacks every week? An arithmetic sequence can also be generated by subtracting a constant value from the previous term. 

That steady number you add or subtract every time has a name. The constant value added or subtracted to find the next term in an arithmetic sequence is called the common difference. 

Let's find the common difference in this pattern: 2, 5, 8, 11...
Step 1: Look at the first term (2) and the second term (5). 
Step 2: Figure out what you add to 2 to get 5. (2 + 3 = 5).
Step 3: Check the next term just to be sure. Does 5 + 3 = 8? Yes! 
Step 4: The common difference is +3.

### 2. Geometric Sequences (The Zombie Virus)
Now, imagine a video game about a zombie virus. One zombie infects two people. Then those two infect four. Then four infect eight. The numbers aren't just adding slowly; they are multiplying!

A geometric sequence is a numerical pattern where each consecutive term is found by multiplying the previous term by a constant value. 

Just like you can subtract, you can also divide. A geometric sequence can also be generated by dividing the previous term by a constant value. Imagine a giant pizza getting sliced in half over and over. 

The constant value used to multiply or divide terms in a geometric sequence is called the common ratio.

Let's find the common ratio in this pattern: 3, 6, 12, 24...
Step 1: Look at the first term (3) and the second term (6).
Step 2: Ask yourself what you multiply by 3 to get 6. (3 x 2 = 6).
Step 3: Check the next term to be sure. Does 6 x 2 = 12? Yes!
Step 4: The common ratio is x2.

## Nature’s Favorite Numbers: Famous Patterns

Some patterns are so famous that mathematicians gave them their own special names. 

### Square Numbers
Imagine arranging pennies into a perfect square on your desk. A 1 by 1 square, a 2 by 2 square, a 3 by 3 square. 
Square numbers form a numerical pattern where every term is the mathematical product of an integer multiplied by itself. 

Here is how we build them step-by-step:
Step 1: Multiply 1 by itself (1 x 1 = 1).
Step 2: Multiply 2 by itself (2 x 2 = 4).
Step 3: Multiply 3 by itself (3 x 3 = 9).
Step 4: Multiply 4 by itself (4 x 4 = 16).
Step 5: Multiply 5 by itself (5 x 5 = 25).
Step 6: Multiply 6 by itself (6 x 6 = 36).

Because of this math, the sequence of square numbers begins with the terms 1, 4, 9, 16, 25, and 36. 

### Triangular Numbers
What if you arranged your pennies into triangles instead, kind of like bowling pins? 
Triangular numbers form a numerical pattern representing the total number of dots required to construct progressively larger equilateral triangles. 

Here is how to build them step-by-step:
Step 1: Start with 1 dot at the top of your triangle (Total = 1).
Step 2: Add a new row of 2 dots underneath (1 + 2 = 3).
Step 3: Add a new row of 3 dots underneath that (3 + 3 = 6).
Step 4: Add a new row of 4 dots underneath that (6 + 4 = 10).
Step 5: Add a new row of 5 dots underneath that (10 + 5 = 15).
Step 6: Add a new row of 6 dots underneath that (15 + 6 = 21).

So, the sequence of triangular numbers begins with the terms 1, 3, 6, 10, 15, and 21. 

### The Fibonacci Sequence
This pattern is famous because it shows up everywhere in nature—like in the spirals of seashells or the petals on a flower! 
The Fibonacci sequence is a numerical pattern where each term is calculated as the sum of the two immediately preceding terms. 

Here is how we calculate it step-by-step:
Step 1: Start with the numbers 0 and 1. 
Step 2: Add them together to get the next term (0 + 1 = 1).
Step 3: Add the two newest terms together (1 + 1 = 2).
Step 4: Add the two newest terms together (1 + 2 = 3).
Step 5: Add the two newest terms together (2 + 3 = 5).
Step 6: Add the two newest terms together (3 + 5 = 8).

Because of this, the standard Fibonacci sequence begins with the numbers 0, 1, 1, 2, 3, 5, and 8.

## Unlocking the Future: Rules, Guesses, and Generalizations

How do we figure out a pattern's secret rule? There are two different ways to do this.

### The Two Types of Rules
1. **Recursive Rules (The Relay Race)**
   A recursive rule defines the next term in a sequence based entirely on the mathematical value of the immediately preceding term. 
   If the rule is just "add 5 to the last number," that's recursive. It's like a relay race where you have to get the baton from the runner right behind you. The problem is, if you want to find the 100th term, you have to figure out all 99 terms before it!

2. **Explicit Rules (The Teleporter)**
   An explicit rule calculates the value of any term in a sequence directly from the numerical position of that term within the sequence.
   Imagine you want the 100th term. Instead of counting up to 100, an explicit rule might be "multiply the position number by 5." 
   Step 1: Take your target position (100).
   Step 2: Multiply it by 5. (100 x 5 = 500).
   You instantly know the 100th term is 500! It works like a fast-travel teleporter in a video game.

### From Hunch to Law
When you try to find a rule, your first step is usually making a really smart guess. 
A mathematical conjecture is an educated prediction or conclusion formed on the basis of observed patterns and incomplete information. It’s you looking at a few numbers and saying, "Hey, I think this pattern is multiplying by 3!" 

Once you test your guess and prove it works perfectly every time, it levels up. A mathematical generalization involves creating an overarching algebraic formula or rule that correctly applies to all terms within a given sequence. It goes from being a smart guess to a rock-solid rule that never fails.

## Function Tables: The Input-Output Machines

A great way to test your rules is by using a function table. Think of it like a pizza oven. You put raw dough in one side (the input), the oven does its magic (the rule), and a hot pizza comes out the other side (the output). 

An input-output rule describes the specific mathematical operation applied to a starting input value to produce the corresponding output value.

To keep everything neat, we use a table. A function table organizes input and output values to demonstrate the mathematical relationship between two variables. 

| Input (Position) | Output (Term) |
| :---: | :---: |
| 1 | 4 |
| 2 | 8 |
| 3 | 12 |
| 4 | 16 |

Let's find the explicit rule step-by-step:
Step 1: Look at the first row. The input is 1, the output is 4. What can we do to 1 to get 4? We could add 3, or we could multiply by 4.
Step 2: Test "add 3" on the second row. Input is 2. Does 2 + 3 = 8? No. So "add 3" is wrong.
Step 3: Test "multiply by 4" on the second row. Does 2 x 4 = 8? Yes!
Step 4: Test it on the third row. Does 3 x 4 = 12? Yes!
The rule is to multiply the input by 4!

## Dancing Together: Comparing Two Patterns

Sometimes you will have two patterns racing side-by-side. 

Identifying a relationship between two separate numerical patterns requires comparing the corresponding terms located in the exact same position in both sequences. 
You can't compare the 1st term of one pattern with the 5th term of another. You have to compare Term 1 to Term 1, and Term 2 to Term 2. 

Let’s look at two patterns side-by-side:

| Position | Pattern A (Starts 0, Add 2) | Pattern B (Starts 0, Add 4) |
| :---: | :---: | :---: |
| 1st Term | 0 | 0 |
| 2nd Term | 2 | 4 |
| 3rd Term | 4 | 8 |
| 4th Term | 6 | 12 |

Let's find the relationship step-by-step:
Step 1: Look at the 2nd Term. Pattern A is 2, Pattern B is 4. Notice that 4 is double 2 (2 x 2 = 4).
Step 2: Look at the 3rd Term. Pattern A is 4, Pattern B is 8. Notice that 8 is double 4 (4 x 2 = 8).
Step 3: Look at the 4th Term. Pattern A is 6, Pattern B is 12. Notice that 12 is double 6 (6 x 2 = 12).

Because of this, we know: If Pattern A starts at 0 with the rule 'add 2' and Pattern B starts at 0 with the rule 'add 4', every term in Pattern B is exactly twice the value of its corresponding term in Pattern A.

### Making it Visual: Graphing
Did you know you can draw these numbers like a picture? 

Corresponding terms from two different numerical patterns can be paired together and written as an ordered pair for graphing. 
For our table above, we take a number from Pattern A and its partner from Pattern B and wrap them in parentheses like this: (2, 4), then (4, 8), then (6, 12). 

Graphing ordered pairs generated from two numerical patterns onto a coordinate plane visually demonstrates the proportional or algebraic relationship between the sequences. 
When you plot these points on graph paper, they create a perfectly straight line! It's like drawing a treasure map that proves your math is exactly right.
