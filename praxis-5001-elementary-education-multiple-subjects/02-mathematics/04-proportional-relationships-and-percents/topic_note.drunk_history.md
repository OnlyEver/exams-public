Listen to me, Tony, slide that bowl of peanuts out of the way and look at this napkin, because I am about to explain the absolute fabric of reality to you. You think the universe runs on magic? No! It runs on comparison! The entire cosmos, from the way my Aunt Carmela judges a pasta sauce to how the mob splits a heist, is built on a mathematical comparison of two quantities indicating how many times one value contains or is contained within the other. We call this beautiful, volatile relationship a ratio. Imagine Carmela is making her explosive Sunday gravy. She needs three cloves of garlic for every four cans of crushed tomatoes. You can document this culinary blood oath in three ways. If you're aggressive and staring her down, ratios can be expressed using a colon, like 3:4. If you're whispering gossip across the pew at church, ratios can be expressed using the word "to," as in 3 to 4. And if you are stacking the ingredients like bodies in a trunk, ratios can be expressed as a fraction, one on top of the other, 3/4. Do not let the different outfits fool you, Tony! The ratio "3 to 4" is mathematically equivalent to the fraction 3/4. It is the exact same recipe, the exact same reality, just wearing a different disguise. 

Now, what happens when Carmela’s entire extended family crashes dinner? You can't just throw in random amounts of garlic, or the whole neighborhood will revolt. You need equivalent ratios, which are ratios expressing the identical relationship between two quantities. You keep the peace by scaling up or down evenly. Multiplying both terms of a ratio by the same non-zero number produces an equivalent ratio—so multiply the 3 and the 4 by two, and now you have 6 cloves and 8 cans. Boom, the gravy tastes identical! Conversely, if half the family gets indicted and doesn't show up, dividing both terms of a ratio by the same non-zero number produces an equivalent ratio. The harmony of the sauce is preserved! 

But Tony, what happens when the getaway car peels out of Carmela’s driveway? Now we aren't just comparing garlic to tomatoes; we're comparing miles driven to hours passed. A rate is a special type of ratio comparing two quantities with different units of measure. The cops don't care about a "rate" of 120 miles in 2 hours, though. That’s clunky. They want a unit rate, which is a rate in which the second quantity in the comparison is exactly one unit. They want to know your speed for *one* single hour! To calculate a unit rate from a general rate, you just divide the first quantity by the second quantity. 120 miles divided by 2 hours equals 60 miles per 1 hour. It’s the same logic when Carmela goes to the deli to buy prosciutto. She’s hunting for a unit price, a specific type of unit rate measuring the cost of one single item or unit of measure. She wants to know the price per single pound so she knows exactly who is trying to rip her off!

When two rival families finally sit down and agree that their operations are perfectly balanced, they’ve created a proportion. A proportion is an algebraic equation stating that two distinct ratios are equal. It is a treaty! When these two gangs are locked in this perfect, unwavering truce, we say they hold a proportional relationship, meaning two quantities hold a proportional relationship if the ratio of one quantity to the other remains constant. No one gets greedy; the ratio never flinches. In this underworld, there is always a boss getting his cut, right? The constant of proportionality is the constant ratio of the dependent variable to the independent variable. If the boss demands 500 bucks for every single job you pull, that 500 is the constant. We write this ironclad rule as the linear equation y = kx, where the variable k represents the constant of proportionality. If you graph this syndicate’s earnings, two things happen. First, a graph representing a proportional relationship always passes through the origin. Why? Because the origin on a Cartesian coordinate plane is the point (0,0), and if you pull exactly zero jobs, you kick exactly zero dollars up to the boss! Second, a graph representing a proportional relationship is always a straight line. The boss's cut never wavers, never bends, never shows mercy!

But what if someone is holding out on the boss? You have an unknown value, a rat in the equation! You solve it at the sit-down, but listen closely: setting up a proportion requires aligning matching units in the numerators and matching units in the denominators. If "stolen diamonds" are on the top floor on the left side of the table, "stolen diamonds" must be on the top floor on the right side! To interrogate the numbers and find the rat, cross-multiplication is an algebraic method used to solve for an unknown value in a proportion. You multiply diagonally! And the math never lies, Tony, because the cross products of two equivalent fractions are always equal to each other. It’s a mathematical polygraph test! The heist architect uses the same exact trick with blueprints. The scale factor is the ratio of a length on a scale drawing to the corresponding length on the actual object. The mastermind knows that scale factors can be used within proportional equations to calculate actual dimensions from a model, allowing him to know exactly how thick the real bank vault is just by measuring a tiny line on his napkin!

Eventually, all this stolen loot has to be laundered, and the underworld standardizes everything into percents. Why? Because a percent represents a specific ratio whose second term is exactly the number 100. It is the ultimate equalizer! The English word "percent" literally translates to "per one hundred". You have to be fluid, Tony, like a money launderer moving cash through a casino. To convert a decimal number to a percent, multiply the decimal number by 100. It instantly looks bigger and cleaner! If you need to hide it again, to convert a percent value to a decimal number, divide the percent value by 100. And if you have a messy, fractured cut of the loot? To convert a fraction to a percent, divide the numerator by the denominator and multiply the resulting decimal by 100. Suddenly, your weird 3/8ths cut is a clean 37.5%! Whenever you are settling accounts, a percent problem can be solved by setting the percent value over 100 equal to the part over the whole. It’s foolproof! Or, if the boss just barks at you for his cut right now, to find a specific percent of a given number, convert the percent to a decimal and multiply by the given number. 

You have to track how the turf changes, too! If you take over a new neighborhood, percent increase is calculated by dividing the absolute amount of increase by the original amount and multiplying by 100. But if the feds raid your warehouses and you lose half your territory, percent decrease is calculated by dividing the absolute amount of decrease by the original amount and multiplying by 100. It’s a tragedy, but it’s math! To cover their tracks, the family opens a terrible boutique selling ugly tracksuits. So they have to swallow their pride and take a markdown, which is a price reduction from the original price of an item calculated using a percentage.
