Welcome to one of the coolest and most important ideas in all of math! Seriously, if you want to understand how video game levels are sized, how pizza places price a massive pie, or how to get the most out of your allowance, you have to understand one awesome trick: **comparison**. 

When we just look at numbers by themselves, they don't tell us much. If I tell you a new skateboard costs 50 dollars, is that a good deal? It depends on the skateboard! We figure out the world by comparing things to other things. That is the magic behind proportional reasoning. 

Let’s jump in and learn exactly how these comparisons work, how to draw them out, and how to use them to solve everyday problems.

## 1. The Art of Comparison: Ratios and Rates

First up: **A ratio is a mathematical comparison of two quantities indicating how many times one value contains or is contained within the other.** Think of it like a recipe for making the perfect slime. You might use 3 ounces of glue for every 4 pinches of glitter. 

You can write this exact relationship in three perfectly acceptable ways:
1.  **Ratios can be expressed using a colon:** 3:4
2.  **Ratios can be expressed using the word "to":** 3 to 4
3.  **Ratios can be expressed as a fraction:** 3/4

> **Crucial Fact:** Don't let the different looks confuse you. **The ratio "3 to 4" is mathematically equivalent to the fraction 3/4.** It is exactly the same concept just wearing a different disguise.

### Making Things Bigger or Smaller: Equivalent Ratios
What if you want to make enough slime for your whole class? You need to make a bigger batch without ruining the recipe. You need an **equivalent ratio**.

**Equivalent ratios are ratios expressing the identical relationship between two quantities.** 
How do we find them? It’s super easy!
*   **Multiplying both terms of a ratio by the same non-zero number produces an equivalent ratio.** 
    Let's try it:
    1. Take our slime ratio 3:4.
    2. Multiply both numbers by 2 (3 x 2 = 6, and 4 x 2 = 8).
    3. Write the new equivalent ratio: 6:8.
*   **Dividing both terms of a ratio by the same non-zero number produces an equivalent ratio.**
    Let's try it:
    1. Take a big ratio like 30:40.
    2. Divide both numbers by 10 (30 / 10 = 3, and 40 / 10 = 4).
    3. Write the new equivalent ratio: 3:4.

### Rates and Unit Rates
Now, what happens when we compare things that aren't measured in the same way? 
**A rate is a special type of ratio comparing two quantities with different units of measure.** If your character in a game runs 100 meters in 2 seconds, that's a rate because meters and seconds are totally different things.

But "100 meters in 2 seconds" sounds clunky. We usually want to know what happens in just *one* second. 
**A unit rate is a rate in which the second quantity in the comparison is exactly one unit.** 

> **How to find it:** **To calculate a unit rate from a general rate, divide the first quantity by the second quantity.** 
> Let's do the math:
> 1. Look at the problem (100 meters in 2 seconds).
> 2. Set up the division: 100 / 2.
> 3. Solve to get the unit rate: 50 meters per 1 second (50 m/s).

This happens in real life all the time. Ever look closely at the price tags for snacks at the grocery store? You are looking at a **unit price**, which **is a specific type of unit rate measuring the cost of one single item or unit of measure.** It helps you know if a giant bag of chips is actually a better deal than a small one!

## 2. Drawing the Math: Visualizing Proportionality

Human beings are highly visual creatures. Sometimes staring at fractions is tough, so we draw pictures to make the numbers easier to see. We have two awesome tools for this:

**1. The Tape Diagram**
Imagine snapping flat, rectangular pieces of Lego together. **A tape diagram is a visual model using rectangles to represent ratios.** If the ratio of cats to dogs at an animal shelter is 2:3, you draw 2 blocks for cats and 3 blocks for dogs side-by-side. Also, **a tape diagram is a visual model using rectangles to represent proportional relationships.** It lets you visually see all the different parts making up the whole.

**2. The Double Number Line**
Imagine two subway trains running on parallel tracks. **A double number line is a visual model showing equivalent ratios along two parallel number lines scaled differently.** 
For example, the top line tracks "Meters" (0, 50, 100, 150) and the bottom line exactly tracks "Seconds" (0, 1, 2, 3). You just look straight up and down to find the equivalent ratio!

## 3. When Ratios Lock Together: Proportions

When you set two identical ratios equal to each other, you have built a proportion. By definition, **a proportion is an algebraic equation stating that two distinct ratios are equal.** (For example: 3/4 = 6/8).

When two things are locked into this kind of perfect harmony, we say they hold a proportional relationship. Precisely stated: **Two quantities hold a proportional relationship if the ratio of one quantity to the other remains constant.**

### The Constant of Proportionality (k)
Let's say we are looking at how a dependent variable (y, like the allowance you earn) changes based on an independent variable (x, like the hours you do chores). We can find a magic number that links them. 

**The constant of proportionality is the constant ratio of the dependent variable to the independent variable.** 
If you make 15 dollars every single hour you rake leaves, 15 is your constant. In algebra, we write this perfectly as **the linear equation y = kx, where the variable k represents the constant of proportionality.**

### Graphing the Relationship
If you draw a true proportional relationship on a piece of graph paper, two things must mathematically happen every single time:
1.  **A graph representing a proportional relationship always passes through the origin.** Why? Because if you work zero hours, you earn zero dollars! 
2.  **A graph representing a proportional relationship is always a straight line.** The rate never bends; it is perfectly constant.

> **Fun Fact:** **The origin on a Cartesian coordinate plane is the point (0,0).** It is the absolute ground zero of the math universe!

## 4. Solving Proportions: Cross-Multiplication and Scale

Often in math, we are given three pieces of a proportion and asked to find the missing fourth piece. 

First, the golden rule of setup: **Setting up a proportion requires aligning matching units in the numerators and matching units in the denominators.** If "Meters" are on top on the left side of the equals sign, "Meters" absolutely *must* be on top on the right side. 

How do we solve it? We use an awesome algebraic trick.
**Cross-multiplication is an algebraic method used to solve for an unknown value in a proportion.** 

Why does cross-multiplication work? It’s not magic; it’s a mathematical certainty! **The cross products of two equivalent fractions are always equal to each other.** 
Let's prove it with 3/4 = 6/8:
1. Multiply across the first diagonal: 3 x 8 = 24.
2. Multiply across the second diagonal: 4 x 6 = 24.
3. Compare the answers: 24 = 24. It works flawlessly!

### Building Worlds: The Scale Factor
Video game designers and mapmakers use proportions to shrink huge worlds onto your screen. They do this using a **scale factor**, which **is the ratio of a length on a scale drawing to the corresponding length on the actual object.** 

Because a map and the real world are proportional, **scale factors can be used within proportional equations to calculate actual dimensions from a model.**

## 5. Percents: The Universal Translator

Comparing fractions like 7/8 and 4/5 in your head is annoying. Wouldn't it be easier if every fraction in the world just agreed to use the exact same bottom number? 

That is exactly what a percent is. **A percent represents a specific ratio whose second term is exactly the number 100.** In fact, language tells us this secret plainly: **The English word "percent" literally translates to "per one hundred".**

### Conversions: Fluency Between Formats
To be a master of math, you must be able to fluidly convert numbers between decimals, fractions, and percents. 

| Operation | The Rule | Step-by-Step Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Decimal to Percent** | **To convert a decimal number to a percent, multiply the decimal number by 100.** | 1. Start with 0.45.<br>2. Multiply 0.45 x 100.<br>3. Write the answer: 45%. |
| **Percent to Decimal** | **To convert a percent value to a decimal number, divide the percent value by 100.** | 1. Start with 82%.<br>2. Divide 82 / 100.<br>3. Write the answer: 0.82. |
| **Fraction to Percent** | **To convert a fraction to a percent, divide the numerator by the denominator and multiply the resulting decimal by 100.** | 1. Start with 3/4.<br>2. Divide 3 / 4 to get 0.75.<br>3. Multiply 0.75 x 100 to get 75%. |

### Setting Up Percent Problems
Every percent problem in the world can be solved with one beautifully simple framework: **A percent problem can be solved by setting the percent value over 100 equal to the part over the whole.**

Part / Whole = Percent / 100

If you just need a quick calculation, you can use the algebraic shortcut: **To find a specific percent of a given number, convert the percent to a decimal and multiply by the given number.** 
Let's find 20% of 50:
1. Convert 20% to a decimal (20 / 100 = 0.20).
2. Multiply 0.20 x 50.
3. The answer is 10.

## 6. Real World Proportions: Percent Change and Money

The world isn't stuck in place; prices and values constantly go up and down. We measure this movement using percent change. The trick is to always compare the change to where you *started*.

*   **Percent increase is calculated by dividing the absolute amount of increase by the original amount and multiplying by 100.** 
    Let's say a toy goes from 10 dollars to 12 dollars.
    1. Find the absolute increase: 12 - 10 = 2.
    2. Divide by the original amount: 2 / 10 = 0.2.
    3. Multiply by 100: 0.2 x 100 = 20%.
*   **Percent decrease is calculated by dividing the absolute amount of decrease by the original amount and multiplying by 100.**
    Let's say a toy goes from 10 dollars to 8 dollars.
    1. Find the absolute decrease: 10 - 8 = 2.
    2. Divide by the original amount: 2 / 10 = 0.2.
    3. Multiply by 100: 0.2 x 100 = 20%.

### The Retail Dance: Markups and Markdowns
Every time you walk into a store, you are swimming in percents:
*   The store bought the cool sneakers for cheap, but they charge you more. **A markup is a price increase added to the cost price of an item to determine the final selling price.**
*   Then, it goes on sale! **A markdown is a price reduction from the original price of an item calculated using a percentage.** 

### Making Money with Money: Simple Interest
Finally, let's talk about saving money. When you put money in a bank (or borrow it), you charge or pay "rent" on that money. That rent is called interest. 

> **The Golden Formula:**
> **Simple interest is mathematically calculated using the formula Interest equals Principal times Rate times Time.** 
> I = P x R x T

*   **I** = Interest earned/paid
*   **P** = Principal (the original chunk of money)
*   **R** = Rate (the percent)
*   **T** = Time (usually in years)

**Warning:** Don't let the formula trick you! **In the simple interest formula, the rate must be expressed as a decimal or fraction before multiplying.** 
Let's calculate the interest on 100 dollars saved for 1 year at a 5% rate:
1. Convert 5% to a decimal (5 / 100 = 0.05).
2. Plug numbers into the formula: Interest = 100 x 0.05 x 1.
3. Multiply them together. The interest is 5 dollars!
