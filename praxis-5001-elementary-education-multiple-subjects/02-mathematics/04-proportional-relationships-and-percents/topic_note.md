Welcome to one of the most beautiful and fundamentally important ideas in all of [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics). Seriously! If you want to understand how the [universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe) scales, how [grocery stores](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grocery_store) price [apples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apple), or how [architects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Architect) build [skyscrapers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skyscraper), you have to understand one profoundly simple trick: **comparison**. 

When we observe the world, [numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number) sitting alone don't tell us much. If I tell you a [car](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Car) costs \$1,000, is that good? It depends on the car! We understand the universe by comparing things to other things. That is the essence of [proportional reasoning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proportionality_%28mathematics%29). 

Let’s roll up our sleeves and figure out exactly how these comparisons work, how to visualize them, and how to use them to solve [problems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Problem_solving).

## 1. The Art of Comparison: Ratios and Rates

At its core, **a [ratio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ratio) is a mathematical comparison of two [quantities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantity) indicating how many times one value contains or is contained within the other.** Think of it as a [recipe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recipe). If you are making the perfect batch of [lemonade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lemonade), you might use 3 [cups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cup_%28unit%29) of lemon juice for every 4 cups of [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water). 

You can write this exact relationship in three perfectly acceptable ways:
1.  **Ratios can be expressed using a [colon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colon_%28punctuation%29):** \$3:4$
2.  **Ratios can be expressed using the word "to":** \$3 \text{ to } 4$
3.  **Ratios can be expressed as a [fraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fraction_%28mathematics%29):** $\frac{3}{4}$

> **Crucial Fact:** Do not let the different formatting fool you. **The ratio "3 to 4" is mathematically equivalent to the fraction 3/4.** It is exactly the same concept just wearing a different hat.

### Making Things Bigger or Smaller: Equivalent Ratios
What if you want to make lemonade for the whole [neighborhood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neighbourhood)? You need to scale up your recipe without ruining the taste. You need an **equivalent ratio**.

**Equivalent ratios are ratios expressing the identical relationship between two quantities.** 
How do we find them? It is wonderfully simple:
*   **[Multiplying](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication) both terms of a ratio by the same non-zero number produces an equivalent ratio.** (e.g., multiply 3:4 by 2, and you get 6:8).
*   **[Dividing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_%28mathematics%29) both terms of a ratio by the same non-zero number produces an equivalent ratio.** (e.g., divide 30:40 by 10, and you get back to 3:4).

### Rates and Unit Rates
Now, what happens when we compare things that aren't measured in the same way? 
**A [rate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rate_%28mathematics%29) is a special type of ratio comparing two quantities with different [units of measure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_of_measurement).** If you drive 100 [miles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mile) in 2 [hours](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hour), that's a rate because miles and hours are fundamentally different units.

But "100 miles in 2 hours" is clunky. We usually want to know what happens in *one* hour. 
**A [unit rate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rate_%28mathematics%29) is a rate in which the second quantity in the comparison is exactly one unit.** 

> **How to find it:** **To calculate a unit rate from a general rate, divide the first quantity by the second quantity.** 
> *Example: 100 miles $\div$ 2 hours = 50 miles per 1 hour (50 mph).*

This is everywhere in daily life. Have you ever looked closely at [price tags](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Price_tag) at the grocery store? You are looking at a **unit price**, which **is a specific type of unit rate measuring the cost of one single item or unit of measure.** It’s how you know if the giant jar of [peanut butter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peanut_butter) is actually a better deal than the small one!

## 2. Drawing the Math: Visualizing Proportionality

Human beings are highly [visual](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visual_perception) creatures. Sometimes staring at fractions is tough, so we draw pictures to make the numbers concrete. We have two fantastic tools for this:

**1. The Tape Diagram**
Imagine snapping flat, rectangular pieces of [Lego](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lego) together. **A tape diagram is a visual model using [rectangles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle) to represent ratios.** If the ratio of boys to girls in a class is 2:3, you draw 2 blocks for boys and 3 blocks for girls. Furthermore, **a tape diagram is a visual model using rectangles to represent proportional relationships.** It allows students to visually see the parts making up the whole.

**2. The Double Number Line**
Imagine two trains running on parallel tracks. **A double number line is a visual model showing equivalent ratios along two parallel [number lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_line) scaled differently.** 
For example, the top line tracks "Miles" (0, 50, 100, 150) and the bottom line exactly tracks "Hours" (0, 1, 2, 3). You can just look up and down to find the equivalent ratio!

## 3. When Ratios Lock Together: Proportions

When you set two identical ratios equal to each other, you have built a [proportion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proportionality_%28mathematics%29). By definition, **a proportion is an [algebraic equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equation) stating that two distinct ratios are equal.** (For instance, $\frac{3}{4} = \frac{6}{8}$).

When two things are locked into this kind of perfect harmony, we say they hold a proportional relationship. Precisely stated: **Two quantities hold a proportional relationship if the ratio of one quantity to the other remains constant.**

### The Constant of Proportionality ($k$)
If we are looking at how a [dependent variable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dependent_and_independent_variables) ($y$, like money earned) changes based on an [independent variable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dependent_and_independent_variables) ($x$, like hours worked), we can find a magic number that links them. 

**The constant of proportionality is the constant ratio of the dependent variable to the independent variable.** 
If you make \$15 every single hour, \$15 is your constant. In [algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra), we write this perfectly as **the linear equation $y = kx$, where the [variable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variable_%28mathematics%29) $k$ represents the constant of proportionality.**

### Graphing the Relationship
If you plot a true proportional relationship on a piece of [graph paper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_paper), two things must mathematically happen every single time:
1.  **A [graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_of_a_function) representing a proportional relationship always passes through the [origin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Origin_%28mathematics%29).** Why? Because if you work zero hours, you earn zero dollars! 
2.  **A graph representing a proportional relationship is always a [straight line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_%28geometry%29).** The rate never bends; it is perfectly constant.

> **Fun Fact:** **The origin on a [Cartesian coordinate plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system) is the point (0,0).** It is the absolute ground zero of the mathematical universe!

## 4. Solving Proportions: Cross-Multiplication and Scale

Often in math, we are given three pieces of a proportion and asked to find the missing fourth piece. 

First, the golden rule of setup: **Setting up a proportion requires aligning matching units in the [numerators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fraction_%28mathematics%29%23Numerator) and matching units in the denominators.** If "Miles" are on top on the left side of the [equals sign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equals_sign), "Miles" absolutely *must* be on top on the right side. 

How do we solve it? We use a brilliant algebraic trick.
**Cross-multiplication is an algebraic method used to solve for an [unknown value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variable_%28mathematics%29) in a proportion.** 

Why does cross-multiplication work? It’s not magic; it’s a mathematical certainty! **The cross products of two equivalent fractions are always equal to each other.** 
*Try it: $\frac{3}{4} = \frac{6}{8}$. Multiply across the diagonals. \$3 \times 8 = 24$. And \$4 \times 6 = 24$. It works flawlessly!*

### Building Worlds: The Scale Factor
Architects and [mapmakers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartography) use proportions to shrink the world down onto paper. They do this using a **[scale factor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scale_factor)**, which **is the ratio of a length on a scale drawing to the corresponding length on the actual object.** 

Because a [map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Map) and the real world are proportional, **scale factors can be used within proportional equations to calculate actual [dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension) from a model.**

## 5. Percents: The Universal Translator

Comparing fractions like $\frac{7}{8}$ and $\frac{4}{5}$ in your head is obnoxious. Wouldn't it be easier if every fraction in the world just agreed to use the exact same denominator? 

That is exactly what a [percent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Percentage) is. **A percent represents a specific ratio whose second term is exactly the number 100.** In fact, language tells us this secret plainly: **The English word "percent" literally translates to "[per one hundred](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Percentage)".**

### Conversions: Fluency Between Formats
To be a master of math, you must be able to fluidly convert numbers between [decimals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal), fractions, and percents. 

| Operation | The Rule | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Decimal to Percent** | **To convert a decimal number to a percent, multiply the decimal number by 100.** | \$0.45 \times 100 = 45\%$ |
| **Percent to Decimal** | **To convert a percent value to a decimal number, divide the percent value by 100.** | \$82\% \div 100 = 0.82$ |
| **Fraction to Percent** | **To convert a fraction to a percent, divide the numerator by the denominator and multiply the resulting decimal by 100.** | $\frac{3}{4} = 0.75 \rightarrow 75\%$ |

### Setting Up Percent Problems
Every percent problem in the world can be solved with one beautifully simple proportional framework: **A percent problem can be solved by setting the percent value over 100 equal to the part over the whole.**

$$\frac{\text{Part}}{\text{Whole}} = \frac{\%}{100}$$

If you just need a quick [calculation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculation), you can use the algebraic shortcut: **To find a specific percent of a given number, convert the percent to a decimal and multiply by the given number.** (e.g., To find 20% of 50, multiply \$0.20 \times 50 = 10$).

## 6. Real World Proportions: Percent Change and Money

The world isn't static; prices and [values](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Value_%28economics%29) constantly fluctuate. We measure this movement using [percent change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relative_change_and_difference). The key here is to always compare the change to where you *started*.

*   **Percent increase is calculated by dividing the absolute amount of increase by the original amount and multiplying by 100.** 
*   **Percent decrease is calculated by dividing the absolute amount of decrease by the original amount and multiplying by 100.**

### The Retail Dance: Markups and Markdowns
Every time you walk into a [store](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Retail), you are participating in a web of percents:
*   The store bought the [shirt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shirt) for cheap, but they charge you more. **A [markup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Markup_%28business%29) is a price increase added to the cost price of an item to determine the final selling price.**
*   Then, it goes on sale! **A markdown is a price reduction from the original price of an item calculated using a percentage.** 

### Making Money with Money: Simple Interest
Finally, let's talk about banking. When you lend out [money](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Money) (or borrow it), you charge [rent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Renting) on that money. That rent is called interest. 

> **The Golden Formula:**
> **Simple interest is mathematically calculated using the [formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formula) Interest equals Principal times Rate times Time.** 
> $$I = P \times R \times T$$

*   **$I$** = Interest earned/paid
*   **$P$** = Principal (the original chunk of money)
*   **$R$** = Rate (the percent)
*   **$T$** = Time (usually in [years](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Year))

**Warning:** Don't let the formula trap you! **In the simple interest formula, the rate must be expressed as a decimal or fraction before multiplying.** If your interest rate is 5%, you must plug it into the formula as \$0.05$.

### The Big Picture

Whether you are drawing **tape diagrams**, mapping distances with a **scale factor**, hunting for a cheap **unit price** on coffee, or calculating the **simple interest** on a loan, you are doing the exact same mathematical magic: you are expressing how two quantities relate to one another. Master these comparisons, and you master the way the numerical world operates.
