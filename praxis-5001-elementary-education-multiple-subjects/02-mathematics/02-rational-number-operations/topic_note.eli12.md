# The Joy of Rational Numbers: Slicing Up the Universe

Hello! Welcome to the awesome world of numbers. Have you ever noticed that the real world isn't just made of perfect, whole things? Sure, you can have one whole video game controller or two whole sneakers. But what about when you eat half a pizza? Or what if you owe your friend three dollars for a snack? To talk about the real world, we need a special family of numbers. 

Simply put, **a rational number is any number capable of being expressed as a fraction of two integers.** Integers are just whole numbers, like 5, 0, or -2. So, if you can write a number as a fraction with a top integer and a bottom integer, it belongs in this family! Because of this rule, **integers, fractions, and terminating or repeating decimals are all examples of rational numbers.** The number 5 is rational because you can write it as 5/1. A decimal like 0.75 is rational because it's just 3/4 in disguise.

> **The Golden Rule of the Denominator**
> There is only one major rule to remember when making these fractions: **the denominator of a fraction representing a rational number cannot be zero.** You can never have a zero on the bottom. Why? Because **division by zero is undefined in mathematics.** Imagine trying to divide a pizza into zero slices. You can't do it! The whole idea just breaks down.

Before we start doing math with these numbers, let's meet two "invisible" helpers that keep our math balanced. 
First, **zero is the additive identity for rational numbers.** This is a fancy way of saying that **adding zero to a rational number does not change the mathematical value of the rational number.** 

*Example: Adding Zero*
1. Take a rational number: 8
2. Add zero to it: 8 + 0
3. The value stays exactly the same: 8

Next, **one is the multiplicative identity for rational numbers.** This means that **multiplying a rational number by one does not change the mathematical value of the rational number.** 

*Example: Multiplying by One*
1. Take a rational number: 12
2. Multiply it by one: 12 * 1
3. The value stays exactly the same: 12

## The Dance of Signs: Positives and Negatives

Rational numbers can be positive (like earning points in a game) or negative (like losing points). Let's look at how they interact!

### Addition and Subtraction
When you add numbers together, their signs tell you what to do. 
**Adding two rational numbers with the same sign results in a sum that has the exact same sign as the addends.** If you earn 5 points and earn 3 more points, you have 8 positive points! If you lose 2 points and lose 4 points, you are down 6 points (negative).

But what if the signs are different, like a tug-of-war? **Adding two rational numbers with different signs requires subtracting the smaller absolute value from the larger absolute value.** (Absolute value just means looking at the number without its positive or negative sign). And who wins the tug-of-war? **When adding two rational numbers with different signs, the sum takes the sign of the addend with the larger absolute value.**

*Example: Adding different signs (-10 + 4)*
1. Find the absolute values (just the numbers without the signs): 10 and 4.
2. Subtract the smaller absolute value from the larger absolute value: 10 - 4 = 6.
3. Look back at the original problem to see which number had the larger absolute value. The number -10 has the larger absolute value.
4. Give your answer the same sign as that larger number. Since -10 is negative, the final answer is -6.

Now, let's look at subtraction. Here is a cool trick: **subtracting a rational number is mathematically equivalent to adding the additive inverse of that specific rational number.** 

What is an additive inverse? It’s just the opposite version of your number! **The additive inverse of a positive rational number is the negative counterpart of that specific number** (and the reverse is true, too). Because they are perfect opposites, **the sum of any rational number and the additive inverse of that identical rational number is always zero.** 

*Example: Subtracting by using the additive inverse (7 - (-3))*
1. Identify that this is a subtraction problem, so we will change it to adding the additive inverse.
2. Find the additive inverse of -3, which is simply positive 3.
3. Change the subtraction sign to an addition sign.
4. Add the numbers together normally: 7 + 3 = 10.

### Multiplication and Division
Multiplication and division have a very clear, simple set of rules for positive and negative signs. 
*   **The product of two rational numbers with the exact same sign is always a positive number.** 
*   **The quotient of two rational numbers with the exact same sign is always a positive number.** 
(If the signs match, the answer is always positive. Yes, even a negative times a negative equals a positive!)

But if the signs are mixed up and disagree with each other:
*   **The product of two rational numbers with different signs is always a negative number.**
*   **The quotient of two rational numbers with different signs is always a negative number.**

| Operation Type | Same Signs | Different Signs |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Multiplication** | Always Positive | Always Negative |
| **Division** | Always Positive | Always Negative |

## The Machinery of Algorithms: Fractions and Decimals

Rational numbers usually show up as fractions or decimals. Here is how you handle both!

### The Way of the Fraction
Fractions are like pieces of a pizza. 
If you want to combine pizza slices, the slices have to be cut to the exact same size. Because of this, **adding and subtracting fractions requires finding a common denominator before performing the operation.** 

*Example: Adding Fractions (1/2 + 1/4)*
1. Notice that the denominators (the bottom numbers, 2 and 4) are different.
2. Find a common denominator. Both 2 and 4 can fit into 4.
3. Change 1/2 into an equivalent fraction with 4 on the bottom by multiplying the top and bottom by 2. This makes it 2/4.
4. Add the numerators (the top numbers) together: 2 + 1 = 3.
5. Keep the denominator exactly the same: 4. The final answer is 3/4.

Multiplication and division are different. You don't need the pieces to be the same size first! Therefore, **multiplying and dividing fractions does not require finding a common denominator.**

*Example: Multiplying Fractions (2/3 * 4/5)*
1. **When multiplying fractions, the numerators are multiplied together to form the new numerator.** Multiply the top numbers: 2 * 4 = 8.
2. **When multiplying fractions, the denominators are multiplied together to form the new denominator.** Multiply the bottom numbers: 3 * 5 = 15.
3. Write your final fraction: 8/15.

Division is just multiplication in disguise! **Dividing by a fraction is mathematically equivalent to multiplying by the reciprocal of that specific fraction.** How do you find a reciprocal? **The reciprocal of a fraction is formed by swapping the numerator and the denominator of the fraction.** 

*Example: Dividing Fractions (1/2 / 3/4)*
1. Find the reciprocal of the second fraction (3/4) by swapping the top and bottom to get 4/3.
2. Change your division sign to a multiplication sign.
3. Multiply the top numerators together: 1 * 4 = 4.
4. Multiply the bottom denominators together: 2 * 3 = 6.
5. Write your final answer: 4/6. 

### The Way of the Decimal
Decimals are just fractions that use tens, hundreds, and thousands.
First rule: **To add or subtract decimals, the numbers must be vertically aligned by their decimal points before performing the operation.** You have to line up the dots so you are adding tenths to tenths!

*Example: Multiplying Decimals (0.2 * 0.3)*
1. Ignore the decimals for a moment and multiply the numbers like normal whole numbers: 2 * 3 = 6.
2. Look back at the original problem. Count the decimal places in the first factor (0.2 has 1 place).
3. Count the decimal places in the second factor (0.3 has 1 place).
4. **When multiplying decimals, the total number of decimal places in the product equals the sum of the decimal places in the factors.** Add them together: 1 + 1 = 2 total decimal places.
5. Place the decimal point in your answer so it has exactly 2 decimal places: 0.06.

Division is a little different. **When dividing a decimal by a decimal, the decimal point in the divisor must be moved to the right to create a whole number.** But to keep the problem fair, you have to do the same thing to both sides! **Moving the decimal point in the divisor requires moving the decimal point in the dividend by the exact same number of places to the right.** 

*Example: Dividing Decimals (2.5 / 0.5)*
1. Look at the divisor (0.5) and move the decimal point one place to the right to make it a whole number: 5.
2. Move the decimal point in the dividend (2.5) exactly one place to the right to match, making it: 25.
3. Divide the new whole numbers: 25 / 5.
4. The final answer is 5.

## The Magic of Reversibility: Inverse Operations

Math is like a magical two-way street. **An inverse operation can be used to mathematically reverse the effect of another operation.** You can always un-do what you just did!

*   **Addition and subtraction are inverse operations.**
*   **Multiplication and division are inverse operations.**

We can use this trick to double-check our homework. **The inverse relationship between addition and subtraction can be used to check the accuracy of a calculated sum or difference.** 

*Example: Checking Subtraction with Addition*
1. Let's say you calculate that 10 - 4 = 6. 
2. **The inverse relationship between addition and subtraction dictates that if value A plus value B equals value C, then value C minus value B must equal value A.** So, take your answer (6) and the number you subtracted (4).
3. Add them together: 6 + 4.
4. If the sum matches your starting number (10), your math is 100% correct!

It works for multiplication, too. **The inverse relationship between multiplication and division dictates that if value A times value B equals value C, then value C divided by value B must equal value A.** 

We also use these tools to solve mysteries! **The inverse relationship between multiplication and division can be used to solve for an unknown value in a mathematical equation.** By doing the opposite math, **using inverse operations allows for the isolation of an unknown variable in an algebraic equation.**

*Example: Finding an Unknown Variable (3 * X = 12)*
1. Look at the operation happening to the unknown letter X. It is being multiplied by 3.
2. Choose the inverse operation of multiplication, which is division.
3. Divide both sides of the equation by 3. (12 / 3 = 4).
4. Now X is totally isolated by itself! X = 4.

## Putting It All Together: Multistep Problems

Most real-world math has more than one step. **Multistep mathematical problems require applying the standard order of operations to determine the correct sequence of calculations.** Without this rule, everyone would calculate things in a different order and get different answers!

> **The Standard Order of Operations**
> **The standard order of operations dictates evaluating parentheses first, followed by exponents, then multiplication and division from left to right, and finally addition and subtraction from left to right.** 

*Example: Multistep Order of Operations ( 5 + (3 * 2) )*
1. Look for parentheses first. We have (3 * 2).
2. Calculate the math inside the parentheses: 3 * 2 = 6.
3. Rewrite the problem with your new number: 5 + 6.
4. Do the addition step: 5 + 6 = 11.

Before we can even do the math, we have to translate the English words into numbers. **Translating real-world situations into mathematical expressions requires mapping specific vocabulary words to corresponding operations.** Listen for these clues:
*   **The word total or sum in a mathematical word problem typically indicates the addition operation.**
*   **The word difference or the phrase "how many more" in a mathematical word problem typically indicates the subtraction operation.**
*   **The word product or times in a mathematical word problem typically indicates the multiplication operation.**
*   **The word quotient or per in a mathematical word problem typically indicates the division operation.**

## The Final Frontier: Interpreting the Remainder

Now we step out of the classroom and into reality. **Division problems involving real-world contexts often result in a remainder that must be interpreted based on the specific situation.** When you have leftovers, your brain has to decide what to do with them. Let's look at three ways to handle a remainder.

### 1. The Discarded Remainder
**In some real-world division problems, the remainder must be completely discarded.** 

*Example: Buying Video Game Skins*
1. Let's say you have 10 dollars, and cool game skins cost 3 dollars each.
2. Divide your money by the cost: 10 / 3 = 3 with a remainder of 1.
3. **When determining how many full items can be purchased with a set amount of money, the remainder of the division operation is discarded.** The store won't sell you 1/3 of a skin! 
4. **When discarding a remainder in a division problem, the final answer is solely the whole number quotient.** So, your final answer is exactly 3 skins (and you keep your 1 dollar leftover).

### 2. The Rounded-Up Remainder
**In some real-world division problems, the remainder requires rounding the quotient up to the next consecutive whole number.** 

*Example: Minivans for a Trip*
1. You have 13 players on a soccer team, and each minivan holds 5 players. 
2. Divide the players by the seats: 13 / 5 = 2 with a remainder of 3.
3. If you only rent 2 minivans, 3 players get left at home! 
4. **When determining the number of containers or vehicles needed to hold a specific quantity, any remainder requires rounding up the quotient to the next whole container or vehicle.** So, you must round up to 3 minivans.

### 3. The Continuous Remainder
**In some real-world division problems, the remainder is expressed as a fraction or decimal to represent a precise shared continuous quantity.** Continuous just means something you can safely chop up into smaller pieces, like a giant cookie!

*Example: Sharing Pizza*
1. You have 5 giant pizza slices to share equally between 2 friends.
2. Divide the slices by the friends: 5 / 2 = 2 with a remainder of 1.
3. **When dividing a continuous quantity like food or fabric equally, the remainder is typically expressed as a fraction or decimal.** You can easily cut that last slice in half!
4. Write your answer as a fraction: Each friend gets 2 and 1/2 slices.
