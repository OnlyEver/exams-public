Imagine a postal system that guarantees the delivery of a million-page manuscript by mailing each page in a separate envelope, yet somehow ensuring the recipient reads them in perfect numerical order. Now imagine an entirely different system that hurls thousands of postcards at a recipient every second, not caring if a few blow away in the wind, because the sheer volume alone conveys the message. 

In the physical world, these two systems seem absurd. In [computer networking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_network), they are the foundational engines of global communication. 

Whenever a computer communicates, it utilizes the [Open Systems Interconnection (OSI) model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OSI_model). While the lower layers handle electrical signals and [IP addresses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IP_address), **[TCP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transmission_Control_Protocol) and [UDP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/User_Datagram_Protocol) both operate at the [Transport layer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transport_layer) of the [Open Systems Interconnection (OSI) model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OSI_model)**. Their job is to govern *how* data travels. Furthermore, to ensure data reaches the correct application—whether it is a [web browser](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Web_browser), an [email client](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Email_client), or a [video game](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Video_game)—**[TCP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transmission_Control_Protocol) and [UDP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/User_Datagram_Protocol) both use [port numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Port_%28computer_networking%29) to direct network traffic to specific applications**.

![Network layering models illustrate how protocols like TCP and UDP operate at the Transport layer to manage data delivery between applications.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Internet_layering.svg)

> **The [Network Socket](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Network_socket)**: If an [IP address](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IP_address) is the street address of an apartment building, the [port number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Port_%28computer_networking%29) is the specific apartment number. **An [IP address](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IP_address) combined with a specific [port number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Port_%28computer_networking%29) creates a [network socket](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Network_socket)** (e.g., `192.168.1.50:443`). This combination allows a single computer to maintain thousands of simultaneous, distinct conversations.

To succeed as an [IT professional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information_technology), you must master the mechanics of these two [protocols](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communication_protocol) and memorize the critical [port numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Port_%28computer_networking%29) they utilize. These are not arbitrary numbers to be crammed for an exam; they are the literal diagnostic coordinates you will use every day to troubleshoot a failing [server](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Server_%28computing%29), a disconnected [mapped drive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drive_mapping), or a silent [email client](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Email_client).

## The Tale of Two Protocols: TCP vs. UDP

The [transport layer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transport_layer) fundamentally offers you a choice: Do you need perfect reliability, or do you need absolute speed?

### Transmission Control Protocol (TCP): The Meticulous Courier

**[Transmission Control Protocol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transmission_Control_Protocol) (TCP) is a [connection-oriented](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connection-oriented_communication) transport protocol.** It is designed for applications where every single [bit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bit) of data must arrive perfectly intact. 

Because it is [connection-oriented](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connection-oriented_communication), **[TCP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transmission_Control_Protocol) requires a formal setup sequence before data transmission begins.** It establishes this connection through a strict mechanism: **[TCP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transmission_Control_Protocol) uses a [three-way handshake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Handshake_%28computing%29) to establish a network connection.** The initiator sends a SYN (synchronize) [packet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Network_packet), the receiver replies with a SYN-ACK (synchronize-acknowledge), and the initiator finalizes with an ACK (acknowledge). Only then does the data flow.

![TCP requires a three-way handshake (SYN, SYN-ACK, ACK) to formally establish a reliable connection before data transmission begins.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Three-way-handshake-example.gif)

Once the connection is live, **[TCP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transmission_Control_Protocol) provides reliable data delivery across a network.** It achieves this through several brilliant engineering mechanisms:
*   **Acknowledgments:** **[TCP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transmission_Control_Protocol) uses [acknowledgments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acknowledgement_%28data_networks%29) to verify the successful receipt of data [packets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Network_packet).** If the sender does not receive an acknowledgment within a specific timeframe, it assumes the data was lost.
*   **Retransmission:** Because of those [acknowledgments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acknowledgement_%28data_networks%29), **[TCP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transmission_Control_Protocol) can [retransmit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Retransmission_%28data_networks%29) lost or corrupted data [packets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Network_packet)**, guaranteeing the file arrives completely.
*   **Sequencing:** [Packets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Network_packet) often take different paths across the [internet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internet) and arrive out of order. **[TCP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transmission_Control_Protocol) performs sequencing to ensure [packets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Network_packet) are reassembled in the correct order.**
*   **Flow Control:** If a fast [server](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Server_%28computing%29) is overwhelming a slow [smartphone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smartphone), **[TCP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transmission_Control_Protocol) performs [flow control](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flow_control_%28data%29) to manage the rate of data transmission between devices**, preventing [network congestion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Network_congestion).

When you download a [PDF](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PDF) or load a [web page](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Web_page), you are using [TCP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transmission_Control_Protocol). If a single [packet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Network_packet) was missing from a software update, the [executable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Executable) file would be corrupted. [TCP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transmission_Control_Protocol) prevents this.

### User Datagram Protocol (UDP): The Unfiltered Firehose

What if you are on a live voice call, and a single [millisecond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Millisecond) of audio gets dropped? You do not want the [protocol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communication_protocol) to pause the entire conversation, request the sender to [retransmit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Retransmission_%28data_networks%29) the lost audio, and play it out of sync three seconds later. You simply want the [protocol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communication_protocol) to keep moving. 

This is where [UDP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/User_Datagram_Protocol) shines. **[User Datagram Protocol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/User_Datagram_Protocol) (UDP) is a [connectionless](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connectionless_communication) transport protocol.** It operates on the philosophy of "fire and forget."

*   **No Setup:** **[UDP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/User_Datagram_Protocol) does not require a formal setup sequence before sending data.** There is no [handshake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Handshake_%28computing%29); it just starts transmitting.
*   **No Guarantees:** **[UDP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/User_Datagram_Protocol) provides unreliable data delivery without delivery guarantees.** "Unreliable" is not an insult here; it is a technical term meaning the [protocol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communication_protocol) does not track the data's journey.
*   **No Tracking:** **[UDP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/User_Datagram_Protocol) does not use [acknowledgments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acknowledgement_%28data_networks%29) to verify the successful receipt of data [packets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Network_packet).** 
*   **No Redundancy:** Because there are no [acknowledgments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acknowledgement_%28data_networks%29), **[UDP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/User_Datagram_Protocol) cannot [retransmit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Retransmission_%28data_networks%29) lost or corrupted data [packets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Network_packet) automatically.**

By stripping away sequencing, [acknowledgments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acknowledgement_%28data_networks%29), and [flow control](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flow_control_%28data%29), **[UDP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/User_Datagram_Protocol) generates lower network overhead compared to [TCP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transmission_Control_Protocol).** This makes it incredibly fast. Consequently, **[UDP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/User_Datagram_Protocol) is frequently utilized for [real-time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real-time_computing) applications like [voice over IP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voice_over_IP) and [video streaming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Streaming_media)**, where speed is paramount and occasional [packet loss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Packet_loss) is imperceptible to the human eye or ear.

![UDP is ideal for real-time applications like Voice over IP (VoIP), where maintaining uninterrupted communication speed is more important than perfect data reliability.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Voip-typical.gif)

## The IT Professional's Map: Common Ports and Protocols

As a support technician, you will frequently hear users say, "The [internet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internet) is broken." The [internet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internet) is rarely broken. Usually, a specific service operating on a specific [port](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Port_%28computer_networking%29) is blocked by a [firewall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Firewall_%28computing%29) or misconfigured. 

To troubleshoot, you must know what traffic belongs on what [port](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Port_%28computer_networking%29).

### 1. Core Network Foundations

Before a computer can browse the web or send an email, it must configure itself on the [local network](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_area_network) and find its way around.

*   **[DHCP (Dynamic Host Configuration Protocol)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynamic_Host_Configuration_Protocol):** How does a [laptop](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laptop) magically get an [IP address](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IP_address) when it joins a [Wi-Fi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wi-Fi) network? **[DHCP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynamic_Host_Configuration_Protocol) automatically assigns [IP addresses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IP_address) and configuration settings to network devices.** Because the [client](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Client_%28computing%29) doesn't yet have an [IP address](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IP_address), it relies on broad, [connectionless](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connectionless_communication) [UDP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/User_Datagram_Protocol) [broadcasts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Broadcasting_%28networking%29). **[DHCP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynamic_Host_Configuration_Protocol) operates over [UDP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/User_Datagram_Protocol) port 67 for [server](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Server_%28computing%29) traffic** and **operates over [UDP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/User_Datagram_Protocol) port 68 for [client](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Client_%28computing%29) traffic**.

![A typical DHCP session uses UDP broadcasts to automatically discover the server and assign IP configuration settings to new clients on a network.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/DHCP_session.svg)

*   **[DNS (Domain Name System)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_Name_System):** Computers route traffic using [IP addresses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IP_address), but humans use names like `google.com`. **[DNS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_Name_System) resolves human-readable [domain names](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_name) into [IP addresses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IP_address).** For lightning-fast lookups, **[DNS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_Name_System) primarily uses [UDP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/User_Datagram_Protocol) port 53 for standard [client](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Client_%28computing%29) queries.** However, when massive amounts of directory data must be synchronized reliably between [DNS servers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Name_server), **[DNS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_Name_System) uses [TCP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transmission_Control_Protocol) port 53 for [server](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Server_%28computing%29) [zone transfers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNS_zone_transfer).** 

![DNS resolves human-readable domain names into machine-routable IP addresses, primarily utilizing UDP port 53 for rapid client queries.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/DNS_Architecture.svg)

### 2. The Web Browsing Experience

When a user types a [URL](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/URL) into their [browser](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Web_browser), they rely on Hypertext Transfer Protocols.

*   **[HTTP (Hypertext Transfer Protocol)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypertext_Transfer_Protocol):** **[HTTP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypertext_Transfer_Protocol) transmits unencrypted [web page](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Web_page) data across the [internet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internet).** Because the data must arrive flawlessly, **[HTTP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypertext_Transfer_Protocol) operates over [TCP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transmission_Control_Protocol) port 80.** However, because it is unencrypted, anyone [eavesdropping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eavesdropping) on the network can read the traffic.
*   **[HTTPS (Hypertext Transfer Protocol Secure)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HTTPS):** To solve the security flaw of [HTTP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypertext_Transfer_Protocol), [HTTPS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HTTPS) was created. **[HTTPS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HTTPS) transmits encrypted [web page](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Web_page) data to protect user privacy**, securing [passwords](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Password) and banking details. **[HTTPS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HTTPS) operates over [TCP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transmission_Control_Protocol) port 443.** 

### 3. Remote Management and Access

[IT professionals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information_technology) rarely walk to a physical [server room](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Server_room) to manage equipment. We use remote management protocols. 

*   **[Telnet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telnet):** One of the oldest protocols, **[Telnet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telnet) provides unencrypted remote [command-line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Command-line_interface) access to network devices.** Because it sends [passwords](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Password) in [cleartext](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cleartext), it is highly vulnerable to interception. **[Telnet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telnet) operates over [TCP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transmission_Control_Protocol) port 23.** 
*   **[SSH (Secure Shell)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Secure_Shell):** This is the modern, secure replacement for [Telnet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telnet). **[SSH](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Secure_Shell) provides encrypted remote [command-line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Command-line_interface) access to network devices**, ensuring all administrative traffic is hidden from eavesdroppers. **[SSH](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Secure_Shell) operates over [TCP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transmission_Control_Protocol) port 22.**

![An IT professional logging into a remote network device using an SSH client, providing a secure, encrypted command-line session over TCP port 22.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/OpenWrtPuTTY.png)

*   **[RDP (Remote Desktop Protocol)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Remote_Desktop_Protocol):** Sometimes you need more than a command line; you need a [mouse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_mouse) and a [desktop environment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Desktop_environment). **[RDP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Remote_Desktop_Protocol) provides a [graphical user interface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graphical_user_interface) to connect to another computer over a network.** It is heavily used in [Windows](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microsoft_Windows) environments. **[RDP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Remote_Desktop_Protocol) operates over [TCP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transmission_Control_Protocol) port 3389.**

### 4. The Mailroom: Sending and Receiving Email

Email relies on a surprisingly fragmented architecture. Sending mail uses one protocol, while retrieving it uses entirely different ones.

*   **[SMTP (Simple Mail Transfer Protocol)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simple_Mail_Transfer_Protocol):** This is the outbound mail worker. **[SMTP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simple_Mail_Transfer_Protocol) is used to send email messages from a [client](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Client_%28computing%29) to a [server](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Message_transfer_agent).** Furthermore, **[SMTP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simple_Mail_Transfer_Protocol) is used to route email messages between different email servers** across the [internet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internet). **[SMTP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simple_Mail_Transfer_Protocol) operates over [TCP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transmission_Control_Protocol) port 25.**

![The SMTP transfer model demonstrates how email is routed from a sender's client and transferred between mail servers over TCP port 25.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/SMTP-transfer-model.svg)

*   **[POP3 (Post Office Protocol version 3)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Post_Office_Protocol):** This is an older retrieval method. **[POP3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Post_Office_Protocol) downloads email messages from an email server to a local [client](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Email_client).** Once downloaded, the emails are typically deleted from the [server](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Server_%28computing%29), meaning they only exist on that single device. **[POP3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Post_Office_Protocol) operates over [TCP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transmission_Control_Protocol) port 110.**
*   **[IMAP (Internet Message Access Protocol)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internet_Message_Access_Protocol):** This is the modern standard for email retrieval. **[IMAP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internet_Message_Access_Protocol) synchronizes email messages across multiple client devices and the [server](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Message_transfer_agent).** If you read an email on your phone, [IMAP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internet_Message_Access_Protocol) ensures it marks as "read" on your [laptop](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laptop). **[IMAP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internet_Message_Access_Protocol) operates over [TCP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transmission_Control_Protocol) port 143.**

### 5. File Transfers and Network Storage

Moving files across a network securely and efficiently is a core requirement of any corporate environment.

*   **[FTP (File Transfer Protocol)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File_Transfer_Protocol):** As the name implies, **[FTP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File_Transfer_Protocol) transfers files between network systems.** Uniquely, [FTP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File_Transfer_Protocol) requires two distinct channels to function. **[FTP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File_Transfer_Protocol) uses [TCP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transmission_Control_Protocol) port 20 for data transfer**, and **[FTP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File_Transfer_Protocol) uses [TCP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transmission_Control_Protocol) port 21 for control signals** (the commands that tell the [server](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Server_%28computing%29) to list directories or initiate the download). 

![The File Transfer Protocol (FTP) architecture relies on two separate TCP channels: one for control signals (port 21) and one for actual data transfer (port 20).](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/FTP_model.png)

*   **[SMB (Server Message Block)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Server_Message_Block):** This is the backbone of [Windows](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microsoft_Windows) file sharing. **[SMB](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Server_Message_Block) provides shared access to files, [printers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Printer_%28computing%29), and [serial ports](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Serial_port) on a [local network](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_area_network).** When a user maps a "Z: Drive" to a corporate [server](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File_server), they are using [SMB](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Server_Message_Block). **Modern [SMB](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Server_Message_Block) operates directly over [TCP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transmission_Control_Protocol) port 445.**

![In Windows environments, mapping a network drive utilizes the SMB protocol over TCP port 445 to securely access shared files and directories.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Map_Network_Drive.PNG)

*   **[AFP (Apple Filing Protocol)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apple_Filing_Protocol):** The Apple equivalent of [SMB](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Server_Message_Block). **[AFP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apple_Filing_Protocol) offers file services specifically for [macOS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/macOS) networks.** **[AFP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apple_Filing_Protocol) operates over [TCP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transmission_Control_Protocol) port 548.**
*   **[NetBIOS over TCP/IP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NetBIOS_over_TCP/IP):** Before modern [DNS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_Name_System) and [SMB](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Server_Message_Block) became the standard, older Windows networks relied on [NetBIOS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NetBIOS). It handles distinct tasks across three ports: **[NetBIOS over TCP/IP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NetBIOS_over_TCP/IP) provides name registration and resolution services on [UDP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/User_Datagram_Protocol) port 137**, it **provides [datagram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Datagram) distribution services on [UDP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/User_Datagram_Protocol) port 138**, and it **provides session services on [TCP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transmission_Control_Protocol) port 139.** 

### 6. Network Management and Directory Services

Managing hundreds of users and [switches](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Network_switch) requires specialized, automated protocols.

*   **[LDAP (Lightweight Directory Access Protocol)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lightweight_Directory_Access_Protocol):** When a user logs into a corporate computer, the machine must verify their credentials against a central database (like [Microsoft Active Directory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Active_Directory)). **[LDAP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lightweight_Directory_Access_Protocol) is used to query and modify distributed directory information services.** **[LDAP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lightweight_Directory_Access_Protocol) operates over [TCP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transmission_Control_Protocol) port 389.**
*   **[SNMP (Simple Network Management Protocol)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simple_Network_Management_Protocol):** Network administrators need to know if a [router](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Router_%28computing%29) is overheating or if a switch port is failing. **[SNMP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simple_Network_Management_Protocol) is used to collect and organize information about managed devices on [IP networks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internet_Protocol).** **[SNMP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simple_Network_Management_Protocol) agents receive standard queries on [UDP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/User_Datagram_Protocol) port 161**, while **[SNMP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simple_Network_Management_Protocol) network management stations receive trap messages on [UDP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/User_Datagram_Protocol) port 162** (alerts generated by the devices themselves).

![SNMP allows administrators to manage and monitor network devices using UDP port 161 for standard queries and UDP port 162 for automated trap alerts.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/SNMP_communication_principles_diagram.PNG)

*   **[SLP (Service Location Protocol)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Service_Location_Protocol):** To make life easier for users, **[SLP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Service_Location_Protocol) allows computers to find [local area network](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_area_network) services without prior configuration**, such as automatically discovering a shared [printer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Printer_%28computing%29). **[SLP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Service_Location_Protocol) operates over [TCP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transmission_Control_Protocol) port 427 and [UDP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/User_Datagram_Protocol) port 427.**
