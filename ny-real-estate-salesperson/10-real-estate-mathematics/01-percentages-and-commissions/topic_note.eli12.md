Imagine trading video games with your friends. Usually, there are rules about how many older games equal one new game. In real estate, people buy and sell houses, but the rules mostly revolve around percentages—kind of like slicing a giant pizza into very specific pieces. If you understand how the pizza gets sliced, you will understand exactly how money moves when someone buys a house! We are going to look at how the person helping sell the house gets paid, how banks charge for lending out money, and how houses can gain or lose value over time just like rare collectible cards.

## The Universal Engine: The T-Bar Method

Math in real estate is like a simple puzzle with three pieces. If you have two pieces, you can always find the third. You never have to memorize confusing formulas! 

To make this super easy, professionals use the **T-bar method**. The T-bar method is a visual math tool that places the partial amount on the top half of the T and the total amount and rate on the bottom half. 

Think of the "Part" as a few slices of pizza, the "Total" as the whole pizza, and the "Rate" as the percentage (like 50%).

| Part (The Slice) |
| :---: |
| **Total (The Whole) x Rate (The Percentage)** |

Here are the rules for this helpful tool. The horizontal line between top and bottom means "divide," and the vertical line between the two bottom numbers means "multiply." 
1. In the T-bar math method, the top number is always divided by the known bottom number to find the missing variable.
2. In the T-bar math method, the two bottom numbers are multiplied together to find the top partial amount.

Every single thing we are about to learn—from the money the agent makes to the extra money a bank charges—uses this exact same T-bar puzzle!

## The Physics of Getting Paid: Broker Commissions

When a real estate agent helps someone sell a house, they get a reward called a commission. A real estate commission is typically calculated as a percentage of the final property sales price. 

Using our T-bar tool, the "Total" is the sales price, the "Rate" is the commission percentage, and the "Part" is the commission money paid to the agent. This means the total commission amount equals the property sales price multiplied by the commission rate.

> **Calculating Total Commission:**
> Total Commission = Final Sales Price x Commission Rate

Let's do the math step-by-step. Imagine a house sells for \$1,500,000 and the negotiated commission rate is 6%.
1. Change the percentage into a decimal so it is easier to multiply: 6% becomes 0.06.
2. Multiply the sales price by the decimal: \$1,500,000 x 0.06.
3. The total commission is \$90,000.

What if you are missing a puzzle piece? You can easily work backward! 
* To calculate the sales price based on a known commission amount and a known commission rate, divide the total commission amount by the commission rate.
* To calculate the commission rate based on a known commission amount and a known sales price, divide the total commission amount by the sales price.

### The Commission Cascade: Splits and Co-Brokerage

Usually, the agent who lists the house doesn't get to keep that whole \$90,000. It is a team effort! 

A **co-brokerage commission split** distributes the total commission amount between the listing broker's firm (the team selling the house) and the selling broker's firm (the team helping the buyer). 

Let's calculate what happens if they share it evenly (50/50):
1. Take the total commission: \$90,000.
2. Multiply it by half (0.50): \$90,000 x 0.50.
3. The result: Each firm gets \$45,000.

But wait, you are just one player on your firm's team. You don't get the whole team's share! A salesperson's commission amount is calculated by multiplying the employing broker's share of the commission by the salesperson's agreed percentage split. 

Let's say your agreement says you get to keep 60% of what your firm makes on the deal.
1. Take your firm's share: \$45,000.
2. Change your split to a decimal: 60% becomes 0.60.
3. Multiply the firm's share by your split: \$45,000 x 0.60.
4. Your take-home pay is \$27,000. 

### Reverse Engineering: Net Amount to the Seller

The person selling the house usually doesn't care about the big starting price of the house. They only care about what they get to keep in their bank account. The net amount to the seller after commission equals the gross sales price minus the total commission amount. 

Sometimes, a seller will say, *"I need to walk away with exactly \$940,000 to buy my next house!"* How do you figure out what the starting price of the house needs to be when the commission takes a chunk out of it?

> **Calculating Required Sales Price:**
> Required Sales Price = Desired Net / (100% - Commission Rate)

To find the required sales price to yield a specific net amount to the seller after commission, divide the desired net amount by 100 percent minus the commission rate. 

Let's do the math if the seller needs \$940,000 and the commission is 6%:
1. Start with 100 percent and subtract the commission rate: 100% - 6% = 94%.
2. Turn 94% into a decimal: 0.94.
3. Divide the amount the seller wants by that decimal: \$940,000 / 0.94.
4. The required sales price must be exactly \$1,000,000.

## The Cost of Leverage: Interest, Points, and LTV

Most people don't have \$1,000,000 sitting in their piggy bank. They have to borrow money from a bank. 

**Interest** is the cost charged by a lender for borrowing money. Think of it like paying rent to use someone else's money. Simple annual interest is calculated by multiplying the outstanding loan principal (the amount you borrowed) by the annual interest rate. 

> **Simple Annual Interest Formula:**
> Annual Interest Amount = Outstanding Principal x Annual Interest Rate

Let's say a buyer borrows \$800,000 at a 5% interest rate. 
1. Change the interest rate to a decimal: 5% is 0.05.
2. Multiply the loan amount by the rate: \$800,000 x 0.05.
3. The simple annual interest is \$40,000 for the year.

Since people usually pay for their loans every month, we need to cut that yearly cost into monthly slices. To find the monthly interest amount on a loan, divide the annual interest amount by 12. 
1. Take the annual interest: \$40,000.
2. Divide by 12 months: \$40,000 / 12.
3. The monthly interest is about \$3,333.33.

You can use the T-bar method puzzle here too!
* To calculate the loan principal based on a known annual interest amount and interest rate, divide the annual interest amount by the annual interest rate.
* To calculate the annual interest rate based on a known annual interest amount and loan principal, divide the annual interest amount by the loan principal.

### Risk Mitigation: Loan-to-Value (LTV) Ratio

Banks want to make sure their money is safe if someone stops paying them back. To stay safe, they use the **Loan-to-Value ratio**, which expresses the mortgage loan amount as a percentage of the property value. 

The maximum permitted loan amount equals the property value multiplied by the lender's maximum Loan-to-Value ratio. 

Let's find the maximum loan for a \$1,000,000 house if the bank's maximum LTV is 80%:
1. Turn 80% into a decimal: 0.80.
2. Multiply the property value by the decimal: \$1,000,000 x 0.80.
3. The maximum loan the bank will give is \$800,000.

The buyer has to pay the rest of the money upfront in cash. The required down payment percentage for a property purchase equals 100 percent minus the Loan-to-Value ratio percentage.
1. Start with 100%.
2. Subtract the LTV percentage: 100% - 80%.
3. The required down payment is 20%.

There is a big rule here! How does the bank decide what the property is actually worth? The property value used in a Loan-to-Value percentage calculation is the lesser of the appraised property value or the agreed purchase price. The "appraised value" is what an expert judges it to be worth. The bank always picks the smaller number so they don't take on too much risk!

### Manipulating the Yield: Points and Origination Fees

Sometimes buyers want to lower their interest rate so their monthly "money rent" is cheaper. They can do this by paying a fee upfront. 

A **discount point** is an upfront fee paid to the lender at closing to lower the interest rate on a mortgage. Think of it like paying a bunch of coins to unlock a permanent discount in a video game! 

Here is the golden rule: One mortgage discount point equals one percent of the total loan amount. Keep in mind, mortgage points are calculated based on the loan amount rather than the property purchase price. 

> **Calculating Mortgage Points:**
> Dollar Cost of Points = Loan Amount x Number of Points (as a %)

Let's calculate the cost if a buyer gets an \$800,000 loan and buys 2 discount points.
1. Turn 2 points (which means 2%) into a decimal: 0.02.
2. The dollar cost of mortgage points is calculated by multiplying the loan amount by the number of points expressed as a percentage: \$800,000 x 0.02.
3. The cost for these points is \$16,000.

Banks also charge a fee just for doing the paperwork to set up the loan. A **loan origination fee** is often expressed mathematically as points or as a percentage of the loan amount. 

Let's calculate a 1% origination fee on an \$800,000 loan:
1. Turn 1% into a decimal: 0.01.
2. Multiply the loan amount by the decimal: \$800,000 x 0.01.
3. The origination fee is \$8,000. The buyer pays this just to get the loan started!

## The Dimension of Time: Appreciation, Depreciation, and Profit

Houses and buildings don't always stay the same price forever. Sometimes they become more valuable like a rare comic book, and sometimes they lose value like an old bicycle.

**Appreciation** is the increase in the financial value of a property over time. **Depreciation** is the decrease in the financial value of a property over time. 

Let's calculate appreciation! The total dollar amount of property appreciation equals the original property value multiplied by the appreciation rate. 
If a \$500,000 house goes up in value by 10%:
1. Turn 10% into a decimal: 0.10.
2. Multiply the original value by the decimal: \$500,000 x 0.10.
3. The property went up in value by \$50,000.

If you want a shortcut to find the brand-new price without finding the difference first, you can do this: The new property value after appreciation equals the original value multiplied by one plus the appreciation rate.
1. Add the rate (0.10) to 1: 1 + 0.10 = 1.10.
2. Multiply the original value by this new number: \$500,000 x 1.10.
3. The new value is \$550,000.

The exact same idea works for losing value. The total dollar amount of property depreciation equals the original property value multiplied by the depreciation rate. The new property value after depreciation equals the original value multiplied by one minus the depreciation rate. 

Let's say a commercial building originally worth \$1,000,000 loses 5% of its value:
1. Subtract the rate (0.05) from 1: 1 - 0.05 = 0.95.
2. Multiply the original value by this new number: \$1,000,000 x 0.95.
3. The new value after depreciation is \$950,000.

### Measuring Success: Rates of Return

When someone finally sells their house or building, they want to know how well they did compared to when they started. 

The percentage rate of profit on a real estate investment is calculated by dividing the dollar profit amount by the original cost of the property. 

Let's say you bought land for \$200,000 and sold it for \$250,000.
1. Find the dollar profit amount by subtracting: \$250,000 - \$200,000 = \$50,000.
2. Divide the profit by the original cost: \$50,000 / \$200,000.
3. The answer is 0.25, which means you made a 25% profit rate!

What if you lost money? The percentage rate of loss on a real estate investment is calculated by dividing the dollar loss amount by the original cost of the property. 

Let's say you sold that \$200,000 land for only \$160,000.
1. Find the dollar loss amount: \$200,000 - \$160,000 = \$40,000.
2. Divide the loss by the original cost: \$40,000 / \$200,000.
3. The answer is 0.20, which is a 20% loss rate.

Notice that you always divide by the *original cost*, just like using your starting score in a game to see how much you've improved. If you can master this math, you will always know exactly how the real estate money game is played!
