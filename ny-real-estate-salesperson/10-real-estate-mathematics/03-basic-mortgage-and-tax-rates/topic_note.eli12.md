Buying a house isn't just about picking out your favorite bedrooms and checking out the backyard. That is only half of the real estate job! The other half is all about math. Before a family can move in, we have to figure out exactly how much money they can borrow and pay the different rules and taxes the state charges. Think of it like a video game: you can't unlock the new base until you have the right amount of coins and meet the level requirements. For a real estate agent in New York, knowing this math is super important. People don't just buy a house with happy feelings; they buy it using very careful math about what they can afford and what taxes they owe.

## The Architecture of Affordability: Mortgage Qualification

To figure out how much house a person can afford, you have to look at their money the way a bank does. Banks don't just look at how much money is sitting in a piggy bank right now. They look at how much money comes in every month compared to how much goes out.

The starting point for all this math is **monthly gross income**. Monthly gross income represents a borrower's total earned income before any taxes or deductions are removed. Imagine you earn \$100 a month mowing lawns, but your parents take out \$20 to pay for a window you accidentally broke. Your *gross* income is the full \$100 before anything is taken away.

From this starting amount, banks test a person's money in two different ways. Think of these tests like two doors. You have to be skinny enough to fit through *both* doors to get the loan.

### The Front-End Ratio
The first test is the **housing expense ratio**. The housing expense ratio compares a borrower's projected monthly housing costs to the borrower's monthly gross income. It basically looks at how much of your total monthly money would go just toward paying for the house. The housing expense ratio is commonly referred to in the real estate industry as the **front-end ratio**.

Here is how you do the math:
1. Find the borrower's monthly gross income.
2. Find the required housing expense ratio.
3. To calculate a maximum monthly housing payment, an individual must multiply the borrower's gross monthly income by the required housing expense ratio.

### The Back-End Ratio
The second test is the **total debt ratio**. The total debt ratio compares all of a borrower's monthly debt obligations to the borrower's monthly gross income. This means it looks at the house payment *plus* everything else you owe, like student loans, car payments, and credit card bills. The total debt ratio is commonly referred to in the real estate industry as the **back-end ratio**.

Here is how you do the math:
1. Find the borrower's monthly gross income.
2. Find the required total debt ratio.
3. To calculate a maximum total monthly debt payment, an individual must multiply the borrower's gross monthly income by the required total debt ratio.

### The "Lower of the Two" Rule
So, which number does the bank actually use? They do the math for both tests. Then, lenders qualify borrowers for a mortgage based on the **lower** of the maximum payments calculated from the housing expense ratio and the total debt ratio. If a buyer has no other debts, the front-end ratio is usually what limits them. If they have a ton of credit card debt, the back-end ratio pulls their allowed house payment way down.

### The Three Benchmark Standards
Depending on what kind of loan a buyer gets, the rules change. You need to know the exact percentages for the three big types of loans:

| Loan Type | Max Housing Expense (Front-End) | Max Total Debt (Back-End) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Conventional** | 28 percent | 36 percent |
| **Federal Housing Administration (FHA)** | 31 percent | 43 percent |
| **Department of Veterans Affairs (VA)** | *Not utilized* | 41 percent (Single ratio) |

Let's spell that out so it's super clear:
* Conventional mortgage loans typically require a maximum housing expense ratio of 28 percent.
* Conventional mortgage loans typically require a maximum total debt ratio of 36 percent.
* Federal Housing Administration mortgage loans typically require a maximum housing expense ratio of 31 percent.
* Federal Housing Administration mortgage loans typically require a maximum total debt ratio of 43 percent.
* Department of Veterans Affairs mortgage loans typically utilize a single maximum total debt ratio of 41 percent. (They completely skip the front-end ratio to make things easier for veterans!)

## The Price of Changing Hands: NYS Real Estate Transfer Tax

When you trade a really rare trading card, sometimes the place hosting the trade takes a small fee. New York State does the same thing when a house is sold. The **New York State real estate transfer tax** is based on the final sale price of the real property. The New York State real estate transfer tax is customarily paid by the property seller at closing.

The base New York State real estate transfer tax rate is **two dollars for every five hundred dollars** of the purchase price.

Here is a tricky rule: New York doesn't like dealing with leftover pieces of money. Any fractional portion of five hundred dollars in a purchase price is treated as a full five hundred dollars when calculating the New York State real estate transfer tax.

To calculate the New York State real estate transfer tax, an individual divides the purchase price by five hundred, rounds any decimal up to the next whole number, and multiplies that whole number by two dollars.

Let's look at the step-by-step math for a house that sells for \$400,250:
1. Divide the purchase price by 500. (\$400,250 / 500 = 800.5)
2. Look at your answer. If there is a decimal, round it UP to the very next whole number. (800.5 rounds up to 801)
3. Multiply that new whole number by \$2. (801 * \$2 = \$1,602)
4. The final transfer tax the seller pays is \$1,602.

## The Seven-Figure Cliff: The NYS Mansion Tax

New York has a special rule if a house is super expensive. The **New York State mansion tax** is an additional tax calculated as a percentage of the total purchase price. Unlike the transfer tax that the seller pays, the New York State mansion tax is customarily paid by the property buyer at closing.

Here are the three big rules for this tax:
1. The New York State mansion tax applies exclusively to residential property sales. It does not apply to businesses or stores.
2. The New York State mansion tax is triggered when a residential property sale price reaches exactly one million dollars or greater.
3. The New York State mansion tax rate is exactly one percent of the entire purchase price.

This is why you will see houses priced at \$999,999. If a buyer pays \$999,999, they don't owe this tax at all! But if they pay exactly \$1,000,000, they suddenly owe 1 percent of the whole million.

Let's do the step-by-step math for a \$1,000,000 house:
1. Check if the price is exactly \$1,000,000 or more. (Yes, it is).
2. Multiply the total purchase price by 1 percent (0.01). (\$1,000,000 * 0.01 = \$10,000)
3. The buyer must pay a \$10,000 mansion tax.

## Financing the Dream: Mortgage Recording Taxes

The transfer and mansion taxes are based on how much the *house* costs. But New York also charges a tax on the *loan* itself. The **mortgage recording tax** is assessed exclusively on the principal loan amount rather than the property purchase price. The "principal loan amount" is simply the exact amount of money you are borrowing from the bank. Because this is a tax on borrowing money, the New York State mortgage recording tax is customarily paid by the borrower at closing.

This tax is actually made of two parts you have to calculate:
1. The basic New York State mortgage recording tax rate is **fifty cents for every one hundred dollars** of mortgage debt.
2. New York State imposes a special additional mortgage recording tax of **twenty-five cents for every one hundred dollars** of mortgage debt.

### The Exemption for Everyday Homeowners
To help out normal families, the state gives a little discount. New York State allows a **ten thousand dollar deduction** from the principal loan amount when calculating the *special additional* mortgage recording tax.

However, the ten thousand dollar deduction for the special additional mortgage recording tax applies exclusively to one-family or two-family dwellings. It does not work for huge apartment buildings.

Let's look at the step-by-step math if a family borrows \$400,000 for a single-family house:

First, find the basic tax:
1. Divide the total loan by 100. (\$400,000 / 100 = 4,000)
2. Multiply that number by fifty cents (\$0.50). (4,000 * \$0.50 = \$2,000 basic tax)

Next, find the special additional tax:
3. Subtract the \$10,000 deduction from the total loan. (\$400,000 - \$10,000 = \$390,000)
4. Divide this new number by 100. (\$390,000 / 100 = 3,900)
5. Multiply that number by twenty-five cents (\$0.25). (3,900 * \$0.25 = \$975 special additional tax)

Finally, add them together:
6. Add the basic tax and special additional tax. (\$2,000 + \$975 = \$2,975 total mortgage recording tax).

## The Ongoing Commitment: Annual Real Property Taxes

Even after the buyer moves in, the math isn't over. Every year, you have to pay taxes to your town to help pay for schools, parks, and roads. 

The **annual real property tax** is calculated by multiplying the assessed value of the property by the local tax rate.

But wait, what is "assessed value"? It is usually not the price you paid for the house. Your house has a "market value" (what it would sell for today). An **assessment ratio** is the specific percentage of a property's market value used by a municipality to determine the property's assessed value. 

To determine the assessed value of a property, an individual must multiply the property's market value by the municipality's assessment ratio.

Here are the step-by-step instructions for finding the assessed value:
1. Find the market value of the house. (Let's say it is \$500,000).
2. Find the town's assessment ratio. (Let's say it is 40 percent, or 0.40).
3. Multiply the market value by the assessment ratio. (\$500,000 * 0.40 = \$200,000).
4. The assessed value is \$200,000. Property taxes are based *only* on this number!

### The Mathematics of Mills
Towns don't usually talk about tax rates using normal percentages. They use a special word called "mills." 

A tax rate expressed in **mills** represents the required tax amount per one thousand dollars of assessed property value.

So, what is a mill? 
* One mill is mathematically equal to **0.001 dollars**.
* One mill is mathematically equal to **one-tenth of one cent**. 

It sounds tiny, but it adds up! To calculate the annual property tax using mills, an individual must divide the assessed property value by one thousand and multiply the result by the mill rate.

Let's do the step-by-step math for that house with a \$200,000 assessed value, if the town charges 50 mills:
1. Take the assessed value and divide it by 1,000. (\$200,000 / 1,000 = 200).
2. Take that answer and multiply it by the mill rate (50). (200 * 50 = \$10,000).
3. The annual property tax the homeowner must pay is \$10,000.
