Selling a house is a lot like trading a super rare video game console. The person selling the console wants to get the highest amount of allowance money possible. The person buying it wants to spend the lowest amount of money possible. Standing right in the middle of this tug-of-war are real estate agents. Agents are bound by something called "fiduciary duties," which is just a fancy legal term for an unbreakable promise of loyalty, like being a best friend. Knowing who the agent is making that promise to—and who gets in trouble when things go wrong—is the most important rule in the New York real estate game.

The rules of being an agent are like the instruction manual for every single house sale in New York. If you learn how these rules work, you can handle anything, whether you are selling a giant \$5,000,000 mansion or helping someone rent a \$2,500-a-month apartment.

Let us break down how this team works.

## The Primary Poles: Sellers and Buyers

Every sale starts with a simple question: Whose side are you on? In real estate, you are almost never a neutral referee. You are a coach fighting for your specific team.

### The Seller’s Agent
When someone decides to sell their house, they hire an expert to help them. **A seller's agent represents the property owner in a real estate transaction.** When they team up, the agent gives the owner a guarantee: **a seller's agent owes absolute fiduciary duties to the property owner.** This means their loyalty is 100% locked to the seller.

What does this look like in real life? It means **a seller's agent must seek a sale at the price and terms most acceptable to the property owner.** 

Here is a step-by-step math example of how an agent might try to get a better price for their seller. Let's say a buyer offers \$800,000, but the seller wants 5% more:
1. Start with the buyer's offer: \$800,000.
2. Multiply the offer by 5% (0.05) to find the extra amount: 800,000 * 0.05 = \$40,000.
3. Add the extra amount to the original offer: 800,000 + 40,000 = \$840,000. 

Even if the buyer offered a lot of money, the agent's job is to push for that \$840,000!

But being loyal to the seller does not mean you are allowed to cheat the buyer. 
> **The Baseline of Fair Dealing:** Even though the agent is trying to get the most money out of the buyer, **a seller's agent must treat all prospective buyers with fairness and honesty.** 

This means you can't lie about broken things. **A seller's agent must disclose all known material defects of a property to prospective buyers.** If you know the roof leaks every time it rains, you must tell the buyer. You can't keep it a secret just to help your seller win.

### The Buyer’s Agent
On the opposite team is the purchaser. **A buyer's agent represents the purchaser in a real estate transaction.** Just like the seller has a loyal coach, **a buyer's agent owes absolute fiduciary duties to the purchaser.** 

Your job here is the exact opposite of the seller's agent: **A buyer's agent must seek a property at a price and terms acceptable to the purchaser.** You are looking for a great deal, a discount, or extras included. And just like the other team, **a buyer's agent must treat all property sellers with fairness and honesty.**

The biggest difference between the two teams is how they handle secrets. Imagine you are a buyer's agent, and you accidentally find out the seller is moving away tomorrow and is desperate to sell their house for cheap. Do you keep that a secret? No way! **A buyer's agent must disclose to the purchaser any known information regarding the seller's willingness to accept a lower price.** That secret is like a cheat code for getting a lower price, and your loyalty promise means you have to share it with your buyer.

## The Leasing Parallel: Landlords and Tenants

What if you are just renting an apartment instead of buying a whole house? The exact same tug-of-war rules apply! Just swap the words around.

*   **A landlord's agent represents the property owner in a leasing transaction.** Just like a seller's agent, **a landlord's agent owes absolute fiduciary duties to the property owner.** Because they are loyal to the owner, they **must negotiate lease terms most favorable to the property owner** (like higher monthly rent). However, they **must treat all prospective tenants with fairness and honesty.**
*   On the flip side, **a tenant's agent represents the prospective renter in a leasing transaction.** They make their unbreakable promise because **a tenant's agent owes absolute fiduciary duties to the prospective renter.** They **must negotiate lease terms most favorable to the prospective renter** (like getting a free month of rent). And, of course, **a tenant's agent must treat all property owners with fairness and honesty.**

## The Delegation Problem: Subagents vs. Broker's Agents

Here is a truth about real estate: you can't be everywhere at once. Sometimes you need to hire outside helpers to get the job done. But when you bring in outside helpers, there is a very scary legal question: *Who gets in trouble if the helper makes a big mistake?* 

This is called **vicarious liability**, which is a big word that just means taking the blame for someone else's mess. (Like if your little brother breaks a lamp, but your parents blame *you* because you were in charge). There are two types of helpers: Subagents and Broker's Agents.

### The Traditional Danger: The Subagent
A long time ago, it was common to use a helper called a subagent. 

**A subagent is a real estate licensee engaged by a listing broker to represent the seller.** Even though the subagent has never even met the seller, the legal loyalty connects them directly. **A subagent owes absolute fiduciary duties to the seller.**

The danger here is who gets blamed. If a subagent lies and tells a buyer, "Yes, this house comes with a free swimming pool," and it doesn't, the buyer will be really mad. Who takes the blame? 
> **The Subagency Liability Chain:** **A seller holds vicarious liability for the actions of a subagent**, and **a listing broker holds vicarious liability for the actions of a subagent.** 

Think about how risky that is! The poor seller gets in trouble for a lie told by someone they never even met. Because this is so dangerous, people rarely use subagents anymore.

### The Modern Solution: The Broker's Agent
To fix the blame problem, the real estate world created a new type of helper: the Broker's Agent. Think of them like a shield.

**A broker's agent is an agent engaged by a primary listing agent or a primary buyer's agent.** Their job is just to help out—**a broker's agent assists the primary agent in locating a property or a party to a transaction.** 

One important rule is that **a broker's agent does not work for the same brokerage firm as the primary agent.** (They are from a completely different real estate company). 

How does this shield protect the client from taking the blame? 
1.  **A broker's agent does not have a direct agency relationship with the client of the primary agent.** 
2.  Because there is no direct link, **a client cannot provide instructions directly to a broker's agent.** (The client has to tell their main agent, who then tells the helper).
3.  Because the client is not the boss of the helper, the shield works: **A client does not hold vicarious liability for the actions of a broker's agent.** 

If the broker's agent makes a huge mistake, the client is completely safe! But someone still has to take the blame. **A primary real estate agent holds vicarious liability for the actions of an engaged broker's agent.** 

Even though the client won't get in trouble for them, the helper still has to be good: **A broker's agent owes fiduciary duties to the client of the primary agent.** They must still act like a loyal friend, even though the client can't boss them around or take the blame for them.

| Feature | Subagent | Broker's Agent |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Engaged By** | Listing Broker | Primary Agent (Listing or Buyer's) |
| **Owes Duty To** | Seller | Client of the Primary Agent |
| **Client Control** | Direct | Indirect (must go through Primary) |
| **Client Liability** | **Yes** (Vicarious Liability) | **No** (Firewall active) |
| **Agent Liability** | Listing Broker is liable | Primary Agent is liable |

## The Paradox of Dual Agency

What happens if your buyer wants to buy the exact same house that you are selling for your seller? 

You have now entered **Dual Agency**. 

**A dual agent represents both the buyer and the seller in the exact same real estate transaction.** (Or, if we are talking about rentals, **a dual agent represents both the landlord and the tenant in the exact same leasing transaction.**)

Think about how hard this is. You promised the seller you'd get them the highest price, and you promised the buyer you'd get them the lowest price. You can't do both! 

So, the rules change. **A dual agent forfeits the fiduciary duty of undivided loyalty to the represented parties.** You have to put down your weapons and stop fighting for just one team. You become a totally neutral referee. You help with the paperwork, but **a dual agent cannot advocate for one party to the detriment of the other party.** You can't give the buyer tips on how to offer less, and you can't give the seller tips on how to ask for more.

### The Rules of the Tightrope
Because a dual agent stops fighting for their client, the State of New York treats this very carefully. 

**New York law requires a dual agent to obtain informed written consent from all represented parties.** Everyone has to sign a paper saying they are okay with this setup. 

Also, you have to be excellent at keeping secrets! 
*   **A dual agent must maintain confidentiality regarding the maximum price a buyer will pay.**
*   **A dual agent must maintain confidentiality regarding the minimum price a seller will accept.**

If you know the buyer has \$100 left in their piggy bank, but they only offer \$50, you cannot whisper to the seller, "Ask for more, they have another \$50!" That is breaking the secret rule and cheering for the wrong team.

> **Pro Tip for the Real World:** Getting someone to sign a permission slip at the last second when a buyer suddenly shows up is messy. Smart agents use **advance consent**. **Advance consent to dual agency allows parties to agree to dual agency before a specific conflict arises.** It's like having your parents sign a permission slip at the beginning of the school year just in case you go on a field trip later.

## The Elegant Compromise: Designated Agency

Being a dual agent is awkward because clients want a coach who will fight for *them*. 

Imagine you work for a giant real estate company with 500 agents. You represent a buyer. Another agent in your company represents a seller. Your buyer wants that seller's house. Because you both wear the same company jersey, the *company* is a dual agent. That means both clients would lose their loyal coach. That feels totally unfair to the clients!

New York has a super smart fix for this: **Designated Agency**. 

**Designated agency is a specialized form of dual agency.** It lets the big company stay neutral as a referee, but it gives the clients their fierce coaches back. 

Here is how it works: **A dual agent broker can appoint one salesperson to represent the buyer and another salesperson to represent the seller.** The big boss stays out of it, but the two agents are allowed to play hard! 

Unlike a normal dual agent who has to be a referee, **a designated sales agent owes the full fiduciary duty of undivided loyalty to the assigned client.** The designated buyer's agent can fight for a super low price, and the designated seller's agent can fight for a super high price. The tug-of-war is back on!

But, because the main company is still technically in the middle, they still need permission. **Designated agency requires informed written consent from both the buyer and the seller.** 

### Summary: Mastering Agency

When you take your New York real estate test, remember that these rules are just about knowing who your friends are and who takes the blame. 
*   Know if you are trying to make prices go up (Seller/Landlord) or make them go down (Buyer/Tenant). 
*   Know that if you use a **Subagent**, your client takes the blame if things go wrong. But if you use a **Broker's Agent**, *you* take the blame to protect your client. 
*   Know that **Dual Agency** means you stop fighting and keep everyone's secrets, while **Designated Agency** lets two agents in the same company be loyal coaches for opposite teams. 

Learn these rules, and you will be a real estate master!
