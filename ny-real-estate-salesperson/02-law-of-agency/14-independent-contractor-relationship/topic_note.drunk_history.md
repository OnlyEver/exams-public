That is the old-school world of a W-2 worker. In a common law employer-employee relationship, the employer controls both the final results of the work and the specific methods used to achieve those results. 

But we aren't employees in this business. We are rogue bounty hunters! In an independent contractor relationship, the hiring party dictates the desired final results without controlling the specific methods used to achieve those results. Your broker says, "Bring me a signed closing disclosure for this Tribeca loft." They dictate the result! But they absolutely do not control your methods. They don't care if you woo the buyer over artisanal martinis, prospect from a hot air balloon, or use carrier pigeons to send your comps. 

But here is where the reality TV drama of real estate law kicks in. We have a massive paradox! The New York State Department of State requires a sponsoring broker to supervise the licensed real estate activities of an independent contractor. Wait, what? How can a broker legally review your escrow accounts, check your disclosures, and hover over your fiduciary duties without accidentally exerting so much control that you magically transform into a W-2 employee? If they watch you too closely, BAM, you're an employee, and the government smashes the broker with fines.

To stop the entire real estate industry from spontaneously combusting under the weight of this paradox, the federal government dropped a legal smoke bomb. The Internal Revenue Service classifies qualifying real estate agents as statutory non-employees under Section 3508 of the tax code. "Statutory non-employee" is your magic forcefield! Because of this beautiful piece of legislation, mandatory broker supervision over licensed real estate activities does not invalidate a real estate agent's independent contractor status. They can supervise your legal compliance without accidentally adopting you.

But the IRS doesn’t just hand out magic forcefields for free. You must sacrifice three things at the altar to claim this status. First, a statutory non-employee must hold a current and valid real estate license. Obviously! Second, a statutory non-employee must receive compensation based entirely on sales production or output. You only eat what you kill! Consequently, a statutory non-employee cannot receive compensation based on the number of hours worked. If you spend eighty hours staging an overpriced Staten Island duplex and it doesn't sell, your paycheck is zero. Third, the Internal Revenue Service requires a written contract between the broker and the real estate agent to establish an independent contractor relationship. This isn't a handshake over a beer. The independent contractor agreement must explicitly state that the real estate agent will not be treated as an employee for federal tax purposes. 

Because you aren't an employee, your broker is not your mother, your warden, or your commanding officer. The law draws strict, aggressive boundaries. If you want to sleep until noon and prospect at midnight, that is your chaotic prerogative. A sponsoring broker cannot require a real estate independent contractor to attend office sales meetings. Listen closely, because this is where the financial illusions shatter. A real estate independent contractor cannot receive a base salary. Ever.
