Imagine you are playing a team video game like Capture the Flag. You cannot be on both the Red Team and the Blue Team at the exact same time, right? If you try to help both sides win, you will just end up losing the game for everyone. In real estate, loyalty works the exact same way. An agent has to pick one team and stick to it. We call this **single agency**. It is the invisible rule that decides who you protect, who you give secret tips to, and who you are just basically nice to. If you mess up this rule in New York, you don't just lose a game—you can lose your real estate license!

## The Physics of Loyalty: The Single Agency Principle

At its core, **single agency is an agency arrangement where a broker represents only one party in a real estate transaction.** This means you pick a side. **A single agent represents exclusively the buyer or exclusively the seller.** Because you cannot try to get a super high price for the seller and a super low price for the buyer at the same time, **a single agent is strictly prohibited from representing both the buyer and the seller in the same transaction.** It is totally against the rules!

When you make this team official, the person you are helping becomes your "principal." **The principal in a single agency relationship can be the property buyer**, or on the flip side, **the principal in a single agency relationship can be the property seller**. To this team captain, **a single agent owes fiduciary duties exclusively to one principal.** Fiduciary is just a fancy word for "super-loyalty"—meaning you guard their secrets and fight for their best interests.

But what about the person on the other team? 

> **The Principal vs. The Customer**
> **The unrepresented party in a single agency relationship is legally classified as the customer.** You don't owe the customer your super-loyalty or your secrets. But you also cannot cheat them! **A single agent owes the customer the duty of honesty**, and **a single agent owes the customer the duty of fair dealing.** You treat them fairly, like a referee would in a sports match.

| Relationship | Your Legal Role | Duties Owed | Example Scenario |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Principal** | Teammate (Fiduciary) | Super-loyalty, keeping their secrets, telling them everything you know, and following their legal rules. | Helping them figure out the smartest amount of money to offer. |
| **Customer** | Fair Player | Honesty, fair dealing, and showing them all offers. | Telling a buyer that the house's roof leaks, even though they aren't on your team. |

## The Mechanics of the Demand Side: Buyer and Tenant Agency

For a long time, real estate rules mostly helped the people selling the houses. Buyers used to walk through houses without anyone on their side, thinking the friendly agent showing the house was their teammate. Today, buyers have their own teammates! 

**Buyer agency occurs when a real estate broker represents the purchaser in a transaction.** In this setup, the buyer is your principal. Because of this, **a buyer's agent owes absolute fiduciary loyalty to the buyer.** You have to fiercely negotiate the best deal for them, find out if the house has hidden problems, and keep their budget a total secret. 

Because you are perfectly loyal to the buyer, **in a buyer agency relationship, the property seller is treated as a customer.** 

This doesn't just happen when buying houses. It happens with renting apartments, too! **Tenant agency occurs when a real estate broker represents a lessee seeking to rent a property.** (Lessee is just the legal word for a renter). The rules are exactly the same: the renter is the principal, and the landlord is just the customer.

To make this team official, you don't just do a high-five. **A buyer representation agreement formally establishes a buyer agency relationship.** This is a written contract that explains exactly how you will help them, how long you will be on their team, and how you will earn your allowance (your payment). 

## The Money Paradox: Compensation vs. Representation

Sometimes people think, "Whoever pays you is your boss." In New York real estate, that is completely false! 

**The source of a real estate broker's compensation does not determine the agency relationship.**

Think about it like this: If your friend's mom buys you a pizza for helping your friend study, you are still helping your friend, not the mom! Real estate works exactly like that. You can be 100% loyal to the buyer, even if the money comes from somewhere else. There are three ways you can get paid:

1. **A buyer's broker can receive commission payments directly from the buyer.** (Your teammate pays you).
2. **A buyer's broker can receive commission payments directly from the seller.** (The other team pays you).
3. **A buyer's broker can receive compensation through a commission split with the listing broker.** (The other team's agent shares their payment with you).

Let's look at how a math breakdown for a commission split works using simple steps! Imagine a house sells for \$100,000, and the total commission is 6%. The listing broker agrees to split it evenly with the buyer's broker.
Step 1: Find the total commission. Multiply the house price by 6% (or 0.06). \$100,000 * 0.06 = \$6,000.
Step 2: Find the split. Divide the total commission by 2 for an even split. \$6,000 / 2 = \$3,000.
Step 3: The buyer's broker receives \$3,000!

How you get paid is agreed on early, because **a buyer representation agreement specifies the method of compensating the buyer's broker.** 

However, if the opposing team is paying you, there are strict rules so no one feels tricked. **Payment of the buyer's broker by the seller requires full disclosure to all transaction parties.** Also, **payment of the buyer's broker by the seller requires informed consent from all transaction parties.** Everyone has to know and agree that the seller is paying the very person who is trying to get a lower price for the buyer!

## Transaction Geometry: In-House vs. Cooperative Sales

Real estate deals mostly happen in two ways: either one single company handles everything, or two different companies work together. The way your single agency "team rule" works changes depending on the setup.

### The In-House Sale
**An in-house sale involves a single real estate brokerage firm handling the entire transaction.** This means **in an in-house sale, the listing brokerage procures the buyer for its own listing.** (Procures just means "finds"). 

If a company promises to only represent one side, things get tricky. If the company represents the seller, what do they do when a buyer walks into their office wanting to buy that exact house? 

**A brokerage practicing strict single agency must treat the buyer as a customer during an in-house sale.** They cannot give the buyer secret advice or help them strategize. They can write down the buyer's offer and act honestly, but their true loyalty stays glued to the seller. 

### The Cooperative Sale
The much more common way is a team-up. **A cooperative sale involves two distinct real estate brokerage firms working on the same transaction.** One company helps the seller, and a "cooperating broker" from another company brings the buyer.

In this team-up, the agent bringing the buyer can act in one of two ways:
1. **As a Buyer's Agent:** **In a cooperative sale, the cooperating broker can represent the buyer as a buyer's agent.** (This is the normal, modern way).
2. **As a Subagent:** **In a cooperative sale, the cooperating broker can represent the seller as a subagent.** (This is a weird, outdated rule where the agent bringing the buyer is actually secretly loyal to the *seller*!).

Because subagency is confusing and leaves the buyer with no real teammate, the modern rule is strict: **A buyer's agent must reject any offer of subagency extended by the listing broker.** You cannot be the buyer's teammate and agree to be a subagent for the seller at the same time. 

## The Rule of First Contact: NY RPL Section 443

All of these complicated rules about loyalty and money are completely useless if everyday people don't understand them. New York State has strict rules to protect people. 

**New York Real Property Law Section 443 mandates the disclosure of agency relationships.** This isn't just a friendly tip; it is the law. People need to know exactly whose team you are on before they show you their cards.

To make sure nobody accidentally spills a secret to the wrong person, **New York real estate licensees must present the agency disclosure form at the first substantive contact.** 

What does "substantive contact" mean? It is the exact moment the conversation goes from "Nice weather we're having!" to serious real estate talk. It is the moment a buyer says, "I have an allowance of \$800,000 from the bank, but I really only want to spend \$750,000." 

Before those secret numbers leave their mouth, you must hand them the disclosure form. They have to understand if you are their loyal teammate, or if you are just a fair referee who might tell their secrets to the seller!
