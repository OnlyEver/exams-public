Listen to me, grab your drink, and look me dead in the eye, because if you want to survive New York real estate, you have to understand the invisible war zone you are stepping into. It’s not about knowing the square footage of a pre-war classic six on the Upper West Side; it’s about knowing exactly whose legal bulletproof vest you are wearing. In this jungle, loyalty is a zero-sum game. Welcome to the rule of single agency. Right out of the gate, you need to understand that single agency is an agency arrangement where a broker represents only one party in a real estate transaction. Think of it like being a highly paid, elite bodyguard. You cannot stand in the middle of a shootout and try to jump in front of bullets for both sides. That means a single agent represents exclusively the buyer or exclusively the seller. You pick a side of the arena, and you stay there. Do not try to play a double agent, because a single agent is strictly prohibited from representing both the buyer and the seller in the same transaction. If you try to play both sides, the Department of State will yank your license so fast your head will spin. Why? Because a single agent owes fiduciary duties exclusively to one principal. 

Who is this principal? They are your VIP, the very center of your legal universe. Depending on who hires you, the principal in a single agency relationship can be the property buyer, or, if you're guarding the other side of the fortress, the principal in a single agency relationship can be the property seller. You will fall on a sword for your principal. You will negotiate ruthlessly for them. But what about the other guy wandering into the open house? The one without a bodyguard? The unrepresented party in a single agency relationship is legally classified as the customer. They are a bystander. You do not take a bullet for the bystander. But here is the catch—you can't just shove them into traffic either! You cannot lie to them or scam them. A single agent owes the customer the duty of honesty. If you know the property's foundation is crumbling like a bad pie crust, you have to tell them. Furthermore, a single agent owes the customer the duty of fair dealing. You treat them fairly, but your heart, your strategy, and your secrets belong solely to your principal.

Let's look at the demand side, because the game has fundamentally changed. Back in the day, buyers walked into an open house thinking the nice agent giving them warm cookies was on their side. Wrong. But the market evolved, and now, buyer agency occurs when a real estate broker represents the purchaser in a transaction. And it's not just for buyers looking for a mortgage. Say someone is hunting for a commercial lease or a killer apartment—tenant agency occurs when a real estate broker represents a lessee seeking to rent a property. The exact same legal magic happens: a buyer's agent owes absolute fiduciary loyalty to the buyer. If your buyer tells you their absolute max budget is \$1.5 million but they want to open negotiations at \$1.2 million, you lock that max budget in the deepest vault of your brain. Because of this undivided allegiance, in a buyer agency relationship, the property seller is treated as a customer. The seller gets your honesty, but your buyer gets your absolute, unyielding devotion.

Now, here is where everyone’s brains completely short-circuit. Let's talk about the money, because there is a stubborn myth that whoever signs the check owns your soul. False! In New York real estate, the source of a real estate broker's compensation does not determine the agency relationship. Let that sink in. Your legal loyalty is completely divorced from the cash flow. It’s like a heist movie where the bank actually funds the bank robbers—your mission doesn't change just because of whose vault the cash came from. Sure, sometimes a buyer's broker can receive commission payments directly from the buyer, like a straightforward retainer fee. But wait, here’s the kicker: a buyer's broker can receive commission payments directly from the seller. Yes! The seller can hand you a check for actively destroying their profit margins on behalf of your buyer. Or, in the most common scenario, a buyer's broker can receive compensation through a commission split with the listing broker. 

But if the enemy camp is paying your fee, there are rules to this paradox. Payment of the buyer's broker by the seller requires full disclosure to all transaction parties. You can't just quietly pocket the opposing side's gold. Even more severely, payment of the buyer's broker by the seller requires informed consent from all transaction parties. The seller must formally agree, "Yes, I am paying the agent who is actively working to get my price down," and the buyer must agree, "Yes, I know my agent is being paid by the seller, but I trust their absolute loyalty to me." To avoid a massive brawl at the closing table over who gets paid what, we use paperwork. The prenup of real estate! A buyer representation agreement formally establishes a buyer agency relationship. It’s a binding contract that locks in your loyalty, and crucially, a buyer representation agreement specifies the method of compensating the buyer's broker. It’s all settled in black and white before anyone even looks at a floor plan.

Now we have to talk about the geometry of the deal, because the structure of the transaction dictates how you behave. Imagine the whole deal happens inside your own office. An in-house sale involves a single real estate brokerage firm handling the entire transaction. Picture it: your desk is on the left, representing the seller. Your buddy’s desk is on the right, and he just brought in the buyer. In an in-house sale, the listing brokerage procures the buyer for its own listing. If your brokerage is playing by the strict rules of single agency, you have a massive conflict of interest looming. So what do you do? A brokerage practicing strict single agency must treat the buyer as a customer during an in-house sale. The brokerage's loyalty was pledged to the seller first. The buyer gets fairness, honesty, and a polite handshake, but they are just a customer in that house. 

But usually, we’re out in the wild. A cooperative sale involves two distinct real estate brokerage firms working on the same transaction. The classic showdown. Firm A has the listing. Firm B brings the buyer. In a cooperative sale, the cooperating broker can represent the buyer as a buyer's agent. That’s the modern standard. But beware the absolute insanity of real estate history! Historically, in a cooperative sale, the cooperating broker can represent the seller as a subagent. This is madness! It means the agent driving the buyer around in their car actually owes their fiduciary loyalty to the seller they’ve never even met! If you are hired to protect the buyer, you cannot play this dangerous game. A buyer's agent must reject any offer of subagency extended by the listing broker. You swipe left on subagency immediately. You are the buyer's advocate, not the seller's secret agent.

So, when do you whip out your badge and declare who you work for? Do you wait until the closing table? Do you wait until they're sobbing over the escrow deposit? Absolutely not. New York Real Property Law Section 443 mandates the disclosure of agency relationships. This isn't a friendly suggestion; it's the state law. The consumer must know exactly whose side you are on before they spill their guts. To stop them from accidentally revealing their entire negotiating strategy, New York real estate licensees must present the agency disclosure form at the first substantive contact. "Substantive contact" is the exact moment the vibe shifts from casual chit-chat to high-stakes strategy. It's the moment the buyer says, "I have \$800,000 in cash but don't tell the seller," or the seller says, "I'm facing foreclosure and need to move by Friday." Freeze! Before those words leave their mouth, you slide that disclosure form across the table. You look them in the eye and say, "Before you tell me where the bodies are buried, you need to know exactly who I answer to."
