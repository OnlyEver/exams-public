In the mechanics of a [real estate transaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_estate_transaction), legal [loyalty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Loyalty) is not a spectrum; it is an absolute vector. Just as a compass needle cannot simultaneously point to two true norths, a [real estate broker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_estate_broker) practicing **single agency** directs their [fiduciary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiduciary) allegiance to one, and only one, side of the table. [Agency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law_of_agency) is the invisible legal framework that governs who you protect, who you advise, and who merely receives your basic honesty. Misunderstanding this framework in [New York](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_York_%28state%29) does not just cost you a [commission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commission_%28remuneration%29)—it costs you your [license](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_estate_license). 

## The Physics of Loyalty: The Single Agency Principle

At its core, **single agency is an agency arrangement where a [broker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Broker) represents only one party in a real estate transaction.** This is the bedrock of transparent representation. A single agent represents exclusively the [buyer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buyer) or exclusively the [seller](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vendor). Because you cannot simultaneously fight for a higher price for the seller and a lower price for the buyer, a single agent is strictly prohibited from representing both the buyer and the seller in the same transaction. 

When you establish this relationship, the person you represent becomes your **[principal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principal_%28commercial_law%29)**. The principal in a single agency relationship can be the property buyer, or the principal in a single agency relationship can be the property seller. To this principal, a single agent owes [fiduciary duties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiduciary) exclusively. You become their advocate, their shield, and their confidant. 

![Fiduciary duty is built on absolute loyalty, encompassing both good faith and the avoidance of conflicts of interest—principles that strictly govern a single agent's representation of a principal.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Fiduciary_duty_diagram.png)

But what about the other side of the table? 

> **The Principal vs. The Customer**
> The unrepresented party in a single agency relationship is legally classified as the **[customer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Customer)**. You do not owe the customer your loyalty, your [advocacy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Advocacy), or your [confidentiality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confidentiality). However, you are not permitted to deceive them. A single agent owes the customer the duty of honesty and a single agent owes the customer the duty of [fair dealing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Implied_covenant_of_good_faith_and_fair_dealing).

| Relationship | Your Legal Role | Duties Owed | Example Scenario |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Principal** | [Advocate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Advocacy) & [Fiduciary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiduciary) | Undivided loyalty, [confidentiality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confidentiality), [full disclosure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disclosure_%28law%29), [obedience](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obedience_%28human_behavior%29), [accounting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accounting). | Advising them on how low to offer, or how high to [counter-offer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Offer_and_acceptance). |

## The Mechanics of the Demand Side: Buyer and Tenant Agency

For decades, real estate heavily favored the seller. Buyers routinely wandered through transactions unrepresented, mistakenly believing the friendly agent showing them the house worked for them. Today, the demand side of the market has its own champions. 

**Buyer agency occurs when a real estate broker represents the [purchaser](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Purchasing) in a transaction.** In this arrangement, the buyer is the principal. Consequently, a buyer's agent owes absolute fiduciary loyalty to the buyer. You must fiercely [negotiate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Negotiation) the best terms for the purchaser, uncover hidden property flaws, and keep the buyer’s financial ceiling strictly confidential. 

Because of this exclusive allegiance, in a buyer agency relationship, the property seller is treated as a customer. 

This dynamic is not limited to sales. In the commercial and [residential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Residential_area) [rental markets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Renting), **tenant agency occurs when a real estate broker represents a [lessee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lease) seeking to rent a property.** The legal framework is identical: the tenant is the principal, and the [landlord](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Landlord) is the customer.

To codify this allegiance, you do not rely on handshakes. **A [buyer representation agreement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buyer_brokerage) formally establishes a buyer agency relationship.** This [contract](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contract) clearly maps out the scope of your duties, the duration of your representation, and—crucially—how you will be paid. 

## The Money Paradox: Compensation vs. Representation

There is a stubborn, intuitive [fallacy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fallacy) that "he who pays the piper calls the tune." In New York real estate law, this is demonstrably false. 

**The source of a real estate broker's [compensation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Remuneration) does not determine the agency relationship.**

Imagine you hire a [defense attorney](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Criminal_defense_lawyer), but your employer agrees to pay the [legal fees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Attorney%27s_fee). Does that attorney suddenly represent your employer? No. Their fiduciary duty remains anchored to you. Real estate operates on the same logic. You can represent the buyer absolutely, while the money to pay you flows from various channels:

1. **Direct from the source:** A buyer's broker can receive [commission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commission_%28remuneration%29) payments directly from the buyer.
2. **From the opposing side:** A buyer's broker can receive commission payments directly from the seller. 
3. **Through the listing:** A buyer's broker can receive compensation through a commission split with the [listing broker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_estate_broker) (who was paid by the seller from the transaction proceeds).

How this is handled is [negotiated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Negotiation) upfront, because a buyer representation agreement specifies the method of compensating the buyer's broker. 

However, if money is crossing lines from the opposing party, the law demands [transparency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transparency_%28behavior%29). **Payment of the buyer's broker by the seller requires full disclosure to all transaction parties.** Furthermore, payment of the buyer's broker by the seller requires [informed consent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Informed_consent) from all transaction parties. The seller must understand that they are funding the very advocate who is negotiating against them, and the buyer must be assured that this payment does not compromise the agent's absolute loyalty.

## Transaction Geometry: In-House vs. Cooperative Sales

Real estate transactions generally take two structural forms: they either happen entirely under one [brokerage's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brokerage_firm) roof, or they bridge two separate brokerages. The way single agency interacts with these structures dictates your daily operational rules.

### The In-House Sale
**An in-house sale involves a single real estate brokerage firm handling the entire transaction.** In this scenario, the listing brokerage procures the buyer for its own listing. 

If a brokerage is committed to representing only one party per transaction, a structural friction arises. If the firm represents the seller, how do they handle the buyer who just walked through their door? 

**A brokerage practicing strict single agency must treat the buyer as a customer during an in-house sale.** They cannot offer that buyer fiduciary advice or strategic negotiation [tactics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tactic_%28method%29). They can write up the offer, present it fairly, and act honestly, but their ultimate loyalty remains fixed to the seller. 

### The Cooperative Sale
Far more common is the cooperative model. **A cooperative sale involves two distinct real estate brokerage firms working on the same transaction.** The listing broker represents the seller, and a "cooperating broker" brings the buyer.

In a cooperative sale, the cooperating broker can act in one of two ways:
1. **As a Buyer's Agent:** In a cooperative sale, the cooperating broker can represent the buyer as a buyer's agent. (This is the modern standard).
2. **As a Subagent:** Historically, in a cooperative sale, the cooperating broker can represent the seller as a [subagent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subagent). In this outdated model, the broker bringing the buyer actually owes their fiduciary loyalty to the *seller*.

Because subagency creates massive [liability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legal_liability) and destroys buyer representation, the rules of modern buyer agency are strict: **A buyer's agent must reject any offer of subagency extended by the listing broker.** You cannot represent the buyer while simultaneously accepting an invitation to act as a subagent for the seller. 

## The Rule of First Contact: NY RPL Section 443

All of these intricate rules of loyalty and compensation are useless if the consumer does not understand them. New York State enforces [consumer protection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consumer_protection) with rigid [statutory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statute) timelines. 

**[New York Real Property Law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law_of_New_York_%28state%29) Section 443 mandates the [disclosure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disclosure_%28law%29) of agency relationships.** This is not a casual suggestion; it is the law. The consumer must know exactly where your compass needle points before they reveal their hand.

![New York State codifies its strict consumer protection rules and agency requirements within its Consolidated Laws, which all real estate licensees are legally bound to follow.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Mckinneysnewyorkcplr.JPG)

To prevent accidental breaches of confidentiality, **New York [real estate licensees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_estate_license) must present the agency disclosure form at the first substantive contact.** 

What constitutes "substantive contact"? It is the moment the conversation shifts from casual pleasantries to concrete, actionable real estate strategy. It is the moment a buyer tells you, "I am pre-approved for \$800,000, but I don't want to spend more than \$750,000," or a seller says, "I am getting [divorced](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divorce) and need to [liquidate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liquidation) this property by December." 

Before those words leave their mouth, the disclosure form must be in their hands. They must understand whether you are their fiduciary advocate, or whether you are simply a [facilitator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Facilitator) who will treat them as a customer while reporting their secrets back to your actual principal.
