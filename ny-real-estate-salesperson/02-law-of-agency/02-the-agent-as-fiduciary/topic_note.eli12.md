When a homeowner asks you to sell their house, it’s a huge deal. It’s like a friend trusting you with their most expensive video game console and saying, "Please trade this for me and get the best deal you can." You are not just a helper anymore; the law says you are acting as them. **A real estate agent owes strict fiduciary duties to the principal under the common law of agency.** "Fiduciary" is just a fancy word for someone who is trusted to take care of someone else's super important stuff. The "principal" is your boss—the person who hired you. Once you take the job, you have to be totally honest, careful, and loyal. It is the highest level of trust in the law, like being the team captain and promising not to let your team down.

## The Boundaries of Power: The Special Agent

Before we talk about what you have to do, we need to talk about what your job actually is. You don't get to control your client's whole life or all their money.

**A real estate broker acts as a special agent when hired to find a buyer for a specific property.** Think of a special agent like a friend you give \$15 to with exactly one rule: "Go buy us a large pepperoni pizza." They can't use the money to buy a skateboard instead. **A special agent has limited authority to perform a specific task for the principal.** Your only task is to market the house and find a buyer.

Because your job is so specific, you don't have the power to make final promises for your client. **A real estate agent lacks the authority to bind a principal to a contract unless granted a formal power of attorney.** A formal power of attorney is like a super-permission slip. Without it, even if a buyer brings a billion dollars in cash, you cannot sign the paperwork to sell the house. You can only bring the offer to your boss so *they* can sign it.

## The Anatomy of a Fiduciary: OLD CAR

To help agents remember all the rules of being super-trustworthy, the real estate world uses a handy memory trick. **The acronym OLD CAR represents the six common law fiduciary duties owed by a real estate agent to a principal.**

**OLD CAR stands for Obedience, Loyalty, Disclosure, Confidentiality, Accounting, and Reasonable Care.**

Each letter reminds you of a different way you must protect your client. Let's look at how each one works.

### Obedience: The Duty of Execution
**The fiduciary duty of obedience requires a real estate agent to follow the lawful instructions of the principal.** If your client says, "Only let people look at my house on Sunday mornings," you have to do exactly that. It's their house, so it's their rules!

But what if the client asks you to break a rule? **A real estate agent must refuse to obey a principal's instruction if the instruction violates fair housing laws or other statutes.** If a seller says, "I only want to sell my house to people who don't wear glasses," that is illegal discrimination. The law is always bigger than the boss. You have to say no, and if they keep insisting, you have to quit being their agent.

### Loyalty: The Undivided Allegiance
**The fiduciary duty of loyalty requires a real estate agent to place the principal's interests above all other interests**, including your own! It’s like playing on a basketball team; if your teammate is open for an easy shot, you pass them the ball instead of taking a hard shot yourself just to look cool. You put the team first.

The biggest way to break this rule is to try and sneakily make extra money for yourself. **A real estate agent violates the duty of loyalty by secretly buying the principal's property for the agent's own profit.** Imagine your client is selling an awesome bike for really cheap because they don't know what it's worth. You can't secretly buy it yourself just to turn around and sell it to someone else for more money. That is cheating your client.

### Full Disclosure: The Flow of Information
Your client needs to know everything that is happening so they can make good choices. **The fiduciary duty of full disclosure requires a real estate agent to inform the principal of all material facts related to the transaction.** A "material fact" is simply any piece of information that is important enough to change someone's mind. No hiding things!

This rule comes up every time a buyer wants to buy the house. **A real estate agent must present all offers to the seller principal to fulfill the fiduciary duty of full disclosure.** It doesn't matter if the house is priced at \$100,000 and someone offers \$5 for it. Even if you think it's a joke, it's not your house. **Failing to present a low offer to a seller principal constitutes a violation of the fiduciary duty of disclosure.**

You also can't keep secrets about who the buyer is. **A real estate agent must disclose the agent's personal relationship to a buyer when presenting an offer to a seller principal.** If your best friend or your brother is trying to buy the house, you must tell your client. Otherwise, your client might wonder if you are trying to get a better deal for your friend instead of for them!

### Confidentiality: The Iron Vault
"Disclosure" is about what you *must* tell your client, but "confidentiality" is about what you *must not* tell anyone else. **The fiduciary duty of confidentiality prohibits a real estate agent from sharing the principal's sensitive personal or financial information.**

When a client hires you, they might tell you private things, like "I lost my job and need to sell my house right now!" If you tell a buyer about this, the buyer will use that secret to offer less money. Because of this, **a real estate agent must not disclose the minimum price a seller principal will accept without explicit permission.**

This rule has a special superpower. **The fiduciary duty of confidentiality is the only fiduciary duty that survives the termination of the agency relationship.** That means even if you stop working for the client, you can never, ever tell their secrets. The vault stays locked forever!

### Accounting: The Sanctity of Funds
Real estate involves a ton of money changing hands. **The fiduciary duty of accounting requires a real estate agent to report the status of all funds received on behalf of the principal.** You have to track every single penny, just like you would carefully track your allowance or the score in a video game.

There is a very strict rule about where the money goes. **A real estate agent is strictly prohibited from commingling client funds with the agent's personal or business bank accounts.** "Commingling" just means mixing things up. You cannot put your client's money into your own piggy bank, even if you just want to keep it safe for a day.

Here is a quick step-by-step math example of how an agent tracks money for a client without mixing it:
1. The buyer hands you a first check for a \$500 deposit.
2. The buyer later hands you a second check for a \$1,500 deposit.
3. You calculate the total by adding them together: 500 + 1500 = 2000.
4. You must put that entire \$2,000 into a special trust account just for the client, completely separate from your own money.

### Reasonable Care and Skill: The Professional Standard
When someone hires you, they expect you to actually be good at your job. **The fiduciary duty of reasonable care and skill requires a real estate agent to perform duties with the competence expected of a licensed professional.** You have to use your brain, price the house right, and fill out the paperwork correctly.

But being smart also means knowing what you *don't* know. You are a real estate agent, not a lawyer. **A real estate agent violates the duty of reasonable skill and care by providing legal advice to a client without a law license.** If your client asks a tricky legal question, you have to say, "I am not a lawyer, you should ask one!" Trying to guess the law is like trying to fix a car engine when you only know how to ride a bike—you will just break it.

## The Breakdown of Trust: Misrepresentation

When an agent lies or messes up the truth, everything falls apart. In the law, this is called misrepresentation. **Agent misrepresentation occurs when a real estate agent makes a false statement of material fact regarding a property or transaction.**

If you say, "This house has the prettiest yard," that's just your opinion. But if you say, "This roof is brand new!" when it is actually 20 years old, that is misrepresentation.

There are two main ways this happens:

> **Fraudulent Misrepresentation**
> **Fraudulent misrepresentation is an intentional deception made by a real estate agent for personal or financial gain.** This means lying on purpose to get money. It’s like knowing a video game disc is scratched and broken, but telling someone it works perfectly just so they will buy it from you.

> **Negligent Misrepresentation**
> **Negligent misrepresentation occurs when a real estate agent fails to exercise reasonable care in verifying the accuracy of a statement made to a party.** This means being super sloppy. It’s like saying, "I think this game is for two players," without even looking at the back of the box to check. You didn't mean to lie, but you were too lazy to find the truth, and someone got hurt because of it.

### The Cascading Consequences of Misrepresentation

In New York, the rules are tough. If an agent lies or is super careless, bad things happen to the deal, the agent, and the boss.

**1. The Collapse of the Transaction**
If the buyer finds out they were tricked, they can hit the "do-over" button. **A buyer can seek rescission of a real estate contract if the seller's agent committed material misrepresentation.** Rescission just means canceling the deal completely and giving the buyer their money back.

And if you ruined the deal, you don't get paid. **A real estate agent who commits misrepresentation can lose the right to receive a commission for the transaction.** You can't get a reward for cheating!

**2. Regulatory Annihilation**
The government watches out for bad agents. **The New York Department of State can suspend a real estate license due to agent misrepresentation.** A suspension is like getting a time-out where you aren't allowed to work. If the lie was really bad, **the New York Department of State can revoke a real estate license due to agent misrepresentation.** Revoking means they tear up your license permanently—game over!

They can also make you pay penalties. **The New York Department of State can impose financial fines on a real estate agent for breaching fiduciary duties.**

**3. Civil Liability**
The trouble doesn't stop with losing your license. **A real estate agent is personally liable for civil damages resulting from the agent's own fraudulent misrepresentation.** "Personally liable" means the money comes out of your own pocket. If your lie caused \$10,000 worth of damage to the house, the judge will make you pay \$10,000 out of your own piggy bank!

**4. Collateral Damage to the Principal**
This is the scariest part for your client. Because you are acting as them, they can get in trouble for what *you* do. **A principal can face civil liability for the misrepresentations made by their hired real estate agent.** Even if the boss had no idea you were lying to people, the buyer can still sue the boss!

This is exactly why OLD CAR is so important. When you become a real estate agent, you hold someone else's safety in your hands. You have to be smart, honest, and totally loyal.
