Listen to me, rookie. Slide your pretzel bowl over here and pay attention, because the second a homeowner hands you the keys to their co-op, your entire existence changes. You aren't just a marketer with a shiny headshot and a Zillow account. You undergo a metaphysical mind-meld. Under the common law of agency, a real estate agent owes strict fiduciary duties to the principal. In the eyes of the law, you cease to be "you" and you become an extension of them. You are tethered to their financial soul. 

But before you get delusions of grandeur, understand your clearance level. You aren't the king; you are a highly specialized mercenary. A real estate broker acts as a special agent when hired to find a buyer for a specific property. Notice the word *special*. It means narrow. Focused. A special agent has limited authority to perform a specific task for the principal. You are a matchmaker, not the bride! Because your power is contained to simply finding that willing buyer, a real estate agent lacks the authority to bind a principal to a contract unless granted a formal power of attorney. If a tech bro walks into your Sunday open house and offers full asking price in cash on the spot, you cannot sign that purchase agreement for your seller. You bring the gold to the king; you don't wear the crown!

So, how do you remember the rules of this high-stakes mind-meld? You remember the getaway vehicle. The acronym OLD CAR represents the six common law fiduciary duties owed by a real estate agent to a principal. Write this on a napkin: OLD CAR stands for Obedience, Loyalty, Disclosure, Confidentiality, Accounting, and Reasonable Care. Let’s take this clunker for a spin.

First up is Obedience. You are the hands on the steering wheel, but they are the GPS. The fiduciary duty of obedience requires a real estate agent to follow the lawful instructions of the principal. If they demand you only show the house to buyers wearing purple hats on Tuesdays, you start checking for purple hats. But—and this is a massive "but"—you are not a hitman! A real estate agent must refuse to obey a principal's instruction if the instruction violates fair housing laws or other statutes. If your client whispers, "Don't show my house to *those* types of families," you hit the brakes, rip off the steering wheel, and walk away. Obedience ends where discrimination begins.

Next, Loyalty. No reality TV backstabbing allowed. The fiduciary duty of loyalty requires a real estate agent to place the principal's interests above all other interests. No putting your commission breath before their bottom line. And definitely no pulling a super-villain side hustle! A real estate agent violates the duty of loyalty by secretly buying the principal's property for the agent's own profit. If you walk into their brownstone, realize it's wildly underpriced, and secretly set up a shell LLC to buy it yourself so you can flip it for a quick million... that is absolute treason. You cannot prey on the person you are sworn to protect.

Then we have Disclosure. You are the all-seeing eye of the transaction. The fiduciary duty of full disclosure requires a real estate agent to inform the principal of all material facts related to the transaction. You don't get to filter reality to spare their feelings. This means a real estate agent must present all offers to the seller principal to fulfill the fiduciary duty of full disclosure. If a buyer offers \$10 and a half-eaten sandwich for a penthouse, you march that sandwich to your client! Failing to present a low offer to a seller principal constitutes a violation of the fiduciary duty of disclosure. It's not your house; you don't get to be the bouncer at the velvet rope. And speaking of spilling the tea, you must expose your own conflicts. A real estate agent must disclose the agent's personal relationship to a buyer when presenting an offer to a seller principal. If the buyer submitting the offer is your cousin, your college roommate, or your business partner, the seller has a right to know exactly who sits across the chessboard from them.

Now, enter the Iron Vault: Confidentiality. The fiduciary duty of confidentiality prohibits a real estate agent from sharing the principal's sensitive personal or financial information. If your seller is going through a messy, bankruptcy-inducing divorce and needs cash yesterday, you swallow the key to that vault. A real estate agent must not disclose the minimum price a seller principal will accept without explicit permission. You do not whisper to the buyer's agent at the coffee shop, "Hey, they'll take \$500,000 if you just ask." And here is the spookiest part of the getaway car: The fiduciary duty of confidentiality is the only fiduciary duty that survives the termination of the agency relationship. Long after the listing expires, long after you’ve retired to Florida, the vault stays locked. You take their secrets to the grave!

"A" is for Accounting. Treat their money like it is radioactive plutonium. The fiduciary duty of accounting requires a real estate agent to report the status of all funds received on behalf of the principal. There is a firewall between their money and your money. A real estate agent is strictly prohibited from commingling client funds with the agent's personal or business bank accounts. You don't put their \$50,000 earnest money deposit into your checking account "just for the weekend" to cover your country club dues! That’s commingling, and it’s financial poison.

Finally, Reasonable Care and Skill. You price the house right, you use the right forms. The fiduciary duty of reasonable care and skill requires a real estate agent to perform duties with the competence expected of a licensed professional. But knowing your skill means knowing your limits! You are a closer, not a Supreme Court justice. A real estate agent violates the duty of reasonable skill and care by providing legal advice to a client without a law license. If they ask, "Hey, what does this zoning easement clause mean for my pending lawsuit?" you tell them to call an attorney. Stay in your lane!

What happens when you crash the OLD CAR? The law calls it Misrepresentation. It’s reality distortion. Agent misrepresentation occurs when a real estate agent makes a false statement of material fact regarding a property or transaction. You tell them the roof is new, but it's actually held together by moss and prayers. 

There are two flavors of this disaster. Flavor one is venomous: Fraudulent misrepresentation is an intentional deception made by a real estate agent for personal or financial gain. You knew the basement floods, you painted over the water lines, and you lied to secure your commission. That's fraud. Flavor two is sloppy: Negligent misrepresentation occurs when a real estate agent fails to exercise reasonable care in verifying the accuracy of a statement made to a party. You didn't *mean* to lie, but the seller told you it was on city sewer, you were too lazy to check the tax records, and whoops—it's a cracked septic tank from 1974. 

When you do this, the fallout is biblical. First, the transaction implodes. A buyer can seek rescission of a real estate contract if the seller's agent committed material misrepresentation. The contract is legally erased, turning to dust. Because you destroyed the deal, a real estate agent who commits misrepresentation can lose the right to receive a commission for the transaction. You get nothing! 

Then the state regulatory hammer drops. The New York Department of State can suspend a real estate license due to agent misrepresentation. You’re benched, cut off from your livelihood. Or worse, the New York Department of State can revoke a real estate license due to agent misrepresentation, executing your career on the spot. Plus, they will raid your wallet—the New York Department of State can impose financial fines on a real estate agent for breaching fiduciary duties.

Oh, you thought it was over? Here come the lawsuits. A real estate agent is personally liable for civil damages resulting from the agent's own fraudulent misrepresentation. You will be paying out of your own pocket to fix that flooded basement you lied about! 

But here is the absolute, most heartbreaking tragedy of all. Remember the mind-meld? A principal can face civil liability for the misrepresentations made by their hired real estate agent. You just got your innocent client sued because *you* lied or got sloppy! You dragged them down into the legal abyss with you! 

So drink your water, rookie. It’s high stakes out there. Protect the principal, drive the OLD CAR, and for the love of all that is holy, don't lie about the septic tank.
