Listen to me, slide that cocktail napkin over here. You’re about to take the New York real estate exam, and you think this job is just unlocking doors and pointing at crown molding. It is not! It is a high-stakes transfer of sheer financial power. When a client hires you, they are essentially handing you the launch codes to their financial livelihood. In the eyes of the law, an agency relationship in real estate is created the exact moment a principal delegates authority to an agent to act on that principal's behalf. You become their legal avatar. You negotiate for them, you speak for them, you bind them. But you can’t just swipe the launch codes while they aren't looking. Mutual consent between the principal and the agent is an absolute, non-negotiable requirement for the creation of an agency relationship. You both have to look each other in the eye and agree to turn the keys. And let me be crystal clear about who you are looking at: New York real estate law requires the principal to possess legal capacity to enter into a valid agency contract. That means you cannot forge an agency bond with a toddler, someone heavily sedated, or anyone legally incompetent, no matter how much mutual consent you both think you have. They need to be of sound mind and legal age to hand over that kind of power.

Now, how do we actually build this magical bridge of authority? The best way, the safest way, is what we call express agency. Think of this as the master blueprint for a heist. Express agency is created through a clear, explicit agreement between the principal and the agent. There is no guessing. They say, "I want you to be my real estate agent," and you say, "I am in." Now, fun fact for your exam: an express agency agreement can be established either orally or in writing under New York law. You could literally scream your mutual agreement across a crowded subway car and it technically counts! But unless you enjoy losing your commission to a technicality, you put it in ink. When you are representing the person ditching a property, you use a listing agreement, which is a written contract that creates an express agency relationship between a property seller and a real estate broker. It’s their formal invitation to sell their fortress. On the flip side, if you are helping someone hunt for a fortress, you use a buyer representation agreement. That is a written contract that creates an express agency relationship between a property buyer and a real estate broker. Those are your golden tickets. 

But here is where agents completely blow it, and you need to listen closely because this will happen to you. Imagine you are hosting a Sunday open house. You have no buyer representation agreement with anyone walking through that door. An unrepresented guy wanders into the kitchen, loves the marble islands, and asks you, "Hey, what do you think the seller would actually take for this place?" If you lean in and whisper, "Well, the seller is going through a nasty divorce, so I'd say offer fifty grand under asking," congratulations! You just stepped on a legal landmine known as implied agency. Implied agency is created solely by the actions and conduct of the parties rather than by a formal written or oral agreement. You didn't sign anything, but you started acting like his getaway driver, so the law treats you like his getaway driver! An implied agency occurs the exact second a real estate agent provides advice or opinions to a customer that only a represented client should receive. You gave him negotiation strategy! And here is the terrifying part: an implied agency legally binds the principal and agent to fiduciary duties despite the absence of a formal contract. You are now legally locked into confidentiality, loyalty, and accountability to this random guy in the kitchen, entirely by accident. You must guard your advice like state secrets until the paperwork is signed!

Sometimes, though, the cart goes before the horse in a good way. Imagine a reality TV scenario: you know a landlord desperately needs a commercial tenant, but you don't have an agency agreement with him yet. You happen to find a perfect bakery willing to pay top dollar, you negotiate the terms on your own, and you slap the finished deal on the landlord’s desk. You acted totally rogue, without authority. But the landlord looks at it, tears up, and says, "This is beautiful, I’ll sign it." Boom! That is agency by ratification. This occurs when a principal retroactively approves the previously unauthorized actions of a person acting as an agent. They adopt your rogue mission as their own. But the law isn't stupid. For agency by ratification to be legally valid, the principal must have full knowledge of all material facts surrounding the unauthorized act. If you hid the fact that the bakery is actually a front for your cousin's illegal gambling ring, the ratification is entirely void. You can't retroactively bless a deal if you're blindfolded to the truth.

Now, let's flip the script. What if the client is the one causing the chaos? Imagine a seller leaves their totally unlicensed, idiot nephew sitting at the dining room table during an open house, sorting through paperwork and wearing a suit. The seller's actions make the nephew look like the boss. A poor buyer walks in, assumes the nephew is running the show, and hands him a \$10,000 earnest money check, which the nephew promptly uses to buy a jet ski. The seller cannot scream in court, "He wasn't my agent!" The law will drop the hammer on them with agency by estoppel. Agency by estoppel is created when a principal intentionally or negligently causes a third party to believe an individual is the principal's authorized agent. The law says, hey, you dressed the kid up like a guard, you put him at the desk, you created the illusion! Therefore, agency by estoppel prevents a principal from denying an agency relationship if a third party justifiably relied on the principal's conduct. This entire drama relies on the legal concept of apparent authority. Apparent authority exists when a principal's actions give a third party a reasonable belief that an agent has the authority to act. The seller made it *apparent*, the buyer fell for it, and the seller is now legally stuck with the consequences.

Alright, take a breath and take a sip of your drink, because I am about to shatter the biggest myth in real estate finance. Everyone—buyers, sellers, and rookie agents—thinks that whoever writes the check is the person you represent. Wrong! The payment of compensation does not legally determine the existence of an agency relationship. The flow of money and the flow of loyalty are two entirely different rivers. Let me prove it: an agency relationship is completely valid even if the agent receives no compensation whatsoever. We call this a gratuitous agency, which is an agency relationship where the real estate agent acts on behalf of the principal without receiving any fee or reward. If you help your mom buy a condo and waive your fee because you love her, you are a gratuitous agent. Do you owe her less legal loyalty because you're working for free? Absolutely not! You still owe her every drop of fiduciary duty. 

And it gets even wilder at the closing table. In New York, the party paying the real estate broker's commission is not automatically the principal in the agency relationship. Picture this: you represent a buyer. You fight tooth and nail for them, getting the price slashed. But when the dust settles at closing, the money paying your commission is technically carved out of the seller's proceeds. The seller is paying you! But remember the rule: a buyer's agent can receive compensation from the property seller while still strictly representing the buyer's interests. You can literally pocket the opponent's gold while remaining fiercely loyal to your client. 

And before you ask me what the "standard" rate is for that gold, let me stop you right there. If you ever use the phrase "standard commission rate," the antitrust police will metaphorically kick down your door. There is no standard rate. Compensation amounts in real estate agency agreements are determined strictly by negotiation between the broker and the principal. You want a flat fee? Negotiate it. You want a percentage? Negotiate it. You want an hourly rate? Negotiate it. It is entirely up to the hustle between you and the person giving you those launch codes! Now finish your drink, you've got an exam to pass!
