Imagine walking into a [bank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bank), pointing to a stranger in the lobby, and telling the teller, "Whatever she decides to do with my money is fine by me." In everyday life, such a sweeping delegation of financial power sounds absurd. Yet, in [New York](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_York_%28state%29) [real estate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_estate), transactions of exactly this magnitude happen every day through the legal mechanism of [agency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law_of_agency). An agency relationship is created when a [principal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principal_%28commercial_law%29) delegates authority to an [agent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agent_%28law%29) to act on the principal's behalf. 

This delegation is the molecular bond of the real estate industry. When a client hands you the keys to their \$2 million [Manhattan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manhattan) [cooperative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Housing_cooperative) or a suburban [estate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Estate_%28land%29) in [Westchester](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Westchester_County%2C_New_York), they are not just asking you to open doors. They are giving you the legal power to [negotiate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Negotiation), represent, and bind their interests in the [open market](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_market). This power cannot simply be declared by the agent; [mutual consent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meeting_of_the_minds) between the principal and the agent is an essential requirement for the creation of an agency relationship. Furthermore, New York real estate law requires the principal to possess [legal capacity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capacity_%28law%29) to enter into a valid agency [contract](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contract)—meaning they must be of sound mind and legal age. You cannot form a valid agency relationship with a [minor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minor_%28law%29) or an individual deemed [legally incompetent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Competence_%28law%29), no matter how mutually agreeable the arrangement seems.

![Manhattan housing cooperatives. High-value transactions, such as the sale of these Central Park West properties, necessitate the precise delegation of legal authority from clients to real estate agents.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Central_Park_West_buildings_over_Lake_crop.jpg)

Understanding exactly how this invisible thread of legal authority is spun, recognized, and sometimes accidentally tangled is the foundation of your career. Let us examine the mechanics of how agency is actually created.

## Express Agency: The Blueprint of Authority

The most secure, unambiguous way to establish representation is through **express agency**. Express agency is created through a clear, [explicit agreement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Express_contract) between the principal and the agent. In this scenario, nothing is left to guesswork. The principal says, "I want you to be my agent," and you reply, "I accept." 

> **The Rule of Expression**
> An express agency agreement can be established either [orally](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oral_contract) or in writing under [New York law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law_of_New_York). However, while oral agreements are legally valid, standard professional practice and the [statute of frauds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statute_of_frauds) regarding [commissions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commission_%28remuneration%29) practically necessitate written contracts to protect your livelihood.

When we codify this express consent in writing, it typically takes one of two primary forms, depending on which side of the closing table you sit:

| Agreement Type | Definition & Purpose | Typical Real-World Application |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **[Listing Agreement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Listing_contract)** | A written contract that creates an express agency relationship between a property seller and a [real estate broker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_estate_broker). | You sit down with a homeowner, agree on a [marketing strategy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marketing_strategy), and sign a document granting you the authority to market their home. |
| **[Buyer Representation Agreement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buyer_broker)** | A written contract that creates an express agency relationship between a property buyer and a real estate broker. | You meet a prospective purchaser at a [coffee shop](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coffeehouse), outline your services, and sign a document committing you to finding them a property. |

In both cases, the boundaries of your authority are mapped out on paper. You know exactly who you represent, what you are authorized to do, and when your authority expires. 

## Implied Agency: The Accidental Fiduciary

[Physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics) teaches us that certain forces, like [gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity), act upon us whether we consciously acknowledge them or not. [Real estate law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Property_law) has a similar force: **[implied agency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Implied-in-fact_contract)**.

Implied agency is created solely by the actions and conduct of the parties rather than by a formal written or oral agreement. You do not need a pen to sign away your [legal liabilities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legal_liability); you just need a loose tongue. If you begin acting like someone's agent, the law will treat you as their agent. 

How does this happen in the day-to-day reality of a real estate salesperson? Consider a bustling Sunday open house. An unrepresented buyer walks in. You strike up a conversation. If you simply point out the [property boundaries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boundary_%28real_estate%29) or hand them a pre-printed spec sheet, you are performing [ministerial acts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ministerial_act). But if they ask, "Do you think the seller would take \$50,000 below asking?" and you reply, "Well, they're getting [divorced](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divorce), so they are desperate to sell—I'd say start low," you have crossed a dangerous threshold. 

An implied agency occurs when a real estate agent provides advice or opinions to a customer that only a represented client should receive. By offering strategic negotiation advice, your conduct implies you are looking out for their best interests. 

Why does this matter? Because an implied agency legally binds the principal and agent to [fiduciary duties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiduciary) despite the absence of a formal contract. You have accidentally become a dual agent (if you already represented the seller), carrying all the severe legal liabilities that accompany [fiduciary duty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiduciary), entirely by accident. You must vigilantly guard your advice until formal agency is established.

![A conceptual diagram of fiduciary duties. When implied agency is accidentally established, the agent becomes legally bound to strict legal standards of loyalty and good faith towards the principal.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Fiduciary_duty_diagram.png)

## Post-Hoc Authority: Ratification

Sometimes, action precedes authority. Imagine a scenario where a client asks you to find a [commercial tenant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commercial_property) for their [Brooklyn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brooklyn) storefront, but you haven't formally signed an agency agreement yet. You find a perfect tenant, negotiate the [lease](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lease) terms, and present the finished deal to the [landlord](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Landlord). You acted without formal authority, but the landlord loves the deal, signs the lease, and enthusiastically accepts the tenant.

This is **agency by [ratification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ratification)**. Agency by ratification occurs when a principal retroactively approves the previously unauthorized actions of a person acting as an agent. 

The principal essentially looks at the completed action and says, "You didn't have my authority when you did this, but I approve of it now, and I adopt your actions as my own." However, the law demands fairness. For agency by ratification to be legally valid, the principal must have full knowledge of all [material facts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Materiality_%28law%29) surrounding the unauthorized act. A principal cannot validly ratify a contract if the "agent" hid the fact that the new tenant is actually the agent's [brother-in-law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sibling-in-law). Ignorance destroys the validity of ratification.

## The Illusion of Authority: Estoppel and Apparent Authority

We must also look at agency from the perspective of the innocent third party. What happens when a principal creates an illusion of agency, causing a buyer or seller to trust an [imposter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Impostor) or an unauthorized representative? 

This brings us to **agency by [estoppel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Estoppel)**. Agency by estoppel is created when a principal intentionally or [negligently](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Negligence) causes a third party to believe an individual is the principal's authorized agent. If the third party acts on that belief, the principal is legally "estopped" (stopped or prevented) from later claiming, "That person wasn't actually my agent!"

> **The Shield of Estoppel**
> Agency by estoppel prevents a principal from denying an agency relationship if a third party justifiably relied on the principal's conduct. It is a [legal doctrine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doctrine) designed to protect innocent third parties from harm.

Agency by estoppel relies on the legal concept of **[apparent authority](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apparent_authority)**. While [actual authority](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Actual_authority) is what the principal explicitly gives the agent, apparent authority exists when a principal's actions give a third party a reasonable belief that an agent has the authority to act. 

**A Practical Example:** 
Suppose a property owner allows their nephew to hold an open house, answer buyer questions, and accept written offers on the dining room table, even though the nephew is not licensed and has no actual authority to sell the house. A buyer, reasonably believing the nephew is an authorized agent based on the owner's conduct (leaving the nephew in charge of the property), hands over a \$10,000 [earnest money deposit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earnest_payment). If the nephew runs off with the money, the owner cannot later claim, "He wasn't my agent!" The owner's [negligence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Negligence) created *apparent authority*, resulting in an agency by estoppel.

## The Separation of Agency and Compensation: Breaking the Illusion

We now arrive at the most pervasive misunderstanding in real estate—a misconception that traps both clients and novice agents alike. It is the intuitive, yet entirely false, assumption that *whoever writes your [check](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cheque) is the person you represent.*

Let us be unequivocal: The payment of [compensation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Remuneration) does not legally determine the existence of an agency relationship. 

The flow of money and the flow of fiduciary loyalty are two completely distinct currents in real estate law. You can be paid by your client, you can be paid by the opposing party, or you can be paid by absolutely no one. 

### The Gratuitous Agency
To prove that compensation is uncoupled from representation, consider that an agency relationship is completely valid even if the agent receives no compensation. This is known as a **gratuitous agency**—an agency relationship where the real estate agent acts on behalf of the principal without receiving any [fee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fee) or reward. 

If you help your sister buy a house and waive your commission entirely, you are a gratuitous agent. Do you owe her less [loyalty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duty_of_loyalty) because you are working for free? Absolutely not. You still owe her the full spectrum of fiduciary duties (obedience, loyalty, disclosure, [confidentiality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confidentiality), accountability, and [reasonable care](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duty_of_care)). The absence of a paycheck does not dilute your legal liability.

### The Source of the Commission
In a standard [residential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Residential_area) closing, the seller typically pays a standard broker's commission out of the sale proceeds. This commission is then split between the listing broker and the buyer's broker. 

Because the money ultimately originates from the seller's [equity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equity_%28finance%29), buyers historically assumed the agent showing them homes actually worked for the seller. This is why New York mandates strict [agency disclosure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disclosure_%28law%29) forms. The party paying the real estate broker's commission is not automatically the principal in the agency relationship. 

In fact, standard practice dictates that a [buyer's agent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exclusive_buyer_agent) can receive compensation from the property seller while still strictly representing the buyer's interests. Your fiduciary loyalty remains locked to the buyer, even though the listing broker is the one cutting your co-brokerage check at the closing table. 

### The Negotiability of Compensation
Finally, when formalizing the agency relationship, how is that compensation actually calculated? Is there a state-mandated 6% fee? Is there a "standard" rate published by the local real estate board? 

No. [Price fixing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Price_fixing) is a severe violation of federal [antitrust laws](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_antitrust_law). Therefore, compensation amounts in real estate agency agreements are determined strictly by negotiation between the broker and the principal. Whether you charge a [flat fee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flat_fee), an hourly consulting rate, or a percentage of the closing price, that number is solely the result of the explicit agreement between you and the client whose authority you wield.

***

By mastering how agency is formed—whether meticulously through an [express contract](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Express_contract), dangerously through implied actions, retroactively through ratification, or defensively through estoppel—you elevate yourself from a mere door-opener to a true fiduciary. Understand these mechanics, guard your words, and always secure your authority in writing.
