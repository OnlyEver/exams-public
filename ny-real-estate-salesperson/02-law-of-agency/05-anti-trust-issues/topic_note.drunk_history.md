Listen to me, put the peanuts down and look me in the eyes. You’re about to take the New York Real Estate Salesperson exam, and you think your biggest hurdle is calculating square footage or memorizing lead paint disclosures. It’s not. The real monster in the closet, the thing that will literally yank your license off the wall and throw you in federal lockup, is antitrust law. The entire free market economy is a beautiful, chaotic cage match. Competition is the adrenaline that forces us to be better, faster, and cheaper to win the client. Antitrust laws promote free market competition by restricting monopolies from taking over the ring and preventing artificial market manipulation from rigging the game. They exist to make sure nobody gets to turn our glorious, competitive brawl into an orchestrated ballet. 

To keep this cage match fair, we have two giant bouncers working the door. On the national level, the Sherman Antitrust Act is the primary federal law prohibiting anti-competitive business practices in the United States. He’s the federal heavyweight. But because we work in the greatest city in the world, we also have our own state bouncer. The Donnelly Act is the primary New York state law prohibiting anti-competitive business practices. These laws do not mess around, but to trigger their wrath, a very specific heist-movie dynamic has to happen: real estate antitrust violations require an agreement between two or more independent business entities. You cannot conspire with yourself. It’s like a tango; it takes two distinct, rival brokerages deciding to stop competing and start colluding. 

This is where people get confused. They think any strict rule about money is a conspiracy. Wrong! Inside your own brokerage, your managing broker is the supreme dictator of their own little island. Therefore, an internal agreement regarding compensation between a single real estate broker and the broker's affiliated salespersons does not constitute an antitrust conspiracy. A real estate brokerage firm can legally dictate the exact commission rates charged by the firm's own affiliated licensees. If your broker says, "In this house, we don't list anything for less than a five percent split," that is perfectly legal business policy. It’s a single organism. The crime only occurs when your broker walks across the street to a completely separate, competing brokerage and says, "Hey, how about we *both* refuse to go below five percent?" Boom. You just crossed the line from a business strategy into an illegal cartel.

And that brings us to the first deadly sin: Price Fixing. Price fixing occurs when competing real estate brokers agree to set identical commission rates. It’s the ultimate betrayal of the free market. But it doesn’t just stop at commission percentages! Price fixing also occurs when competing real estate brokers agree to set uniform fees for specific real estate services. If you and a rival agent from another firm agree over drinks to both charge a flat four-hundred-dollar fee for every lease processing application, you are price fixing. And don't try to cry to the judge that four hundred dollars is a totally fair deal for a tenant! The courts do not care about your bleeding heart. Price fixing violates antitrust laws even if the agreed-upon prices are considered reasonable by the involved parties. The crime is the act of robbing the consumer of their right to shop around for a better deal. 

Because of this, I need you to burn this into your brain: real estate commission rates are fully negotiable between the principal and the broker. There is no official rate, no state mandate, no board-approved minimum. It is a bazaar, and everything is up for negotiation. If you are sitting in a luxury high-rise listing presentation, and the seller leans across the marble island and says, "Isn't six percent the standard rate?" you must bite your tongue! A real estate agent violates antitrust laws by claiming that a standard commission rate exists within the local market. If you say, "Yep, that’s just what everyone charges," you have artificially anchored the price and spoken an antitrust violation into existence. 

Now, let's talk about the "Mean Girls" cafeteria dynamic of the real estate world: Group Boycotting. This occurs when two or more competing brokers agree to withhold patronage from a third business entity. Imagine a new, wildly cheap flat-fee brokerage opens up in Brooklyn. The traditional brokers are panicking. If they quietly text each other and decide to freeze out the new guy, that is highly illegal. An example of group boycotting occurs when competing real estate firms mutually agree to avoid showing the listings of a specific discount brokerage. You cannot starve a competitor out of the market by conspiring to boycott their inventory!

Then there’s Market Allocation, which is when rival brokers act like mob bosses divvying up a city map. Market allocation occurs when competing brokers agree to divide geographic territories to eliminate mutual competition. "I'll take everything in Manhattan north of 59th Street, you take everything south, and we stay out of each other's way." You’ve just created two localized monopolies! But it’s not just geography. Market allocation also occurs when competing brokers agree to divide potential clients based on property price ranges. If Broker A says, "I'll only take the starter homes under half a million," and Broker B says, "I'll strictly handle the luxury penthouses," they have unlawfully carved up the consumer base.

And if you want to see a developer get thrown to the wolves, look for a Tie-in Arrangement. This is an economic hostage situation. A tie-in arrangement requires a buyer to purchase a second distinct product as a mandatory condition for purchasing the primary product. For example, a tie-in arrangement violates antitrust laws when a developer sells a vacant lot only on the condition that the buyer hires a specific real estate broker. "You want this prime piece of dirt? Fine, but you have to use my cousin's brokerage to buy it." It completely ruins the buyer's right to hunt the open market for their own agent. 

Here is the most terrifying part of all of this: you do not need a signed contract in blood to get caught. Antitrust enforcement agencies can penalize real estate professionals for unwritten anti-competitive agreements. They know criminals don't leave receipts! Even worse, antitrust enforcement agencies can penalize real estate professionals for implied anti-competitive agreements based on parallel business behavior. If three major competing brokerages all miraculously raise their minimum commissions to the exact same number, on the exact same Tuesday, and add the exact same admin fee, the feds will look at that synchronized swimming routine and infer a conspiracy. 

This paranoia is why trade association meetings are practically minefields. Real estate professionals face antitrust liability for merely discussing compensation models with competing brokers at trade association meetings. If you are eating rubber chicken at a local Board of Realtors banquet, and the guy next to you starts complaining about commission compression and says, "We really all need to hold the line," you don't just sit there. Silence is complicity! You stand up, you loudly announce you are leaving, and you run out of that room like it's on fire.

Because if you don't, the consequences will vaporize your entire life. Violations of the federal Sherman Antitrust Act can result in severe financial penalties, and worse, violations of the federal Sherman Antitrust Act can result in criminal imprisonment. We are not talking a slap on the wrist. Individual violators of the Sherman Antitrust Act face maximum prison sentences of up to ten years! Financially, individual violators of the Sherman Antitrust Act face criminal fines of up to one million dollars. And if your brokerage is found guilty? Corporate violators of the Sherman Antitrust Act face criminal fines of up to one hundred million dollars. 

Think you can hide behind New York state lines? The Donnelly Act will finish the job. A violation of the New York Donnelly Act is classified as a Class E felony. Say goodbye to your real estate license forever. Financially, under the New York Donnelly Act, an individual violator can face criminal fines of up to one hundred thousand dollars, while under the New York Donnelly Act, a corporate violator can face criminal fines of up to one million dollars. 

So finish your drink. When you get into that exam room, and later when you get your license and walk into a closing, remember the bouncers. Compete fiercely, negotiate your own deals, and never, ever make a back-alley pact with the enemy.
