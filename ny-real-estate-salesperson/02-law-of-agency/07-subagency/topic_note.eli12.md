Imagine you are the captain of a capture-the-flag team. You can't guard your flag and steal the other team's flag all by yourself, so you pick teammates to help you. In your team's eyes, those teammates are an extension of you. They are playing for your team to win, even if you aren't holding their hand the whole time. 

In New York real estate, this team-building idea is called **subagency**. When a person selling a house (the homeowner) hires a main real estate agent (the listing broker), that main agent often asks outside agents to help find a buyer. This creates a chain of command where an outside agent from a totally different company is brought onto the team to act on behalf of the seller. Figuring out who is on whose team, who is responsible for mistakes, and who owes what to whom is super important. It is not just about passing a test; it is about knowing how to show houses and make deals without getting anyone into major trouble.

## The Architecture of Subagency

To understand how subagency works, let's look at how this relationship is built. It doesn't happen by accident just because two agents are standing in the same room.

**Subagency is an agency relationship created when a primary agent appoints another agent to perform tasks for the principal.** In our real estate world, the homeowner is the "principal" (the big boss), the main agent is the primary agent, and the outside helper who brings the buyer is the subagent. Because the helper's power comes straight from the top, **a real estate seller functions as the ultimate principal in a subagency relationship.**

But a main agent can't just draft anyone onto the team whenever they feel like it. They need permission from the boss. **A listing broker must obtain the seller's express consent before offering subagency to other real estate brokers.** The seller has to make this choice and say "yes" when they first sign the paperwork to sell their house. 

If the seller says yes, how does the main agent tell the rest of the world? They use a huge online database for houses, kind of like a giant multiplayer server lobby. **An offer of subagency is frequently communicated to cooperating brokers through a Multiple Listing Service.** When an agent logs into this system, they can see an invite to be a subagent at the bottom of the screen. 

How does the connection finally happen? **A cooperating broker creates a subagency relationship by accepting the listing broker's offer of subagency.** They accept this offer simply by showing the house to a buyer and putting in an offer for that buyer as a subagent.

## The Allegiance: The Intelligence Agent for the Seller

This is where things can get confusing. If you are a subagent, you might spend weeks driving a buyer around to look at houses. You buy them pizza. You learn about their favorite sports and what kind of backyard they want. It feels super natural to think of them as your client or your teammate. 

But legally? They are on the opposite team. **A real estate subagent represents the seller rather than the prospective buyer.** 

Because the subagent is basically a helper for the main agent, **a real estate subagent owes absolute fiduciary duties to the seller of the property.** "Fiduciary" is just a fancy word for being 100% legally loyal. You are like a spy sent out into the field to get the best possible win for the seller. 

> **The Fiduciary Duties of a Subagent (OLD CAR)**
> **The fiduciary duties a subagent owes to a seller include obedience, loyalty, disclosure, confidentiality, accounting, and reasonable care.**

To do these duties properly, **a subagent must act exclusively in the best financial interests of the seller.** That means you want the seller to make as much money as possible. 

Think about what this means in real life. Let's do some quick math steps to see how this works:

1. You are standing with a buyer looking at a house priced at \$550,000. 
2. The buyer tells you: "We are going to offer \$500,000 today." 
3. Then the buyer whispers: "But we actually have enough money in the bank to go up to \$550,000 if we have to, and because our apartment lease ends next week, we are desperate to buy right now!"

If you are a subagent, you cannot keep this a secret just to help the buyer get a cheaper house. That would break your duty of loyalty to the seller. By law, **a subagent must disclose any information learned about the buyer's negotiating strategy to the seller.** Also, **a subagent must disclose any information learned about the buyer's financial status to the seller.** You are legally required to take that top-secret info—how much money the buyer really has and how desperate they are—and hand it directly to the seller so the seller can get the highest price possible.

## The Line in the Sand: Obligations to the Buyer

If a subagent is basically a spy gathering info for the seller, what exactly do they owe to the buyer? 

The rule is simple: **a real estate subagent does not owe fiduciary duties to a prospective buyer.** The buyer is just a *customer* at a store, not your *client*. You don't owe them loyalty, you don't have to keep their secrets, and you definitely don't have to obey them if what they want hurts the seller.

But just because you don't owe them total loyalty doesn't mean you can be a bad guy. The rules of real estate protect everyone at a basic level. Even though you work for the seller, **a real estate subagent owes a duty of honesty to a prospective buyer,** and also **a duty of fairness to a prospective buyer.** You can't lie to them, you can't trick them, and you can't hide huge problems with the house. 

The biggest part of being fair is about the actual building itself. **A real estate subagent must disclose all known material property defects to a prospective buyer.** If you notice the basement is completely flooded, or if the main agent told you the roof leaks every time it rains, you *must* tell the buyer. Helping the seller make money does not give you permission to hide major, real-life problems with the house.

### Summary of Obligations

| Duty / Obligation | Owed to the Seller (Principal) | Owed to the Buyer (Customer) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Fiduciary Duties (OLD CAR)** | Yes – Absolute obligation. | No. |
| **Financial Best Interests** | Yes – Must act exclusively for the seller. | No. |
| **Confidentiality** | Yes – Keep seller's secrets. | No – Must share buyer's secrets with seller. |
| **Honesty & Fairness** | Yes. | Yes – Baseline requirement. |
| **Material Defect Disclosure**| Yes. | Yes – Must disclose known defects. |

## The Chain of Peril: Vicarious Liability

If subagency means a seller gets lots of extra helpers trying to sell their house, why doesn't everyone love it? The answer is a scary legal rule called *vicarious liability*.

Vicarious liability is like when your parents have to pay for a broken window if you throw a baseball through it. It means a boss is legally responsible for the actions of their helper, even if the boss wasn't there and didn't know about it. In a subagency, this creates a long chain of blame. 

If a subagent lies to a buyer about where the backyard ends, who gets in trouble? The subagent gets in trouble, for sure. But because the subagent was helping out the main agent, **a listing broker bears vicarious liability for the actions and misrepresentations made by a subagent.** 

The blame goes even higher up the ladder. Because the seller is the big boss who said it was okay to use helpers, **a seller bears vicarious liability for the actions and misrepresentations made by a subagent.** 

Imagine being a homeowner and finding out you are being sued for lots of money because an agent you have never even met lied to a buyer. Because this risk is so huge, **New York real estate sellers frequently reject offers of subagency to avoid vicarious liability.** Instead, people today usually prefer having agents who work only for the buyer, which completely cuts off the seller's risk of getting blamed.

## The Shield of Disclosure: Section 443

We have learned that subagency creates a big mix-up between what feels right and what the law says. The buyer naturally thinks the friendly agent driving them to look at houses is on *their* team. To stop this terrible mix-up from happening, New York State has a strict rule.

**Section 443 of the New York Real Property Law requires a subagent to provide a prospective buyer with a written agency disclosure form.** 

This form is not a contract that locks you in. It's like a sports jersey that clearly shows which team you play for. It makes sure the buyer isn't accidentally tricked into giving away their secret game plan to the opposing team. 

In video games, timing a jump is everything. In real estate, timing this form is everything. You can't wait until the very end to hand the buyer this paper. **A subagent must present the statutory New York agency disclosure form to a prospective buyer before obtaining any confidential information.** The exact second the buyer opens their mouth to talk about their secret maximum budget, an emergency they are having, or how fast they need to move, you have to hit the pause button. You must slide that Section 443 form to them, explain that you are a subagent working for the seller, and make sure they understand that any secrets they tell you will be shared with the seller.
