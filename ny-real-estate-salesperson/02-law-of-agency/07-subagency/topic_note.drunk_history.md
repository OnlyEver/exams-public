Listen to me, buddy, put the fries down and look at me. You're about to step into the New York real estate market, and if you don’t understand what I’m about to tell you, you are going to accidentally become a double agent, get yourself sued, and drag a poor, unsuspecting homeowner down with you. We need to talk about the wildest undercover operation in property law: subagency. 

Picture this. You’re a real estate agent. You meet a nice couple. You spend three weeks driving them around Westchester in your Honda Civic, buying them lattes, listening to their dreams of a wrap-around porch, and wiping their tears when they lose a bidding war. You feel like their best friend. You feel like their champion. But legally? In a subagency scenario? You are a sleeper agent planted by the enemy. 

Let's break down how this heist is orchestrated. Subagency is essentially an agency relationship created when a primary agent appoints another agent to perform tasks for the principal. Think of it like a mob boss, a caporegime, and a street soldier. The mob boss is the homeowner—the ultimate principal in a subagency relationship. The homeowner hires a listing broker, who acts as the primary agent, the caporegime managing the operation. But the listing broker can't find a buyer all by themselves, so they need to deputize outside soldiers from completely different brokerages. 

Now, the listing broker can't just go around recruiting mercenaries willy-nilly. A listing broker must obtain the seller's express consent before offering subagency to other real estate brokers. The homeowner has to explicitly sign off on this infiltration tactic in their listing agreement. Once the boss gives the blessing, how does the listing broker broadcast this recruitment drive to the rest of the real estate underworld? They put out the Bat-Signal on the Multiple Listing Service. An offer of subagency is frequently communicated to cooperating brokers through the MLS, right there at the bottom of the listing ticket, dangling a commission split like a shiny piece of bait. 

And how do you, the outside agent, sign up for this covert op? You don't sign a blood oath. A cooperating broker creates a subagency relationship simply by accepting the listing broker's offer of subagency—which you do by showing up, bringing the buyer through the front door, and submitting an offer. 

This is where the psychological thriller kicks in. You are driving the buyers around, but plot twist: a real estate subagent represents the seller rather than the prospective buyer. You are working for the homeowner! You’ve never even met the homeowner! Yet, because you accepted that subagency mission, a real estate subagent owes absolute fiduciary duties to the seller of the property. 

You are legally bound to OLD CAR. No, not the Honda Civic you're driving—the acronym! The fiduciary duties a subagent owes to a seller include obedience, loyalty, disclosure, confidentiality, accounting, and reasonable care. You are their knight in an off-the-rack suit. This means a subagent must act exclusively in the best financial interests of the seller. You exist to get that homeowner the biggest, fattest bag of cash possible. 

Imagine you are standing in the kitchen with your buyers. They whisper to you, "Hey, we're going to offer \$700,000, but our lease is up on Friday and we are so desperate we'll actually pay up to \$750,000." 

Sirens should be going off in your head! You cannot keep that a secret to be a "bro" to the buyers. You are a wiretap for the seller! By law, a subagent must disclose any information learned about the buyer's negotiating strategy to the seller. And it doesn't stop there—a subagent must disclose any information learned about the buyer's financial status to the seller. You have to march straight to the listing broker and say, "The marks are desperate, squeeze them for the full \$750,000." 

So, what on earth do you owe the poor buyers who think you're their buddy? The cold, hard truth is that a real estate subagent does not owe fiduciary duties to a prospective buyer. They are a customer. A target. You don't owe them loyalty or confidentiality. But, because this is a civilized society and not an actual pirate ship, you aren't allowed to be a cartoon villain. A real estate subagent owes a duty of honesty to a prospective buyer, and simultaneously owes a duty of fairness to a prospective buyer. You can't lie to their faces. You can't forge documents. And if you notice the basement smells like a swamp and the ceiling is actively caving in, you have to sing like a canary. A real estate subagent must disclose all known material property defects to a prospective buyer. You protect the seller's wallet, but you don't help them cover up a collapsing foundation. 

Now, you might be thinking, "Wow, sellers must love having an army of undercover agents tricking buyers into overpaying!" 

Wrong! It is actually a legal nightmare. Why? Two words: Vicarious Liability. It’s like a legal contagion. It means the boss is responsible for the screw-ups of the soldier. If you, the subagent, lie to the buyer and tell them the property line extends all the way to the lake when it actually ends at the dumpster, who gets sued? You do, yes. But because the listing broker authorized you, a listing broker bears vicarious liability for the actions and misrepresentations made by a subagent. 

And the contagion travels all the way to the top. Because the homeowner is the grand mastermind of this agency chain, a seller bears vicarious liability for the actions and misrepresentations made by a subagent. Imagine being a sweet old lady selling your townhouse, and suddenly you are slapped with a \$100,000 lawsuit because some rogue agent from a brokerage you’ve never heard of lied to a buyer. Because of this terrifying chain of doom, New York real estate sellers frequently reject offers of subagency to avoid vicarious liability. It's just too risky.

But if you *are* operating as a subagent, how do you prevent the buyers from accidentally confessing their deepest financial secrets to you before they realize you work for the enemy? New York State has a shield for this. It's the Miranda Warning of real estate. Section 443 of the New York Real Property Law requires a subagent to provide a prospective buyer with a written agency disclosure form. 

This form clearly spells out, "I AM NOT YOUR AGENT. I WORK FOR THE SELLER." And timing is the difference between keeping your license and getting dragged before the state board. You cannot wait until you're at the closing table popping champagne to hand this over. A subagent must present the statutory New York agency disclosure form to a prospective buyer before obtaining any confidential information. 

The exact millisecond the buyer opens their mouth to say, "We have a ton of cash but we're getting a divorce and need to buy quickly," you must throw your hand up, interrupt them, and slide that Section 443 form across the hood of your Civic. You make them sign it. You make them understand the game they are playing. 

Now finish your beer. We've got open houses to run, and you've got undercover operations to legally disclose!
