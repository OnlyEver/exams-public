Imagine a [master architect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Architect) who is contracted to design and build a [skyscraper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skyscraper). The architect cannot pour every [cubic yard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cubic_yard) of [concrete](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concrete) or [weld](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Welding) every [steel beam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Structural_steel) alone, so they hire specialized [contractors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_contractor). In the eyes of the project owner, those contractors are an extension of the architect; they are legally bound to build the owner's building and advance the owner's goals, even if the owner never shook their hands. 

In [New York](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_York_%28state%29) [real estate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_estate), this architectural [delegation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Delegation_%28law%29) is called **[subagency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subagent)**. When a [homeowner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homeowner) hires a [listing broker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_estate_broker), that broker often enlists the help of outside agents to find a buyer. This creates a [chain of command](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chain_of_command) where a cooperating agent from an entirely different [brokerage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brokerage_firm) is deputized to act on behalf of the seller. The [legal implications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law) of this chain—who is responsible for whom, and who owes what to whom—form the structural bedrock of modern real estate [transactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_estate_transaction). Understanding these mechanics is not merely about passing a [licensing exam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Occupational_licensing); it is about navigating the daily reality of [open houses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_house), offers, and [liability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legal_liability) without exposing yourself, your broker, or your client to ruinous [lawsuits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lawsuit).

![Just as a skyscraper relies on a complex structural hierarchy to support its weight, real estate transactions rely on a chain of agency and subagency to distribute legal responsibilities.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Skyscraper_structure.png)

## The Architecture of Subagency

To understand how subagency functions, we have to look closely at how the relationship is actually formed. It is not an accident, and it does not happen simply because two agents are standing in the same room.

**Subagency is an [agency relationship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law_of_agency) created when a primary agent appoints another agent to perform tasks for the [principal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principal_%28commercial_law%29).** In our typical real estate scenario, the homeowner is the principal, the listing broker is the primary agent, and the outside agent who brings the buyer is the subagent. Because the subagent's [authority](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Authority_%28management%29) flows entirely downward from the top of this chain, **a real estate seller functions as the ultimate principal in a subagency relationship.**

But a listing broker cannot simply deputize the entire real estate community on a whim. The law requires [permission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consent). **A listing broker must obtain the seller's express consent before offering subagency to other real estate brokers.** This is a deliberate choice the seller makes when signing the exclusive right-to-sell agreement. 

If the seller agrees to allow subagents, how does the listing broker broadcast this to the world? They use the [central nervous system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_nervous_system) of real estate: the MLS. **An offer of subagency is frequently communicated to cooperating brokers through a [Multiple Listing Service](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiple_listing_service).** When you log into the MLS and look at the bottom of a listing ticket, you will see specific [compensation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Remuneration) offered to subagents. 

The creation of the relationship concludes on the other side of town. **A cooperating broker creates a subagency relationship by accepting the listing broker's offer of subagency.** They do this implicitly by showing the [property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_property) to a prospective buyer and explicitly by submitting an offer on that buyer's behalf as a subagent.

## The Allegiance: The Intelligence Agent for the Seller

This is where [human psychology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Psychology) and real estate law violently collide. If you are a subagent, you spend weeks driving a prospective buyer from house to house. You buy them [coffee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coffee). You learn about their children, their dreams, and their [anxieties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anxiety). It feels entirely natural to view them as your client. 

But legally? They are not. **A real estate subagent represents the seller rather than the prospective buyer.** 

Because the subagent operates as an extension of the listing broker, **a real estate subagent owes absolute [fiduciary duties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiduciary) to the seller of the property.** You are effectively an [intelligence agent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Espionage) placed in the field to secure the best possible outcome for the seller. 

> **The Fiduciary Duties of a Subagent (OLD CAR)**
> **The fiduciary duties a subagent owes to a seller include [obedience](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obedience_%28human_behavior%29), [loyalty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duty_of_loyalty), [disclosure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duty_of_disclosure), [confidentiality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confidentiality), [accounting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accounting), and [reasonable care](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duty_of_care).**

![Fiduciary duties demand that an agent acts in good faith and with absolute loyalty to their principal's interests, superseding any allegiance to the customer.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Fiduciary_duty_diagram.png)

To fulfill these duties, **a subagent must act exclusively in the best [financial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finance) interests of the seller.** 

Consider what this means in practice. You are standing in a [living room](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Living_room) with a buyer who says to you, *"We are going to offer \$500,000. We actually have enough [cash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cash) to go up to \$550,000, and because our [lease](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lease) ends next week, we are desperate to close quickly."* 

If you are acting as a subagent, you cannot keep this a secret to help the buyer get a bargain. That would violate your duty of loyalty and disclosure to the seller. By law, **a subagent must disclose any information learned about the buyer's [negotiating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Negotiation) strategy to the seller.** Furthermore, **a subagent must disclose any information learned about the buyer's financial status to the seller.** You are legally obligated to take that vital [intelligence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intelligence_assessment)—the buyer's maximum [budget](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Budget) and their time-sensitive desperation—and hand it directly to the seller so the seller can extract the maximum possible [price](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Price).

## The Line in the Sand: Obligations to the Buyer

If a subagent is essentially an intelligence gatherer for the seller, what exactly do they owe to the buyer? 

The law is clear: **a real estate subagent does not owe fiduciary duties to a prospective buyer.** The buyer is a *[customer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Customer)*, not a *client*. You do not owe them loyalty, you do not owe them confidentiality, and you certainly do not owe them obedience if their instructions [conflict](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conflict_of_interest) with the seller's interests.

However, a lack of fiduciary duty is not a license for villainy. Real estate [markets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_estate_market) rely on baseline [consumer protection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consumer_protection). While you are working for the seller, **a real estate subagent owes a duty of [honesty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Honesty) to a prospective buyer,** as well as **a duty of [fairness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fairness) to a prospective buyer.** You cannot lie, you cannot engage in [fraud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fraud), and you cannot deliberately [misrepresent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Misrepresentation) the condition of the home. 

The most critical application of this fairness involves the physical state of the property. **A real estate subagent must disclose all known material property [defects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latent_defect) to a prospective buyer.** If you notice severe [water damage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water_damage) in the [basement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basement), or if the listing agent mentioned in passing that the [roof](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roof) leaks during heavy [rain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rain), you must tell the buyer. Protecting the seller's financial interests does not extend to covering up objective, material flaws in the real estate itself.

![Subagents are legally obligated to disclose known material property defects to customers, such as structural vulnerabilities that allow water to seep into a basement.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Diagram_of_water_seeping_into_basement.jpg)

### Summary of Obligations

| Duty / Obligation | Owed to the Seller (Principal) | Owed to the Buyer (Customer) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Fiduciary Duties (OLD CAR)** | Yes – Absolute obligation. | No. |
| **Financial Best Interests** | Yes – Must act exclusively for the seller. | No. |
| **Confidentiality** | Yes – Keep seller's secrets. | No – Must share buyer's secrets with seller. |
| **Honesty & Fairness** | Yes. | Yes – Baseline requirement. |
| **Material Defect Disclosure**| Yes. | Yes – Must disclose known defects. |

## The Chain of Peril: Vicarious Liability

If subagency allows a seller to have multiple agents scouring the market on their behalf, why isn't it universally loved? The answer lies in a powerful [legal doctrine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doctrine_%28law%29) called *[vicarious liability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vicarious_liability)*.

Vicarious liability means that a principal is legally responsible for the actions of their agent, even if the principal did not personally commit the act or even know about it. In a subagency relationship, this creates a terrifying chain of liability. 

If a subagent lies to a buyer about the property boundaries or violates [fair housing laws](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fair_housing_%28United_States%29), who gets sued? The subagent, certainly. But because the subagent was acting on the authority of the listing broker, **a listing broker bears vicarious liability for the actions and [misrepresentations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Misrepresentation) made by a subagent.** 

The liability travels even further up the chain. Because the seller is the ultimate principal who authorized the subagency, **a seller bears vicarious liability for the actions and misrepresentations made by a subagent.** 

Imagine being a homeowner and discovering you are being [sued](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lawsuit) for tens of thousands of [dollars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_dollar) because an agent you have never met, working for a brokerage you did not hire, lied to a buyer during a showing. Because of this severe legal exposure, **New York real estate sellers frequently reject offers of subagency to avoid vicarious liability.** The modern trend heavily favors *[buyer agency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buyer_brokerage)*, where the cooperating broker works exclusively for the buyer, severing the seller's liability entirely.

## The Shield of Disclosure: Section 443

We have established that subagency creates an incredible conflict between human assumption and legal reality. The buyer naturally assumes the friendly agent driving them around is *their* agent. To prevent this disastrous misunderstanding, [New York State law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law_of_New_York_%28state%29) forcefully intervenes.

**Section 443 of the [New York Real Property Law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consolidated_Laws_of_New_York) requires a subagent to provide a prospective buyer with a written agency disclosure form.** 

![New York statutory law forcefully intervenes in real estate transactions, requiring mandatory written disclosures to explicitly define an agent's legal allegiance.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Mckinneysnewyorkcplr.JPG)

This form is not a [contract](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contract); it is a profound declaration of [allegiance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allegiance). It clearly states who the agent works for, ensuring the buyer is not tricked into revealing their negotiating strategy to the enemy camp. 

[Timing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time) is everything in [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics), and timing is everything in [real property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_property) law. You cannot wait until the [closing table](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closing_%28real_estate%29) to hand the buyer this form. **A subagent must present the [statutory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statutory_law) New York agency disclosure form to a prospective buyer before obtaining any confidential information.** The precise moment the buyer opens their mouth to discuss their maximum budget, their [divorce](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divorce), or their urgent timeline, you must stop them. You must slide the Section 443 form across the table, explain that you are a subagent representing the seller, and ensure they understand that anything they say can and will be used against them in a real estate negotiation.
