When people buy and sell houses, they want to know exactly how much money they get to keep at the end. Think of it like beating a tough video game and checking your high score. But before you get your final score, the game rules force you to subtract some points. In the real world, the "game rules" are the federal tax laws, and the points subtracted are taxes paid to the government. If you want to help people buy and sell houses, you have to understand how the government (the Internal Revenue Service, or IRS) looks at the money people make. We are going to learn how different kinds of money are grouped, how to measure profits, and why the home you actually live in has special, rule-bending powers!

## The Architecture of Income

To you, a dollar is just a dollar. You can buy the same slice of pizza with a dollar you found on the sidewalk as you can with a dollar you earned mowing lawns. But the IRS sorts money into three totally separate buckets. How you earn the money decides which bucket it goes into, and more importantly, what happens if you lose money.

### 1. Active Income
**Active income** is money earned through direct participation in a business or trade. This means you are actively trading your time, energy, and skills for money. It is just like getting your allowance for cleaning your room or doing chores.

For adults, **wages** are classified by the IRS as active income, and **salaries** are classified by the IRS as active income. If you help someone sell a house and get paid for your hard work, those **commissions** are classified by the IRS as active income. Even extra money given for good service, like **tips**, are classified by the IRS as active income. You did the work actively, so it goes in the active bucket.

### 2. Passive Income
**Passive income** is money earned from enterprises in which the earner does not materially participate. Imagine building a robotic lemonade stand. You set it up once, go to sleep, and wake up with money. You didn't actively stand there pouring lemonade, but the machine made money for you anyway.

In real estate, **rental property income** is generally classified by the IRS as passive income. If someone buys a house, hires a manager to run it, and simply collects rent money every month without doing the daily work, that money goes straight into the passive bucket.

### 3. Portfolio Income
**Portfolio income** is money earned from paper investments. This is like putting your money into a special savings account or buying a rare trading card that goes up in value just by sitting in a binder.

Under the tax rules, **dividends** are classified by the IRS as portfolio income, **interest earned on investments** is classified by the IRS as portfolio income, and **royalties** are classified by the IRS as portfolio income. This is the third bucket.

### The Rule of Loss Containment
Why do we care about these three separate buckets? Because of the rules about losing money!

Sometimes a rental property doesn't make money. Maybe it sits empty, and the owner loses money that year. That loss is called a "passive loss" because it happened in the passive bucket. The IRS has a very strict rule for this: **passive losses can generally only be used to offset passive income.** To "offset" just means using a loss in one place to cancel out a win in another place, so you pay fewer taxes.

But the IRS keeps the lids tight on the other buckets. **Passive losses cannot typically be used to offset active income**, and **passive losses cannot typically be used to offset portfolio income.** 

Think of it like this: If you lose 50 points in a racing video game (a passive loss), you cannot use those lost racing points to get out of doing your math homework (active income). The buckets do not mix!

## The Anatomy of a Capital Gain

When someone buys a house and sells it later, we have to look at the actual profit from the sale. 

First, **real estate is considered a capital asset for federal tax purposes.** Because it is a capital asset, when you sell it, the money you make is called a capital gain. **A capital gain is the profit realized from the sale of a capital asset.** But figuring out the exact profit is not as simple as just looking at the price tag. The IRS uses a step-by-step formula.

### The Timeline of Basis
To find out how much profit can be taxed, we have to trace the history of the house.

1. **The Basis:** This is your starting line. The **basis of a property** is the original purchase price plus acquisition costs (like the fees paid to lawyers to set up the sale). If you buy a skateboard for \$40 and a mandatory safety sticker for \$10, your basis is \$50.
2. **Capital Improvements:** While owning the house, people spend money on it. But there are two very different types of spending:
   * A **capital improvement** is a permanent structural change that enhances the property value.
   * A **capital improvement** is a permanent structural change that extends the useful life of the property. Adding a brand-new roof or building a swimming pool are capital improvements. They make the house permanently better.
   * On the other hand, things break. But **routine maintenance costs for a primary residence are not tax-deductible**, and **repair costs for a primary residence are not tax-deductible**. Fixing a broken window doesn't make the house better than it originally was; it just puts it back to normal. You cannot subtract these costs to save on taxes.
3. **The Adjusted Basis:** Now we find the new starting line. The **adjusted basis of a property** is the original basis plus the cost of capital improvements minus any allowable depreciation. 
*(Note: "Depreciation" is a rule that says things lose value over time, but **homeowners cannot deduct depreciation on a primary residence.** So for the house you actually live in, the depreciation number is just zero!)*

### Calculating the Taxable Gain
When the house is finally sold, we look at the final math.
* The **amount realized from a property sale** is the final sale price minus the costs of selling the property (like paying the real estate agent).
* The **taxable capital gain** is calculated by subtracting the adjusted basis from the amount realized.

Let's look at an example and do the math step-by-step:
Imagine a family buys a house, improves it, and then sells it.

* Step 1: Find the Basis. 
The Original Price (\$200,000) + Buying Fees (\$10,000) = Basis (\$210,000).

* Step 2: Find the Adjusted Basis. 
The Basis (\$210,000) + Adding a New Bedroom/Capital Improvement (\$40,000) = Adjusted Basis (\$250,000).

* Step 3: Find the Amount Realized. 
The Final Sale Price (\$400,000) - Selling Fees (\$20,000) = Amount Realized (\$380,000).

* Step 4: Calculate the Taxable Capital Gain. 
The Amount Realized (\$380,000) - Adjusted Basis (\$250,000) = Taxable Capital Gain (\$130,000).

## The Dimension of Time: Short-Term vs. Long-Term

In the tax world, patience is rewarded. How long you keep the house changes the rules completely.

* A **short-term capital gain** applies to an asset held for exactly one year or less before being sold. If you buy a house and sell it super fast (like a speed-run in a game), you get penalized. **Short-term capital gains are taxed at the taxpayer's ordinary income tax rate**, which is usually a pretty high amount.
* A **long-term capital gain** applies to an asset held for more than one year before being sold. The government likes when people hold onto things for a while to keep neighborhoods stable. Because of this, **long-term capital gains are generally taxed at a lower rate than ordinary income.** 

Waiting just one extra day to cross the one-year mark can save someone thousands of dollars!

## The Crown Jewel of Real Estate Tax Law: The Primary Residence

There is a very special law from the 1990s that you must know. The **Taxpayer Relief Act of 1997 established the current capital gains exclusion rules for primary residences.** 

A "primary residence" just means the main house where you actually live, not a vacation house or a rental. This 1997 law works like an invincibility star in a video game—it protects your money from taxes!

### The \$250,000 / \$500,000 Rule
Under this law, the government lets you keep a huge amount of your profit completely tax-free.
* A **single taxpayer can exclude up to \$250,000** of capital gains from the sale of a primary residence.
* **Married taxpayers filing jointly can exclude up to \$500,000** of capital gains from the sale of a primary residence.

Remember our math example above where the family had a \$130,000 taxable capital gain? If this was their primary residence, that entire \$130,000 is protected because it is less than \$250,000 (for a single person) or \$500,000 (for a married couple). They pay zero taxes on that profit!

### The Two Tests of Eligibility
To get this awesome protection, you have to pass two tests. The IRS wants to make sure this is actually your home.

1. **To qualify for the capital gains exclusion on a primary residence sale, a taxpayer must meet the IRS ownership test.** The IRS ownership test requires the taxpayer to have owned the property for at least two of the five years prior to the sale.
2. **To qualify for the capital gains exclusion on a primary residence sale, a taxpayer must meet the IRS use test.** The IRS use test requires the taxpayer to have used the property as a primary residence for at least two of the five years prior to the sale.

The coolest part? You don't have to do the two years all at once! 
* **The two years of ownership required for the primary residence capital gains exclusion do not have to be consecutive.** (Consecutive just means back-to-back).
* **The two years of primary residence use required for the capital gains exclusion do not have to be consecutive.** 

You could live there for year 1, move away for years 2, 3, and 4, and move back for year 5. Because you lived there for two years total out of the last five, you pass both tests! However, there is a cool-down timer on this power: **Taxpayers can claim the primary residence capital gains exclusion only once every two years.**

## Navigating Primary Residence Deductions

Besides the huge tax break when you sell your house, the government also gives you tax breaks while you live there. A "deduction" is just a way to lower the score of how much money the government thinks you made, which means you pay less in taxes.

### Permitted Deductions
If you own your primary residence, there are three main things you can deduct (subtract) on your taxes:
1. **Property taxes paid on a primary residence are generally deductible from federal income taxes.**
2. When you borrow money to buy a house, you pay extra money back to the bank as a fee. This is called interest. **Mortgage interest paid on a loan used to acquire a primary residence is generally deductible from federal income taxes.** 
3. Sometimes buyers pay a special upfront fee to the bank to make their interest lower. These are called discount points. **Discount points paid to secure a mortgage on a primary residence are generally tax-deductible.**

### What Cannot Be Deducted
But you can't deduct everything! You have to know the limits.
* We already learned that **routine maintenance costs for a primary residence are not tax-deductible**, and **repair costs for a primary residence are not tax-deductible**. Fixing a squeaky door is your own problem, not a tax break.
* Finally, remember that rule about homes losing value over time? **Homeowners cannot deduct depreciation on a primary residence.**
