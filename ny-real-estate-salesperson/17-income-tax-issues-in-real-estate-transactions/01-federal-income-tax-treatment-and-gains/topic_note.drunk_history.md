Because when the ink dries, that brick-and-mortar townhouse phase-shifts into pure capital. And the IRS is the final boss waiting at the exit door to take their cut.

To survive the closing table, you have to understand that the IRS doesn’t just see one big pile of money. They see three strictly quarantined VIP rooms of wealth. If you try to sneak the wrong kind of cash into the wrong room, the bouncers will break your financial kneecaps. 

Room number one is the blood, sweat, and tears room. Here, we find that active income is money earned through direct participation in a business or trade. You hustle, you physically show up, you trade your life force for a paycheck. For you and me, commissions are classified by the IRS as active income. The bartender pouring our drinks? Their tips are classified by the IRS as active income. The nine-to-fivers riding the subway? Their salaries are classified by the IRS as active income, and their hourly wages are classified by the IRS as active income. It’s all active.

Room number two is the velvet-rope dream. Passive income is money earned from enterprises in which the earner does not materially participate. The engine runs while you sleep. When you sell a client a gorgeous Brooklyn duplex and they hire a management company, the rental property income is generally classified by the IRS as passive income. They aren’t swinging the hammers; they’re just cashing the checks.

Room number three is the Wall Street penthouse. Portfolio income is money earned from paper investments. It’s pure financial sorcery. If your client has a fat stock portfolio, their dividends are classified by the IRS as portfolio income. The interest earned on investments is classified by the IRS as portfolio income. And if they wrote a hit song in the nineties, those royalties are classified by the IRS as portfolio income. 

Now, why is the IRS so psychotic about keeping these three rooms totally separated? Because of the Quarantine Rule of Losses. Let’s say your client’s rental property takes a massive hit—the pipes burst, tenants flee, and the building bleeds cash. They’ve generated a passive loss. Can they take that bleeding rental loss, drag it into the active room, and use it to wipe out the taxes on their massive surgeon salary? Absolutely not. Passive losses cannot typically be used to offset active income. Can they drag it upstairs to erase the taxes on their stock market dividends? No! Passive losses cannot typically be used to offset portfolio income. The bio-dome is completely sealed tight, meaning passive losses can generally only be used to offset passive income. You can only put out a passive fire with passive water!

But what happens when your client doesn’t just rent the building out, but decides to sell the whole entire thing? Now we’re talking about the big heist. You see, real estate is considered a capital asset for federal tax purposes. And when you pull off the heist and sell, a capital gain is the profit realized from the sale of a capital asset. 

But the IRS is running a stopwatch the literal second you hand over those keys. If your client flips that property and sells it fast, holding it for exactly one year or less before being sold, a short-term capital gain applies to the asset. And the IRS hates impatient flippers. The penalty for fleeing the scene too fast is brutal: short-term capital gains are taxed at the taxpayer's ordinary income tax rate, which is usually astronomically high. But! If you advise your client to just chill out, pour a drink, and hold the asset for more than one year before being sold, a long-term capital gain applies to the asset. The government loves a patient investor, so to reward them, long-term capital gains are generally taxed at a lower rate than ordinary income. Time is literally a dimension of wealth.

But how do you actually measure the exact amount of the heist? The IRS demands a full autopsy of the property's financial life. You don’t just look at the sale price. You start at the beginning. The basis of a property is the original purchase price plus acquisition costs—like title fees and legal costs. It’s the price of admission. 

Then, over the years, the homeowner starts throwing money at the house. But the IRS divides that money into two ruthless categories. Did you install central air conditioning or build a massive sunroom? Congratulations, a capital improvement is a permanent structural change that enhances the property value, and a capital improvement is a permanent structural change that extends the useful life of the property. But what if you just fix a broken window or patch a leaky roof? Sorry, pal. Repair costs for a primary residence are not tax-deductible. And paying someone to mow the lawn or snake the drain? Nope, routine maintenance costs for a primary residence are not tax-deductible. They just keep the house from falling down; they don't elevate it. 

So, to find out the new starting line, we calculate the mutant Frankenstein number. The adjusted basis of a property is the original basis plus the cost of capital improvements minus any allowable depreciation. (Though, spoiler alert, if your client actually lives there, homeowners cannot deduct depreciation on a primary residence, so that subtraction is a big fat zero). 

Finally, closing day arrives. You take the final sale price, subtract the costs of selling the property like your broker commission and transfer taxes, and that number is the amount realized from a property sale. To find out exactly how much meat is left on the bone for the IRS to chew on, the taxable capital gain is calculated by subtracting the adjusted basis from the amount realized.

Now, lean in close, because I am about to tell you about the greatest legal magic trick in American history. It is the ultimate get-out-of-jail-free card, and it’s the reason people buy houses instead of renting forever. It’s called the Taxpayer Relief Act of 1997. Before this law, people were trapped in a miserable reality-TV survival show, constantly forced to roll their home profits into buying bigger and bigger houses just to hide from the taxman. But the Taxpayer Relief Act of 1997 established the current capital gains exclusion rules for primary residences. 

It blew the doors off the vault! Under this beautiful legislation, a single taxpayer can exclude up to \$250,000 of capital gains from the sale of a primary residence. And if they put a ring on it? Married taxpayers filing jointly can exclude up to \$500,000 of capital gains from the sale of a primary residence! Half a million dollars in profit, completely invisible to the IRS. Poof! 

But the IRS isn't going to just hand over half a million tax-free bucks to any random flipper off the street. To unlock this vault and qualify for the capital gains exclusion on a primary residence sale, a taxpayer must meet the IRS ownership test, AND to qualify for the capital gains exclusion on a primary residence sale, a taxpayer must meet the IRS use test. 

It’s an undercover operation. The IRS ownership test requires the taxpayer to have owned the property for at least two of the five years prior to the sale. Furthermore, the IRS use test requires the taxpayer to have used the property as a primary residence for at least two of the five years prior to the sale. You have to actually live the cover story! 

But here is the phenomenal loophole you can sell to your clients: the two years of ownership required for the primary residence capital gains exclusion do not have to be consecutive! And miracles upon miracles, the two years of primary residence use required for the capital gains exclusion do not have to be consecutive! A client could buy a place, live in it for a year, get transferred to London for three years, rent it out, move back for year five, and boom—they pass the tests. The only catch is they can't spam the cheat code. Taxpayers can claim the primary residence capital gains exclusion only once every two years. 

And as if the final payout wasn't enough, the government literally subsidizes the everyday life of the homeowner to keep them in the game. When a terrified first-time buyer asks you how they can afford the monthly carry, you hit them with the swag bag of write-offs. Did you know property taxes paid on a primary residence are generally deductible from federal income taxes? Oh, it gets better. You know that massive, terrifying loan they took out? Mortgage interest paid on a loan used to acquire a primary residence is generally deductible from federal income taxes. In the first few years of a mortgage, almost the whole payment is interest, making this a colossal shield against the IRS. And if they threw down cash on day one to buy a lower interest rate from the bank, those discount points paid to secure a mortgage on a primary residence are generally tax-deductible!
