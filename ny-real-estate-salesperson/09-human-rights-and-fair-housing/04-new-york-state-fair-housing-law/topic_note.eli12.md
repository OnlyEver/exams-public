Think of fair housing laws in the United States like the rules of a giant video game. The federal Fair Housing Act establishes a baseline of protected classes including race, color, religion, national origin, sex, familial status, and disability. This means no matter where you live in the country, landlords cannot treat you unfairly based on these basic traits. 

But if you are playing the game in New York, the rules level up! The New York State Human Rights Law introduces additional protected housing classes beyond the baseline federal fair housing protections. And if you go all the way into New York City, there is a third, super-strict rulebook you have to follow. 

Learning these rules isn't just about passing a test. It tells you exactly how you are allowed to advertise a house, who you can rent to, and how to treat people fairly.

## The State Standard: The New York State Human Rights Law

The New York State Human Rights Law casts a giant shield over people looking for a place to live. The New York State Division of Human Rights is the government agency responsible for enforcing the New York State Human Rights Law. Think of them as the game's referees. If a landlord breaks a rule, this is the group that investigates and hands out penalties.

Instead of just memorizing a boring list, think of these rules as specific parts of a person's life that a landlord is simply not allowed to poke their nose into.

### Age and Marital Status: The "Quiet Preference" Trap

Age is a legally protected class in housing transactions under the New York State Human Rights Law. This means a New York State landlord cannot legally reject an adult housing applicant simply because the landlord prefers older tenants. Whether someone is 19 or 90 years old, their age cannot be the reason they get turned away.

Also, marital status is a legally protected class in housing transactions under the New York State Human Rights Law. Real estate licensees in New York State must treat unmarried couples exactly the same as married couples during any housing transaction. You can't ask a boyfriend and girlfriend for extra paperwork if you wouldn't ask a married husband and wife for the same paperwork.

Sometimes, people break these rules by accident when writing ads. 

> **The Advertising Trap:** 
> Imagine you write an ad that says an apartment is perfect for "mature couples." Real estate advertising stating a preference for mature couples violates age protections under the New York State Human Rights Law because "mature" sounds like you don't want young adults. Furthermore, real estate advertising stating a preference for mature couples violates marital status protections under the New York State Human Rights Law because the word "couples" leaves out single people.

### Income and Employment Protections

Sometimes landlords try to be picky about how someone gets their money. New York says no way! Lawful source of income is a legally protected class in housing transactions under the New York State Human Rights Law. As long as the money is legal, it all spends exactly the same. For example, if rent is \$1,000, it doesn't matter if that \$1,000 comes from a regular job or from government help.

*   **Vouchers:** New York State landlords cannot legally refuse to rent to a tenant solely because the tenant will pay rent using a Section 8 housing voucher. 
*   **Benefits:** New York State landlords must accept social security benefits as a valid source of income for housing applications. 
*   **Family Payments:** New York State landlords must accept child support payments as a valid source of income for housing applications. Also, New York State landlords must accept alimony payments as a valid source of income for housing applications.

Plus, military status is a legally protected class in housing transactions under the New York State Human Rights Law. Landlords in New York State cannot legally deny an apartment lease to an active-duty military member based on the applicant's service obligations. A landlord can't say no just because they are worried the soldier might have to leave for an army mission later.

### Identity, Biology, and Life Circumstances

New York actively protects the deeply personal parts of who someone is and their medical health. 
*   **Identity:** Sexual orientation is a legally protected class in housing transactions under the New York State Human Rights Law. Likewise, gender identity or expression is a legally protected class in housing transactions under the New York State Human Rights Law.
*   **Health:** Genetic predisposition is a legally protected class in housing transactions under the New York State Human Rights Law. Also, pregnancy-related conditions are legally protected against housing discrimination under the New York State Human Rights Law.

The state also acts as a shield for people in dangerous home situations. Domestic violence victim status is a legally protected class in housing transactions under the New York State Human Rights Law. This means a New York State landlord cannot legally evict a tenant solely because the tenant is a victim of domestic violence. 

Finally, citizenship or immigration status is a legally protected class in housing transactions under the New York State Human Rights Law. A New York State landlord cannot legally deny an apartment to a prospective tenant solely because the tenant is not a United States citizen. As long as they have the money and meet the regular background checks, their passport country doesn't matter.

### The Legal System: Arrest Records

When landlords run background checks, they have to be careful about what they are actually allowed to look at.
*   The New York State Human Rights Law protects housing applicants from discrimination based on prior arrest records resolved in the applicant's favor. If you were arrested but the police realized they made a mistake and let you go, a landlord can't hold that against you.
*   Also, the New York State Human Rights Law protects individuals from housing discrimination based on pending arrests. Since you are innocent until proven guilty in America, a landlord can't punish you for an arrest if you haven't even had your trial yet.

## The Municipal Maximum: The New York City Human Rights Law

If the federal laws are the basic rulebook, and the state laws are the advanced rulebook, the city laws are the ultimate master-level rulebook. The New York City Human Rights Law provides additional housing discrimination protections beyond both federal and New York State laws. 

The New York City Commission on Human Rights is the government agency responsible for enforcing the New York City Human Rights Law. If you work in New York City, there are two extra, super-specific rules you must know:

**1. Lawful Occupation**
Lawful occupation is a uniquely protected housing class under the New York City Human Rights Law. A New York City landlord cannot legally refuse to rent an apartment to an applicant solely because the landlord dislikes the applicant's specific profession. If your job is legal, you're good! A landlord can't turn you away just because they personally dislike lawyers or musicians.

**2. Partnership Status**
Partnership status is a uniquely protected housing class under the New York City Human Rights Law. The partnership status protection in New York City prevents landlords from discriminating against individuals in legally registered domestic partnerships. This just means two people who are officially partners get the exact same fairness as two people who are officially married.

## When the Rules Flex: Legal Exemptions

Sometimes, if a landlord lives in the very same building they are renting out, the rules bend just a little bit. But New York is much stricter about this than the federal government! The federal rules let landlords bend the rules if their building has up to four units. But the New York State owner-occupied fair housing exemption is stricter than the federal exemption because the state exemption caps the limit at two units instead of four. 

Let's do a quick math check. Imagine a landlord lives in a building and wants to know if they get this exemption:
1. Count the total units in the building. Let's say there are 3 units.
2. Look at the New York State maximum allowed limit, which is 2 units.
3. Compare the two numbers: 3 is greater than 2.
4. Because the building has more units than the state allows, it does NOT get the exemption!

Here are the specific times the rules can bend in New York:
1.  **Small Owner-Occupied Buildings:** The New York State Human Rights Law completely exempts owner-occupied one-family buildings from its anti-discrimination housing provisions. Also, the New York State Human Rights Law completely exempts owner-occupied two-family buildings from its anti-discrimination housing provisions. (Remember, the owner *must* live there for this to count!).
2.  **Room Rentals:** The New York State Human Rights Law exempts room rentals in an owner-occupied housing unit from its anti-discrimination provisions. If you are renting out a spare bedroom inside your own apartment, you get to pick who lives with you.
3.  **Single-Sex Rooming:** The New York State Human Rights Law exempts room rentals in housing intended for single-sex occupancy from its anti-discrimination provisions (like an all-girls boarding house).
4.  **Senior Housing:** The New York State Human Rights Law exempts housing explicitly designated for persons age fifty-five or older from its age discrimination provisions. This is how retirement communities are legally allowed to stay just for older folks!

> **Crucial Exam Warning:** 
> Even if a landlord gets an exemption, a real estate agent *never* gets an exemption! And no one is ever allowed to write a discriminatory ad. Also, you can never, ever discriminate based on race. There are zero exceptions for race.

## Disability Accommodations and Modifications

When it comes to protecting people with disabilities, there is a big difference between changing a "rule" and changing a "building."

**Accommodations (Changing the Rules)**
The New York State Human Rights Law requires housing providers to make reasonable accommodations in rules or policies for individuals with disabilities. This doesn't cost the landlord any money; they just have to be nice and flex their rules. 
*   *Example:* A landlord enforcing a strict no-pet policy against a blind applicant using a guide dog violates state disability protections. A guide dog is a medical helper, not a pet! 

**Modifications (Changing the Building)**
If a disabled person physically cannot use their apartment, they are allowed to fix it. Landlords must permit disabled tenants to make reasonable architectural modifications to an apartment at the tenant's own expense. 
*   *Example:* If a tenant uses a wheelchair and needs grab bars in the shower, they can install them. The landlord has to say yes, but the tenant has to pay for the tools and the work.

## Enforcement in the Field: Testers and Retaliation

How do the referees catch people breaking the rules? They use mystery shoppers! Fair housing testing utilizes trained individuals posing as housing applicants to uncover illegal discriminatory practices by real estate professionals. Testers work in pairs, pretending to look for apartments, to see if an agent treats one person wonderfully and the other person poorly.

If someone catches a landlord or agent breaking the rules, they can file a complaint. And if the landlord gets mad about it? Too bad! 
*   Retaliation against an individual for filing a fair housing complaint is strictly illegal under the New York State Human Rights Law. 
*   Retaliation against an individual for filing a fair housing complaint is strictly illegal under the New York City Human Rights Law. 

Retaliation means getting revenge. If a landlord tries to kick someone out or ignore their phone calls just because they complained to the referee, the landlord gets in huge trouble.

As a future real estate salesperson, you are the gatekeeper to housing! Learning these rules makes sure you play the game fairly, ethically, and legally in New York.
