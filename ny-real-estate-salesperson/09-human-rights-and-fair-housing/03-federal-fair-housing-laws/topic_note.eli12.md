Imagine you are trading your favorite video games online. What if the trading site had a rule that said, "No one with freckles is allowed to trade here"? You would probably say, "That makes no sense! Freckles have nothing to do with gaming!" 

In the real adult world, buying, selling, or renting a home is a huge deal. For a long time, the rules weren't fair. People were left out simply because of who they were. That’s why we have civil rights laws. These laws are like the ultimate server rules. They make sure that no matter who you are, you have a fair chance to find a place to live or to open up a business. 

## The Inviolable Foundation: 1866 and *Jones v. Mayer*

Right after the American Civil War, the government made a very important rule to make sure everyone was treated fairly when dealing with property. 

The **Civil Rights Act of 1866** prohibits discrimination in the sale or rental of property based on race. Furthermore, the Civil Rights Act of 1866 prohibits discrimination based on color in addition to race. 

Why is a law from the 1800s still super important today? Because it is incredibly powerful. First, the Civil Rights Act of 1866 applies to both residential and commercial real estate transactions. That means it covers homes where people live (residential) AND places like arcades, pizza shops, or giant office buildings (commercial). Second, there are absolutely no exceptions or exemptions to the Civil Rights Act of 1866 regarding racial discrimination. None. Zero. There are no secret loopholes. 

About a hundred years later, this strict rule was challenged in court, but the judges kept it strong. The 1968 United States Supreme Court case *Jones versus Mayer* upheld the Civil Rights Act of 1866. In a huge win for fairness, the *Jones versus Mayer* decision affirmed that no property owner can legally discriminate on the basis of race. Even if someone is selling their private house to a neighbor without using a real estate agent, they still cannot be racist. 

## The Modern Framework: Title VIII of the Civil Rights Act of 1968

The 1866 law was great for protecting people based on race and color, but what about other unfair things? During the 1960s, the government added a massive update, kind of like a big patch for a video game. 

**Title VIII of the Civil Rights Act of 1968** is commonly known as the federal **Fair Housing Act**. 

When this new law first came out, the original federal Fair Housing Act of 1968 prohibited housing discrimination based on four things: **race, color, religion, and national origin**. ("National origin" just means the country you or your ancestors came from.)

Just having a rule on paper isn't enough; you need a referee to enforce it. The Department of Housing and Urban Development administers and enforces the federal Fair Housing Act. People usually just call them HUD. They act like the referees who step in with penalties if landlords or real estate agents break the rules.

## The Evolution of Equality: Expansions in 1974 and 1988

As time went on, the rules needed a few more updates to be totally fair. Congress expanded the rules twice to protect more people.

1. **The 1974 Expansion:** The Housing and Community Development Act of 1974 added **sex** to the list of federally protected classes. (This means treating men and women fairly).
2. **The 1988 Expansions:** The Fair Housing Amendments Act of 1988 added **familial status** to the list of federally protected classes. At the exact same time, the Fair Housing Amendments Act of 1988 added **disability** to the list of federally protected classes.

Let's look at the scoreboard today. The federal Fair Housing Act protects exactly seven distinct classes of people. You have to memorize exactly what is on this list!

## Navigating Familial Status in Practice

Let's talk about "familial status." What does that actually mean? 

> **Familial Status:** Familial status protections apply to households containing individuals under the age of 18 living with a parent or legal guardian. 

Basically, this means people with kids! A landlord cannot say, "I won't rent to you because you have a loud 5-year-old." The rules also protect families before the kid even arrives. Pregnant women are explicitly protected under the familial status provision of the federal Fair Housing Act. Also, individuals in the process of securing legal custody of a minor are protected under the familial status provision of the federal Fair Housing Act. (For example, if an aunt is adopting her nephew, she is protected).

### The Exemption: Senior Housing
There is one special exception to this rule. Sometimes older folks want to live in quiet retirement neighborhoods with only other seniors. Because age is not a federally protected class, this is actually allowed! Housing designed specifically for older persons is legally exempt from the familial status protections of the federal Fair Housing Act. 

But a landlord can't just slap an "Adults Only" sign on an apartment building because they dislike teenagers. They have to follow strict legal math. The Housing for Older Persons Act of 1995 established the criteria for communities to qualify as exempt from familial status discrimination. 

For example, to legally keep kids out, a community might be required to have 80% of its apartments occupied by at least one person who is 55 or older. Let's do the math to see what that looks like!

**Math Example:**
If a senior apartment building has 150 total apartments, how many of those apartments must have someone 55 or older to meet the 80% rule?

**Step 1:** Find the total number of apartments. (150)
**Step 2:** Change the required percentage (80%) into a decimal. (80 / 100 = 0.80)
**Step 3:** Multiply the total apartments by the decimal. (150 x 0.80)
**Step 4:** The answer is 120. So, at least 120 apartments must have an older adult living in them!

## Defining and Accommodating Disability

Adding "disability" to the law in 1988 changed a lot of things. Disability protections under the federal Fair Housing Act cover individuals with physical or mental impairments. 

To be protected, it has to be a real, serious challenge. An impairment must substantially limit one or more major life activities to qualify as a disability under the federal Fair Housing Act. Major life activities are things you do every day, like walking, seeing, or hearing.

When someone meets this definition, landlords have to do two things: make **accommodations** (changes to the rules) and allow **modifications** (changes to the building).

*   **Rules and Policies:** The federal Fair Housing Act requires housing providers to make reasonable accommodations in rules and policies for individuals with disabilities. 
    *   *Example:* If a building says "No Pets Allowed," the landlord MUST change that rule to let a blind person keep their guide dog. The dog isn't a pet; it's a helper!
*   **Physical Alterations:** The federal Fair Housing Act requires landlords to allow tenants with disabilities to make reasonable modifications to a dwelling at the tenant's expense. 
    *   *Example:* If someone uses a wheelchair, the landlord must let them build a wheelchair ramp. However, the tenant has to pay the \$500 (or whatever it costs) out of their own pocket—meaning, at their own expense. 

### The Boundary of Protection: Addiction and Substance Use

The law has a very strict line when it comes to illegal drugs. Individuals currently engaging in the illegal use of controlled substances are not protected under the disability provision of the federal Fair Housing Act. If someone is breaking the law with illegal drugs right now, a landlord can legally kick them out.

However, the law is very kind to people who are trying to get better. Individuals recovering from drug addiction in an approved treatment program are protected under the disability provision of the federal Fair Housing Act. Overcoming an addiction is treated like a severe medical condition. A landlord cannot say "no" to renting to someone just because they are going to rehab and trying to turn their life around.
