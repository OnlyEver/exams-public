To understand the modern real estate skyline, we have to dig down to the foundation, poured right after the Civil War. The Civil Rights Act of 1866 prohibits discrimination in the sale or rental of property based on race. And because prejudiced minds love to play malicious games with semantics, the Civil Rights Act of 1866 prohibits discrimination based on color in addition to race. Why does an antique piece of legislation matter when you're trying to lease a modern Manhattan commercial loft? Because of its terrifying, magnificent reach! The Civil Rights Act of 1866 applies to both residential and commercial real estate transactions. A bodega? A penthouse? An industrial warehouse? It covers it all. And here is the absolute mic-drop, the ultimate legal checkmate you must brand into your brain: there are absolutely no exceptions or exemptions to the Civil Rights Act of 1866 regarding racial discrimination. None. Zero. You can't claim your building is a private club or a tiny duplex to get out of it. 

If anyone thought they could find a loophole in that absolute standard, the Supreme Court dropped a judicial anvil on their heads a century later. The 1968 United States Supreme Court case Jones versus Mayer upheld the Civil Rights Act of 1866. In a landmark ruling that rattled the windows of every brokerage in America, the Jones versus Mayer decision affirmed that no property owner can legally discriminate on the basis of race. Period. 

But while that 1866 law was a laser beam focused solely on race and color, the rest of the housing market was still the Wild West, full of landlords acting like tyrannical reality TV judges voting people out of apartments for terrible reasons. So, Congress dropped the motherlode. Title VIII of the Civil Rights Act of 1968 is commonly known as the federal Fair Housing Act. When it first hit the books, the original federal Fair Housing Act of 1968 prohibited housing discrimination based on race, color, religion, and national origin. Four VIP categories permanently etched onto the guest list. But laws are just polite suggestions unless you have a bouncer at the door. To make sure property owners weren't just nodding and winking, the Department of Housing and Urban Development administers and enforces the federal Fair Housing Act. HUD is the ultimate casino pit boss, utilizing investigators and massive fines to ruin the day of any agent who thinks they can cheat the system.

Now, society shifts, the market shifts, and the law had to evolve. Over the next few decades, Congress updated the VIP list. In the disco era, the Housing and Community Development Act of 1974 added sex to the list of federally protected classes. Then came the blockbuster double-feature of the neon era: the Fair Housing Amendments Act of 1988 added familial status to the list of federally protected classes. At the exact same time, the Fair Housing Amendments Act of 1988 added disability to the list of federally protected classes. 

Drink this in and memorize the final recipe. The federal Fair Housing Act protects exactly seven distinct classes of people. The magnificent seven. The seven federally protected classes are race, color, religion, national origin, sex, familial status, and disability.

Let’s talk about the absolute chaos of children, because "familial status" is a legal tripwire. It isn't just a fancy phrase for a holiday family reunion. Familial status protections apply to households containing individuals under the age of 18 living with a parent or legal guardian. You cannot deny a lease to a stressed-out single dad just because his toddler uses the hallways as a racetrack. And the law is aggressively proactive. It protects the *blueprint* of a kid! Pregnant women are explicitly protected under the familial status provision of the federal Fair Housing Act. It also protects the *paperwork* of a kid! Individuals in the process of securing legal custody of a minor are protected under the familial status provision of the federal Fair Housing Act. You cannot shut the door on someone before the child even legally crosses the threshold.

But what if people want to retire, drink martinis, and play pickleball without tripping over a tricycle? Because age isn't federally protected, developers can build a literal fortress of solitude. Housing designed specifically for older persons is legally exempt from the familial status protections of the federal Fair Housing Act. However, you can't just slap a "No Kids Allowed" sign on a rusty apartment complex and call it a retirement home to dodge a lawsuit. To prevent fake adult-only clubs, the Housing for Older Persons Act of 1995 established the criteria for communities to qualify as exempt from familial status discrimination. You have to meet strict, relentless statistical thresholds—like 80% of units having someone 55 or older—to legally pull up the drawbridge against the toddlers.

Now, we have to navigate the 1988 disability rules, because this is where careless agents trip over their own shoelaces and plunge directly into a lawsuit. Disability protections under the federal Fair Housing Act cover individuals with physical or mental impairments. But a papercut doesn't count. The threshold is high: an impairment must substantially limit one or more major life activities to qualify as a disability under the federal Fair Housing Act. We're talking walking, seeing, hearing, or caring for oneself—the main plotlines of human existence. 

When your client hits that threshold, you must understand the difference between changing the rulebook and swinging a hammer. First, the ink. The federal Fair Housing Act requires housing providers to make reasonable accommodations in rules and policies for individuals with disabilities. This is free. It means if your snooty co-op has a strict "no pets" policy, you accommodate the blind tenant's guide dog because it's not a pet, it's a lifeline. Second, the wood and nails. The federal Fair Housing Act requires landlords to allow tenants with disabilities to make reasonable modifications to a dwelling at the tenant's expense. You hear that? If they need to widen a doorway for a wheelchair or install grab bars in the shower, the tenant pays for the construction. The landlord just has to get out of the way and let the remodeling happen.

Finally, let’s talk about the sharpest, most violently misunderstood line in the entire law: substances. The law treats active illegal drug use like an active grenade in a kitchen. Individuals currently engaging in the illegal use of controlled substances are not protected under the disability provision of the federal Fair Housing Act. If your tenant is actively dealing or using illegal narcotics in apartment 4B, you can launch them out of there with an eviction cannon. But if they have turned their life around? It’s a totally different movie. Individuals recovering from drug addiction in an approved treatment program are protected under the disability provision of the federal Fair Housing Act. The federal government recognizes past addiction as a monstrous health battle they are actively winning. If they are faithfully attending rehab or a methadone clinic, denying them housing is a direct, catastrophic violation of federal law.
