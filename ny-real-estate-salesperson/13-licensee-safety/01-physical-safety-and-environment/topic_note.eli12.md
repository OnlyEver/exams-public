Selling houses is basically an exercise in staying safe and playing smart. A lot of new agents spend hours studying rules and money, but they forget to protect the most important thing of all: themselves! We use passwords to protect our video game accounts and use banks to keep our allowances safe. Yet, sometimes people forget to have a safety plan when showing houses. If you want to be a great real estate agent in New York, you have to treat your safety with the same super-focus you use when trying to beat a tough video game level.

When you think about it, real estate is a job where you agree to meet strangers, often alone, inside empty houses. To stay safe, we need strict rules for who we meet, where we walk, and how we keep track of everyone.

## The Architecture of Identity and the Initial Encounter

Before you ever take someone to look at a house, you need to set up a safe zone. The biggest risk is not knowing who you are meeting. Your first job is to figure out exactly who they are by following strict rules.

### Controlling the Digital and Physical Boundary
Your safety plan starts before you even meet anyone. Think about playing an online game—you wouldn't give out your real-life address in the public chat! It is exactly the same here. **A real estate licensee should avoid publishing a personal home phone number on public marketing materials.** And to keep your real-life base totally secret, **a real estate licensee should avoid publishing a personal home address on public real estate websites.** You want people to only contact you through your professional work office.

When you are inside your work base, the rules are simple. **A real estate licensee working alone in the office after hours should keep the main entrance doors locked.** If you are doing extra work late at night, you get to choose who comes inside. 

### The Staging Ground: The Brokerage Office
When a new person (often called a prospect) wants to see a house, it is tempting to just meet them right at the front door to save time. Don't do it! **A real estate licensee should conduct the initial meeting with a new client at the real estate brokerage office.** 

Meeting at your office is like having the home-field advantage in a sports game. You are in your safe zone with your teammates around. While you are there, **a real estate licensee should introduce a new prospect to at least one colleague at the real estate office.** This isn't just to be polite. It shows the new person that someone else has seen their face and knows who they are.

### Verifying the Variable: Identity Intake
When people walk into your office, you need to know their stats. **Real estate brokerages should utilize a Prospect Identification Form for all new walk-in clients.** Think of this like a sign-up sheet for a new club.

> **The Prospect Identification Form**
> This form does two very important things:
> 1. A **Prospect Identification Form collects basic contact information from a new client**, like their name and phone number.
> 2. A **Prospect Identification Form collects vehicle details from a new client**, like what kind of car they drive and their license plate number.

But you can't just trust what they write down. **A real estate licensee should require a new prospect to provide a government-issued photo identification**, like a driver's license. In fact, **a government-issued photo identification is required before a real estate licensee shows properties to a new prospect.** To be extra safe, **a real estate brokerage should keep a photocopy of a new prospect's identification on file at the office.**

## The Mechanics of Departure and Transit

Once you know exactly who the person is, you need a plan for leaving the office. If you just disappear to look at a house, your team won't know how to help you if you ever need it.

### The Flight Plan: Property Itineraries
Before you walk out the door, **a real estate licensee must leave a detailed itinerary of all scheduled property showings at the brokerage office.** An itinerary is basically a treasure map of exactly where you are going. 

To make a good itinerary, you must include two things:
1. **A property showing itinerary must include the specific property addresses being visited.**
2. **A property showing itinerary must include the estimated return time to the real estate office.**

Here is how you figure out your return time using simple math. Let's say you plan to spend 30 minutes at the house, and it takes 15 minutes to drive each way:
1. First, write down the driving time to get to the house: 15 minutes.
2. Next, add the time you will spend showing the house: 15 + 30 = 45 minutes.
3. Finally, add the time it takes to drive back to your office: 45 + 15 = 60 minutes.

So, you write down that you will be back in exactly 60 minutes (which is 1 hour). If that hour passes and you haven't called, your team knows to check on you!

### Communication and Contingencies
Before leaving, **a real estate licensee should ensure a mobile phone is fully charged before departing for property showings.** A phone with a dead battery is just a heavy piece of glass! Also, **a real estate licensee should pre-program emergency contact numbers into a mobile phone on speed dial.** If you are scared, it is hard to type in numbers, so speed dial makes it easy.

You also need a secret plan with your office. **A real estate licensee should establish a predetermined distress code word to use during an emergency.** Like a secret password with your friends, **a distress code word allows a real estate licensee to discreetly signal the office for help over a phone call** without making the person you are with suspicious. (For example, asking the office to find the "purple folder" when everyone knows there is no purple folder!)

### The Physics of Transit
When it is time to leave, **a real estate licensee should require the prospect to drive a separate vehicle to the property showing.** This rule is super important. **Having the prospect drive a separate vehicle prevents the real estate licensee from being trapped in a car with a stranger.** 

If you ever feel unsure about someone, you can use multiplayer mode! **A real estate licensee should use a buddy system when showing properties to an unfamiliar prospect.** Bringing a partner makes everything much safer.

## The Environment: Arriving at the Property

When you finally get to the house, your goal is to set things up safely.

### Asset Management: Vehicles and Valuables
Where you park your car is a big deal. **A real estate licensee should park the vehicle on the street instead of the property driveway.** Why? **Parking a vehicle on the street prevents the vehicle from being blocked in by another car.** If you park in the driveway, someone else could park right behind you, trapping your car. Also, **a real estate licensee should keep vehicle keys physically accessible at all times during a property showing**, like right in your pocket.

You should also be careful about what you wear and bring. **A real estate licensee should avoid wearing expensive jewelry to property showings.** Also, **a real estate licensee should avoid carrying large amounts of cash during property showings** (like having \$500 in your wallet). You don't want to carry around flashy things that bad guys might want to steal.

### Securing the Stage: Preparing the Vacant Home
An empty house can have dark, scary corners. Because of this, **a real estate licensee should arrive early to a vacant property showing to inspect the premises.** You want to look around before the client gets there.

While looking around, you must do two things:
1. **A real estate licensee should turn on all lights in a vacant property before the prospect arrives.** Lights get rid of scary shadows.
2. **A real estate licensee should unlock all necessary exit doors in a vacant property before the prospect arrives.** You always want a fast way out, just in case!

## Navigating Space: The Tour Protocol

When the person arrives to look at the house, how you walk around together matters a whole lot.

| Tactical Showing Practice | Why it Matters (The Rules of Safety) |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Let Them Lead** | **A real estate licensee should direct the prospect to walk in front of the licensee during a property tour.** Why? **Walking behind the prospect ensures the real estate licensee never turns away from the prospect.** You always want to keep your eyes on them! |
| **Stay Out of Traps** | **A real estate licensee should avoid entering confined spaces alongside a prospect during a property showing.** A confined space is a small room with only one way out. Stay in the hallway instead! |

### Identifying Confined Spaces
What is a confined space? It is basically a dead-end in a maze.
* **Attics are considered confined spaces presenting a safety risk during property showings.**
* **Basements are considered confined spaces presenting a safety risk during property showings.**
* **Walk-in closets are considered confined spaces presenting a safety risk during property showings.**

Just point out these tricky rooms from the doorway and let the client walk inside all by themselves.

## The Conclusion: Securing the Asset

When an open house is finally over and everyone goes home, the house is empty again. 

Before you leave and lock up, **a real estate licensee must thoroughly inspect all rooms at the conclusion of an open house.** This means checking behind doors and under stairs. Why do we do this? **Inspecting all rooms after an open house ensures no unauthorized individuals remain hidden inside the property.** 

Every single rule we talked about—from checking photo IDs to parking your car on the street—works together like pieces of a puzzle to keep you safe. Always stick to the rules and protect yourself first!
