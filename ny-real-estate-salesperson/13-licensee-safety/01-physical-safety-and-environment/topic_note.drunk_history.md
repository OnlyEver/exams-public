Listen to me. Put down the pretzel and look me in the eyes. I know you’ve been studying for the New York Real Estate Exam by memorizing amortization schedules, agency disclosures, and the agonizing details of riparian rights. That’s great. You know how to protect a client’s escrow funds. But you know what you are completely failing to underwrite? Your own pulse! 

Real estate is the only profession where we put on a tailored suit, drive to a totally vacant, isolated building, and willingly meet a complete stranger we met on the internet. It is a psychological heist movie, and you are the vault! If you want to survive in this industry, you have to treat your physical safety with the exact same paranoid, meticulous, iron-clad precision that you apply to a commercial lease agreement. 

First things first: you have to secure your public footprint. You wouldn’t commingle a client’s earnest money with your grocery budget, so why are you commingling your personal life with your MLS profile? You must avoid publishing a personal home phone number on public marketing materials. You also absolutely avoid publishing a personal home address on public real estate websites. You are building a digital firewall. You only exist at the brokerage. And speaking of the brokerage, if you are working alone in the office after hours, you must keep the main entrance doors locked. You control the ingress and egress of your own fortress. No unauthorized encumbrances walking through the door at 9:00 PM!

Now, let’s say a brand new lead calls you up. They want to see a vacant colonial on Elm Street right now. Human nature says, "Go meet them!" I am telling you, absolutely not. A property showing is the closing table; you don't go to the closing table without underwriting the buyer first! You should always conduct the initial meeting with a new client at the real estate brokerage office. Make them come to your home turf. 

When this walk-in prospect arrives, your office needs to hit them with the intake HUD-1: the brokerage should utilize a Prospect Identification Form for all new walk-in clients. This isn't just polite paperwork. This form does two vital things: it collects basic contact information from a new client, and critically, it collects vehicle details from a new client. I want their make, model, and license plate. But self-reported data is basically an unverified Zestimate—worthless. A government-issued photo identification is required before you show properties to a new prospect. No ID, no showing. It is the earnest money of meeting me. And just to clear the title on their identity, the real estate brokerage should keep a photocopy of that new prospect's identification on file at the office. 

While you've got them in the lobby, you need to notarize their physical presence. You should introduce that new prospect to at least one colleague at the real estate office. You are establishing a human witness. You are looking the prospect in the eye and silently saying, *My colleague Dave just memorized your face, pal.* 

Alright, the identity checks out. It's time to head to the property. But before you vanish into the field, you have to file your flight plan. You must leave a detailed itinerary of all scheduled property showings at the brokerage office. This is non-negotiable. That property showing itinerary must include the specific property addresses being visited, and it must include your estimated return time to the real estate office. If that estimated return time hits and you aren't back, the broker knows the transaction has defaulted and it's time to send the cavalry. 

Before you walk out the door, check your gear. You should ensure your mobile phone is fully charged before departing for property showings. A dead battery is a breached contract. Furthermore, you should pre-program emergency contact numbers into that mobile phone on speed dial so you aren't fumbling through your contacts when things go sideways. You also need a "force majeure" clause—you should establish a predetermined distress code word to use during an emergency. This distress code word allows you to discreetly signal the office for help over a phone call without tipping off the person you are with. If you call the broker and say, "Hey, can you pull the *neon file* for Elm Street?", they know the neon file doesn't exist. They know to call the police. 

Now, we head to the cars. And I mean *cars*, plural. You should require the prospect to drive a separate vehicle to the property showing. Never, ever play chauffeur to a stranger. Having the prospect drive a separate vehicle prevents you from being trapped in a car with a stranger. You are not joint tenants in a Honda Civic! And look, if your gut is telling you something is off, or you are dealing with a totally unfamiliar prospect, you should use a buddy system when showing properties. You co-broker the showing. Two agents fundamentally shift the power dynamic. 

You arrive at the listing. How you approach the physical environment is everything. You should park your vehicle on the street instead of the property driveway. Driveways are illiquid assets! They are architectural traps. Parking a vehicle on the street prevents your vehicle from being blocked in by another car. You need an immediate, unobstructed vector of escape. And to make sure you can execute that escape, you should keep your vehicle keys physically accessible at all times during a property showing. Do not bury them at the bottom of a tote bag! 

Also, look at yourself. You are not a walking jumbo loan. You should avoid wearing expensive jewelry to property showings, and you should avoid carrying large amounts of cash. Don't make yourself a lucrative target of opportunity. 

Because you are a professional, you should arrive early to a vacant property showing to inspect the premises. You clear the title of the environment before the buyer gets there. You do two things immediately: First, you should turn on all lights in a vacant property before the prospect arrives to eliminate every shadow and blind corner. Second, you should unlock all necessary exit doors in a vacant property before the prospect arrives. You never lock yourself inside a building with a stranger. You always maintain a secondary route of egress. 

The client arrives. The tour begins. Welcome to the physics of space management. You should direct the prospect to walk in front of you during a property tour. Why? Because walking behind the prospect ensures you never turn away from them. You never expose your back to an unverified contingency! 

As you navigate the floorplan, you must protect your escape routes. You should avoid entering confined spaces alongside a prospect during a property showing. A confined space is any room where your only exit is blocked by the person you're touring with. So what falls into this category? Attics are considered confined spaces presenting a safety risk during property showings. Basements are considered confined spaces presenting a safety risk. Walk-in closets are considered confined spaces presenting a safety risk. When you get to the primary bedroom's massive walk-in closet, you stand at the threshold. You gesture gracefully into the space and let them walk in alone. You don't get trapped in a closet just like you don't get trapped in a bad mortgage!

Finally, let’s talk about open houses. The chaotic bazaar of real estate. When the scheduled time ends and the last guest supposedly leaves, the event isn't over. You must thoroughly inspect all rooms at the conclusion of an open house. You check behind every door, in every shower, and behind every curtain. Inspecting all rooms after an open house ensures no unauthorized individuals remain hidden inside the property. You must clear the structure of hidden squatters just like you clear a title of hidden liens! 

Every single one of these protocols—from locking your office doors to making them walk in front of you—is an interlocking mechanism of your survival. You manage the risk of the transaction for your clients, but you have to manage the risk of the environment for yourself. Now finish your drink, we've got an inspection to get to!
