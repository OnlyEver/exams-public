A house is a lot like your own body! The wood frame is like the skeleton, and the electrical wires are like the nervous system that sends signals everywhere. But what about the plumbing? Think of the plumbing network as the body's blood vessels and digestive system. When you are playing a video game, you do not just look at how cool your character's armor is; you check their hidden health stats. In real estate, buyers might look at shiny kitchen counters, but it is super important to look at the hidden stats of a house: where the water comes in, how the heat works, what the pipes are made of, and how waste leaves the building. Spotting an old, rusty pipe or a tiny water heater before buying a house can save you from a surprise bill of \$5,000 or more! Let's look at how the water in a house actually works.

## The Incoming Supply: Governing the Flow

Think about how you get internet. A main cable runs down your street, and a smaller wire brings it into your house. Water works the exact same way. 

A water service pipe connects a building's internal plumbing system to the municipal water main, which is a giant pipe buried under the street. The town (also called a municipality) is the boss of this connection. If there is a huge leak or someone does not pay their bill, city workers will come out and close a specific valve. A curb valve allows a municipality to shut off the water supply to a specific building from the street. 

Once the water enters the house, the homeowner is in control. Building codes mandate a main water shut-off valve located inside a property near the water service pipe entry point. If a pipe breaks in the kitchen and water is spraying everywhere like a broken fountain, turning this valve is your main defense to stop a huge flood!

But wait—the town pumps water through the streets with a ton of force so it can travel miles and miles. If that super-strong water blasted straight into your house, your sinks and toilets would break. To fix this, water pressure regulators lower high municipal water pressure to a safe level for interior plumbing fixtures.

## Hot Water Heating: Capacity Meets Demand

Nobody likes getting blasted with freezing cold water halfway through a shower! Making sure a house has enough hot water is not just about having a big tank. It is about matching the tank's power to the family's needs.

> **Water Heater Sizing Logic**
> You might think a giant house automatically needs a giant heater, but the square footage of the house does not really matter. Instead, water heater sizing requirements are directly influenced by the total number of bathrooms in a residential property (since multiple people might shower at once) and water heater sizing requirements are directly influenced by the total number of bedrooms in a residential property (which helps guess exactly how many people live there). 
> 
> Let's do a quick math example to show how plumbers might estimate the number of gallons needed based on bedrooms and bathrooms:
> 1. Start with the number of bedrooms. Let's say there are 3 bedrooms.
> 2. Multiply that by 10 gallons to estimate the basic bedroom needs: 3 x 10 = 30 gallons.
> 3. Count the number of bathrooms. Let's say there are 2 bathrooms.
> 4. Multiply that by 10 gallons: 2 x 10 = 20 gallons.
> 5. Add those two amounts together: 30 + 20 = 50 gallons total needed for the tank.

Most of the time, residential water heater tanks are typically sized between 40 and 80 gallons. But the size of the tank is only part of the story. There are two big scores you need to check, just like looking at a sports car's top speed and acceleration:

1. **Recovery Rate:** Imagine drinking a whole slushie and waiting for your brain freeze to go away before you can take another sip. The recovery rate of a water heater dictates how quickly the heater can bring a specific volume of cold water to the target temperature.
2. **First-Hour Rating:** This is the ultimate test. The first-hour rating indicates the maximum amount of hot water a water heater can deliver in one hour starting from a full tank. If your whole family needs to shower before school, this number tells you if the last person gets nice warm water or freezing ice cubes!

## The Anatomy of Pipes: Supply and Waste Materials

Just like bikes can be made of heavy steel or super-light carbon fiber, the pipes in a house can be made of different materials. Figuring out what pipes a house has tells you a lot about how old it is and if it will need expensive repairs soon.

### Metallic Pipes: From Steel to Copper

**Galvanized Steel:** In older houses built before you were born, you might find galvanized steel pipes. Galvanized steel pipes are rigid metal pipes coated with a layer of zinc. That zinc is meant to protect the pipe, but over a long time, the zinc coating on galvanized steel pipes deteriorates over decades of use. When that happens, the deterioration of the zinc coating in galvanized steel pipes causes severe internal rusting. This rust clogs up the pipe like a thick milkshake clogging a straw! Internal rust and mineral buildup in galvanized steel pipes severely restrict water flow. Because of this, restricted water flow in galvanized steel pipes causes noticeably low water pressure at the plumbing fixtures. Galvanized steel pipes have a typical functional lifespan of 40 to 50 years before requiring replacement. 

**Copper:** Copper is like the all-star player of plumbing. Copper piping is highly resistant to internal rust and corrosion. Because of this, copper plumbing pipes have a functional lifespan exceeding 50 years! However, copper piping is an expensive material choice for residential plumbing water supply lines. It also has one major weakness: rigid copper water pipes can easily freeze and burst in unheated building spaces during winter. Imagine leaving a soda can in the freezer until it explodes—that is what happens to copper pipes!

**Cast Iron:** You will not see these used for fresh drinking water. Instead, cast iron pipes are historically used for main soil stacks in multistory buildings, and cast iron pipes are historically used for underground plumbing drainage lines. Cast iron pipes are extremely heavy and difficult to install compared to plastic piping alternatives. But they have a secret superpower: cast iron drainage pipes provide significant sound dampening for flowing wastewater compared to plastic pipes. This means if someone flushes the toilet upstairs, you will not hear the water rushing down the walls!

### The Plastic Revolution: PVC, CPVC, and PEX

Plastic pipes changed the game, but you have to use the right plastic for the right job, especially when it comes to hot and cold water.

## Sanitary Waste Systems and Venting: The Digestive Tract

Getting clean water into a house takes a lot of pressure, but getting dirty water out just uses gravity—like rolling a ball down a hill. Sanitary waste systems use gravity to transport wastewater from a building to a municipal sewer line or septic tank. 

Inside the house, dirty water travels through two specific types of pipes:
* A soil pipe carries wastewater containing solid waste from toilets to the main sewer line. 
* A waste pipe carries wastewater free of solid waste from sinks and showers to the main sewer line.

Sometimes, these pipes get clogged with hair or food. To fix this, plumbers use cleanouts. Cleanouts are accessible pipe openings used by plumbers to clear physical blockages in a drainage system.

### Venting and Traps: Mastering Air Pressure

You cannot talk about water flowing without talking about air. Try taking a drinking straw, putting it in a glass of water, covering the top hole with your thumb, and lifting it out. The water stays stuck inside the straw! But if you lift your thumb to let air in, the water drops out. 

Pipes work exactly the same way. To let water flow down the drain, air has to come in. A vent stack is a vertical plumbing pipe extending through a building's roof. Vent stacks allow outside air to enter the plumbing drainage system. By doing this, vent stacks equalize air pressure within the drainage system. This equalized air pressure from vent stacks allows wastewater to flow freely through drainage pipes without siphoning (getting stuck or sucked backwards). 

This brings us to the coolest and most important part of the drain: the trap. 

If you look under your bathroom sink, you will see the pipe curves in a "U" shape before going into the wall. A plumbing trap is a curved section of pipe located beneath a drainage fixture. Because of its shape, plumbing traps retain a small amount of water to create a physical seal inside the pipe. Why trap water there? The water seal in a plumbing trap prevents toxic sewer gases from flowing backward into the building. It literally plugs the hole so stinky, dangerous air from the sewer cannot get into your bathroom! 

If you ever walk into a house that has been empty for a long time and smell something like rotten eggs, it usually means the water in the trap evaporated. Just turning on the sink for a few seconds fills the trap back up, sealing out the smell like magic.
