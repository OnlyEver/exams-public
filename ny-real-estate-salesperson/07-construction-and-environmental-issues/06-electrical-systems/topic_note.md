Electricity entering a structure is not magic; it is a highly regulated, physical transfer of [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy) governed by strict boundaries of ownership and immutable [laws of physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laws_of_science). For a [real estate salesperson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_estate_broker), a property’s [electrical system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrical_wiring) is a map of its capabilities and limitations. Whether a client intends to install [central air conditioning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air_conditioning), upgrade a commercial kitchen, or simply secure a standard [homeowner's insurance policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home_insurance), the configuration of [wires](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrical_wiring), [panels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distribution_board), and [breakers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circuit_breaker) dictates what is financially and physically possible. Understanding how this system works—from the [utility pole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Utility_pole) on the street to the living room [outlet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AC_power_plugs_and_sockets)—is not just an academic exercise. It is the difference between a smooth closing and a derailed transaction when an inspector spots an overloaded, obsolete [electrical panel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distribution_board). 

## The Boundary Line: Utility Versus Landowner Responsibilities

To understand a property's electrical [infrastructure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infrastructure), you must first understand exactly where the public [grid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrical_grid) ends and [private property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Private_property) begins. When a tree branch brings down a wire, or an underground line faults, the immediate question is: *Who pays to fix it?* 

The [electrical utility company](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_utility) is responsible for bringing [electricity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electricity) to the property [meter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electricity_meter). They construct and maintain the infrastructure required to deliver [power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_power) right up to the edge of the building's internal system. Specifically, the utility company maintains ownership of the **[overhead service drop wiring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Service_drop)**, which is the bundle of [cables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrical_cable) connecting the street's utility pole to the building. If the property is fed from below ground, the utility company maintains ownership of the **[underground service lateral wiring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Undergrounding)** that connects the neighborhood [utility transformer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distribution_transformer) to the meter. 

Crucially, the utility company also maintains ownership of the **[electric meter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electricity_meter)** itself. It is their measuring instrument, attached to the side of the building, used to track consumption for billing. 

The demarcation line is the meter. The property owner is responsible for all electrical wiring from the utility meter into the building. The moment the [current](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_current) passes through that glass dome, it becomes the landowner's [liability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legal_liability). The property owner maintains ownership of the main electrical panel and every inch of wiring branching out from it. If the connection between the meter and the main panel frays, the property owner must hire the [electrician](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrician) to resolve it.

![A residential service drop routing power from the utility pole down to the electric meter. The meter serves as the literal demarcation line between public utility infrastructure and private landowner liability.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Residence_service_drop.JPG)

## The Physics of the Circuit: Voltage, Amperage, and Wattage

You cannot advise a buyer on whether a home can support their needs without understanding the metrics of electricity. Think of electricity like [water flowing through a plumbing system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydraulic_analogy). 

*   **[Voltage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voltage)** is the measure of electrical pressure pushing current through a wire. In our water analogy, voltage is the water pressure in the pipes. 
*   **[Amperage (Amps)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ampere)** is the measure of the rate of electrical current flow. This is the actual volume of water moving past a specific point inside the pipe at any given second.
*   **[Wattage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Watt)** is the measure of total [electrical power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_power) consumed. It represents the total amount of "[work](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Work_%28physics%29)" being done—the total amount of water successfully delivered to put out a fire or turn a [waterwheel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water_wheel).

![The hydraulic analogy of electricity visually demonstrates how voltage acts as the pressure pushing through the system, while amperage represents the physical volume and rate of the flow.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Electronic-hydraulic_analogy.svg)

Because wattage represents the total functional output, it is the product of pressure and flow. 

> **The Power Formula:**
> Wattage is calculated by multiplying voltage by amperage. 
> *(Watts = Volts × Amps)*

### 110-Volt vs. 220-Volt Service: Reading the Demand

Different [appliances](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home_appliance) demand different levels of electrical pressure. In the United States, [electrical systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mains_electricity) are standardized into two primary tiers of residential delivery: 110-volt and 220-volt service. 

Standard residential outlets in the United States operate on **[110-volt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mains_electricity_by_country)** electrical service. This level of pressure is typically used for standard household [lighting fixtures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_fixture) and small household appliances, like [televisions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Television), lamps, and [blenders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blender). A 110-volt electrical system uses **[one hot wire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Live_wire)** (carrying the pressurized current to the appliance) and **[one neutral wire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ground_and_neutral)** (providing the return path to complete the [circuit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrical_network)). 

However, heavy-duty appliances require double the electrical pressure. **[220-volt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mains_electricity)** electrical service is typically required for major equipment. [Electric stoves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_stove) typically require 220-volt electrical service. [Electric clothes dryers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clothes_dryer) typically require 220-volt electrical service. Central air conditioning units typically require 220-volt electrical service. Because of the massive demand, a 220-volt electrical system uses **[two hot wires](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Split-phase_electric_power)** to deliver the necessary pressure. 

*Real Estate Application:* When walking a property with buyers who intend to install a heavy-duty appliance—such as a [kiln](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kiln), a professional electric range, or central air conditioning—you must look at the electrical panel to see if 220-volt service is available in the desired location. 

## The Anatomy of the Electrical Panel

The electrical panel is the beating heart of a property's infrastructure. The **[main electrical panel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distribution_board)** acts as the central distribution point for electricity entering a building. It takes the massive, raw supply of power from the meter and safely routes it throughout the structure. 

**Distribution panels** divide the main electrical feed into smaller, manageable **[branch circuits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrical_wiring_in_North_America)** (e.g., "Kitchen Outlets," "Master Bedroom Lighting"). 

Inside these electrical distribution panels, you will find a **[main circuit breaker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circuit_breaker)**. This is the master switch. The main breaker can shut off all electrical power to the building simultaneously. More importantly for a [real estate agent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_estate_broker), the main breaker rating indicates the total amperage capacity of the electrical service available to the property. 

*   Modern residential construction typically requires a minimum electrical service capacity of **100 amps**.
*   New residential construction often features **200-amp** electrical service capacity to handle modern technology, [electric vehicle chargers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charging_station), and advanced [HVAC systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heating%2C_ventilation%2C_and_air_conditioning).

If you are selling a 1950s home with an original 60-amp service, your buyer will likely need to spend several thousand dollars upgrading the panel before modern insurance companies will issue a policy or before they can install central air conditioning.

### Breakers vs. Fuses: Protecting the Wires

When too much current (amperage) flows through a wire, the [friction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friction) creates immense heat. Without intervention, overloaded wires will melt their [insulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Insulator_%28electricity%29) and ignite the wood [framing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Framing_%28construction%29) of a house. 

To prevent this, distribution panels contain **[circuit breakers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circuit_breaker)** to protect branch circuits from overloading. A circuit breaker is a mechanical switch that automatically trips to stop electrical flow during an [overload](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Overcurrent). Once the hazard is resolved, a tripped circuit breaker can be physically reset to restore electrical flow simply by flipping the switch back.

![The internal mechanism of a miniature circuit breaker, exposing the mechanical switch that automatically trips to physically break the circuit and stop dangerous electricity flow during an overload.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Circuitbreaker.jpg)

The physical size of the breaker tells you the voltage of the circuit it protects:
*   110-volt electrical circuits connect to a **[single-pole circuit breaker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circuit_breaker)** in the distribution panel. A single-pole circuit breaker occupies **one slot** in an electrical panel.
*   220-volt electrical circuits connect to a **[double-pole circuit breaker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circuit_breaker)** in the distribution panel. Because it utilizes two hot wires, a double-pole circuit breaker occupies **two slots** in an electrical panel and features a joined switch.

Older distribution panels, often found in historic or unrenovated [New York](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_York_%28state%29) homes, contain **[fuses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fuse_%28electrical%29)** to protect branch circuits from overloading rather than modern mechanical breakers. A fuse contains a thin metal wire specifically designed to melt during an electrical overload. By melting, it sacrifices itself to break the circuit and stop the dangerous flow of electricity. Because it destroys itself to protect the home, a blown fuse must be physically replaced with a brand new fuse to restore electrical service. 

## The Veins of the House: Wiring Materials

Not all wires are created equal. The environment in which a wire is placed dictates the physical protection it requires. You must be able to visually identify the differences between common wiring materials, as their presence (or absence) dictates [building code](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Building_code) compliance.

| Wiring Type | Core Characteristics | Installation & Usage |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **[Romex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-metallic_sheathed_cable)** | Romex is a brand name for **non-metallic sheathed** electrical cable. The wires in Romex are encased in a flexible [plastic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plastic) jacket. Inside, Romex contains multiple insulated electrical wires alongside a bare [copper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copper) [ground wire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ground_%28electricity%29). | Romex is the most common wiring material used in modern residential construction. Because its outer jacket is plastic, it is easily routed through wooden [wall studs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wall_stud). |
| **[BX Cable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Armored_cable)** | BX cable is a type of **armored electrical cable**. It features a flexible metallic outer sheathing. This metallic sheathing protects internal electrical wires from physical damage (like wayward [nails](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nail_%28fastener%29) or [rodent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rodent) chewing). | BX cable comes pre-manufactured with electrical wires already inside the sheathing. It is frequently seen in older homes and areas requiring moderate physical protection. |
| **[Greenfield](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrical_conduit)** | Greenfield is a type of **flexible metal [electrical conduit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrical_conduit)**. Unlike BX, Greenfield is installed as an empty flexible metallic tube. | [Electricians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrician) pull electrical wires through Greenfield tubing *after* the tubing is installed. This allows wires to be upgraded or replaced later without tearing open walls. |
| **[Conduit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrical_conduit)** | Electrical conduit is a **rigid pipe** used to route electrical wiring. Electrical conduit can be manufactured from metal or plastic ([PVC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyvinyl_chloride)). | Electrical conduit provides superior physical protection for electrical wiring. Because of the high risk of physical impact and the need for constant layout changes, electrical conduit is commonly required for wiring in [commercial buildings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commercial_property). |

![Non-metallic sheathed cable, commonly referred to by the brand name Romex, features multiple wires wrapped inside a flexible plastic jacket. It is the standard for modern residential wall interiors.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Romex_cable.agr.jpg)

![Rigid electrical conduit physically isolates and protects electrical wiring from impact. Unlike residential Romex, conduit is standard in commercial construction to prevent accidental damage to live wires.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Cast_conduit.jpg)

*Real Estate Application:* If you are touring a retail space with a client looking to open a [coffee shop](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coffeehouse), you will notice rigid electrical conduit running along the exposed ceilings, not white plastic Romex. Commercial spaces require conduit because a wayward [forklift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forklift) or ongoing renovations could easily pierce a plastic jacket, whereas rigid metal [pipe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pipe_%28fluid_conveyance%29) ensures the live wires remain protected. 

## The Rule of Law: The National Electrical Code

Electricity is entirely unforgiving of human error. Therefore, its installation is heavily policed by [municipal law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Municipal_law). 

The **[National Electrical Code](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Electrical_Code)** (NEC) establishes nationwide [safety standards](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Safety_standard) for electrical wiring and nationwide safety standards for electrical equipment. The NEC acts as the baseline rulebook for how wires must be sized, how panels must be grounded, and where specific types of outlets must be located to prevent [fires](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fire) and [electrocution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrocution).

![The National Electrical Code (NEC) book, which serves as the foundational regulatory baseline governing the safe installation of electrical wiring and equipment across the United States.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/NEC_2008.jpg)

However, [real estate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_estate) is inherently local. Local [municipalities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Municipality) in New York can adopt their own specific electrical codes. Crucially, local electrical codes in New York can be stricter than the National Electrical Code. For example, [New York City](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_York_City) has exceptionally stringent electrical regulations regarding the use of metal conduit in residential [high-rises](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-rise_building), far exceeding the baseline national standards. 

To ensure these laws are upheld, **[building inspectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Building_inspector)** verify that new electrical installations comply with the applicable electrical code. When a property owner upgrades a panel or wires an addition, they must pull a [permit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Construction_permit). The municipal building inspector will visually examine the work before the walls are sealed. 

If a homeowner performs unpermitted electrical work, it can severely complicate a real estate transaction. A buyer's [home inspector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home_inspection) will easily spot incorrectly wired Romex or an illegal [subpanel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distribution_board). [Lenders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Creditor) may refuse to fund the loan, and insurance companies may deny coverage until a licensed electrician corrects the violations and the local building inspector verifies compliance with the New York municipal code. Understanding these rules ensures you can guide your clients safely through the physical and legal hazards of [property transfer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alienation_%28property_law%29).
