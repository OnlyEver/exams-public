Let’s talk about your client types. Standard residential outlets in the United States operate on 110-volt electrical service. This is your baseline, your first-time buyer looking at a starter condo. 110-volt service is typically used for standard household lighting fixtures, and 110-volt service is typically used for small household appliances—your blenders, your lamps, your TVs. A 110-volt electrical system is a solo agent operation; it uses one hot wire to bring the pressure in, and one neutral wire to provide a return path and close the deal.

Central air conditioning units typically require 220-volt electrical service! Because of this insane demand, a 220-volt electrical system uses two hot wires. It’s a massive co-listing bringing double the pressurized juice to satisfy the whale!

All this terrifying power has to be managed, which brings us to the brokerage office of the house. The main electrical panel acts as the central distribution point for electricity entering a building. It's the managing broker, taking in all the raw power from the street. From there, distribution panels divide the main electrical feed into smaller branch circuits—one for the kitchen, one for the master bath, just like team leads dividing up sales territories. 

Inside those electrical distribution panels, you will find a main circuit breaker. This is the big red kill switch on the managing broker's desk; the main breaker can shut off all electrical power to the building simultaneously. More importantly for your appraisal, the main breaker rating indicates the total amperage capacity of the electrical service. If you look at a panel in a mid-century tear-down and it says 60 amps, that deal is on life support. Modern residential construction typically requires a minimum electrical service capacity of 100 amps just to keep the lights on and the modern world spinning. But if you want to sell a true luxury spec home? New residential construction often features 200-amp electrical service capacity to handle all those EVs and wine fridges.

But what happens when a team gets too greedy? What happens when too much power surges? The system has to protect the wires so the house doesn't literally ignite. Distribution panels contain circuit breakers to protect branch circuits from overloading. Think of them as your brokerage's compliance officers. A circuit breaker automatically trips to stop electrical flow during an overload. The beauty of the modern world is that once the paperwork is fixed, a tripped circuit breaker can be physically reset to restore electrical flow. You just flip the switch back!

But older properties? Older distribution panels contain fuses to protect branch circuits from overloading. A fuse is like an old-school mob enforcer; it contains a metal wire designed to literally melt during an electrical overload. It falls on its sword for the family. Because it self-destructs, a blown fuse must be physically replaced to restore electrical service. You gotta buy a whole new guy to stand guard!

And those compliance officers come in different sizes based on the deal. 110-volt electrical circuits connect to a single-pole circuit breaker in the distribution panel. It’s a standard desk; a single-pole circuit breaker occupies one slot in an electrical panel. But those 220-volt whale circuits? They connect to a double-pole circuit breaker in the distribution panel. They need double the desk space! A double-pole circuit breaker occupies two slots in an electrical panel to handle both hot wires.

Now, how does this power actually travel through the walls? The agents on the ground! The wires! You gotta know the wardrobe of your electrical system. First, there’s Romex. Romex is a brand name for non-metallic sheathed electrical cable. It’s the standard Century 21 agent in a cheap poly-blend suit. Romex contains multiple insulated electrical wires, and Romex contains a bare copper ground wire, all snuggled together. The wires in Romex are encased in a flexible plastic jacket. Because it’s so easy to bend and weave through wood studs, Romex is the most common wiring material used in modern residential construction. 

But what if the neighborhood is rough? You need armor. BX cable is a type of armored electrical cable. It features a flexible metallic outer sheathing. The metallic sheathing of BX cable protects internal electrical wires from physical damage, like wayward nails driven by idiot DIYers. Now, BX cable comes pre-manufactured with electrical wires already inside the sheathing—it arrives fully armored and ready to fight right out of the box.

Then you have Greenfield. Greenfield is a type of flexible metal electrical conduit. It’s like staging the getaway car for a heist. Greenfield is installed as an empty flexible metallic tube. You park the armor first! Electricians pull electrical wires through Greenfield tubing AFTER the tubing is installed! That way, if they need to swap out the crew later, they just pull new wires through the empty armor without tearing open the drywall.

And for the really hardcore commercial deals? You need the Brink’s truck. Electrical conduit is a rigid pipe used to route electrical wiring. It does not flex. It does not yield. Electrical conduit can be manufactured from metal, or electrical conduit can be manufactured from plastic. Either way, electrical conduit provides physical protection for electrical wiring against extreme hazards. Because forklifts are driving around and retail tenants are constantly doing crazy renovations, electrical conduit is commonly required for wiring in commercial buildings.
