Electricity in a house isn't magic. It follows strict rules of physics and property ownership, kind of like the boundaries in a video game. If someone wants to buy a house and add a giant gaming room or central air conditioning, the wires and switches inside the house decide if that is possible or if it will cost too much money. Understanding how this system works—from the utility pole on the street to the outlet in the living room—is extremely important. It helps make sure a house is safe and ready to be sold without any surprise problems!

## The Boundary Line: Utility Versus Landowner Responsibilities

To understand a house's electricity, you first have to know where the city's power grid ends and the private house begins. Think of it like a pizza delivery. The delivery driver brings the pizza to your door, but once they hand it to you, it's yours to take care of!

The electrical utility company is responsible for bringing electricity to the property meter. They take care of getting the power right to the edge of the house. Specifically, the utility company maintains ownership of the overhead service drop wiring connecting the utility pole to the building. Sometimes, the wires are buried underground like a secret tunnel. If that's the case, the utility company maintains ownership of the underground service lateral wiring connecting the utility transformer to the meter. 

The utility company maintains ownership of the electric meter, too. The meter is the little glass bowl on the side of the house that tracks how much power you use so they know what to charge your parents.

The electric meter is the boundary line! The property owner is responsible for all electrical wiring from the utility meter into the building. Once the power goes past that meter, it is the homeowner's job to fix any problems. Also, the property owner maintains ownership of the main electrical panel (the big grey box full of switches inside the house).

## The Physics of the Circuit: Voltage, Amperage, and Wattage

Let's talk about how electricity actually moves. It helps to think about electricity like water flowing through a garden hose to wash a car.

*   **Voltage** is the measure of electrical pressure pushing current through a wire. In our water hose example, voltage is how hard the water sprays out.
*   **Amperage (Amps)** is the measure of the rate of electrical current flow. This is like how thick the hose is and how much water is moving past a certain spot every second.
*   **Wattage** is the measure of total electrical power consumed. This is the total amount of water that actually hits the car to get the job done. 

Because wattage is the total amount of work being done, wattage is calculated by multiplying voltage by amperage. 

Let's look at an example to see how much power a video game console uses. 

1. First, find the voltage (the electrical pressure). For standard outlets, the voltage is 110 volts.
2. Second, find the amperage (the rate of current flow). Let's say the console uses 2 amps.
3. Multiply the voltage by the amperage. (110 x 2)
4. The answer is 220 watts! That means 220 watts is the measure of total electrical power consumed by the console.

### 110-Volt vs. 220-Volt Service: Reading the Demand

Different things you plug in need different amounts of electrical pressure. In the United States, houses have two main levels of power: 110-volt and 220-volt.

Standard residential outlets in the United States operate on 110-volt electrical service. This 110-volt service is typically used for standard household lighting fixtures, like the lamp on your desk. Also, 110-volt service is typically used for small household appliances, like TVs, blenders, and phone chargers. A 110-volt electrical system uses one hot wire (to bring the pressurized power in) and one neutral wire (to give the power a path back out). 

But giant appliances need double the pressure! For example, electric stoves typically require 220-volt electrical service. Electric clothes dryers typically require 220-volt electrical service. Central air conditioning units typically require 220-volt electrical service. Because they need so much power, a 220-volt electrical system uses two hot wires instead of just one!

## The Anatomy of the Electrical Panel

The electrical panel is the beating heart of a house. The main electrical panel acts as the central distribution point for electricity entering a building. It takes all that power coming from the meter outside and safely shares it out to the rest of the rooms. 

Distribution panels divide the main electrical feed into smaller branch circuits (for example, sending some power to the "Kitchen" and some to the "Bedrooms"). 

Inside these electrical distribution panels, you will find a main circuit breaker. This is the master switch for the whole house! The main breaker can shut off all electrical power to the building simultaneously. 

The main breaker rating indicates the total amperage capacity of the electrical service. Modern residential construction typically requires a minimum electrical service capacity of 100 amps. However, because people have so many electronics today, new residential construction often features 200-amp electrical service capacity. If you buy an old house with a tiny electrical panel, you might have to spend \$2,000 or more to upgrade it to hold enough power!

### Breakers vs. Fuses: Protecting the Wires

When too much electricity tries to crowd through a wire, it causes friction and gets super hot. If we don't stop it, the wire could melt and start a fire! 

To keep us safe, distribution panels contain circuit breakers to protect branch circuits from overloading. Think of a circuit breaker like a safety switch. A circuit breaker automatically trips to stop electrical flow during an overload. If you plug in a hairdryer, a microwave, and a vacuum all at the same time and the power clicks off, the breaker did its job! A tripped circuit breaker can be physically reset to restore electrical flow. You just walk over and flip the switch back on.

How do these switches fit into the panel?
*   110-volt electrical circuits connect to a single-pole circuit breaker in the distribution panel. Because it's smaller, a single-pole circuit breaker occupies one slot in an electrical panel.
*   220-volt electrical circuits connect to a double-pole circuit breaker in the distribution panel. A double-pole circuit breaker occupies two slots in an electrical panel because it uses two hot wires!

Older houses are a bit different. Older distribution panels contain fuses to protect branch circuits from overloading instead of flippable switches. A fuse contains a metal wire designed to melt during an electrical overload. Because the wire melts and breaks on purpose to save the house, a blown fuse must be physically replaced to restore electrical service. You have to throw the old one away and screw in a brand new fuse.

## The Veins of the House: Wiring Materials

Not all wires look the same. The type of wire a builder uses depends on where it goes and how much protection it needs.

## The Rule of Law: The National Electrical Code

Just like sports have referees to make sure everyone plays safely, electricity has a giant rulebook. 

The National Electrical Code establishes nationwide safety standards for electrical wiring. It also establishes nationwide safety standards for electrical equipment. This rulebook tells builders exactly how to handle electricity so nobody gets hurt or starts a fire.

However, your own town can make up extra rules, too! Local municipalities in New York can adopt their own specific electrical codes. Sometimes, local electrical codes in New York can be stricter than the National Electrical Code.

To make sure everyone follows the rules, building inspectors verify that new electrical installations comply with the applicable electrical code. A building inspector is like a referee who comes to the house to check the wires before the walls are closed up. If a homeowner tries to do the wiring themselves without following the rules, the house might be unsafe to sell!
