Listen to me, slide your glass over and focus, because you are about to walk into the absolute wildest reality TV show on earth: New York real estate. You think your job is just unlocking doors and pointing at granite countertops? Oh, my naive, sweet friend. Underneath the manicured lawns and the fresh paint, a property is a living, breathing, digesting organism. When a house gets cut off from city utilities, it has to survive in the wilderness on its own. You are going to be dealing with invisible toxins, underground digestive tracts, and literal insect mob families trying to eat your client's equity. If you don't know the environmental laws, your deals will blow up at the closing table like a kitchen fire in a five-star restaurant. 

Let's start with the lifeblood: water. Imagine the Safe Drinking Water Act as a massive, heavily armed federal bouncer. This is a federal law protecting public drinking water supplies throughout the entire nation. Its job is to make sure the nationwide punch bowl isn't poisoned. In our neck of the woods, the New York State Department of Health is the local club manager. They are the ones who actually regulate those public drinking water standards within New York. 

But how does the bouncer know who to protect? The state has a very strict VIP list. A public water system is officially defined as a system with at least five service connections. Alternatively, a public water system is defined as a system regularly serving an average of at least 25 individuals daily for at least 60 days a year. If you hit those numbers, the bouncer protects you. The government watches your water. 

But what if you buy a cabin in the woods? The moment you are off the grid, the bouncer turns his back. The Safe Drinking Water Act does not regulate private drinking water wells. Period. You are in the Wild West now. This means homeowners with private wells are solely responsible for testing their own water supply, and they are solely responsible for maintaining their own water supply. No municipal superhero is coming to save them. Because nobody is watching, the New York State Department of Health strongly recommends testing private well water at least once a year for coliform bacteria—which is a polite, scientific way of saying "fecal contamination." You do not want to be drinking that. You also have to watch out for the rest of the toxic boyband of common health-hazardous contaminants in private well water, which include coliform bacteria and nitrates, along with their terrible backup singers, lead and radon. 

When you get a buyer for one of these off-grid properties, the banks get incredibly paranoid. The lenders are bankrolling this survival show, and many mortgage lenders require private well water potability testing prior to approving real estate loans. Think of a water potability test as a chemical background check. A water potability test evaluates the drinking water for sanitary quality, and it actively identifies the presence of health-threatening contaminants in drinking water. If you are dealing with government-backed loans, do not even try to negotiate this. Federal Housing Administration mortgage loans explicitly require water potability tests for properties relying on a private water supply. Veterans Affairs mortgage loans also explicitly require water potability tests for properties relying on a private water supply. 

And if you're doing deals in the ritzy enclaves downstate, brace yourself. Westchester County law requires a water potability test upon signing a contract of sale for any property served by a private well. They don't mess around; the second the ink is dry, the test happens. And under Westchester County law, the seller of a property with a private well must arrange and pay for the mandated water test. They make the seller pull out their wallet and play host!

But safe water isn't enough if it just trickles out like a sad teardrop. You also need a stress test. Water quantity testing for a private well measures the flow rate in gallons per minute. This water quantity testing ensures an adequate continuous water supply is available for the home so your client can run the dishwasher, take a shower, and flush the toilet all at the same time without the house gasping for breath.

Speaking of flushing toilets, let's talk about the dark side of the moon: sewage. When public sewer access is unavailable, private sewage disposal systems are utilized. These are brilliant, disgusting little miracles where a private sewage disposal system processes wastewater directly on the property. Your yard essentially becomes its own digestive tract. 

A standard private septic system utilizes two main organs: a septic tank and a soil absorption field. Think of the septic tank in a private sewerage system as the stomach. It is a massive underground vault that separates solid waste from liquid wastewater. The heavy solids sink, the greasy scum floats, and the somewhat-clear liquid in the middle—the effluent—moves to the intestines. That's your soil absorption field. A soil absorption field distributes the liquid effluent from the septic tank into the surrounding soil, letting the earth act as a giant biological filter. 

You cannot just haphazardly bury a tank and some pipes like a pirate hiding treasure. The New York State Department of Health establishes the regulatory standards for residential on-site wastewater treatment systems. The sacred, unbending cookbook for this is Appendix 75-A. New York State residential septic system design standards are outlined in Appendix 75-A of the State Sanitary Code. 

Before you even design a system, you have to audition the dirt. This is called a percolation test, and it measures the rate at which water drains through the soil. A percolation test is absolutely required to determine if a property's soil is suitable for a septic system absorption field. If the soil is basically solid rock and doesn't drink the water, it fails the audition. 

When your client is buying an existing home, they have to investigate the health of the current system. Do not rely on a standard home inspector for this! Standard home inspections do not typically include a functional evaluation of private sewage disposal systems. The regular guy just turns on the sink; he's not an underground gastroenterologist. Instead, a septic dye test is frequently performed during real estate transactions. A septic dye test is a non-invasive performance test used to identify failing septic systems. It’s like a CSI sting operation. A septic dye test involves flushing a massive amount of fluorescent dye down the plumbing. Then, the inspector goes outside and visually checks for surface wastewater breakouts in the yard. If radioactive green water starts bubbling up in the petunias, the system is dead. And trust me, the banks are watching. Many lenders require a satisfactory septic system inspection before funding a mortgage on a property with private sewerage. If that yard glows green, the bank locks the vault.

Finally, you have to protect the actual skeleton of the house from the mob. The insect mob. A wooden house sitting on dirt is basically an all-you-can-eat buffet for nature's demolition crew. Common wood-destroying insects in New York include subterranean termites and carpenter ants. But don't forget the aerial and specialized assault units: common wood-destroying insects in New York also include carpenter bees and powderpost beetles. 

To deal with this, you need a forensic accountant of the insect world. A formal Wood Destroying Insect inspection evaluates a property for visible evidence of active wood-destroying insect infestations, meaning the bugs are currently robbing the joint. But it also evaluates a property for visible evidence of past wood-destroying insect damage, because even if the mob is gone, they might have chewed through the vault's load-bearing walls. 

You can't just use any exterminator. An inspector must be certified by the New York State Department of Environmental Conservation to perform a formal Wood Destroying Insect inspection. And to legally put this on paper, they need an elite sniper badge. A New York State Department of Environmental Conservation Category 7C certification is required to legally complete an NPMA-33 termite inspection report. The NPMA-33 is the standard reporting form used for a formal Wood Destroying Insect inspection. It is the official rap sheet that the government banks demand to see. Just like with the well water, Federal Housing Administration mortgage lenders routinely mandate a formal Wood Destroying Insect inspection before approving a home loan, and Veterans Affairs mortgage lenders routinely mandate a formal Wood Destroying Insect inspection before approving a home loan. 

You have to warn your buyers because standard residential home inspections do not automatically include specialized evaluations for termites. You need the Category 7C specialist to find the clues. Subterranean termites, for example, dry out easily, so they construct visible mud tubes to travel safely between underground colonies and a home's wooden structure. It's like they're building enclosed subway tunnels up the concrete foundation. Physical evidence of a termite infestation includes those mud tubes, as well as hollowed-out wood beams that look fine on the outside but are totally destroyed inside. You're also looking for the discarded wardrobes of the flying scouts; physical evidence of a termite infestation includes the presence of termite swarmers and discarded insect wings littered on the window sills.

If your Category 7C inspector finds that the house is basically held together by termite spit and dreams, your client needs an escape hatch to protect their \$50,000 down payment. That is why real estate contracts often include a pest inspection contingency. A pest inspection contingency is the ultimate ripcord; it allows the buyer to cancel the purchase contract if severe wood-destroying insect damage is found.
