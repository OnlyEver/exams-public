Imagine your buyer closes on a gorgeous commercial lot in Brooklyn. Pop the champagne, right? Wrong. Because decades ago, a dry cleaner spilled a cocktail of industrial solvents into the ground. Under the Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Act, the federal government imposes strict liability for environmental contamination. Strict liability means it does not matter if your buyer didn't spill the toxic sludge; if they own the land now, they own the cleanup bill! It is the ultimate real estate prenuptial nightmare: you marry the property, you marry its toxic debt.

But wait, there is a golden shield! The Superfund Amendments and Reauthorization Act created the innocent landowner immunity status. This is your buyer's get-out-of-jail-free card, but the government doesn’t just hand it out because your client has a nice smile. You have to earn it before closing. Completing a Phase I Environmental Site Assessment helps establish the innocent landowner defense under federal environmental law. 

Think of a Phase I Environmental Site Assessment as a forensic background check on a property’s shady past. The sole purpose here is that a Phase I Environmental Site Assessment identifies potential or existing environmental contamination liabilities on a property. We are playing detective, not surgeon. Therefore, a Phase I Environmental Site Assessment does not involve the collection of physical environmental samples, and it absolutely does not involve laboratory testing of soil or water. Instead, you rely on stalking the property’s history. A Phase I Environmental Site Assessment includes a physical visual inspection of the property to look for weird stains or dead grass. It also involves interviews with current and past property owners to see if anyone slips up and mentions the old chemical shed. Finally, a Phase I Environmental Site Assessment includes a review of historical property records, like digging up 1950s aerial photographs to see if the lot used to be a junkyard. 

Now, let's say your Phase I detective work uncovers that the property used to be a gas station. Cue the dramatic music. A Phase II Environmental Site Assessment is conducted if a Phase I assessment identifies potential environmental contamination. This is where we stop looking and start poking. It’s the invasive medical exam of real estate. A Phase II Environmental Site Assessment involves collecting physical soil samples from the site. We also need to know what’s swimming underneath, so a Phase II Environmental Site Assessment involves laboratory testing of groundwater. 

If those lab results come back glowing green, we move to the operating table. A Phase III Environmental Site Assessment involves the actual remediation of a contaminated site. We aren't just observing anymore; we are curing the patient! A Phase III Environmental Site Assessment includes designing a specific environmental cleanup plan tailored to whatever horrible sludge we found. Then, a Phase III Environmental Site Assessment involves the physical removal or treatment of toxic materials from a property. We are talking excavators, bio-treatments, hauling away tons of poisoned earth. 

But you don’t just stitch up the patient and walk away! A Phase IV Environmental Site Assessment involves the long-term management of a previously contaminated site. It’s like putting the dirt on parole. A Phase IV Environmental Site Assessment includes ongoing monitoring of groundwater quality through permanent testing wells. We do this because a Phase IV Environmental Site Assessment ensures that prior environmental remediation efforts remain effective over time. You have to make sure the toxic ghost doesn't come back to haunt the water supply!

Speaking of haunting the water supply, do you know what the ultimate subterranean saboteur is? Buried tanks. Underground storage tanks pose a significant environmental risk due to the potential for undetectable leaks. They sit in the dark, rusting away, and a pinhole leak can go unnoticed for years. When they fail, leaking underground storage tanks can severely contaminate local groundwater supplies, turning an entire neighborhood's drinking water into a petroleum cocktail. 

Now, how do you know if a tank is legally "underground"? The federal government defines an underground storage tank as a tank system having at least 10 percent of its combined volume underground. And here is the trick that catches every rookie agent: that 10 percent underground volume calculation for an underground storage tank includes the connected underground piping! You could have a massive tank sitting right there on the grass, but if it has a massive root system of buried pipes, congratulations, it’s legally an underground storage tank. 

Who polices this mess? It depends on the scale. Commercial underground storage tanks containing petroleum are regulated by the Environmental Protection Agency. But because we are operating in the Empire State, the New York State Department of Environmental Conservation regulates underground storage tanks within New York State. However, there is mercy for the little guys. Federal underground storage tank regulations exempt tanks storing heating oil used directly on the premises—which is why half the historic homes in Westchester aren't federal crime scenes. Also, federal regulations exempt farm tanks holding 1,100 gallons or less of motor fuel used for noncommercial purposes. But listen to me carefully: if you are representing a seller and you know there is a rusted metal beast sleeping under the rose garden, you cannot keep your mouth shut! Real estate licensees must disclose the known presence of an underground storage tank to potential property buyers. Do not risk your license over a buried secret!

Let's look up from the dirt for a second. We have to talk about the air, specifically the mechanical lungs of the building. Chlorofluorocarbons are synthetic chemical compounds previously used widely in refrigeration systems. If you walk into an old commercial building and see a massive, ancient chiller, beware! Chlorofluorocarbons were commonly used as refrigerants in older air conditioning units. You probably know them by their stage name: Freon is a widely known trade name for certain chlorofluorocarbon refrigerants. 

Back in the day, everyone thought they were a miracle, until we realized they were the atmosphere's worst enemy. Chlorofluorocarbons are officially classified as ozone-depleting substances because the release of chlorofluorocarbons into the atmosphere contributes to the destruction of the Earth's ozone layer. They literally punch holes in the sky! Because of this absolute environmental heist, the federal Clean Air Act regulates the use and disposal of chlorofluorocarbons. In fact, the production of new chlorofluorocarbons was officially banned in the United States in 1996. 

This means your client cannot just take a sledgehammer to an old rooftop AC unit! Federal law strictly prohibits the intentional venting of chlorofluorocarbons into the open atmosphere. If the system needs repairs, technicians must use specialized equipment to capture and recycle chlorofluorocarbons during appliance servicing. And if it’s time to scrap the whole system? Property owners must hire certified technicians to properly dispose of appliances containing chlorofluorocarbons. You vent it, you pay for it in massive federal fines.
