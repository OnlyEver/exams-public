Slide that coaster over here and listen to me, because if you're going to survive as a real estate agent in New York, you need to understand the absolute legal gauntlet of seller disclosures. For centuries, this state was a wild, unforgiving frontier for homebuyers. New York State historically follows the legal doctrine of caveat emptor regarding residential real estate purchases. The Latin phrase caveat emptor translates directly to buyer beware. It literally meant if you bought a house and found out the basement was holding hands with a family of feral raccoons, the judge would look at you and say, "Tough luck, you bought it!" 

But we don't live in the dark ages anymore. The state finally decided to force some transparency at the negotiating table, and they did it with a sledgehammer called Article 14. New York Real Property Law Article 14 governs the Property Condition Disclosure Act. The whole point of this statutory beast is to stop sellers from playing a game of high-stakes hide-and-seek. The Property Condition Disclosure Act requires sellers to provide a Property Condition Disclosure Statement to residential homebuyers. We just call it the PCDS. It's a massive, multi-page confessional booth where the seller has to spill the beans on the property.

Now, before you go slapping this form on every deal in town, you have to know who is actually invited to this party. The Property Condition Disclosure Act applies to the sale of one-to-four family residential real estate properties. That's your sweet spot. But real estate is full of weird cousins and VIP transactions, and the state has a bouncer at the door waving certain deals right past the disclosure line.

Let’s talk about the exemptions. The Property Condition Disclosure Act does not apply to the sale of cooperative apartments. Why? Because when you buy a co-op, you are technically buying shares of stock in a corporation, not actual bricks and mortar! Similarly, the Property Condition Disclosure Act does not apply to the sale of condominium units. They have their own dense offering plans. And if there’s no house at all? The Property Condition Disclosure Act does not apply to the sale of vacant land. What if a mega-investor rolls up? The Property Condition Disclosure Act does not apply to the sale of multi-family properties containing five or more residential units. The second that fifth unit exists, the state classifies it as a commercial deal, and those heavyweights can fend for themselves. And if the house still has the fresh plastic wrapper on it? The Property Condition Disclosure Act exempts the sale of newly constructed residential property that has never been inhabited. 

Then you have the people who literally have no clue what’s going on inside the walls because they didn't live there or they're legally forced to sell. The Property Condition Disclosure Act exempts property transfers made by a fiduciary during the administration of a decedent's estate. The Property Condition Disclosure Act exempts property transfers resulting from a foreclosure sale. The Property Condition Disclosure Act exempts property transfers made pursuant to a court order. 

Finally, you have the family drama exemptions. If property is moving between people who already know each other's secrets, the state steps back. The Property Condition Disclosure Act exempts property transfers from one co-owner to another co-owner. The Property Condition Disclosure Act exempts property transfers made to a spouse or a blood relative. And if a marriage goes up in a spectacular ball of flames? The Property Condition Disclosure Act exempts property transfers between spouses resulting from a divorce decree.

Now, let me tell you a story about the greatest heist in New York real estate, a heist that just got shut down. For over twenty years, attorneys treated the PCDS like a bad dinner date—they just paid to get out of it! Prior to March 2024, New York sellers could avoid providing the Property Condition Disclosure Statement by giving the buyer a \$500 credit at closing. It was a golden ticket! Throw \$500 on the closing table, and poof, you didn't have to disclose a thing. But my friend, that era is dead and buried. As of March 20, 2024, New York law eliminates the \$500 credit opt-out provision for property condition disclosure. New York residential sellers can no longer pay a \$500 credit in lieu of providing a Property Condition Disclosure Statement. Compliance is now absolute. The get-out-of-jail-free card is gone.

When you tell your sellers they are legally forced to fill this out, they are going to sweat. They’ll picture themselves getting sued because they didn't know a termite sneezed in the attic ten years ago. You have to calm them down with the magic words: actual knowledge. The Property Condition Disclosure Statement is strictly based on the seller's actual knowledge of the property's condition at the time of signing. It is an open-book test of their memory, not an archeological dig! The Property Condition Disclosure Act does not impose an affirmative duty on sellers to investigate the property for defects. Listen to me: sellers are not required to conduct new home inspections to complete the Property Condition Disclosure Statement. Sellers are not required to search public records to complete the Property Condition Disclosure Statement. 

The Property Condition Disclosure Statement contains mandated questions allowing the seller to answer yes, no, unknown, or not applicable. If they genuinely do not know the age of the roof, "unknown" is the golden, legal truth! So, what exactly are they being grilled on? The Property Condition Disclosure Statement includes specific questions about the property's structural condition and mechanical systems. 

But Mother Nature is getting angry, and the state knows it. So, a 2024 amendment to the Property Condition Disclosure Act requires sellers to disclose property information regarding flood risk. They want the whole watery truth. A 2024 amendment to the Property Condition Disclosure Act requires sellers to disclose the flood insurance history of the property. Sellers must disclose if the property is located in a FEMA-designated 100-year floodplain on the Property Condition Disclosure Statement. Sellers must disclose if the property is located in a FEMA-designated 500-year floodplain on the Property Condition Disclosure Statement. And to top off this Noah's Ark of paperwork, sellers must disclose whether federal law requires the property owner to obtain and maintain flood insurance. 

Timing is everything in this business. You can't just slide this form across the table while they are popping champagne at closing. Sellers must deliver the Property Condition Disclosure Statement to the buyer before the buyer signs a binding contract of sale. Alternatively, sellers must deliver the Property Condition Disclosure Statement to the buyer's real estate agent before the buyer signs a binding contract of sale. Once the ink dries on the deal, a copy of the fully executed Property Condition Disclosure Statement must be affixed to the real estate purchase contract. 

But houses are living, breathing nightmares. What if, three weeks after signing the PCDS, a pipe explodes and floods the kitchen? The seller can't just whistle and look the other way! Sellers must revise the Property Condition Disclosure Statement before closing if new information makes the original statement inaccurate. And if they try to pull a fast one? Sellers are liable for actual damages suffered by buyers if the sellers provide a false Property Condition Disclosure Statement. Furthermore, sellers are liable for actual damages suffered by buyers if the sellers willfully fail to provide a revised Property Condition Disclosure Statement. Actual damages means the seller is writing a check for the exact financial cost of fixing their lies!

Now, you will eventually get a sly client who says, "Let's just sell it 'as-is' and skip this whole headache." Do not fall for the illusion! The Property Condition Disclosure Act does not prevent buyers and sellers from entering into an as-is purchase agreement. But signing an as-is purchase agreement does not waive the seller's legal obligation to provide a Property Condition Disclosure Statement! You can absolutely sell a broken, crumbling house, but you still have to hand over a form admitting it's broken.

On the flip side, do not let your buyers get lazy just because they have this form in their hands. The Property Condition Disclosure Statement is not a substitute for an independent home inspection conducted by the buyer! The Property Condition Disclosure Statement does not constitute a warranty of any kind by the seller. The Property Condition Disclosure Act preserves the buyer's obligation to conduct property inspections and investigations. Caveat emptor still breathes! The state just gave the buyer a flashlight, but they still have to walk down into the dark basement themselves.

One last crazy curveball before I order us another round. You're out in western New York or upstate, selling a gorgeous rustic farm. You need to know about the invisible landmines. New York Real Property Law Section 242 governs seller disclosures regarding uncapped natural gas wells. Yes, you heard me, actual, literal gas wells left open in the earth! Under this law, sellers must disclose the existence of uncapped natural gas wells on the property being sold. 

But just like the PCDS, it's about what the seller actually knows. The requirement to disclose uncapped natural gas wells applies only if the seller has actual knowledge of the wells. If they know about it, they have to speak up early. Sellers must inform the purchaser about known uncapped natural gas wells prior to entering into a contract for the sale of the property. If they know there's a methane geyser out by the oak tree and they keep their mouths shut? Boom! Buyers who suffer a financial loss due to undisclosed uncapped natural gas wells can recover actual damages from the seller. 

So memorize the rules, guard the disclosures, and for the love of real estate, don't ever let a client try to hide a flood or a gas well! Now cheers to passing that exam!
