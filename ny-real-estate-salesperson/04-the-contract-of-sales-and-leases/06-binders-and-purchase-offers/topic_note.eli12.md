Imagine trading a super rare video game console with your friend. If you just stand in the living room and say, "I'll give you \$500 for it," it's really just words floating in the air. In the real world, you might have an understanding, but in the eyes of New York real estate law, nothing has actually happened!

Trading a house or land is a huge deal. Because houses are so expensive and permanent, the law needs proof that is much stronger than just human memory or a high-five. There is a big difference between people being eager to "close the deal" and the strict rules of the law. This is why we have a carefully planned paper trail. When you learn to be a real estate salesperson, you are learning how to use special paperwork that turns a simple wish to buy a house into a real, legal trade.

If you can master the special forms used in this process—like binders, purchase offers, and the very strict rules of the Statute of Frauds—you will keep your clients safe, protect your job, and make sure everyone gets what they promised!

## The Anatomy of the Deal: Two New Yorks, Two Methods

New York is a massive state, and the rules are actually split in half. How you fill out the paperwork for a house in Manhattan or Long Island is completely different from how you do it in Syracuse or Buffalo. We have to learn about the downstate "binder" and the upstate "purchase offer."

### The Downstate Dance: The Binder

In downstate New York (like New York City and Long Island), before you get to the giant, official legal contract, you use a special document called a binder. 

Think of a binder like calling "dibs" on a house. **A binder is a preliminary agreement used to secure a real estate property before drafting a formal contract.** To show the seller you aren't just joking around, **a binder typically includes a small monetary deposit to demonstrate the buyer's good faith.** 

Let's look at a math example of how this deposit works:
1. The buyer wants to buy a house for \$500,000.
2. The real estate agent asks for a 1/100 (or 1 percent) good faith deposit.
3. You calculate the deposit by dividing the total price by 100. So, \$500,000 / 100 = \$5,000. The buyer hands over \$5,000 to show they are serious!

But you have to remind your client what this piece of paper actually means. **In downstate New York, a binder is generally treated as a non-binding agreement to agree rather than a formal contract.** It doesn't legally force the seller to sell or the buyer to buy. Because it's not a super strict rule yet, **a binder deposit is fully refundable if the parties fail to execute a formal contract of sale.** If everyone changes their minds the next day, the buyer gets their \$5,000 back!

So, why even use it? Because **a binder serves to outline the basic transaction terms agreed upon by the buyer and seller.** It creates a simple list of things like the price, the move-in date, and if the refrigerator is included. Once everyone signs it, **the basic terms outlined in a real estate binder are forwarded to the respective real estate attorneys.** 

At this point, the real estate agent passes the ball to the lawyers. **A real estate attorney uses the agreed-upon binder terms to draft the formal contract of sale.**

### The Upstate Reality: The Purchase Offer

If you travel to upstate New York, the rules totally change! Up there, people skip the binder and go straight to the big leagues with a purchase offer. **A purchase offer is a document submitted by a buyer proposing specific terms for acquiring a property.**

Here is the most important difference to remember: **In upstate New York, a signed purchase offer often functions as the binding contract of sale upon seller acceptance.** 

When your buyer signs this, they are making a massive legal move. If the seller signs it too, boom—you are instantly locked into a legal contract. Because a real estate agent is helping make this powerful contract without a lawyer sitting right there, the law demands a safety net. **Upstate New York purchase offers prepared by real estate licensees must include an attorney approval contingency clause.** 

> **The Attorney Approval Contingency**
> What does that big phrase mean? **An attorney approval contingency allows legal counsel to review and cancel the purchase offer within a specified timeframe** (usually 3 to 5 days). This means that even though the real estate agent helped make the deal fast, the actual lawyers still get to look at it and can hit the "cancel" button if it's bad for their client.

### Offer, Counteroffer, and Acceptance

Whether you are using a formal contract downstate or a purchase offer upstate, agreeing to a deal works a specific way.

**Acceptance of a purchase offer occurs when the seller signs the document without making any changes.** It has to be an exact copy, like matching two puzzle pieces perfectly.

But what if a seller likes the \$500,000 price but wants to change the move-in day from June 1st to June 15th? **Any modification to a purchase offer by the seller constitutes a counteroffer rather than an acceptance.** 

By changing that one date, the seller just did two things:
1. They made a brand-new offer to the buyer.
2. They totally destroyed the buyer's first offer. **A counteroffer automatically terminates the original purchase offer.** The seller can't change their mind later and say, "Okay, let's do the June 1st idea!" because that June 1st offer has been deleted forever.

| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **What is it?** | Just a promise to try to make a deal. | A real, binding rule once the seller signs. |
| **Money Down** | Small good faith deposit; you get it back if the deal fails. | Bigger deposit that follows strict contract rules. |
| **What happens next?** | Sent to lawyers so they can write the *real* contract. | *This is* the contract, sent to lawyers to review just in case. |

## The Boundary Lines of Your License: The *Duncan and Hill* Doctrine

As a real estate agent, you will look at legal papers every single day. But remember: you are an agent, not a lawyer! **Real estate licensees must avoid the unauthorized practice of law when handling binders and purchase offers.** 

Pretending to be a lawyer is a quick way to get into huge trouble. If a buyer says, "I want to buy this house, but only if the seller leaves their pet turtle," you might want to write a fancy legal sentence about the turtle. Stop right there! **Real estate licensees cannot draft custom legal clauses for insertion into binders or purchase offers.** 

The exact rules for what you are allowed to do were decided by a famous judge. **The Duncan and Hill Realty versus Department of State court decision established rules for New York licensees filling out contract forms.**

To keep things moving without needing a lawyer to hold your hand, **the Duncan and Hill decision permits licensees to fill in blanks on pre-printed real estate purchase offers.** This means you can play "Mad Libs" with forms that are already made. But you can't just print any random form you find online. **Pre-printed purchase offers filled out by licensees must be approved by a recognized joint committee of the bar association and realtors.** 

When you use the official form, you just act like a super helpful writer filling in names, dates, and prices. The second you try to make up your own legal rules, you are breaking the law!

## The Iron Shield: The Statute of Frauds

Let's go back to the buyer shouting a \$750,000 offer in the living room. Why does the law ignore this completely? It's all because of a super old rule from the 1600s made to stop people from lying in court. 

Here in our state, **the New York Statute of Frauds is codified in the General Obligations Law.** (Codified just means it's written down officially in the law books).

**The fundamental purpose of the Statute of Frauds is to prevent fraudulent claims concerning the transfer of real property interests.** Before this rule existed, someone could get three friends to lie and say, "We heard you promise to give us your house for ten dollars!" and the court might believe them. This rule stops people from playing dirty tricks. 

Because of this, **the Statute of Frauds requires all contracts for the sale of real estate to be in writing to be legally enforceable.** A handshake means zero. A pinky promise means zero. **An oral contract for the sale of real estate is completely unenforceable under the Statute of Frauds.** If you just say it out loud, it doesn't count.

### The Anatomy of an Enforceable Writing

To satisfy this big rule, the written paper doesn't have to be a 100-page book. But it absolutely must check three boxes:
1. **A written real estate contract must clearly identify the contracting buyer and seller to satisfy the Statute of Frauds.** We need to know exactly who is playing the game!
2. **A written real estate contract must adequately describe the subject property to satisfy the Statute of Frauds.** We need to know exactly which house or address we are talking about.
3. **A written real estate contract must state all essential terms and conditions of the sale to satisfy the Statute of Frauds.** The most important stuff, like the price, has to be listed clearly.

### The "Party to be Charged"

Sometimes people get confused about *who* actually needs to sign the paper. 

**The Statute of Frauds requires real estate contracts to be signed by the party to be charged.** 

Who is that? **The party to be charged is the individual against whom enforcement of the real estate contract is sought.** 

Let's imagine this: The seller signs a paper promising to sell their home to the buyer for \$500,000, but the buyer forgets to sign it. A week later, the seller changes their mind and says "No way." Can the buyer force the seller to sell?
*Yes.* The buyer is using the paper against the seller. The seller is the "party to be charged." Because the seller's signature is right there on the paper, they are locked in. 

Now flip it around: Can the seller force the buyer to buy?
*No.* The seller wants to use the paper against the buyer, making the buyer the "party to be charged." But wait—the buyer never signed it! So the seller has no power to force them. 

*Easy trick to remember: You can only force someone to follow the rules if you have their signature on the paper.*

## The One Exception: The Short-Term Lease

The Statute of Frauds controls almost everything in real estate, but there is one special exception when it comes to renting. 

**The Statute of Frauds requires real estate leases for a period exceeding one year to be in writing.** If you want to rent a store for two years, or an apartment for 18 months, you absolutely have to get it on paper. 

But, **an oral real estate lease for a period of exactly one year or less is legally enforceable in New York.** 

Why? Because courts know that quick, short rentals happen all the time (like renting a room month-to-month). Even though it's a terrible idea to not write it down, if a landlord verbally promises to let someone rent an apartment for exactly 12 months for \$2,000 a month, the law says that spoken promise is actually a real, binding deal!

## Summary for the Professional

When you start your career, your biggest job is keeping things clear and honest. Make sure you know where you are working—downstate binders that need real lawyers to make the contract, or upstate purchase offers that need an attorney checkup clause. Know your limits—you can fill in the blanks, but never pretend to be a lawyer because of the *Duncan and Hill* rules. And most importantly, always, always write it down! Because under the General Obligations Law and the Statute of Frauds, if it isn't written on paper, signed by the party to be charged, and full of all the right details, it's nothing more than a wish floating in the air.
