Think of real estate rules in New York like two totally different video games. 

In one game (residential, which means places where people live, like apartments), you are playing in "Story Mode." The game has built-in rules to make sure you are safe, you don't lose all your health points, and the game is fair. 

In the other game (commercial, which means places for businesses, like a pizza shop or a shoe store), you are playing in "Survival Mode." There are almost no safety nets. You and the other players have to write your own rules, and whatever you agree to is what happens.

If you are helping someone find an apartment, you have to know the Story Mode rules. If you are helping someone open a store, you have to know the Survival Mode rules. Mixing them up will cause a lot of trouble! Let's look at exactly how they work.

## The Fundamental Divide: Statutory Protection vs. Contract Law

To understand these two worlds, you need to know how the law looks at the people playing the game. 

Because everyone needs a safe place to sleep, the law tries to protect everyday people. On the flip side, businesses are expected to know how to protect themselves. Because of this, **commercial leases generally lack the statutory consumer protections granted to residential tenants.** ("Statutory" just means a rule written into the law by the government.) 

When you help a business rent a space, you are in a world where **commercial lease terms are primarily governed by contract law rather than statutory housing regulations.** This means whatever the landlord and the business owner write down in their contract is what matters, not special housing laws.

This huge difference shows up in how buildings are taken care of and what happens if people get into an argument.

### The Implied Warranty of Habitability
Imagine buying a brand-new video game console. You expect it to turn on and play games, right? The law thinks about apartments the exact same way. 

By law, **every residential lease in New York includes a non-waivable implied warranty of habitability.** "Implied warranty of habitability" is just a fancy way of saying "a promise that the place is safe to live in," and "non-waivable" means a landlord cannot force you to give up this protection. **The implied warranty of habitability requires residential landlords to keep premises safe and suitable for human living.** If the heat breaks in the winter, the landlord *must* fix it.

But for businesses, there is no automatic promise. **Commercial leases do not include a statutory implied warranty of habitability.** If a toy store rents a building with a leaky roof, and their lease contract says "tenant fixes the roof," the toy store has to fix it or deal with wet toys. The government will not step in to help.

### Eviction and Self-Help
Sometimes, a renter stops paying money, and the landlord wants them to leave. 

If this happens in an apartment, the landlord has to follow a strict, slow legal process. **Residential landlords are prohibited from using self-help eviction methods against residential tenants.** A landlord cannot just change the locks, pack up a family's stuff, or turn off their electricity to kick them out. 

However, because business spaces don't involve human homes, the rules are tougher. **Commercial leases may include provisions allowing a landlord to use peaceful self-help to recover possession of the premises.** If a business stops paying rent, and their contract allows it, the landlord can actually show up in the middle of the night and change the locks on the store!

## Residential Leases: The Architecture of Tenant Protection

When you are helping people find apartments to live in, there are a lot of state laws that keep rent prices fair, help people stay in their homes, and protect their money.

### Rent Regulation: ETPA and the End of Luxury Decontrol
Imagine if you went to buy your favorite candy bar, and suddenly the store owner raised the price to \$500 because they knew you really wanted it. That wouldn't be fair. New York knows that if housing prices go up too high, people won't be able to afford to live in their own neighborhoods. 

To stop this, a law called **the Emergency Tenant Protection Act of 1974 establishes the framework for rent stabilization in eligible New York municipalities.** Rent stabilization is like a speed limit for rent prices; it keeps them from going up too fast.

But cities don't just use this rule for fun. **Municipalities in New York can adopt the Emergency Tenant Protection Act if the local housing vacancy rate falls below five percent.** A vacancy rate below 5% means the city is completely packed, almost all apartments are full, and renters need protection from crazy price jumps. 

> **Important Note:** **The Emergency Tenant Protection Act applies exclusively to residential properties.** It never applies to businesses! 

For people living in apartments, this is a huge shield. **Rent stabilization under the Emergency Tenant Protection Act limits the percentage by which a landlord can increase rent upon lease renewal.** 

Who decides the math? **Local Rent Guidelines Boards dictate the maximum allowable rent increases for rent-stabilized apartments.** 

Let's look at a step-by-step math example of how this board limits a rent increase:
1. Find the current rent: Let's say the tenant pays \$1,000 per month.
2. Find the allowed percentage limit: The board decides rent can only go up by 3% this year.
3. Multiply the rent by the limit: \$1,000 x 0.03 = \$30.
4. Add that amount to the old rent: \$1,000 + \$30 = \$1,030. The landlord cannot charge more than \$1,030.

Also, **rent stabilization guarantees residential tenants the right to renew their leases.** The landlord cannot just say, "I don't want you here anymore." When it's time to sign a new lease, **rent-stabilized tenants in New York have the right to choose between a one-year or a two-year lease renewal term.**

For a long time, landlords had a trick. **Luxury decontrol previously allowed landlords to remove apartments from rent regulation based on high rent amounts and high tenant income.** It meant if an apartment got expensive enough and the renter was rich enough, the landlord could take away the rent protections. But in 2019, the state stepped in. **The Housing Stability and Tenant Protection Act of 2019 permanently eliminated luxury decontrol in New York.** That trick is gone forever. 

### Security Deposits: The 14-Day Rule
A security deposit is extra money you give your landlord at the beginning of a lease. It’s like giving your friend \$20 to hold onto when you borrow their favorite video game, just in case you scratch the disc. 

In the past, landlords could ask for tons of money upfront. Now, **New York residential landlords cannot charge a security deposit exceeding the amount of one month of rent.** Also, **New York residential landlords are prohibited from collecting the last month of rent in advance along with a security deposit.** You only pay the first month of rent and one month's security deposit when you move in.

The state also watches where this money goes. **New York landlords of buildings with six or more residential units must place security deposits in an interest-bearing bank account.** This is like a piggy bank that slowly earns a few extra pennies for the renter over time.

When the renter moves out, the landlord has to move fast. **A residential landlord must return a tenant's security deposit within fourteen days after the tenant vacates the apartment.** 

To make sure the landlord doesn't make up fake damages, **residential tenants have a statutory right to request a pre-move-out inspection to identify potential security deposit deductions** before they hand in the keys. If the landlord does keep some of the money to fix something like a broken window, **residential landlords must provide an itemized written statement of deductions if retaining any portion of a security deposit.** This is like a grocery receipt showing exactly what every single penny was spent on.

### Subleasing and the Duty to Mitigate
Sometimes things change. Maybe someone gets a new job far away and needs to move out before their lease is over. 

If this happens, **New York residential tenants hold a statutory right to sublease their apartments.** Subleasing is when you let someone else move in and pay the rent for the rest of your time. Even if the landlord is grumpy about it, **a residential landlord cannot unreasonably withhold consent to a residential tenant's sublease request.**

What if the renter just leaves the apartment entirely and disappears? **New York residential landlords possess a statutory duty to mitigate damages if a tenant abandons the property.** "Mitigate damages" means trying to shrink the money problem. **The statutory duty to mitigate damages requires residential landlords to make reasonable efforts to re-rent an abandoned apartment.** The landlord must try to find a new person to live there so the old renter doesn't get stuck paying for an empty room.

## Commercial Leases: The Realm of the Negotiated Contract

Now let's walk out of the apartment building and step into the retail store on the street corner. We are back in "Survival Mode."

Because businesses are expected to know how to argue for their own rights, **commercial landlords are exempt from the strict fourteen-day security deposit return deadline mandated for residential landlords.** The landlord can hold the money for as long as their specific contract says. 

Also, **New York commercial landlords have no statutory duty to mitigate damages upon a tenant's abandonment of the leased space.** If a business goes bankrupt, locks the doors, and leaves, the landlord can let the store sit completely empty and still sue the business owner for all the remaining rent money! 

And remember subletting? **Commercial tenants depend entirely on specific lease contract language for any right to assign or sublet the space.** If the contract doesn't explicitly say "you are allowed to sublease," then the business is absolutely not allowed to do it.

### Structuring the Commercial Lease: Clauses and Economics
Because there are no "Story Mode" rules helping them out, businesses and landlords have to build every single rule into their contract. 

*   **The Use Clause:** Landlords want to control what kind of shops are in their building. So, **a commercial lease use clause strictly dictates the permissible business activities a tenant may conduct in the rented space.** If your contract says you are renting the space for a "lemonade stand," you cannot suddenly turn it into a hot dog cart.
*   **Renewals:** Businesses do not automatically get to stay when their time is up. **A commercial lease renewal option grants the tenant the contractual right to extend the lease term.** Because they want to know how much it will cost later, **commercial lease renewals frequently specify predetermined rent increases for the extended term.**

### Escalation Clauses: Hedging Against Inflation
Commercial leases last a long time—sometimes 10 or 20 years! Think about how much an allowance might need to go up in 10 years as things get more expensive. Landlords know that repairs, taxes, and heating will cost more in the future. 

To cover these future costs, **rent escalation clauses allow commercial landlords to pass increased building operating costs directly to tenants.** "Escalation" just means going up.

Here are the three main ways rent goes up:
1.  **Consumer Price Index (CPI) Escalation:** This is based on the whole economy. **A Consumer Price Index escalation clause adjusts commercial rent proportionally to changes in the rate of inflation.** If everything in the country costs more, the rent goes up.
2.  **Porter's Wage Escalation:** A "porter" is a person who cleans and fixes the building. **A porter's wage escalation clause bases commercial rent increases on the hourly wage of local unionized building maintenance workers.** If the cleaning workers get a raise, the rent goes up to help pay them.
3.  **Direct Operating Expense Escalation:** This is based on exact math. **A direct operating expense escalation clause passes specific building maintenance costs to the commercial tenant.** 

Let's look at a step-by-step math example of a direct operating expense:
1. Find the cost of the repair: The landlord pays \$500 to paint the outside of the building.
2. Find the tenant's share: The business rents 1/10 of the building's total space.
3. Multiply the cost by the tenant's share: \$500 x (1/10) = \$50.
4. The business has to pay exactly \$50 to help cover the painting cost.

### The Financial Triad: SNDA and Estoppel
When a commercial building is sold to a new owner, or the landlord gets a loan from a bank, everyone wants to make sure the game keeps playing smoothly. To do this, they use some standard agreements. 

First is an SNDA agreement, which organizes the rules between the business, the landlord, and the bank:
*   **Subordination:** Think of the bank as the final boss. **A subordination clause makes a commercial tenant's leasehold interest secondary to the landlord's mortgage.** The bank's loan is always the most important thing.
*   **Non-Disturbance:** The business wants a promise in return. **A non-disturbance agreement protects a commercial tenant from eviction during a landlord's mortgage foreclosure.** It means even if the landlord goes broke and the bank takes over, the business won't get kicked out as long as they keep paying rent. 
*   **Attornment:** The new boss steps in. **An attornment clause requires a commercial tenant to acknowledge a new property owner as the landlord.** 

Finally, there is an important piece of paper used when a building is being sold. **An estoppel certificate legally binds a commercial tenant to the factual lease terms acknowledged within the document.** It acts like a screenshot of your video game save file. If you sign an estoppel saying you only paid a \$5,000 security deposit, you can't try to tell the new owner later that you actually paid \$10,000. It locks in the facts.

## Summary: The Two Universes at a Glance

Mastering New York real estate means keeping these two games completely separate in your head. The table below shows the big differences you need to know.

| Feature | Residential Leases (Living Spaces) | Commercial Leases (Business Spaces) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Implied Warranty of Habitability** | Mandatory. Landlord must keep it safe to live in. | None. Property is taken exactly as defined in the contract. |
| **Eviction Mechanism** | Strict court process; changing locks (self-help) is prohibited. | Peaceful self-help is allowed if written in the contract. |
| **Rent Increases** | Capped by Rent Guidelines Boards if ETPA applies. | Decided by the market, escalations, and the contract. |
| **Security Deposits** | Max 1 month; 14-day return with an itemized receipt. | Governed purely by whatever the contract says. |
| **Duty to Mitigate Damages** | Landlord must make an effort to find a new renter. | None. Landlord can let space sit empty and keep charging rent. |
| **Subleasing Rights** | Protected by law; landlord cannot unreasonably say no. | Entirely dependent on the contract's rules. |
