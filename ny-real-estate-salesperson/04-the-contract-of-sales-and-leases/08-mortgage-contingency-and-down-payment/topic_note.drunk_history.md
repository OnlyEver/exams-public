Listen to me, rookie. Slide those peanut bowls out of the way, I need to draw you a map on these napkins before you completely blow your first New York real estate deal. You think selling property here is just about pointing out exposed brick and subway tile? No! It is a high-stakes hostage negotiation duct-taped to a ticking time bomb. If you don’t understand the exact physics of how the buyer’s money is trapped, and the precise conditions under which they are allowed to run away, the entire transaction detonates in your face.

Let’s start with the hostage: the buyer’s cash. In New York, the timeline is brutal. The buyer typically pays the contract down payment at the exact time of signing the contract of sale. That is the moment the fantasy becomes reality. And if you are working anywhere downstate New York—the five boroughs, Long Island, Westchester—you better warn your clients to bring a heavy bag, because a customary down payment is ten percent of the agreed purchase price. 

Why do we force them to lock up \$100,000 on a million-dollar apartment? Because that down payment serves as liquidated damages for the seller if the buyer unjustifiably breaches the contract. Imagine your buyer signs, the seller takes the house off the market, and two weeks later your buyer says, "Actually, the vibes are off, I'm moving to Bali." That is an unjustified breach. The seller gets to keep that ten percent as a pre-agreed penalty for the agonizing pain of a ruined deal!

But who physically holds the hostage? You? Me? Absolutely not! New York real estate brokers generally do not hold down payments for residential sales contracts. We do not touch the radioactive money! Instead, in New York, it is customary for the seller's attorney to hold the contract down payment in an escrow account. 

Now, the second that seller's attorney deposits the check, they undergo a bizarre legal mutation. They are no longer just the seller's attack dog. The escrow agent holding the down payment acts as a fiduciary to both the buyer and the seller. They become a completely neutral guardian of the contract. 

They can't just shove this money into their checking account to buy a new BMW. New York law strictly prohibits attorneys and brokers from commingling down payment funds with personal funds. Commingling occurs when an escrow agent mixes client down payment funds with the agent's personal or operating funds. It is the ultimate cardinal sin of real estate! If an attorney's coffee money touches your client's down payment, they are facing disbarment. Instead, the down payment is often held in a specialized escrow account called an Interest on Lawyer Account. And here is a piece of beautiful, systemic karma: the interest earned on an Interest on Lawyer Account is paid directly to the New York State Lawyers Fund for Client Protection. So while your buyer's money sits there, the pennies it generates go to a giant statewide safety net to pay back people who got robbed by shady lawyers. 

But what if the deal goes sideways? A full-blown reality TV screaming match where the seller screams "Unjustified breach!" and the buyer screams "I want my money back!" The escrow attorney? They instantly freeze. If a down payment dispute arises between the buyer and seller, the escrow agent cannot distribute the funds. They cannot play judge. They are totally paralyzed, forced to hold those disputed down payment funds until receiving a written agreement signed by both parties or a formal court order. If the standoff lasts forever and a down payment dispute remains unresolved, the escrow agent must deposit the disputed funds with the appropriate court. They literally hand the bag of cash to the state, dust off their hands, and tell the buyer and seller to fight it out in front of a judge.

So, how does a buyer get their money back without a legal bloodbath? They use the escape hatch! A mortgage contingency clause allows a buyer to cancel a real estate contract without penalty if the buyer cannot obtain financing. 

But it’s not just a vague, "Hey, I'll try to find the cash" handshake. It is a razor-sharp, heavily engineered trapdoor. A proper mortgage contingency clause specifies the exact loan amount the buyer must attempt to secure, specifies the maximum interest rate acceptable to the buyer, and outlines a specific deadline by which the buyer must secure a mortgage commitment. 

Your buyer also can't pull a bait-and-switch. A buyer must act in good faith and apply for the specific type of mortgage described in the contingency clause. If the contract says they are getting a boring, standard loan, they cannot secretly apply for a balloon-payment loan funded by a crypto-cartel. Applying for a different loan type than specified in the mortgage contingency may constitute a breach of contract, and bam—there goes the down payment as liquidated damages! 

The holy grail they are hunting for is a mortgage commitment letter, which is a formal document from a lender agreeing to provide the specified loan. Obtaining a formal mortgage commitment letter from a lender typically satisfies the mortgage contingency clause. The escape hatch slams shut, the deal is locked, and we are going to the closing table!

But what if the bank says no? What if your buyer gets rejected? Rookie, listen to me, this is where agents get sued! If a buyer fails to secure a mortgage commitment by the contingency deadline, the buyer must actively notify the seller to cancel the contract. You have to sound the alarm! You cannot just sit there in silence sobbing into your iced coffee! Failure to notify the seller of an unapproved mortgage by the contingency deadline can result in the buyer forfeiting the down payment. The seller's attorney will assume you got the loan because you didn't say otherwise. But, if you do your job and actively notify them, letting the buyer legally cancel a contract due to an unmet mortgage contingency, the escrow agent must return the down payment to the buyer. You survive to sell another day!

Now, you better know your loan flavors, because they radically change the rules of the game. A traditional mortgage contingency requires the buyer to apply for a conventional loan from an institutional lender—a standard big bank. Easy. 

But government-backed loans? Total chaos. FHA and VA loans have stricter minimum property condition requirements than traditional conventional mortgages. An FHA appraiser is like a tyrannical health inspector in a dive bar; they will flag a missing handrail or a chipped piece of lead paint from three blocks away! Because of this, a mortgage contingency for a VA or FHA loan often requires the seller to complete specific property repairs before closing. 

Furthermore, the government refuses to let their buyers get ripped off. An FHA mortgage contingency must include an FHA amendatory clause. This is a magical shield that allows the buyer to terminate the contract without penalty if the property appraises for less than the purchase price. The military gets the exact same armor; a VA mortgage contingency allows a veteran buyer to cancel the contract without penalty if the property fails to meet the appraised value.

Finally, what if there is no bank? What if the seller is playing banker? That’s a purchase money mortgage contingency, which outlines the terms under which the seller will provide financing to the buyer. Or, picture a heist movie where your buyer tries to slip on a disguise and just take over the seller's current life. In an assumable mortgage contingency, the transaction is dependent on the buyer being approved to take over the seller's existing loan. But the seller can't just moonwalk out of the bank's crosshairs! In an assumable mortgage transaction, the seller remains financially liable for the loan unless the lender grants a formal release of liability. If they don't get that release, and your buyer stops paying the mortgage, the bank is coming straight to the seller's new house to collect!
