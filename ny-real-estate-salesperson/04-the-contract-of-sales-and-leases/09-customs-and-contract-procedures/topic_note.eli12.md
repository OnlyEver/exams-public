Imagine trading a super rare video game console with a friend. You wouldn't just hand it over without some ground rules, right? Buying a house in New York is like that, but way bigger. There is an exact set of steps that buyers, sellers, their lawyers, and real estate agents must follow. Knowing who signs what and when isn't just boring paperwork. It's the ultimate rulebook that decides when a house is officially "off the market," how everyone's money stays safe, and when a deal is 100% real.

## The Anatomy of Drafting: Architects and Orchestrators

As a real estate agent, you are like the coach of a sports team, but you aren't the one writing the official rulebook. To keep everything fair and legal, New York has strict rules about who writes the paperwork. 

Usually, **the seller's attorney customarily drafts the real estate contract of sale in New York**. The seller's lawyer writes down the main rules of the trade. However, the real estate world moves fast! To speed things up, **real estate brokers in New York are permitted to fill in the blanks on standardized real estate contracts**. Think of it like a Mad Libs page where the main story is already printed, and you just fill in names, dates, and prices. 

Your job stops right at those blank lines. 

> **Warning:** A real estate broker is strictly prohibited from drafting a real estate contract of sale from scratch. If an agent tries to write the whole contract on a blank piece of paper, they are breaking a huge rule. In the eyes of the law, drafting a real estate contract from scratch by a real estate broker constitutes the **unauthorized practice of law**. Only lawyers are allowed to be lawyers!

Even though your clients will ask you what all the confusing legal words mean, **a real estate broker cannot provide legal advice regarding the terms of a real estate contract**. Your role is to help them agree on the basic stuff—like the purchase price or what day they move in—and give those details to the lawyers to make it official.

### The Attorney Review Safety Net

Because real estate agents aren't lawyers, there is a built-in safety net when they fill in those blanks. Standardized real estate contracts filled out by brokers must contain an **attorney review clause**. 

An attorney review clause makes the real estate contract subject to the approval of the buyer's attorney and the seller's attorney. This gives the lawyers time to double-check the agent's work. This "timeout" or attorney review period typically lasts for **three business days after the real estate contract is signed**. 

During these three days, either lawyer can hit the "pause" button if they see a rule that hurts their player. If a lawyer says "no way," the whole deal stops: **a real estate contract becomes null and void upon disapproval by either attorney during the attorney review period.** ("Null and void" just means it is completely canceled, like a game crashing before you can save.)

| Role | Permitted Actions | Prohibited Actions |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Real Estate Broker** | Help agree on prices; fill in the blanks on pre-printed forms. | Write contracts from a blank page; give legal advice; write custom extra pages. |
| **Seller's Attorney** | Write the main contract; double-check the agent's fill-in-the-blank forms. | N/A (They are full lawyers, so they do all the legal stuff). |
| **Buyer's Attorney** | Review, approve, or cancel the fill-in-the-blank forms during the 3-day timeout; write custom extra pages. | N/A (They are full lawyers, so they do all the legal stuff). |

## The Choreography of Signatures

When it's finally time to sign on the dotted line, there is an exact order you have to follow, almost like a secret handshake. 

In a New York real estate transaction, **the buyer signs the contract of sale first**. Also, **the buyer submits the earnest money down payment at the exact time of signing the contract of sale**. Earnest money is like an upfront allowance or deposit that proves the buyer isn't joking around. 

For example, if the buyer is paying a 10% earnest money deposit on a house that costs \$500,000, here is how you calculate what they owe right then:
1. Find the total price of the house: \$500,000.
2. Change the percentage to a decimal: 10% becomes 0.10.
3. Multiply the house price by the decimal: \$500,000 * 0.10 = \$50,000.

So, the buyer hands over a check for \$50,000 right when they sign!

Why does the buyer go first? Because **the buyer signing first ensures the seller has a financially backed offer before officially taking the property off the market**. If the seller signed first, the buyer could just walk away without losing a penny, leaving the seller stuck with a house nobody bought. Because of this, **the seller signs the contract of sale after the buyer signs the document.**

### Execution versus Delivery

Just because you signed the paper doesn't mean the game is on. There is a big difference between a contract being signed and a contract being officially locked in.

1. **Execution:** **A real estate contract is not fully executed until both the buyer and the seller have signed the document.** "Executed" is just a fancy word for "everyone signed it."
2. **Binding Status:** Even if everyone signed, the deal is still paused. **A real estate contract is not binding until the fully signed contract is delivered to the buyer or the buyer's attorney.** Delivery is like pressing the "Start" button. Until the buyer's side actually receives the finished paperwork, the transaction hasn't officially begun.

## Contract Riders: Customizing the Deal

Standard pre-printed contracts are great for a normal, boring trade. But what if you are making a special trade? Maybe you want the seller to leave their awesome pool table, or maybe the seller needs to stay in the house for an extra week after the sale. 

To add these special rules, lawyers use a **rider**, which is an attachment added to a real estate contract to include additional terms or conditions. Think of a rider like an expansion pack for a video game that adds new levels to the main game. 

Just like the main contract, there are strict rules about who can make these expansion packs. **Only licensed attorneys are permitted to draft custom riders for real estate contracts in New York.** Because of this rule, **real estate licensees are strictly prohibited from drafting custom contract riders.** 

What happens if the main contract says one thing, but the rider says something completely opposite? The new rules win. **Terms within a customized contract rider supersede conflicting terms within the standard printed contract.** ("Supersede" just means they overpower the old rules).

## Environmental Mandates: The Lead-Based Paint Clause

Many houses in New York are super old. While old houses look cool, they sometimes have old, dangerous stuff inside—like lead paint. Lead paint can make kids really sick. 

Because of this, the federal government made a strict rule: **a lead-based paint disclosure must be attached to the sales contract for residential properties constructed before 1978.** (1978 is the year they banned lead paint in houses). This extra form is basically a safety report card. **The lead-based paint disclosure requires the seller to report any known lead-based paint hazards in the property.**

Once the buyer gets this report card, **federal law grants buyers of pre-1978 residential properties a 10-day period to conduct a lead-based paint inspection.** This gives the buyer 10 days to hire an expert to check the house for danger. But, if the buyer is planning to knock the whole house down anyway, or they just don't care to check, they can skip it. **Buyers have the right to waive the 10-day lead-based paint inspection period in writing.** ("Waive" just means saying "No thanks, I'll skip this part.")

To prove that everyone played by the rules and nobody hid any secrets, a lot of people have to sign this disclosure paper:
* **Both the buyer and the seller must sign the lead-based paint disclosure form.**
* The agents don't get to just watch from the sidelines, either! **Real estate agents involved in the transaction must sign the lead-based paint disclosure form to acknowledge compliance.** 

If anyone forgets to sign these lead paint papers, the government can fine them a lot of money. In real estate, doing the paperwork perfectly is exactly what turns a promise into a real, closed deal.
