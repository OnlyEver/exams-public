Grab another pretzel and listen to me, because you think selling real estate in New York is about picking out the right subway tile and hosting wine-and-cheese mixers. It’s not. The entire Manhattan skyline out that window? It is hanging by invisible, high-tension steel cables, and if you don’t know how to weave them, your deals will plummet straight into the East River. Every closing, every fat commission check you're dreaming of, it all hinges entirely on the mastery of one concept: a contract. Legally speaking, a contract is a legally enforceable agreement between competent parties to perform or abstain from a specific act. It’s the legal gravity that keeps a buyer from taking their \$150,000 down payment to a blackjack table in Atlantic City instead of the closing table. 

Let's talk about how these deals are born, because human beings are messy communicators. In an express contract, it's like a high-stakes hostage negotiation—an agreement where the parties explicitly state the exact terms and conditions. No guessing, no winking. You want the washer-dryer included, you state it! But get this wild little secret: an express contract can be created through either oral statements or written documents. If you scream across this bar, "I'll rent your penthouse for \$5,000 a month!" and I yell back, "Done!" we just formed an express oral contract. On the flip side, you have the ghost in the machine: an implied contract. This is a terrifying agreement created by the actions and conduct of the parties involved, without a single explicit word spoken. Like when you sit at this bar and order that ginger ale, the bartender assumes you're going to pay for it based on your conduct. In real estate, implied contracts are jumping without a parachute. If you act like someone's broker long enough, a judge might decide you are one. 

Now, how are the promises structured? Contracts are either mutually assured destruction or one-way bounty hunting. Picture a classic bilateral contract. This is an agreement where both parties make a binding promise to perform a specific action. I promise to hand over the deed to my Brooklyn brownstone, and you promise to hand over a duffel bag full of cash. Both of our necks are on the chopping block right from the jump. That's why a standard real estate sales contract is an example of a bilateral contract. Both the buyer and seller are trapped in a two-way street of legally binding promises.

But what if it's a unilateral contract? This is the bounty hunter of the real estate world—an agreement where only one party makes a binding promise to perform an action. The other guy? He can sit on his couch watching reality TV. He doesn't have to do squat. But *if* he performs, the first guy must pay up. For instance, an open listing agreement is a common example of a unilateral real estate contract. The desperate seller promises to pay a commission *if* you bring a ready, willing, and able buyer, but you, the agent, don't legally promise to find one! You could go on vacation to Miami instead. Similarly, an option contract to purchase real estate is a unilateral contract. The seller is legally chained to the radiator, promising to sell the property at a set price if the buyer decides to buy within a certain window. The buyer can just walk away unharmed, but the seller is absolutely trapped until the clock runs out.

To make these invisible cables hold up in front of a sour-faced New York judge, you need four massive pillars. The essentials of a valid contract are non-negotiable. 

Pillar One: The essentials of a valid contract include legally competent parties. You can't make a multi-million-dollar deal with someone whose brain is currently visiting another dimension. Legal competency requires a party to possess sufficient mental capacity to understand the terms of the contract. And it’s not just about mental clarity; it's about the calendar. Legal competency requires a party to be of legal age to enter into a contract. Here in the concrete jungle, in New York State, the legal age of majority to enter into a binding contract is 18 years old. No 16-year-old TikTok stars buying Tribeca lofts on their own signatures.

Pillar Two: The essentials of a valid contract include mutual agreement between the parties. You must be looking at the exact same blueprint. Mutual agreement is often referred to in contract law as a "meeting of the minds." Sounds romantic, right? Like a telepathic connection. But it's a ruthless, precise sequence. First, mutual agreement requires a clear offer made by one party. Second, mutual agreement requires an unconditional acceptance of the offer by the receiving party. "I'll buy it for \$2 million." "I accept for \$2 million." If the seller says, "I accept, but only if you waive the termite inspection," the termite inspection just shattered the meeting of the minds. That's a counteroffer, not an acceptance. 

Pillar Three: The essentials of a valid contract include a lawful objective. You cannot contract someone to run an illegal underground casino out of a commercial basement space. A contract with an unlawful objective is legally void from its inception. The judge won't even look at it; they'll just point to the exit.

Pillar Four: The essentials of a valid contract include consideration. Consideration refers to anything of value exchanged between parties to legally bind a contract. It’s the lifeblood of the deal! Without consideration, you just have a promised gift, and gifts don't survive the legal thunderdome. Now, valuable consideration in a contract typically consists of money or equivalent tangible assets. The cold hard cash, the cashier's checks. But what if you're dealing with a sweet old grandfather transferring a family estate to his granddaughter? In that beautiful scenario, good consideration can consist of love and affection between family members. Love is literally a legally binding currency if you share a family tree!

Now, remember how I said an express contract could be oral? Erase that from your mind when it comes to the big leagues. New York courts despise "he said, she said" drama. They instituted the ultimate "pics or it didn't happen" law: The New York Statute of Frauds. The New York Statute of Frauds requires all contracts for the sale of real estate to be in writing to be legally enforceable. Handshakes belong in the 1920s; we need ink. Furthermore, the New York Statute of Frauds requires real estate leases exceeding a term of one year to be in writing. A six-month oral lease? Sure, live dangerously. A two-year oral lease? Unenforceable hot air.

What happens when the deal gets poisoned? It falls into one of three dumpsters, and you need to diagnose the rot instantly. First, you have the completely dead-on-arrival situation. A void contract lacks at least one essential element of a valid contract. No consideration? Void. Unlawful objective? Void. And because it's missing a vital organ, a void contract has no legal force or binding effect on any party. It’s a phantom. It never legally existed.

Then you have the sneaky one: the voidable contract. A voidable contract looks perfectly healthy on paper, but it is legally binding unless disaffirmed by the party holding the specific legal right to rescind the agreement. It’s like a car with a secret eject button meant for only one passenger. For example, a contract entered into by a minor is generally voidable by that minor. The 17-year-old can push the eject button and walk away clean, but the landlord is stuck driving the car. Similarly, a contract entered into under duress is voidable by the coerced party. If someone holds a baseball bat to your knees to get you to sign a deed, you can void that sucker the second you get to a safe phone.

And then there's the utter tragedy: the unenforceable contract. An unenforceable contract is an agreement that a court will not compel the parties to fulfill, even if everyone meant well and no one was threatened. Why? Usually a procedural disaster. For instance, an oral contract for the sale of real estate is typically unenforceable due to the Statute of Frauds. You both agreed! The minds met! The consideration was promised! But because it wasn't in writing, the court shrugs and says, "Sorry kid, my hands are tied."

Finally, keep your eyes on the clock. Contracts are living beasts that evolve over time. From the moment the ink dries, you are in the sweaty, nail-biting waiting period. An executory contract is an agreement where one or more parties have not yet completed their required obligations. You signed the purchase agreement, but the buyer is still begging their mortgage broker for financing, and the seller is still packing up their hideous ceramic cat collection. It's executory. Pending. Incomplete.

But then... you make it to the closing table. The holy grail. The bank wires the funds, the keys slide across the polished mahogany, you grab your commission check. Suddenly, the beast transforms. You now have an executed contract—an agreement where all parties have fully performed their required obligations. The promises have been fulfilled, the deal is dead and buried in the best way possible, and you, my friend, are officially a New York real estate survivalist. Now finish that drink, we have a co-op board package to assemble!
