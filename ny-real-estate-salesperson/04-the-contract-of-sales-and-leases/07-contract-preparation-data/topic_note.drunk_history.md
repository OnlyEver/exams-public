Listen to me, rookie. Slide your drink over here and focus, because you’re about to step into the blood sport that is New York real estate. You think selling property is just popping Champagne at open houses and cashing massive commission checks? It’s a heist. A high-stakes, hyper-coordinated heist, and if you hand the wrong blueprints to the crew, the entire operation blows up in your face. 

In a New York real estate heist, you are the scout. You find the target, you wrangle the personalities, but you do not build the vault-cracking machine. In New York State, the seller's attorney typically prepares the formal real estate sales contract. You aren't practicing law; you are feeding the beast the raw data it needs to survive. To trigger this beautiful, terrifying legal machinery, real estate agents provide a memorandum of sale to the seller's attorney to initiate the formal contract preparation. 

Think of the deal sheet as your operational parameters. A memorandum of sale outlines the basic financial terms and dates agreed upon by the buyer and seller. You are setting the absolute boundaries of the transaction. Specifically, a memorandum of sale dictates the specific agreed-upon purchase price—how much total loot is trading hands. Next, you lock in the hostage money; a memorandum of sale dictates the required amount of the buyer's initial down payment, which is the cash they bleed upfront to prove they are serious. Finally, you set the ticking clock. A memorandum of sale specifies the target closing date for the real estate transaction, giving everyone the exact moment the getaway car needs to be running outside the bank. 

But here’s where rookie agents completely ruin the operation. The attorney takes your deal sheet and needs to match it to the physical earth. A real estate sales contract requires a legally sufficient property description to be enforceable. If the legal description is vague, the judge throws the contract in the shredder. Let me burst your bubble right now: a street address alone is legally insufficient as a property description in a formal New York real estate deed. Street names change, mailboxes get knocked over by snowplows. It's garbage data. A street address is commonly used alongside a formal legal description to help laypersons easily identify the property without their brains melting, but it’s just the garnish, not the steak.

So, where do we get the actual, indisputable geometry of the land? We go to the sacred scrolls. The prior deed provides the most accurate legal description of a property for drafting a new sales contract. It’s the historical receipt. But it's also your ultimate lie detector test. A prior deed contains the exact legal names of the current property owners. If your client says his name is Jimmy, but the deed says James Bartholomew III, James is the only guy who can legally sign away the fortress. More importantly, a prior deed determines if the seller holds sole ownership or co-ownership of the property. Is Jimmy a lone wolf, or is he co-owning this place with three bitter ex-wives who all need to sign off on the deal? You better find out before the closing table turns into a reality TV brawl. 

While you're digging through the seller’s dusty file cabinets, beg them for their old title policy. A prior title insurance policy helps the seller's attorney identify existing recorded easements affecting the property. An easement is a legal parasite attached to the land. It’s the utility company having a permanent backstage pass to march across the backyard and hack at power lines while your buyer is trying to host a garden party. You find that out now, not the day before closing.

Next up, we need to verify the physical realm. The deed is the theoretical ghost of the property; the survey is the brutal, boots-on-the-ground reality. A property survey reveals the exact physical boundaries of the land parcel. It proves the dirt exists exactly where the paper says it does. Not only that, but a property survey shows the precise physical location of all buildings and improvements on the land. It proves the house didn't miraculously migrate closer to the highway over the last decade. Crucially, a property survey identifies physical encroachments crossing the property lines. Encroachments are the ultimate neighborly declarations of war. If the guy next door built a spectacular \$50,000 koi pond, and the survey shows it's bleeding three feet onto your seller’s land, that encroachment is a legal landmine waiting to blow up your deal. 

You also need the city’s ledger, because the city always gets its pound of flesh. Bring me the taxes! Property tax bills provide the exact block and lot numbers used for municipal identification, tying your heist into the city's master grid. Furthermore, property tax bills verify the current assessed municipal value of the property, which the buyer's lawyer desperately needs to calculate how badly their client will bleed in taxes next year. But watch your back—unpaid property tax bills indicate the presence of potential municipal liens on the property. If the seller has been dodging the taxman, those unpaid taxes act like a financial chokehold. The city will gladly freeze your entire transaction until they get paid.

And don't forget the Frankenstein factor. Did your seller creatively convert a detached garage into a sketchy three-bedroom apartment? A Certificate of Occupancy proves that a building complies with local building and zoning codes. Without it, the bank will laugh your buyer right out of the lobby. A Certificate of Occupancy confirms that a structure is legally permitted for human habitation. Without it, you aren't selling a house; legally, you're selling a very expensive, unlivable pile of bricks.

Okay, signal the bartender for another round, because we need to talk about how we mathematically trap the real estate. If you’re out in the Hamptons or the upstate wilderness, you’re dealing with the pirate map method: Metes and Bounds. The metes and bounds system describes property lines using specific distances and compass directions. To make the turns on this treasure map, the metes and bounds system relies on physical monuments to mark changes in boundary directions. Walk 50 feet North by Northwest to the rusted iron pipe, then pivot 30 degrees to the ancient oak tree. But you can't just start walking randomly into the woods! A metes and bounds description must start at a specific geographic location called the Point of Beginning. The surveyor plots the entire perimeter from that single anchor. And here is the do-or-die reality of the math: a valid metes and bounds description must return precisely to the Point of Beginning to form a completely enclosed area. If your compass directions leave a three-inch gap at the end, the geometric shape is open. The magic spell fails, the property leaks out into the void, and your contract is entirely defective.

Now, if you’re hustling in Manhattan or Brooklyn, nobody has the patience to measure distances between fire hydrants and subway grates. In the concrete jungle, the lot and block system is the primary legal method for describing properties in densely populated urban areas like New York City. The lot and block system identifies properties using numbers assigned on a subdivision plat map. It’s a giant, municipal game of Battleship laid over the city. But that map isn't just a casual sketch on a napkin. A subdivision plat map must be filed in the public county records to be legally valid for property descriptions. Once the government stamps that map, it's golden. Why is this so beautiful? Because a contract reference to a filed map legally incorporates all boundary details shown on that specific recorded map. You just write "Block 42, Lot 7" in the contract, and boom—the law automatically teleports all the exact geometric boundaries from the county archives straight into your deal. It is pure legal wizardry.

So, we’ve mathematically trapped the land, secured the title, and paid the taxman. Now we walk inside the house, and the real cage match begins. Buyers and sellers will happily shake hands on a \$2 million purchase price and then threaten physical violence over a washing machine. You must ruthlessly police the boundary between the real estate and the movable junk. A formal list of personal property identifies movable items explicitly included in the real estate transaction. If the buyer is mesmerized by the seller’s custom, \$15,000 velvet sectional couch and wants it to stay, you must itemize it! If it’s not on that list, the seller is backing up a U-Haul and taking it. 

On the flip side, what if the seller wants to keep the \$50,000 antique crystal chandelier they bolted to the dining room ceiling? A chandelier is a fixture; it's legally glued to the real estate. Therefore, a real estate sales contract must explicitly list excluded fixtures to prevent future ownership disputes. If you don't carve out that chandelier as a clear exclusion, the buyer owns it the second the ink dries. And trust me, you do not want to be the agent buying a furious seller a replacement crystal chandelier out of your own commission check because you were too sloppy to write it down. 

Welcome to New York real estate, kid. Drink up, grab your deal sheets, and don't mess up the math.
