Imagine you own a brand-new video game console. It belongs to you, but you decide to let your friend borrow it for the weekend. You are giving them the right to play it, but you are not giving them ownership of the console. When the weekend is over, the console comes back to you. This kind of trade is the real-life idea behind a lease. For someone learning about New York real estate, understanding exactly how this "borrowing" works is super important. You aren't just putting people in apartments; you are learning the temporary and strict rules of how property power is shared.

## The Dual Nature of a Lease

To understand how renting works, we have to look at what a lease really is. In the eyes of the law, a lease has two main jobs.

First, a **lease is a bilateral contract between a property owner and a tenant**. "Bilateral" just means two-sided. It means both sides make a promise: the owner promises to let the tenant stay, and the tenant promises to pay. Because it is an agreement, **a lease functions as a contract outlining the conditions and terms of property use**. Think of it like the rules for your allowance: it tells you what you can and cannot do, like whether you can paint the walls or have a pet dog.

Second, and even cooler, **a lease functions as a conveyance of a possessory interest in real property**. That is a fancy way of saying it officially transfers the right to be there and hold the space, even though it's just for a little while.

> **Key Definition:** **A leasehold estate grants a tenant the right to possess and use a property**, but remember this big rule: **a leasehold estate does not grant a tenant ownership of a property**. 

In this setup, everyone gets a legal name. **The property owner granting a lease is legally known as the lessor**. On the flip side, **the tenant receiving a lease is legally known as the lessee**. (An easy trick to remember: the les**see** is the one who gets to **see** the inside of the apartment!)

Because a leasehold estate does not give the tenant ownership, the property has to go back to the owner eventually. **The lessor retains a reversionary right to retake possession of the property after a lease term expires**. "Reversionary" just means "returning." The space always returns to the owner. The big question isn't *if* the owner gets it back, but *when* and *how*.

## The Four Major Leasehold Estates in New York

The *when* and *how* of a lease ending is what creates different legal categories. **New York real estate law recognizes four major types of leasehold estates.** By learning these four, you will know exactly how property trades hands back and forth in any situation.

**The four major leasehold estates are the estate for years, the periodic estate, the estate at will, and the estate at sufferance.**

---

### 1. The Estate for Years: Clockwork Precision

Do not let the name confuse you! An estate for years does not actually have to last for years. By definition, **an estate for years is a leasehold estate with a specific starting date and a specific ending date.**

Because you know exactly when it begins and ends on the very first day, **an estate for years can last for any definite duration of time ranging from days to decades.** A three-day rental for a pop-up sneaker shop is an estate for years. A 99-year agreement for a giant skyscraper is also an estate for years!

**Why this is important:**
The coolest thing about an estate for years is that it has a built-in timer. **An estate for years terminates automatically at the exact end of the specified lease term.** Because both sides agreed to the exact finish line on day one, **an estate for years does not require termination notice from the landlord or the tenant.**

Imagine a lease starts on January 1 and ends on June 30. At midnight on June 30, it is simply game over. The landlord doesn't have to text the tenant to remind them, and the tenant doesn't have to write a letter saying they are leaving. The contract is like an alarm clock that turns itself off.

### 2. The Periodic Estate: The Engine of Automatic Renewal

If an estate for years is an alarm clock that turns itself off, a periodic estate is a clock that just keeps hitting the snooze button to restart. **A periodic estate is a leasehold estate that automatically renews for successive periods.**

In New York, **a month-to-month tenancy is the most common example of a periodic estate.** Every time the month ends, the lease magically copies and pastes itself for another month, over and over again. Think of it like a monthly subscription to a streaming service.

Because it doesn't have a final end date, **a periodic estate continues indefinitely until either the landlord or the tenant provides proper termination notice.** In the real estate world, this is super common. It gives people a lot of flexibility, but someone actually has to step up and cancel it. If nobody says "stop," the lease just keeps rolling!

### 3. The Estate at Will: Flexibility with Legal Guardrails

Sometimes, property owners and the people living there keep things super casual. **An estate at will is a leasehold estate possessing no specific term duration.**

Under the most basic idea of this law, **an estate at will grants either the landlord or the tenant the right to terminate the arrangement at any time.** It is like letting your cousin crash on your couch; either of you can say "Okay, time's up!" whenever you want. But since we are talking about New York, there are some extra rules to protect people.

**The New York Rule:**
Even if a tenant is staying "at will" (like a friend living in a basement with no official timeline), **New York law requires a landlord to provide a minimum of 30 days written notice to terminate an estate at will.** So, a landlord can't just kick them out today; they have to give them a month's warning in writing.

Also, because this setup is usually based on a personal relationship, there is a strict rule about what happens if someone passes away. **The death of either the landlord or the tenant automatically terminates an estate at will.** (This is different from an estate for years, which usually keeps going even if the landlord passes away).

### 4. The Estate at Sufferance: The Holdover Dilemma

Now we get to the trickiest part of renting property. **An estate at sufferance occurs when a tenant remains in possession of a property after a lawful lease expires.**

Look closely at the word *sufferance*. It sounds like "suffering," right? The landlord is "suffering" because the tenant is still there! **An estate at sufferance occurs entirely without the consent of the landlord.** The tenant had permission to be there before (so they aren't quite the same as a burglar trespassing), but that permission has run out, and they refuse to leave.

**A tenant occupying a property under an estate at sufferance is legally called a holdover tenant.** They are holding over their welcome!

**Why this matters to you:**
When this happens, **a landlord possesses the legal right to initiate eviction proceedings against a tenant in an estate at sufferance.** Eviction is the official court process to force someone out so the landlord can get their property back.

But landlords often make a huge mistake here. Let's look at a step-by-step math example to see why accepting money is a bad idea in this situation:

Step 1. A tenant's lease expires, turning them into a holdover tenant who is supposed to leave.
Step 2. The tenant hands the landlord a check for \$2,000 to cover one more month of rent.
Step 3. The landlord does the math: \$0 (current rent) + \$2,000 (new check) = \$2,000 extra dollars! The landlord cashes the check.
Step 4. Because the landlord accepted the \$2,000, the law says the landlord just accidentally gave permission for the tenant to stay!

> **The Rent Trap:** **A landlord's acceptance of rent from a holdover tenant converts an estate at sufferance into a periodic month-to-month estate.** 

By taking that check, the landlord accidentally hits the "reset" button. The tenant is no longer there without permission. They bought another month, and the landlord has to start the whole warning process all over again!

## Summary Comparison of Leasehold Estates

To be super smart at real estate, you should memorize how these rules work. Use this chart to quickly understand the differences:

| Type of Leasehold Estate | Term Duration | Termination / Notice Required | Example Scenario |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Estate for Years** | Definite (Exact start and end) | Ends automatically. No notice needed. | A 5-year store lease; a 1-week beach house rental. |
| **Periodic Estate** | Indefinite (Renews over and over) | Keeps going. Needs proper notice to stop. | A normal month-to-month apartment lease. |
| **Estate at Will** | Indefinite (No specific timeline) | Either side can end it. **NY Law:** 30 days written notice needed by landlord. | Letting a cousin stay until they find a new job. |
| **Estate at Sufferance** | Expired | Eviction needed. Taking rent money resets it to a Periodic Estate. | A holdover tenant who refuses to pack up and leave. |

Understanding these rules makes you a real estate expert. When you look at a lease, you won't just see a boring piece of paper. You will see a clever set of rules about time, permission, and getting property back. That is how the real estate game is truly played!
