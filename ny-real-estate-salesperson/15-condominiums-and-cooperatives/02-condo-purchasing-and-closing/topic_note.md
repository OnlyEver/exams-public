Imagine purchasing a highly complex, multi-layered machine where the components are not gears and wires, but overlapping [property rights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Property_rights_%28economics%29), [statutory regulations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statutory_law), and [municipal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Municipality) [safety codes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Building_code). In [New York](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_York_%28state%29), transferring ownership of a newly constructed [condominium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condominium) unit is exactly this process. It is not merely the exchange of money for keys; it is a meticulously choreographed legal sequence. Before a [concrete](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concrete) [foundation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foundation_%28engineering%29) is fully poured, a developer must legally describe every physical and financial parameter of the future building. Before a buyer can cross the threshold, the [local government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_government) must officially certify the structure’s safety. And at the closing table, a unique matrix of state and municipal [taxes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tax), developer fees, and [insurance premiums](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Insurance) must be perfectly balanced. Understanding the architecture of condominium sales—from the initial legal filings to the specific [closing costs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closing_costs)—is fundamental to navigating and mastering New York [real estate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_estate).

## The Blueprint and the Law: The Martin Act and Offering Plans

To understand New York real estate, you must first understand the invisible regulatory shield that protects [consumers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consumer) from buying an illusion. We call this shield the **[Martin Act](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martin_Act)**. 

The Martin Act is a powerful [New York State](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_York_%28state%29) law that regulates the sale of condominiums and [cooperatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Housing_cooperative). Because buying into a newly constructed building involves purchasing a unit that may not even exist yet, the law requires strict transparency. **The [New York Attorney General](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Attorney_General_of_New_York) enforces the Martin Act**, acting as the absolute gatekeeper for new developments. 

Before a developer (often called a "sponsor") can sell a single condo unit to the public, they must file an **offering plan** with the New York Attorney General. You can think of the offering plan as the [DNA sequence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNA_sequencing) of the condominium. It is a detailed [disclosure document](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disclosure_%28law%29) provided to potential [purchasers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buyer) of a new condominium, designed so that buyers know exactly what they are getting into. 

> **What is inside an Offering Plan?**
> *   Detailed information about the physical aspects of the condominium building ([materials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Building_material), layouts, [amenities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amenity)).
> *   A **declaration** detailing the exact legal rights and obligations of the condominium unit owners.
> *   The projected [real property taxes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Property_tax) for the individual condominium units.
> *   An outline of the estimated monthly **common charges** for the first year of condominium operation. 

Until the New York Attorney General formally accepts this offering plan, the developer's hands are tied. **Sponsors cannot enter into legally binding [contracts of sale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contract_of_sale) until the New York Attorney General accepts the offering plan.** 

## Testing the Waters: The CPS-1 Phase

Drafting an offering plan is an immensely expensive and time-consuming process. Imagine spending millions of dollars legally engineering a 500-page document for a new [luxury](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luxury_real_estate) high-rise, only to discover that buyers aren't interested in that neighborhood. 

To solve this, New York created a "[prototype](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prototype)" phase. **Cooperative Policy Statement Number 1 (CPS-1)** allows developers to test the [market](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Market_%28economics%29) for a new condominium *before* filing a full offering plan. 

However, a developer cannot simply throw up a [billboard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Billboard) and start taking [cash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cash). **The New York Attorney General must approve a CPS-1 application before a developer can begin testing the condominium market.** Once approved, a highly regulated testing period begins.

During the CPS-1 phase, the rules of engagement are incredibly strict:
1.  **Developers are legally permitted to [advertise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Advertising)** the proposed condominium project (with prominent [disclaimers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disclaimer)).
2.  **Developers are strictly prohibited from entering into binding contracts of sale.** There is no actual offering plan yet, so there is nothing legally binding to sell.
3.  **Developers use a [letter of intent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Letter_of_intent)** to gauge potential buyer interest. A **letter of intent** is simply a non-binding agreement indicating a prospective buyer's interest in purchasing a specific condo unit. 
4.  **Developers are strictly prohibited from accepting non-refundable deposits** from potential buyers. Any monetary [deposit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deposit_%28finance%29) collected from a prospective buyer during the CPS-1 phase must be fully refundable.

![An example of a Letter of Intent (LOI). During the CPS-1 phase, developers use these non-binding documents to gauge market interest without violating the legal prohibition against executing binding contracts of sale.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Loi_m.gif)

The CPS-1 phase is about measuring temperature, not locking in commitments. It allows the developer to prove to their [lenders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Creditor) that there is [demand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Demand) before moving forward with the heavy machinery of the full offering plan.

## The Green Light to Live: Certificates of Occupancy

Let us fast forward. The offering plan was accepted, the building is constructed, and your client is eager to move in. But a finished building is not necessarily a legal [dwelling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dwelling). 

Before anyone unpacks a box, the building must receive a **[Certificate of Occupancy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Certificate_of_occupancy)** (often abbreviated as a C of O). This is a document issued by the local government verifying that a building strictly complies with current [building codes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Building_code). More profoundly, a Certificate of Occupancy officially certifies that a building is safe for human habitation. 

*   **The Legal Reality:** A newly constructed condominium unit cannot be legally occupied by a resident without a valid Certificate of Occupancy. 
*   **The Financial Reality:** [Mortgage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mortgage_loan) lenders are highly [risk-averse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Risk_aversion). They will typically refuse to fund a mortgage for a new condominium unit without a valid Certificate of Occupancy. 

![A severe building code violation showing flammable polyurethane foam used in a fire-rated wall. Certificates of Occupancy are issued only after local governments verify that structures are free from such hazards and comply strictly with safety codes.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Puf_canadian_paper_mill.jpg)

### The Temporary Certificate of Occupancy (TCO)
[Construction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Construction) is notoriously messy, and often, the residential units are perfectly safe and finished while the lobby or the basement [gym](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gym) is still under construction. Does the buyer have to wait six months to move in? 

No. The city issues a **Temporary Certificate of Occupancy (TCO)**. A Temporary Certificate of Occupancy allows buyers to legally move into a condominium unit while minor building work is being completed elsewhere on the premises. 

> **Important Fact for the Exam:** A Temporary Certificate of Occupancy in New York is typically valid for **90 days**. Developers must continually renew the TCO until the building is completely finished and eligible for the final, permanent C of O.

## The Toll at the Gate: Condominium Closing Costs

When an aspiring real estate professional looks at closing costs, they must recognize a fundamental truth of New York real estate: **Condominium buyers typically pay higher closing costs compared to buyers purchasing [cooperative apartments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Housing_cooperative).** 

Why? Because when you buy a condominium, you are buying actual [real property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_property) (a [deed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deed)), whereas buying a co-op means buying [shares](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Share_%28finance%29) in a [corporation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corporation). Transferring real property triggers a distinct set of legal mechanisms, taxes, and [insurance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Insurance) requirements.

![Historic housing cooperatives on Central Park West in Manhattan. Unlike buying real property in a condominium, purchasing a co-op means buying shares in a corporation, which significantly changes the associated closing costs and tax structure.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Central_Park_West_buildings_over_Lake_crop.jpg)

Let’s break down the specific physics of condo closing costs into logical categories.

### 1. Due Diligence and Protection
A buyer isn't just paying for the physical walls; they are paying to ensure their legal right to exist within them is unshakeable.
*   **[Title Search Fee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Title_search):** A title search fee is charged to the condominium buyer at closing. This pays professionals to dig through [municipal records](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_record) to verify the seller has clear legal title to the unit, free of [liens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lien) or hidden disputes.
*   **[Title Insurance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Title_insurance):** Once the title is searched, it must be insured. Condominium purchasers must buy title insurance to protect against undisclosed defects in property ownership that the search might have missed.
*   **[Homeowner's Insurance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home_insurance):** Lenders require condominium buyers to present a paid homeowner's insurance policy prior to closing the mortgage loan. The lender will not risk funding a \$1 million [asset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asset) that could burn down tomorrow without insurance in place today.

### 2. Financing and Operating Taxes
*   **New York State Mortgage Recording Tax:** If your client is financing their purchase with a loan, they must pay this tax. The New York State Mortgage Recording Tax is calculated as a specific percentage of the total mortgage loan amount. (Note: Co-op buyers do not pay this, because a co-op loan is technically not a real estate mortgage!).
*   **[Prorated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pro_rata) Real Property Taxes:** Condominium buyers are responsible for paying prorated real property taxes from the official date of closing onward. 
*   **Common Charges:** **Common charges** are mandatory monthly fees paid by condominium owners for the maintenance of shared building areas (hallways, [elevators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elevator), [roofs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roof)). At the closing table, condominium buyers typically prepay a specified number of months of common charges to establish a cushion for the condo association.

### 3. The New York State Mansion Tax
This is a critical concept that frequently trips up buyers. New York State imposes a **[Mansion Tax](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mansion_tax)** on residential property purchases of \$1 million or more. 

Despite the name, a \$1 million apartment in [New York City](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_York_City) is rarely a "mansion." But the tax applies nonetheless. The base New York State Mansion Tax rate is **1 percent of the total property purchase price.** 

Here is the vital mechanic you must explain to your clients: **The New York State Mansion Tax applies to the ENTIRE purchase price** of a condominium sold for \$1 million or more. It is not just taxed on the amount *over* \$1 million. If you buy a condo for \$999,999, the tax is zero. If you buy it for \$1,000,000, the tax is \$10,000. 

Furthermore, this tax is strictly the buyer's burden. **The buyer is legally responsible for paying the New York State Mansion Tax.** And as property values climb, the state takes a larger cut: **The Mansion Tax rate increases [progressively](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Progressive_tax) for residential properties sold at \$2 million or higher in New York City.**

### 4. The Sponsor Sale Add-Ons
When a client buys a *resale* condo from a regular person, the seller typically pays the state and city [transfer taxes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transfer_tax). But when a client buys a *brand-new* condominium directly from a sponsor (the developer), the developer usually forces the buyer to shoulder the seller's traditional costs. 

If your client is buying new construction, prepare them for this reality check:

| Cost Transferred to Buyer | Explanation |
| :--- | :--- |
| **State Transfer Tax** | Condominium buyers purchasing directly from a sponsor are often required to pay the sponsor's New York State [Transfer Tax](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transfer_tax). |
| **City Transfer Tax** | Condominium buyers purchasing directly from a sponsor in New York City are often required to pay the sponsor's Real Property [Transfer Tax](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transfer_tax). |
| **Developer's Attorney Fees** | Purchasers of new construction condominiums from a sponsor are typically required to pay the developer's [attorney fees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Attorney%27s_fee) at closing. |
| **Working Capital Fund** | Sponsor sales in new condominium developments often require the buyer to make a one-time contribution to the building's [working capital](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Working_capital) fund. A working capital fund contribution at closing is typically equivalent to one or two months of the unit's common charges. |
