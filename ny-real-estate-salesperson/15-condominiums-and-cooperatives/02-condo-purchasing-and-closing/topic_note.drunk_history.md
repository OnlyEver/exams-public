Listen to me, rookie. Stop stirring your drink and look me in the eye, because if you want to survive the absolute jungle of New York real estate, you have to understand that selling a newly constructed condo is not just handing over shiny keys to a penthouse. It is a high-stakes heist, a grueling reality TV survival show, and a chaotic restaurant kitchen all rolled into one beautiful, terrifying legal nightmare. You think developers just stack bricks and start cashing checks? Not a chance. They are trapped in a regulatory gauntlet, and it all starts with the ultimate mafia boss of New York property law: the Martin Act. The Martin Act is a New York State law that regulates the sale of condominiums and cooperatives, and it does not mess around. It acts as an iron-clad shield to keep developers from selling thin air to unsuspecting buyers. And who is the terrifying enforcer wielding this shield? The New York Attorney General enforces the Martin Act. They are the ultimate bouncer at the club, and before a developer—also known as a sponsor—can even dream of selling a single unit to the public, developers must file an offering plan with the New York Attorney General. Think of this offering plan as the 5,000-page, painstakingly detailed recipe book for a 50-course meal. It is a detailed disclosure document provided to potential purchasers of a new condominium so everyone knows exactly what kind of beast is being built. This colossal document contains detailed information about the physical aspects of the condominium building—we’re talking the layout of the pipes, the thickness of the glass, the specific Italian marble in the lobby. But it’s not just architecture; it’s a legal constitution. The offering plan includes a declaration detailing the exact rights and obligations of the condominium unit owners, laying down the law on what buyers can and cannot do. It contains the projected real estate property taxes for the condominium units, so buyers know how hard the city will hit their wallets, and it outlines the estimated monthly common charges for the first year of condominium operation so they know what it costs to keep the lights on. And here is the golden rule: sponsors absolutely cannot enter into legally binding contracts of sale until the New York Attorney General accepts the offering plan. If they try to lock a buyer down before the AG gives the nod, they are toast. 

But what if the developer is terrified of cooking this massive 50-course meal without knowing if anyone even wants to eat it? Enter the reality TV pilot episode of real estate: Cooperative Policy Statement Number 1, or CPS-1. This legal loophole allows developers to test the market for a new condominium before filing a full offering plan. However, they can't just go rogue. The New York Attorney General must approve a CPS-1 application before a developer can begin testing the condominium market. Once they get that green light, the game begins, but the rules of engagement are strictly controlled. During the CPS-1 phase, developers are legally permitted to advertise the proposed condominium project. They can throw up billboards and throw lavish parties with fancy renderings. But during the CPS-1 phase, developers are strictly prohibited from entering into binding contracts of sale! The offering plan isn't fully baked yet, so they have nothing legal to sell. Furthermore, during the CPS-1 phase, developers are strictly prohibited from accepting non-refundable deposits from potential buyers. So, how do they know if the hype is real? Developers use a letter of intent during the CPS-1 phase to gauge potential buyer interest. Think of this as a polite wink across the bar; a letter of intent is just a non-binding agreement indicating a prospective buyer's interest in purchasing a specific condo unit. It’s a pinky promise, not a blood oath. And if a buyer is swept up in the romance and hands over cash to hold their spot, any monetary deposit collected from a prospective buyer during the CPS-1 phase must be fully refundable. 

Let's fast-forward. The offering plan was approved, the cranes are gone, the building is actually standing. Your eager buyer is packed and ready to move their velvet sofa into the penthouse. Not so fast! Enter the final boss of the municipal government: the Certificate of Occupancy. A Certificate of Occupancy is a document issued by the local government verifying that a building strictly complies with current building codes. It is the golden ticket that officially certifies that a building is safe for human habitation, basically guaranteeing the roof won't collapse on your client's head while they sleep. This isn't optional, my friend. A newly constructed condominium unit cannot be legally occupied by a resident without a valid Certificate of Occupancy. It is a literal crime to live there without it. And if you think the banks are going to look the other way, think again. Mortgage lenders will typically refuse to fund a mortgage for a new condominium unit without a valid Certificate of Occupancy. They are not about to wire a million dollars for an illegal concrete box. But let’s be real, construction is messy. What if the apartment is flawless, but the basement gym is still missing its flooring? Do you hold up the whole deal? No, you get a lifeline! A Temporary Certificate of Occupancy allows buyers to legally move into a condominium unit while minor building work is being completed. But the clock starts ticking the second it's issued, because a Temporary Certificate of Occupancy in New York is typically valid for 90 days, meaning the developer has to scramble to finish the job or constantly renew the paperwork before the city kicks everyone out.

Now, grab your client's wallet, because we are arriving at the closing table, and it is an absolute bloodbath. You need to warn them early that condominium buyers typically pay higher closing costs compared to buyers purchasing cooperative apartments. Why? Because condo buyers are purchasing actual, physical real property—deeded dirt and air—while co-op buyers are basically just buying shares in a glorified corporate book club. First up on the chopping block: verifying that nobody else owns that dirt. A title search fee is charged to the condominium buyer at closing to verify the seller has clear legal title to the unit, ensuring no secret ex-spouse is going to claim the master bedroom in five years. Once the title is clear, you have to bulletproof it. Condominium purchasers must buy title insurance to protect against undisclosed defects in property ownership that the search might have miraculously missed. Then comes the bank’s paranoia; lenders require condominium buyers to present a paid homeowner's insurance policy prior to closing the mortgage loan, because they won't risk their collateral burning to the ground without a safety net. And speaking of loans, if your buyer is borrowing money, they trigger a massive trap card: condominium purchasers who finance their purchase with a loan must pay the New York State Mortgage Recording Tax. This monster is calculated as a specific percentage of the total mortgage loan amount, and it bites hard. But the hemorrhaging doesn't stop there. Condominium buyers are responsible for paying prorated real property taxes from the official date of closing onward, paying the city for the privilege of existing. They also have to keep the building’s heart beating, which means condominium buyers typically prepay a specified number of months of common charges at the closing table. Remember, common charges are mandatory monthly fees paid by condominium owners for the maintenance of shared building areas. The lobby concierge and the elevator mechanics do not work for free!

Just when your client thinks they have survived the gauntlet, the state of New York kicks down the door and demands the ultimate cover charge. Listen closely, because this breaks people: New York State imposes a Mansion Tax on residential property purchases of 1 million dollars or more. Now, a million bucks in this city gets you a closet with a window, not a mansion, but the state doesn't care. The base New York State Mansion Tax rate is 1 percent of the total property purchase price. And here is the brutal part you must hammer into your client's brain: the New York State Mansion Tax applies to the ENTIRE purchase price of a condominium sold for 1 million dollars or more. It’s not a tax on the excess over a million; if the price tag hits a million, the state takes 1 percent of the whole pie from dollar zero. And there is no splitting the bill here, because the buyer is legally responsible for paying the New York State Mansion Tax. If your client is buying in the big leagues, it gets worse, as the Mansion Tax rate increases progressively for residential properties sold at 2 million dollars or higher in New York City. Finally, if your buyer is purchasing a brand-new unit directly from the developer, prepare them for the absolute wildest heist in the industry: the sponsor add-ons. In a normal resale, the seller pays the transfer taxes, but sponsors flip the script! Condominium buyers purchasing directly from a sponsor are often required to pay the sponsor's New York State Transfer Tax! And if you are in the five boroughs, condominium buyers purchasing directly from a sponsor in New York City are often required to pay the sponsor's Real Property Transfer Tax as well! You are literally paying the developer's taxes for them! It’s madness! On top of that, purchasers of new construction condominiums from a sponsor are typically required to pay the developer's attorney fees at closing. And as one final parting gift, sponsor sales in new condominium developments often require the buyer to make a one-time contribution to the building's working capital fund. This is just a massive piggy bank for the building's future, and a working capital fund contribution at closing is typically equivalent to one or two months of the unit's common charges. So, rookie, when you walk into that closing room, you aren't just a salesperson pointing at nice countertops; you are the guide dragging them through a financial war zone. Master the madness, and you'll own this town. Now finish your drink.
