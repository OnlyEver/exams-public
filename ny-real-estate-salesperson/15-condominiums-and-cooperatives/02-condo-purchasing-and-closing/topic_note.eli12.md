Imagine buying a giant LEGO set that you are actually going to live in, but instead of plastic bricks, it is made out of rules, safety checks, and fees. Buying a brand-new apartment (called a condominium or "condo") in New York is a huge deal. Before a builder pours the first drop of concrete, they have to write down everything about the future building. Before someone can move in, the city has to say it is safe. And when you finally pay for it, there are lots of extra costs to balance, kind of like keeping track of your allowance and buying new video games. Let's learn how this all works!

## The Blueprint and the Law: The Martin Act and Offering Plans

To understand New York real estate, you first need to know about the rulebook that protects regular people from getting tricked. We call this rulebook the **Martin Act**. 

The Martin Act is a New York State law that regulates the sale of condominiums and cooperatives. Because buying into a new building sometimes means paying for an apartment that isn't even built yet, the law makes sure builders are honest. **The New York Attorney General enforces the Martin Act**, acting like the ultimate referee for new buildings.

Before a builder (often called a "developer" or "sponsor") can sell a single condo unit to the public, they must file an **offering plan** with the New York Attorney General. An offering plan is a detailed disclosure document provided to potential purchasers of a new condominium. Think of it like a giant instruction manual so buyers know exactly what they are spending their money on. 

> **What is inside an Offering Plan?**
> *   An offering plan contains detailed information about the physical aspects of the condominium building, like what materials are used and how the rooms are laid out.
> *   An offering plan includes a declaration detailing the rights and obligations of the condominium unit owners. This is a list of rules for what you can and cannot do in your new home.
> *   An offering plan contains the projected real estate property taxes for the condominium units. This tells you how much tax money the city will ask for later.
> *   An offering plan outlines the estimated monthly common charges for the first year of condominium operation. These are fees you pay to help keep the building clean and running smoothly.

The referee has to say "Okay!" before anyone can actually make a deal. Sponsors cannot enter into legally binding contracts of sale until the New York Attorney General accepts the offering plan.

## Testing the Waters: The CPS-1 Phase

Writing that giant instruction manual costs a lot of time and money. Imagine spending all your allowance to build a fancy lemonade stand, only to find out nobody in your neighborhood likes lemonade!

To fix this, New York has a special testing phase. **Cooperative Policy Statement Number 1 (CPS-1)** allows developers to test the market for a new condominium before filing a full offering plan. It is like putting up a "Coming Soon!" sign to see if people actually want to live there.

But builders cannot just do this on their own. **The New York Attorney General must approve a CPS-1 application before a developer can begin testing the condominium market.**

During this CPS-1 test phase, the rules are very strict:
1.  **Developers are legally permitted to advertise** the proposed condominium project. They can show cool pictures of what the building might look like.
2.  **Developers are strictly prohibited from entering into binding contracts of sale.** They cannot make a real deal because the official offering plan is not ready yet.
3.  Developers use a letter of intent during the CPS-1 phase to gauge potential buyer interest. A **letter of intent** is a non-binding agreement indicating a prospective buyer's interest in purchasing a specific condo unit. It just means the buyer is saying, "I might want to buy this later!"
4.  **Developers are strictly prohibited from accepting non-refundable deposits** from potential buyers. If a buyer gives the builder a little money to hold their spot, any monetary deposit collected from a prospective buyer during the CPS-1 phase must be fully refundable. If the buyer changes their mind, they get every single penny back.

The CPS-1 phase is just to see if people are interested, not to lock them in!

## The Green Light to Live: Certificates of Occupancy

Let us skip ahead. The offering plan was accepted, the building is built, and you are ready to move in. Can you just pack your boxes and sleep there? Not yet.

Before anyone can move in, the building needs a giant permission slip. A **Certificate of Occupancy** is a document issued by the local government verifying that a building complies with current building codes. Most importantly, a Certificate of Occupancy officially certifies that a building is safe for human habitation. It means the roof will not cave in and the wires are safe.

*   **The Law:** A newly constructed condominium unit cannot be legally occupied by a resident without a valid Certificate of Occupancy. 
*   **The Money:** Banks do not want to risk losing their money. Mortgage lenders will typically refuse to fund a mortgage for a new condominium unit without a valid Certificate of Occupancy. 

### The Temporary Certificate of Occupancy (TCO)
Sometimes, your apartment is completely finished, but the workers are still painting the lobby. Do you have to wait to move in? 

Nope! The city gives out a pass called a **Temporary Certificate of Occupancy**. A Temporary Certificate of Occupancy allows buyers to legally move into a condominium unit while minor building work is being completed somewhere else in the building. 

> **Important Fact:** A Temporary Certificate of Occupancy in New York is typically valid for 90 days. Builders have to keep renewing this pass until the whole building is 100 percent finished.

## The Toll at the Gate: Condominium Closing Costs

When it is finally time to officially buy the condo, there is a final step called "closing." This is when you pay the money and get the keys. But there are extra fees. 

One big thing to remember: **Condominium buyers typically pay higher closing costs compared to buyers purchasing cooperative apartments.** 

Why? Because when you buy a condo, you own actual, physical property, whereas a "co-op" means you are just buying shares in a company. Let's look at what these extra costs are.

### 1. Due Diligence and Protection
Buyers want to make sure their new home is really theirs and safe from disasters.
*   **Title Search Fee:** A title search fee is charged to the condominium buyer at closing to verify the seller has clear legal title to the unit. This pays a detective to check old records to make sure nobody else secretly owns the apartment.
*   **Title Insurance:** Detectives can miss things. Condominium purchasers must buy title insurance to protect against undisclosed defects in property ownership. If a stranger shows up claiming the apartment is theirs, this insurance protects you.
*   **Homeowner's Insurance:** Lenders require condominium buyers to present a paid homeowner's insurance policy prior to closing the mortgage loan. A bank will not give you a loan if a fire could destroy the building and leave them with nothing!

### 2. Financing and Operating Taxes
*   **New York State Mortgage Recording Tax:** If you borrow money from a bank (a mortgage) to buy the condo, you have to pay a tax on that loan. Condominium purchasers who finance their purchase with a loan must pay the New York State Mortgage Recording Tax. The New York State Mortgage Recording Tax is calculated as a specific percentage of the total mortgage loan amount.
*   **Prorated Real Property Taxes:** You only pay taxes for the time you actually own the place. Condominium buyers are responsible for paying prorated real property taxes from the official date of closing onward. "Prorated" just means paying your fair share.
*   **Common Charges:** **Common charges** are mandatory monthly fees paid by condominium owners for the maintenance of shared building areas (like hallways, elevators, and gyms). Condominium buyers typically prepay a specified number of months of common charges at the closing table so the building has a piggy bank started right away.

### 3. The New York State Mansion Tax
This is a super important rule. New York State imposes a **Mansion Tax** on residential property purchases of 1 million dollars or more. 

Even though a 1 million dollar apartment in New York City is usually just a regular apartment and not a huge mansion, the tax still happens. The base New York State Mansion Tax rate is 1 percent of the total property purchase price. 

Here is the trick: The New York State Mansion Tax applies to the ENTIRE purchase price of a condominium sold for 1 million dollars or more. It is not just taxed on the extra money above 1 million dollars; it taxes the whole thing. And remember, the buyer is legally responsible for paying the New York State Mansion Tax. 

Also, if the apartment is super expensive, the tax gets bigger: The Mansion Tax rate increases progressively for residential properties sold at 2 million dollars or higher in New York City.

**Step-by-Step Math Example for the Mansion Tax:**
Let's say a buyer is getting a condo for \$1,500,000. How much is the base Mansion Tax?
1. Find the total purchase price: \$1,500,000.
2. Check if the price is \$1,000,000 or more. Yes, it is! So the tax applies to the *entire* amount.
3. Find the base tax rate: The rate is 1 percent. To make this a decimal for math, 1 percent is 0.01.
4. Multiply the total purchase price by the tax rate: \$1,500,000 x 0.01 = \$15,000.
5. The total Mansion Tax the buyer must pay is \$15,000.

### 4. The Sponsor Sale Add-Ons
When you buy a used video game from a friend, you just pay your friend. But when you buy a brand-new game from a store, there are extra store fees. It is the same with condos. When you buy a brand-new condo directly from the builder (the sponsor), they often make the buyer pay extra fees!

| Cost Transferred to Buyer | Explanation |
| :--- | :--- |
| **State Transfer Tax** | Condominium buyers purchasing directly from a sponsor are often required to pay the sponsor's New York State Transfer Tax. |
| **City Transfer Tax** | Condominium buyers purchasing directly from a sponsor in New York City are often required to pay the sponsor's Real Property Transfer Tax. |
| **Developer's Attorney Fees** | Purchasers of new construction condominiums from a sponsor are typically required to pay the developer's attorney fees at closing. That means you pay the builder's lawyer! |
| **Working Capital Fund** | Sponsor sales in new condominium developments often require the buyer to make a one-time contribution to the building's working capital fund. A working capital fund contribution at closing is typically equivalent to one or two months of the unit's common charges. This gives the building emergency money right from the start. |
