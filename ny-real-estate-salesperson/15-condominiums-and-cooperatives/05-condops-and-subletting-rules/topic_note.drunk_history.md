Slide that bowl of peanuts over and listen to me, because you look like a rookie who just got handed a nightmare listing: a towering mixed-use building in Manhattan. Downstairs is a bustling, massively profitable luxury grocery store, and upstairs are a hundred residential units. Now, if that entire building operates as a standard cooperative, we have a catastrophic crisis on our hands. And the villain in this horror movie? The IRS.

Historically, Internal Revenue Code Section 216 historically dictated the 80/20 rule for cooperative housing corporations. This was a terrifying, absolute mandate. The historical 80/20 rule required a cooperative to derive at least 80 percent of its gross income directly from its tenant-shareholders. This meant that, mathematically, under the historical 80/20 rule, a cooperative could not receive more than 20 percent of its income from passive commercial sources. 

Do you see the ticking time bomb? If that luxury grocery store on the ground floor is too successful, and their massive rent check pushes the building’s passive commercial income past that 20 percent threshold, boom. The entire building is blown to pieces financially. Why? Because violating the historical 80/20 rule prevented cooperative shareholders from deducting their share of the building property taxes and mortgage interest on their personal tax returns! Imagine looking your high-net-worth client in the eye and telling them their tax deductions evaporated because the grocery store downstairs sold too many organic avocados. Unthinkable!

So, real estate attorneys pulled off a legal heist to save everyone's skin. Developers originally created condops to protect cooperative shareholders from losing tax deductions under the IRS 80/20 rule. 

A condop is a building that incorporates both condominium and cooperative ownership structures. Think of it as the turducken of New York real estate—a co-op stuffed inside a condo. Instead of treating the building as one entity, they legally chop it up. The commercial spaces in a condop are structured as separate condominium units. And who keeps those golden geese? The commercial condominium units in a condop are usually retained by the building developer to rake in that sweet retail rent. 

Meanwhile, they take all the apartments upstairs and bundle them together. In a standard condop structure, the entire residential portion of the building operates as a single condominium unit. Just one massive unit! And here is the genius part: the residential condominium unit in a condop is owned by a cooperative corporation. 

By executing this maneuver, the condop structure completely isolates commercial income from the cooperative corporation. The co-op board only collects maintenance from its residents; it never touches a single penny of the grocery store's rent. Because they are legally shielded from the retail money, isolating commercial income in a condop allowed the residential cooperative to easily meet the 80/20 rule requirements. Crisis averted! 

Now, I should tell you, the government eventually called off the dogs. The Mortgage Forgiveness Debt Relief Act of 2007 amended the strict 80/20 income requirement for cooperatives. Today, current IRS rules provide three alternative tests for cooperative tax deductions based on income, square footage, or expenditures. As long as the building passes just one of those tests, the deductions are safe. But the condop structure remains incredibly popular because developers love keeping those ground-floor spaces for themselves.

But let’s talk about what happens once you’re inside that residential co-op, because you are entering a paranoid, sovereign nation. Co-op boards are obsessed with stability. They treat the building like a gated fortress, which is why cooperative boards generally require shareholders to use the apartment as their primary residence. They want owners who have skin in the game. When a buyer commits and moves in, primary residency in a cooperative generally grants the shareholder full usage rights of the apartment. They are part of the family.

But the second your client gets a two-year job transfer to London and wants to rent out their place, all hell breaks loose. Subletting a cooperative unit transfers temporary occupancy rights to a non-shareholder. To the board, a subtenant is a transient ghost with no loyalty to the building. They despise this. 

How much do they despise it? Well, you better read the proprietary lease, because many cooperative boards prohibit subletting entirely. Flat out banned. End of discussion. 

If you are lucky enough to find a merciful board, cooperative boards that permit subletting typically require the shareholder to live in the unit for a minimum period before subletting. They make you prove your loyalty first—usually making you sweat it out as a resident for a year or two before you can even ask for permission. And don’t think your client can become a permanent absentee landlord. To prevent that, cooperative sublet policies frequently restrict the maximum duration a shareholder can sublet their unit over a set number of years. It’s like a parole limit—maybe they allow two years out of every five-year rolling period. 

And they will make your client pay for the privilege. Cooperative boards can legally charge shareholders a sublet fee for renting out their unit, just siphoning off a chunk of your client's rental income to cover the wear and tear on the elevators. 

Even if you jump through all those flaming hoops, cooperative boards have the absolute right to review and approve or deny any proposed subtenant. But here is a fascinating psychological quirk you need to use to your advantage: cooperative boards apply different scrutiny levels to primary resident purchasers compared to subtenants. When you bring them a buyer, the board turns their bank accounts inside out, obsessing over post-closing liquidity and debt-to-income. But for a subtenant? The original shareholder is still the one on the hook for the maintenance check, so the board's scrutiny of the subtenant shifts heavily toward character. They just want to make sure this person isn't going to flood the bathroom or host midnight drum circles. 

Now, lean in, because I am going to give you the ultimate cheat code for bypassing all of this board madness. 

When a building is first built or converted to a co-op, the developer doesn't magically sell every unit on day one. Sponsor shares are shares in a cooperative building held by the original developer or converter. The law treats these developer holdovers like absolute royalty. Individuals or entities holding sponsor shares are legally classified as Holders of Unsold Shares. 

If you find a listing marketed as a "Sponsor Unit," it means it's owned by a Holder of Unsold Shares, and the normal rules of physics do not apply. Remember that terrifying board interview for buyers? A Holder of Unsold Shares is exempt from cooperative board approval when selling the unit. Your buyer just signs the contract and walks right in! Remember the strict sublet bans? If the developer decides to rent it out instead, a Holder of Unsold Shares is exempt from cooperative board approval when subletting the unit. They can rent it to anyone! And those extortionate sublet fees? A Holder of Unsold Shares is typically exempt from paying cooperative sublet fees. It is a VIP pass written directly into the building's legal DNA.

But you have to warn your buyers about the Cinderella effect. This magic aura is attached to the entity holding the shares, not the plaster on the walls. Sponsor shares lose their Holder of Unsold Shares status once purchased by a bona fide resident. The moment your client buys that sponsor unit, bypasses the board, and moves in, the carriage turns back into a pumpkin. If they ever want to sell or sublet five years down the line, they are completely stripped of their immunity and will be subjected to the exact same grueling board rules, sublet bans, and fees as every other peasant in the building.
