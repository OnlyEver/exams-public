Listen to me, slide that study guide away for a second and look at this napkin. We need to talk about the absolute most dangerous piece of paper you will ever handle as a real estate agent in New York. You’re learning about financing and agency rules, which is great, but all of that is just the opening act. The main event, the grand finale of a closing, is the deed. A deed is a written legal instrument used to transfer ownership of real property from a grantor to a grantee. Think of it as the ultimate, million-dollar baton in a high-stakes relay race. The grantor is the party transferring the real property interest via a deed—they are the ones sprinting and throwing the baton. The grantee is the party receiving the real property interest via a deed. That’s your client, standing there, praying they don’t drop it or catch a baton covered in legal spikes. 

But here is the catch about this relay race: you can't throw the baton from the grave, and you can't throw it at someone who refuses to catch it. A deed must be delivered by the grantor during the grantor's lifetime to effectively transfer property title. If your seller signs the paperwork, puts it in their desk, and kicks the bucket before handing it over, that transfer is entirely dead in the water. Furthermore, a deed must be accepted by the grantee to effectively transfer property title. You can’t just mail a deed to your nemesis, saddle them with a crumbling toxic waste dump, and declare them the new owner! They have to knowingly and willingly grab the baton.

Now, not all batons are created equal. As an agent, the type of deed your client catches dictates how much protective Kevlar they are wearing. The absolute gold standard, the titanium vault of real estate, is the full covenant and warranty deed. A full covenant and warranty deed provides the greatest level of legal protection to a property grantee. Why? Because a full covenant and warranty deed includes all standard property deed covenants. Covenants are essentially unbreakable legal promises woven into the paper. Let me break down these six promises, because they are the armor your buyer wears. 

First, the covenant of seizin guarantees that the grantor is the true and lawful owner of the property. They are looking you in the eye and swearing, "I am not a squatter, I actually own this dirt." Next, the covenant of right to convey guarantees that the grantor has the legal capacity to transfer the property title. You could own the property, but if you are secretly fifteen years old or locked down in a restrictive trust, you can't legally sell it! Third, the covenant against encumbrances warrants that the property is free from undisclosed liens. The seller is promising there is no secret IRS agent or unpaid roofer lurking in the attic waiting to demand a massive check from your buyer. Fourth, we have a twin promise: the covenant against encumbrances warrants that the property is free from undisclosed easements. This means the town snowplow doesn't possess a secret, historic right to drive straight through your client’s new azalea bushes! Fifth is my favorite: the covenant of quiet enjoyment. This has zero to do with noisy neighbors playing bass at 2 AM. The covenant of quiet enjoyment guarantees that the grantee's possession of the property will not be disturbed by third-party title claims. It promises that some estranged nephew isn't going to bust through the living room wall like the Kool-Aid Man, waving a will from 1982 and screaming that he owns half the kitchen. Finally, we have the enforcement muscles: the covenant of further assurance requires the grantor to perform any future legal acts necessary to perfect the grantee's property title—so if the county clerk finds a typo on the paperwork five years from now, the seller must legally come back and fix it. And the covenant of warranty forever requires the grantor to defend the grantee's title against all lawful claims of other parties. If that Kool-Aid Man nephew actually files a lawsuit, the grantor is legally obligated to pull out their checkbook and pay to fight him in court, forever! 

Because this titanium shield is so wildly protective, it puts a massive amount of historical liability on the seller. For this reason, a full covenant and warranty deed is commonly utilized in upstate New York residential real estate transactions. Up there, where you have massive acreage, centuries-old farm boundary disputes, and weird logging rights, buyers absolutely demand maximum protection. 

But if you are practicing down here in the concrete jungle? Good luck getting a seller to sign that. Down here, we use the pragmatic middle ground: the bargain and sale deed. A bargain and sale deed implies that the grantor holds legal title to the property, and a bargain and sale deed implies that the grantor holds possession of the property. Notice I said *implies*, not *guarantees*. Implication is like your buddy saying he’s "pretty sure" he defused the bomb. It’s comforting, but it's not a promise.

This is where the bargain and sale deed splits into two distinct, high-drama paths. On one hand, you have the absolute barebones version: a basic bargain and sale deed without covenants offers no warranties against property encumbrances. The seller is handing you the keys and saying, "I own this, I'm giving it to you, and whatever historical financial curses are attached to it are now your problem." You will rarely see this in a normal client deal, but a basic bargain and sale deed without covenants is commonly used to transfer property during New York tax sales. When a municipality seizes a neglected building because the owner didn't pay their taxes, the city government auctions it off. The city absolutely refuses to vouch for the cleanliness of the title; they just want it off their books! 

On the other hand, the version you will see every single day is a bargain and sale deed with covenants against grantor's acts. This specifically guarantees that the grantor did not encumber the property during the grantor's ownership period. They aren't vouching for the entire history of the universe like a full warranty deed. They are just saying, "Look, from the day I bought this place to the day I’m selling it to you, I paid my taxes, I didn't secretly mortgage it, and I didn't wreck the title. But I make zero promises about the guy who owned it in the 1970s!" Because we have such a massive, aggressive title insurance industry down here handling historical defects, a bargain and sale deed with covenants against grantor's acts is the customary deed used in downstate New York residential real estate sales—we're talking all five boroughs, Long Island, and Westchester. By the way, if you get a hot-shot client relocating from Texas or Florida, they might get confused and ask you for a "Special Warranty Deed." Don't panic. A bargain and sale deed with covenants against grantor's acts is referred to as a special warranty deed in many states outside of New York. It’s the exact same script, just a different stage name.

Now, let’s dive into the absolute lowest rung of the ladder, the reality-TV drama of paperwork: the quitclaim deed. A quitclaim deed provides the least amount of legal protection to a property grantee. It is the ultimate "As-Is" mystery box. A quitclaim deed contains no warranties regarding the property title, and a quitclaim deed contains no covenants regarding the property title. It is completely entirely naked. In fact, a quitclaim deed transfers only the ownership interest that the grantor actually possesses at the exact time of the property transfer. The grantor is legally stating, "If I own any piece of this property—and I am absolutely not promising that I do—I am giving that piece to you right this exact second." 

Why on earth would anyone accept a deed with zero protection? Because it is a razor-sharp scalpel for resolving legal messes without launching a full-blown investigation! For instance, a quitclaim deed is frequently used to clear a cloud on a property title. If a title search turns up a random heir from fifty years ago who *might* technically own one-sixteenth of the driveway, you don't fight them in court. You pay them \$500 to sign a quitclaim deed, which instantly vaporizes any claim they might possess, clearing the cloud. Furthermore, a quitclaim deed is frequently used for real property transfers between family members. If a mother wants to hand off the family home to her son, they don't need to hire inspectors and draft a warranty defending the title against themselves; she just quitclaims her interest directly to him. And of course, a quitclaim deed is frequently used during divorce settlements to transfer property interests. When a judge orders that the ex-husband gets the car but the ex-wife keeps the house, the husband signs a quitclaim, instantly ripping his name off the property rights so he can flee to his new bachelor pad and she can change the locks.

Finally, as an agent, you will occasionally step into the aftermath of a disaster, which requires specialized court and estate paperwork. If a homeowner stops paying their mortgage, the bank forecloses, and the property gets auctioned off on the courthouse steps. A referee's deed is utilized to transfer property ownership following a foreclosure sale in New York. The referee is just an impartial court official managing the auction. Because they have zero personal connection to the property, a referee's deed contains no covenants regarding the transferred property title. The winning bidder is buying a total wild card. And what happens if your seller literally dies before listing the house? An executor's deed is used to transfer real property belonging to the estate of a deceased person. The executor is just the poor soul appointed to clean up the deceased person's financial life. It would be insane to ask an executor to personally guarantee the entire history of a house they never even lived in! Therefore, an executor's deed typically contains a covenant against grantor's acts to warrant that the executor did not encumber the property. The executor is promising, "I managed the estate cleanly during the probate process, but whatever crazy secret mortgages my dead uncle took out in Vegas in 1998 are completely on you!" 

Master the baton pass. Know exactly what kind of Kevlar your client is wearing, and you won't just be a salesperson showing off stainless steel appliances—you'll be the one keeping them completely safe from the Kool-Aid Man.
