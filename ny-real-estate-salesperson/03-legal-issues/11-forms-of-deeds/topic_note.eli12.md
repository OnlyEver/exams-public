Trading a piece of land is a lot like trading a video game console with your friend. You can't just say, "The game is yours now," and call it a day. You need to physically hand it over so your friend actually gets it. In real estate, the physical item you hand over to prove the trade is called a **deed**. A deed is a written legal instrument used to transfer ownership of real property from a grantor to a grantee. 

Let's break those two important words down:
*   The **grantor** is the party transferring the real property interest via a deed. Think of the grantor as the "giver."
*   The **grantee** is the party receiving the real property interest via a deed. Think of the grantee as the "getter."

For the trade to count, printing a piece of paper isn't enough. A deed must be delivered by the grantor during the grantor's lifetime to effectively transfer property title. You can't deliver it after you pass away! Also, a deed must be accepted by the grantee to effectively transfer property title. If your friend refuses to take the paper, the trade doesn't happen.

Why do the different types of deeds matter? Let's use a quick math example. Imagine buying a used bike for \$100, but the previous owner secretly owes the local repair shop \$20 for fixing the tires. If you aren't protected by a good deed, you might have to pay that secret debt! 
Here is how that math works out:
Step 1: You pay the agreed price for the bike (\$100).
Step 2: You discover the secret debt and have to pay it off (-\$20).
Step 3: The actual value of the bike you get is only \$100 - \$20 = \$80.

Different deeds give you different levels of protection to stop you from losing that \$20.

## Covenants: The Physics of Legal Promises

Before we look at the types of deeds, we need to understand the building blocks of protection: **covenants**. 

A covenant is basically a super-strong, legally binding promise. When you buy a house, you want promises that no one else is going to pop up and say they own it, or that there are no secret debts attached to the roof.

There are six standard promises you need to know:

1.  **The Covenant of Seizin:** This guarantees that the grantor is the true and lawful owner of the property. They are promising, "I actually own this and I'm not faking it."
2.  **The Covenant of Right to Convey:** Just because you own something doesn't mean you are allowed to sell it. This guarantees that the grantor has the legal capacity to transfer the property title.
3.  **The Covenant Against Encumbrances (Liens):** A lien is like a secret debt. This warrants that the property is free from undisclosed liens.
4.  **The Covenant Against Encumbrances (Easements):** An easement is a rule letting someone else use your land. This warrants that the property is free from undisclosed easements (like a secret rule letting your neighbor ride their dirt bike through your yard).
5.  **The Covenant of Quiet Enjoyment:** This guarantees that the grantee's possession of the property will not be disturbed by third-party title claims. It means a random stranger won't knock on your door years later claiming the house belongs to them.
6.  **The Covenants of Further Assurance and Warranty Forever:** These are the ultimate backup plans! The **covenant of further assurance** requires the grantor to perform any future legal acts necessary to perfect the grantee's property title (like signing a forgotten piece of paperwork later). The **covenant of warranty forever** requires the grantor to defend the grantee's title against all lawful claims of other parties. If someone sues you for the house, the seller promises to pay for the lawyers to fight them!

## The Gold Standard: Full Covenant and Warranty Deeds

> **Full Covenant and Warranty Deed:** A deed that provides the greatest level of legal protection to a property grantee by giving them all the best promises.

If you are a buyer, this is the best piece of paper you can get! A full covenant and warranty deed provides the greatest level of legal protection to a property grantee. Why? Because a full covenant and warranty deed includes all standard property deed covenants. The seller is making every single promise we listed above!

Because it asks the seller to make so many huge promises, it is not used everywhere. However, a full covenant and warranty deed is commonly utilized in upstate New York residential real estate transactions. People in upstate New York often have large properties with very old histories, so buyers want the maximum protection possible.

## The Pragmatic Middle Ground: Bargain and Sale Deeds

A **bargain and sale deed** implies that the grantor holds legal title to the property. It also implies that the grantor holds possession of the property. But remember, implying something is just dropping a hint; it isn't an actual legal promise!

This type of deed splits into two different versions:

### Basic Bargain and Sale Deed (Without Covenants)
A basic bargain and sale deed without covenants offers no warranties against property encumbrances. The seller is essentially saying, "I own this, and I'm giving it to you, but I promise nothing else. Good luck." 

Because it is so risky, a basic bargain and sale deed without covenants is commonly used to transfer property during New York tax sales. When a city takes over a house because the owner didn't pay their taxes, the city just auctions it off exactly as it is, without making any promises about secret debts.

### Bargain and Sale Deed with Covenants Against Grantor's Acts
A bargain and sale deed with covenants against grantor's acts guarantees that the grantor did not encumber the property during the grantor's ownership period. It means the seller promises: "I didn't mess anything up or add secret debts while *I* lived here, but I have no clue what the guy before me did."

A bargain and sale deed with covenants against grantor's acts is the customary deed used in downstate New York residential real estate sales (like in New York City or Long Island). People downstate usually buy a special insurance to protect against old history, so the seller only promises that things were good during their own time living there.

*Fun Fact:* If you meet someone from outside New York, they might use a different name for this. A bargain and sale deed with covenants against grantor's acts is referred to as a special warranty deed in many states outside of New York.

## The "As-Is" Paperwork: Quitclaim Deeds

> **Quitclaim Deed:** A deed that acts like a giant shrug. It just transfers whatever the person has, even if that turns out to be absolutely nothing.

A quitclaim deed provides the least amount of legal protection to a property grantee. A quitclaim deed contains no warranties regarding the property title, and a quitclaim deed contains no covenants regarding the property title. 

Instead of making promises, a quitclaim deed transfers only the ownership interest that the grantor actually possesses at the exact time of the property transfer. The seller is legally saying, "If I own any piece of this—and I'm not promising that I do—I give it to you."

Why would anyone want this? Because it's super fast for special situations where you don't need a giant legal battle!
1. **Fixing mistakes:** A quitclaim deed is frequently used to clear a cloud on a property title. If there's a confusing rumor (a "cloud") that a distant uncle might own a piece of the house, the uncle can sign this deed to legally say "I don't own it," instantly clearing up the confusion.
2. **Family:** A quitclaim deed is frequently used for real property transfers between family members. If a mom wants to add her son's name to the house, she doesn't need to make giant legal promises to her own son.
3. **Splitting up:** A quitclaim deed is frequently used during divorce settlements to transfer property interests. One person can easily sign over their half of the house to the other person without running a whole new background check on the property.

## Specialized Transfers: When Courts and Estates Get Involved

Sometimes, real estate agents have to help when a bank takes back a house, or when an owner passes away. The rules require special deeds for these moments.

### Referee's Deeds
If someone completely stops paying for their house, the bank might take it back in a process called foreclosure. The court assigns a special helper called a "referee" to auction the house off. 

A referee's deed is utilized to transfer property ownership following a foreclosure sale in New York. Because the referee is just a court helper who never actually lived in the house, a referee's deed contains no covenants regarding the transferred property title. The buyer gets the property as-is.

### Executor's Deeds
When a property owner passes away, the person put in charge of managing their stuff is called an executor. 

An executor's deed is used to transfer real property belonging to the estate of a deceased person. Since the executor never lived there, they can't possibly make promises about the whole history of the house. Because of this, an executor's deed typically contains a covenant against grantor's acts to warrant that the executor did not encumber the property. They just promise that *they* didn't mess up the paperwork or add debts while managing the estate.
