Listen to me, rookie. Slide your drink over and look at this napkin. Imagine this damp piece of paper is a \$2 million parcel in the Hamptons. You think the person holding the deed to this napkin owns every single right to it? You think they’re the undisputed king of their dirt? Wrong! Property rights are like a block of Swiss cheese, completely riddled with invisible holes. As a real estate agent in New York, you aren't just selling bricks and drywall; you are brokering invisible legal forcefields. And the biggest hole you're gonna find lurking on a title report is the easement. Let me blow your mind: an easement is a non-possessory interest in real property. You don't own the dirt, my friend, but an easement grants the right to use the land of another person for a specific purpose. Maybe it's a shared driveway, maybe it's a path to a beach. Because it literally carves away the fee simple owner's absolute freedom, an easement is classified as an encumbrance on real property title. It's a legal backpack of bricks that the property’s title has to carry everywhere it goes.

To understand these forcefields, you have to picture a reality TV neighbor drama. You’ve got two adjacent parcels of land locked in a bitter struggle. We call this setup an easement appurtenant, because an easement appurtenant involves two separately owned adjacent parcels of land. One parcel is the exhausted butler carrying the tray, and the other is the demanding aristocrat. The parcel that gets walked all over is the servient tenement. Literally, the servient tenement is the parcel of land over which an easement runs. Naturally, the servient tenement bears the burden of an easement. Meanwhile, the neighbor sipping espresso while driving across their yard is the dominant tenement. The dominant tenement is the parcel of land that benefits from an easement appurtenant. And here is the kicker that will save your deal at the closing table: because this legal right is baked into the dirt itself, an easement appurtenant runs with the land. When your buyer scoops up that beautiful dominant estate, an easement appurtenant automatically transfers to the new owner when the dominant tenement is sold. You don’t need to negotiate a new driveway deal; the VIP backstage pass just slides seamlessly across the closing table to your client.

But what if there is no snooty neighbor? What if the right belongs to a rogue entity wandering through town? Enter the lone wolf: the easement in gross. An easement in gross benefits a specific individual or business entity rather than a piece of land. Because there’s no adjacent land reaping the rewards, an easement in gross involves only a servient tenement and no dominant tenement. Just one poor property bearing the burden for someone else. There are two flavors of this you will encounter. First, you have the immortal corporate behemoth. A utility company holding the right to run power lines across a residential property holds a commercial easement in gross. And because corporations swallow each other in mergers all the time, a commercial easement in gross is legally transferable to another business entity. ConEd can sell the right to National Grid, and your buyer just has to sit there and watch the bulldozers roll in. On the flip side, what if Old Man Jenkins grants his fishing buddy the right to cross his yard to the lake? That’s personal. Unlike a corporate right, a personal easement in gross typically terminates upon the death of the easement holder. The buddy kicks the bucket, the easement dies with him.

Now, how do these phantom rights spawn into existence? Sometimes it's highly civilized, like a prenuptial agreement for dirt. An easement by grant is expressly created through a written legal document. You write it, you sign it, you record it, boom, done. But sometimes, it's an unspoken accident. Imagine a developer chops a giant lot in half like a pizza, but leaves the sewer pipes connected under both slices. An easement by implication arises from the unexpressed intent of the parties when a single property is subdivided. Even though they forgot to write it down, the infrastructure clearly indicates the original plan.

But what if your client is trapped? Picture a real estate hostage negotiation: a landlocked property situation. Your buyer’s lot is totally surrounded by angry neighbors, practically forcing them to parachute into their own living room. The law abhors useless land, so a landlocked property situation legally justifies the creation of an easement by necessity. An easement by necessity is granted when a property owner has no legally viable access to a public road. You don’t get to just politely ask a neighbor for a path, though. Because you are forcing a landowner to yield against their will, an easement by necessity is created by a court order. A judge literally takes out a judicial crowbar and pries open a driveway. 

If you want to talk about a real heist, though, let’s talk about stealing rights in broad daylight. An easement by prescription is acquired through continuous use of another person's property. It’s a slow-motion robbery. You want to steal the right to cross your neighbor's lawn? The statutory period of continuous use required to establish an easement by prescription in New York State is 10 years. But you can't be sneaky about it like a cat burglar. Creating an easement by prescription requires the property use to be open and notorious—like, "waving to the security cameras" obvious. Furthermore, creating an easement by prescription requires the property use to be hostile and adverse to the true owner's title. You are blatantly disregarding their absolute ownership. And the trap door? Creating an easement by prescription requires the property use to occur without the true owner's permission. The second the owner smiles and says, "Sure, you can use my path," your ten-year heist is ruined. Permission stops the clock instantly.

Oh, and don't forget the ultimate boss: the government. An easement by condemnation is created when the government exercises the power of eminent domain. They widen a highway, they take a slice of your buyer's front yard, they leave a check, and you can't do a thing about it. And if you're out there selling those gorgeous Brooklyn brownstones? You're dealing with structural symbiosis. A party wall easement exists when adjacent property owners share a single dividing structural wall on the property line. Each house leans on the other like two drunks stumbling home; if the wall comes down, both houses fall, so each holds an easement for the support of the other half.

Alright, take a breath. Take a sip. How do we kill these encumbrances? How do we wipe a title clean? The easiest way is a peace treaty: an easement can be terminated by a formal written release from the dominant tenement owner to the servient tenement owner. "I release you from your servitude." Another bulletproof method is becoming your own neighbor. An easement is terminated by merger when a single individual acquires ownership of both the dominant and servient tenements. You can’t have an easement over your own backyard, it just melts into one giant fee simple kingdom! What about our hostage situation? Well, an easement by necessity automatically terminates when the necessity no longer exists. If the county paves a brand new public road right up to that previously landlocked lot, the easement vanishes. Poof! Similarly, an easement created for a specified time frame automatically terminates upon the expiration of that specified period. You bought a five-year easement? On year six, you're just a trespasser. 

Now, pay close attention to this one, because sloppy agents botch this constantly: abandonment. Let’s say the dominant neighbor stops using the path for twenty years. Does the easement die? No! Mere non-use of an easement does not automatically constitute legal abandonment. To wipe it out, terminating an easement by abandonment requires an overt act demonstrating clear intent to permanently relinquish the right. They have to overtly, dramatically show they are done, like building a solid brick wall across their own access door. 

Okay, pivot with me. Easements are about who gets to use the land. But what about a ghost that micromanages what the owner does with their *own* land? Enter the deed restriction. A deed restriction is a private limitation imposed by a grantor on the future use of a property. It's the ultimate control-freak move from beyond the closing table. In the industry, deed restrictions are commonly referred to as restrictive covenants. And just like our appurtenant friend, deed restrictions run with the land. They stick to the title like superglue, meaning deed restrictions legally bind all subsequent owners of the burdened property. You buy the dirt, you buy the rules.

Where do you see these the most? Stepford-wives suburbia. Deed restrictions are frequently used by subdivision developers to maintain uniform neighborhood standards. They don't want your buyer parking a rusted-out RV on the lawn and crashing everyone's property values. Once the developer sells out and leaves, the neighborhood busybodies take over. A homeowners association enforces subdivision deed restrictions through a document called Covenants, Conditions, and Restrictions. We just call them CC&Rs. And if your client paints their mailbox neon green, the HOA isn't just going to write a nasty letter. Property owners can seek a court injunction to legally stop a neighbor from violating a deed restriction. A judge will literally force your client to march down to Home Depot and buy a bucket of beige paint.

It gets incredibly spicy when the rules clash. Let’s say the local municipality zones the block for two-family duplexes, but the private deed restriction says "single-family homes only." Who wins in a bare-knuckle brawl between the mayor and the private deed? Always remember this rule of thumb: when a local zoning ordinance and a deed restriction conflict, the stricter of the two regulations must be followed. The restriction is stricter, so it remains single-family. But what if the HOA gets lazy? What if your client paints that mailbox neon green, and the HOA watches them do it, says nothing for five years, and *then* sues? Your client’s lawyer will unleash a beautiful defense: the legal doctrine of laches can prevent the enforcement of a deed restriction due to an unreasonable delay in asserting the right. You snoozed, you losed, HOA! 

Finally, there is one line a grantor can never cross. The private law of the land bows to civil rights. A deed restriction cannot legally violate local, state, or federal fair housing laws. Back in the dark ages, developers wrote some horrific things into deeds to enforce segregation. Today, a deed restriction prohibiting the sale of a property to specific racial groups is legally void and unenforceable. Even if you're reviewing a deed from 1925 and see that garbage written in black ink, it is a legally dead letter. The Fair Housing Act incinerates it on contact. 

So, rookie. When you're sitting at that closing table next week, you aren't just pushing paper. You are orchestrating the flow of power, predicting where the ghosts live, and protecting your buyer from a lifetime of neighborly warfare. Now finish your drink, we've got contracts to write.
