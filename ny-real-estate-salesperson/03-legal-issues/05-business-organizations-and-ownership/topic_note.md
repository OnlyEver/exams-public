[Ownership](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ownership) in [real estate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_estate) is fundamentally a [legal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law) [technology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Technology). When an individual purchases a rural [single-family home](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Single-family_detached_home), the mechanics of ownership are visible and direct. However, when [capital](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capital_%28economics%29) scales to the level of [Manhattan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manhattan) [high-rises](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-rise_building), [commercial parks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Business_park), or [multi-family](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multi-family_residential) complexes, ownership abstracts into sophisticated legal structures designed to pool [money](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Money), distribute [risk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Risk_management), and optimize [taxation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tax). An aspiring [New York](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_York_City) [real estate professional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_estate_broker) must understand that a building is never just physical [steel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steel) and [glass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glass); it is a manifestation of the [legal entity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legal_entity) that holds its [title](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Title_%28property%29). Understanding these structures dictates how a property is [financed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finance), how catastrophic [liabilities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legal_liability) are absorbed, and exactly what rights your client receives at the [closing table](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closing_%28real_estate%29).

![While ownership of a single-family home is physically direct and visible, large-scale commercial and residential properties require abstract legal structures.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Gingerbread_House_Essex_CT.jpg)

## The Architecture of [Business Entities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Business_entity)

To understand how groups of people own real estate, we must look at the legal containers they use to hold it. Each container handles two fundamental variables differently: **[liability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legal_liability)** (who gets sued if the [roof](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roof) collapses?) and **[taxation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tax)** (how much does the [government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government) take?).

### [Partnerships](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partnership): The Power and the Peril
At its simplest, a **[general partnership](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_partnership)** involves two or more individuals managing a [business](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Business) together. [General partners](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_partner) share equal rights and responsibilities in managing a business. If a roof needs repairing, they decide together. However, this structure carries a massive, inherent risk: general partners have [unlimited personal liability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unlimited_liability) for business [debts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Debt). If a visitor is severely injured on the property and the [damages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Damages) exceed the [insurance policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Insurance_policy), the partners' personal [bank accounts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bank_account), [cars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Car), and homes are at risk.

To solve this liability problem while still allowing people to [invest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Investment), the [law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law) created the **[limited partnership](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limited_partnership)**. A limited partnership consists of at least one general partner and one or more [limited partners](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limited_partner). 
* The general partner runs the day-to-day operations and absorbs the unlimited liability. 
* The limited partners provide capital but do not participate in daily [management](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Management). 

Because they surrender [control](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Control_%28management%29), they gain a legal shield: the liability of a limited partner is restricted to the amount of the limited partner's initial [investment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Investment). If a limited partner invests \$50,000, the absolute maximum they can lose is that \$50,000, regardless of the partnership's debts.

### [Corporations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corporation): The [Artificial Person](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legal_person)
What if [investors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Investor) want no personal liability at all, even for the managers? They create a [corporation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corporation). A **corporation** is an artificial legal entity created under [state law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_law_%28United_States%29). Because the law views the corporation as a distinct, standalone "person," a corporation holds title to real estate in **severalty**—meaning ownership severed from all others, held entirely by the corporate entity alone.

**[Shareholders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shareholder)** own a corporation through the purchase of corporate [stock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stock). Because the corporation is the legal owner of the real estate, the personal [assets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asset) of corporate shareholders are protected from corporate debt liability. 

![Corporate ownership is divided into shares of stock, establishing an artificial legal barrier between the corporation's liabilities and the shareholders' personal assets.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/B%26O_RR_common_stock.jpg)

However, this legal shield comes with a severe financial cost if structured as a traditional **[C-corporation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C_corporation)**. A C-corporation faces [double taxation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double_taxation) on [corporate profits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corporate_tax) and shareholder [dividends](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dividend). The corporation pays [taxes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tax) on its [rental income](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Renting), and when it distributes the remaining [cash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cash) to the shareholders, the shareholders pay taxes on those dividends again. 

To bypass this inefficiency, investors can form an **[S-corporation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S_corporation)**. An S-corporation avoids double taxation by passing [income](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Income) directly to shareholders, taxing the money only once at the individual level.

### The Modern Standard: [Limited Liability Companies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limited_liability_company)
Over time, real estate professionals wanted the best of both worlds without the strict [federal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federal_government_of_the_United_States) limitations placed on S-corporations. The result was the **[Limited Liability Company](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limited_liability_company) (LLC)**. An [LLC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limited_liability_company) combines the liability protection of a corporation with the [pass-through tax](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flow-through_entity) benefits of a partnership. The owners of a Limited Liability Company are referred to as **members**, rather than shareholders or partners. Today, the LLC is the dominant vehicle for holding [commercial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commercial_property) and [investment real estate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_estate_investing).

## Pooling Capital: [Syndicates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syndicate), [Joint Ventures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joint_venture), and [Trusts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trust_%28law%29)

Capital in real estate is rarely supplied by one pocket. It is gathered and aimed at specific targets. 

A **[real estate syndicate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_estate_syndication)** is a group of investors pooling capital to purchase and manage real estate. It is crucial to understand that "syndicate" is a descriptive business term, not a distinct legal entity type. To actually hold title and operate, a real estate syndicate can be structured as a corporation, limited liability company, or limited partnership.

Sometimes, two powerful entities pool their resources not to build an ongoing business, but to execute a single, highly specific project—like erecting a single commercial tower. This is known as a **[joint venture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joint_venture)**, which is a temporary partnership formed for a single [real estate transaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_estate_transaction). Once the project is sold, the venture dissolves.

### Trusts and [REITs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_estate_investment_trust)
Another method of holding property relies on a [fiduciary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiduciary) triangle. A **[trust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trust_%28law%29)** involves a [trustor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trust_%28law%29) transferring property ownership to a [trustee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trustee) for the benefit of a [beneficiary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beneficiary_%28trust%29). The trustee acts as the [legal guardian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legal_guardian) of the asset, managing it strictly for the beneficiary's advantage.

![The fiduciary triangle of a trust: a trustor transfers property to a trustee, who is legally obligated to manage the asset for the beneficiary.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Chart_of_a_trust.jpg)

When you scale the concept of a trust up to a massive, [publicly traded](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_company) [portfolio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portfolio_%28finance%29), you get a **[Real Estate Investment Trust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_estate_investment_trust) (REIT)**. A [REIT](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_estate_investment_trust) pools investor [funds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Investment_fund) to purchase income-producing real estate, allowing a [retail investor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Retail_investor) to own a fraction of a massive [shopping mall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shopping_mall) or [office park](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Business_park). 

To ensure REITs operate as true public investment vehicles and not [tax-evasion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tax_evasion) schemes for a wealthy few, [federal law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federal_law) enforces strict parameters:
1. A Real Estate Investment Trust must have at least 100 shareholders to qualify for special tax treatment.
2. A Real Estate Investment Trust must distribute at least 90 percent of its [taxable income](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taxable_income) to shareholders annually. (Because it passes the money straight through, the REIT itself largely avoids [corporate income tax](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corporate_tax)).

## The [New York](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_York_%28state%29) State of Mind: [Cooperatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Housing_cooperative) vs. [Condominiums](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condominium)

Nowhere in the country is the distinction between property structures as culturally and legally vital as in New York. If you are selling [residential real estate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Residential_area) in [New York City](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_York_City), you are primarily selling cooperatives ([co-ops](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Housing_cooperative)) and condominiums ([condos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condominium)). 

In both structures, the original [developer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_estate_development) is referred to as the **sponsor**. To protect consumers from [fraud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fraud), the state heavily regulates how these properties are brought to market. A cooperative sponsor must file an offering plan with the [New York State Attorney General](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Attorney_General_of_New_York) before selling cooperative shares, and similarly, a condominium sponsor must file an offering plan with the New York State Attorney General before selling units. 

Despite this shared regulatory birth, their legal anatomies are entirely different.

### [Cooperatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Housing_cooperative): The Corporate Building
Look at a classic [pre-war building](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pre-war_architecture) on the [Upper West Side](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Upper_West_Side). It is not [subdivided](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subdivision_%28land%29) legally; the entire cooperative building is legally owned in severalty by a single corporation. 

![Classic pre-war housing cooperatives on Central Park West. Rather than owning their specific apartments, residents own shares in the corporation that holds the deed to the entire building.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Central_Park_West_buildings_over_Lake_crop.jpg)

When your client "buys an [apartment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apartment)" in a co-op, they are not buying physical [walls](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wall) or [floors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Floor). Purchasing a cooperative unit grants the buyer shares of stock in the cooperative corporation. Because stock is movable, paper [wealth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wealth), the interest held by a cooperative owner is classified as **[personal property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Personal_property)** rather than [real property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_property). 

> **Crucial Distinction:** A cooperative owner does not receive a [deed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deed) to the unit. Instead, because they own shares, a cooperative shareholder receives a **[proprietary lease](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lease)** to occupy a specific unit. 

Because the building is legally a [private club](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Private_club) of shareholders, the transfer of cooperative shares typically requires the approval of the cooperative [board of directors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Board_of_directors). This is why co-op board packages are famously rigorous—they are deciding who gets to join their private corporation. 

Financially, the entire building receives a single [property tax](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Property_tax) bill and has a single underlying [mortgage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mortgage_loan). To pay these, a cooperative owner pays a monthly maintenance fee covering the unit's share of building expenses and property taxes. If one neighbor [defaults](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Default_%28finance%29) on their \$3,000 maintenance fee, the rest of the shareholders must absorb the deficit to keep the corporation [solvent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solvency).

### [Condominiums](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condominium): Cubes of Air
If a co-op is a corporate share, a condominium is a [three-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_space) box of real estate suspended in the sky. The **New York Condominium Act** governs the formation and operation of condominiums within the [state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U.S._state), creating the legal framework that allows you to own "[airspace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air_rights)."

![Condominium ownership effectively grants fee simple title to a three-dimensional "cube of air" and the interior space within it, legally distinct from the physical ground below.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Air_rights_318_Third_Ave.jpg)

A condominium owner holds **[fee simple](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fee_simple) title** to the interior space of the individual unit. Because this is true real estate, a condominium owner receives a **[deed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deed)** transferring real property ownership. 

But what about the hallways, the [roof](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roof), and the [gym](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gym)? Condominium owners hold an undivided interest in the building's **common elements** as **[tenants in common](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concurrent_estate)**. Common elements in a condominium include [lobbies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lobby_%28room%29), hallways, [elevators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elevator), and recreational facilities. 

Unlike a co-op, a condo is not a single corporate organism. A condominium owner receives a separate property tax bill for the individual unit. Because there is no private corporate ownership of the entire structure, there is no board of directors with absolute [veto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Veto) power over who buys an apartment. Instead, a condominium association **[board of managers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Board_of_directors)** enforces the [bylaws](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/By-law) and manages the common elements. 

### Summary Comparison: Co-ops vs. Condos

| Feature | Cooperative (Co-op) | Condominium (Condo) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **What You Buy** | [Shares of stock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stock) | Physical interior space |
| **Legal Classification** | [Personal Property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Personal_property) | [Real Property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_property) |
| **Document Received** | [Proprietary Lease](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lease) | [Deed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deed) |
| **Ownership of Unit** | [Corporation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corporation) owns in severalty | Buyer holds [Fee Simple](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fee_simple) title |
| **Common Areas** | Owned by the corporation | Owned as [Tenants in Common](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concurrent_estate) |
| **Property Taxes** | Paid via monthly maintenance fee | Separate [property tax](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Property_tax) bill per unit |
| **Governance** | [Board of Directors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Board_of_directors) | Board of Managers |

Understanding these structures transforms you from a mere [tour guide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tour_guide) of properties into a precise architectural analyst of the deal. Whether you are advising a client to purchase an [LLC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limited_liability_company)-protected [commercial space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commercial_property), warning them about the [personal liability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legal_liability) of a [general partnership](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_partnership), or explaining why they won't get a [deed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deed) at their [co-op](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Housing_cooperative) [closing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closing_%28real_estate%29), you are navigating the invisible [legal frameworks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legal_system) that make New York real estate function.
