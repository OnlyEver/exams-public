When someone buys a piece of land or a house, they aren't just buying dirt and wood. They are buying a set of invisible rules called "rights." These rules decide who gets to use the property, who can sell it, and what happens to the house if the owner passes away. If you want to understand real estate in New York, you need to understand how people share these invisible rules. 

Let's look at how ownership works, starting with playing solo, moving to playing on a team, and learning the rules that keep everything fair.

## The Architecture of Title: Ownership in Severalty

The simplest way to own property is to own it completely by yourself. Think of it like playing a single-player video game where you are the only one holding the controller. We call this an **estate in severalty**. 

The word "severalty" tricks a lot of people. It sounds like "several," which usually means "more than one." But in real estate, it actually comes from the word *sever*, which means to cut off. The term severalty means that the property owner's interest is severed from the interests of all others. You are totally cut off from anyone else having a say!

An estate in severalty occurs when property is owned by a single individual. Since there is only one owner, that person gets to make 100% of the choices. 

Interestingly, an estate in severalty also occurs when property is owned by a single legal entity such as a corporation. The law treats a whole company like it is one single person. So, if a big company buys a giant toy store, that company holds the property in severalty.

## Concurrent Ownership: When Rights Overlap

Once you have more than one person holding the controller, things get a lot more interesting! **Co-ownership** exists when a single real estate property is owned by two or more individuals. 

But it isn't just for human beings. Co-ownership also exists when a single real estate property is owned by two or more legal entities, like if two different companies decided to buy a warehouse together. 

When people share property, we have to ask: *What are the rules of their team?* In New York, there are three main ways to share a property: tenancy in common, joint tenancy, and tenancy by the entirety.

### Tenancy in Common: The Default Standard

Imagine you and your best friend decide to buy a treehouse together. How does the state of New York view your partnership? Well, tenancy in common is the default form of co-ownership for unmarried individuals in New York. Unless your legal paperwork specifically asks for different rules, the state just assumes you are "tenants in common."

The coolest part about being tenants in common is how you share the space. Tenants in common hold an undivided fractional interest in the entire property. What does "undivided" mean? It means each tenant in common possesses the right to occupy the entire property regardless of their fractional ownership share. 

Think of it like a giant swimming pool. You can't draw a line down the middle and say, "I only own the deep end, so you can only swim in the shallow end." You share the *whole* pool! 

Even though you share the whole space, the amount of money you put in can be totally different. Tenants in common can hold unequal ownership shares in a single property. 

Here is how the math for unequal shares works out:
1. Step 1: Let's say a treehouse costs \$100 total.
2. Step 2: You pay \$75. That means you own a 75/100 (which simplifies to a 3/4) ownership share. 
3. Step 3: Your friend pays \$25. Your friend owns a 25/100 (which simplifies to a 1/4) ownership share. 
You both get to play in the whole treehouse, but you technically own a bigger financial slice!

Because you own your specific slice independently, a tenant in common can sell, mortgage, or transfer their specific interest without the consent of the other co-owners. If you want to sell your 3/4 share to a new kid down the street, your friend cannot stop you.

The most important thing to remember about this setup is what happens if someone passes away. Tenancy in common does not include the right of survivorship. That means upon the death of a tenant in common, their ownership interest passes to their heirs according to their will or state law. It does *not* automatically go to the friend they bought the treehouse with.

### Joint Tenancy: The Unities of Survivorship

Joint tenancy is a much stricter, team-focused concurrent form of ownership that includes the right of survivorship. 

The right of survivorship means that a deceased joint tenant's interest automatically passes to the surviving joint tenants. If three friends own a house this way, and one friend passes away, the other two friends instantly absorb that third friend's share. Because this happens like magic the exact second they pass away, a joint tenant's property interest cannot be passed to heirs through a will. The joint tenancy completely ignores the will!

Because this is such a powerful rule, you can't just stumble into it by accident. In New York, a deed must explicitly state the intention to create a joint tenancy with the right of survivorship. You have to spell it out perfectly on the legal paperwork.

To make this team valid, the law requires four things to be perfectly matched, which we call the "four unities" (you can remember them with the word PITT: Possession, Interest, Time, Title). Creating a valid joint tenancy requires the four unities of time, title, interest, and possession. If even one is missing, it breaks!

*   **Unity of possession** requires that all joint tenants have an equal right to possess the entire property. (Just like the swimming pool example!)
*   **Unity of interest** requires that all joint tenants hold equal ownership shares in the property. Nobody can have a bigger slice. 
    Here is the math for equal shares:
    1. Step 1: Count the number of joint tenants. Let's say there are 4 friends.
    2. Step 2: Take the whole property, which is 1, and divide it by the number of friends. 
    3. Step 3: 1 divided by 4 equals 1/4. So, every single friend owns exactly 1/4 of the property.
*   **Unity of time** requires that all joint tenants acquire their ownership interest at the exact same moment. You have to join the team at the same time.
*   **Unity of title** requires that all joint tenants acquire their interest from the exact same deed or legal document. Everyone signs the exact same piece of paper.

### Severing the Unities: Termination of Joint Tenancy

What happens if someone on the team wants to quit? A joint tenant can sell their individual ownership interest in the property without the consent of the other joint tenants. 

But when they sell it to a brand new person, the perfect PITT puzzle breaks! Selling a joint tenant's interest severs the unity of time and title for the new owner. The new buyer didn't sign the paper at the same *time*, and they used a different *title* (deed). 

Because the unities are broken for the new person, the buyer of a joint tenant's interest automatically becomes a tenant in common with the remaining joint tenants. 

However, the team isn't totally destroyed. The remaining original joint tenants maintain their joint tenancy relationship with each other after another co-owner sells their share. So the original friends keep their magic survivorship shield with each other, while the new kid is just a regular tenant in common.

Sometimes, co-owners get into huge fights and refuse to play together anymore, but nobody wants to leave. When this happens, they can go to a judge. A partition suit is a legal action used to force the division or sale of a co-owned property. Any joint tenant or tenant in common can file a partition suit to legally terminate the co-ownership. The judge will just sell the property and hand everyone their share of the money.

### Tenancy by the Entirety: The Marital Estate

Our final type of ownership is a super-special team. Tenancy by the entirety is a specialized form of co-ownership available exclusively to legally married couples. 

The law treats a married couple like they have fused into one single superhero. In New York, property conveyed to a married couple automatically creates a tenancy by the entirety unless the deed specifies otherwise. 

Just like joint tenancy, tenancy by the entirety includes an automatic right of survivorship for the surviving spouse. If one spouse passes away, the other one instantly gets the whole house. 

But because they are legally "one entity," the rules are super strict to protect the family:
*   In a tenancy by the entirety, neither spouse can sell their property share without the explicit consent of the other spouse. You can't just sell half your house to a stranger without asking your husband or wife!
*   Also, in a tenancy by the entirety, neither spouse can partition the property without the explicit consent of the other spouse. (No running to the judge to force a sale on your own).

How do you break this special superhero team? A legal divorce automatically terminates a tenancy by the entirety. Since the marriage is over, the magic team is over, too. Following a divorce, former spouses who owned property as tenants by the entirety automatically become tenants in common. They are now just two regular people with separate shares, and *then* they can ask a judge for a partition suit if they want.

You don't *have* to get divorced to change it, though. A married couple can mutually agree to terminate a tenancy by the entirety through a legally signed agreement. If both spouses agree, they can change the rules whenever they want.

### Summary: Comparing Forms of Co-Ownership

Here is a simple cheat sheet to help you remember the rules for the three types of teams!

| Feature | Tenancy in Common | Joint Tenancy | Tenancy by the Entirety |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Default for:** | Unmarried co-owners | *You must put it on the deed on purpose!* | Married couples |
| **Right of Survivorship?** | No (passes to kids/family in a will) | Yes (ignores the will entirely) | Yes (ignores the will entirely) |
| **Unities Required:** | Possession only | Time, Title, Interest, Possession | Legal Marriage + PITT |
| **Unequal Shares?** | Allowed | Strictly prohibited | Not allowed (You both own the whole thing) |
| **Sell w/o Consent?** | Yes | Yes (but the new buyer becomes a Tenant in Common) | No (Requires explicit permission from your spouse) |
| **Partition Suit?** | Yes | Yes | No (Requires explicit permission from your spouse) |
