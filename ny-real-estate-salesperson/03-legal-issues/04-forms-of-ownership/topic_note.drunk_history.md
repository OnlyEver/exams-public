Listen to me, slide that martini glass out of the way and look at these coasters, because if you're going to survive the New York real estate exam—and more importantly, your first high-stakes closing—you need to understand exactly how people own the ground beneath their feet. It’s not just about handing over a set of keys; it’s about a literal, invisible geometry of rights. Misunderstand this, and you’ll be trying to sell a Brooklyn brownstone for a guy who only legally owns the third floor’s bathroom plumbing.

Let’s start with the absolute simplest scenario. See this single coaster? I’m going to chop the air around it like a ninja. Why? Because the root word here is *sever*. An estate in severalty occurs when property is owned by a single individual. The term severalty means that the property owner's interest is severed from the interests of all others. You are the lone wolf. The solo heist artist. Nobody else has a claim. But get this: an estate in severalty also occurs when property is owned by a single legal entity such as a corporation. The law treats a giant corporate megalith like a single, manufactured Frankenstein’s monster. So if you’re brokering a commercial lease for a corporate-owned warehouse, that’s severalty. It’s one legal person, cleanly severed from the rest of the world.

But New York real estate is rarely that peaceful. Usually, everybody wants a piece of the pie, which brings us to the glorious, dramatic chaos of co-ownership. Co-ownership exists when a single real estate property is owned by two or more individuals. And just like our Frankenstein corporations, co-ownership exists when a single real estate property is owned by two or more legal entities. Basically, you’ve got multiple chefs in the kitchen, and depending on the legal recipe they use, the kitchen might just catch fire.

Let’s say you have two unmarried tech bros who want to buy a luxury duplex in Tribeca. How does the state assume they own it? Tenancy in common is the default form of co-ownership for unmarried individuals in New York. If the deed doesn’t specifically set up some elaborate legal trapdoor, the state automatically stamps them as tenants in common. 

Think of tenancy in common like making a massive pot of roommate chili. Tenants in common hold an undivided fractional interest in the entire property. This means even if Tech Bro A pays for 90% of the duplex and Tech Bro B only pays for 10%, Tech Bro B doesn’t just legally own the broom closet. Each tenant in common possesses the right to occupy the entire property regardless of their fractional ownership share. You can’t put up police tape in the living room; the rights overlap completely. And yes, as I just mentioned, tenants in common can hold unequal ownership shares in a single property. It doesn’t have to be fifty-fifty. 

Now, here is the absolute wildest part of tenancy in common: a tenant in common can sell, mortgage, or transfer their specific interest without the consent of the other co-owners. Tech Bro B could wake up tomorrow, decide he’s moving to Bali, and sell his 10% share to a complete stranger without ever asking Tech Bro A. Suddenly, Tech Bro A is sharing his luxury duplex chili with a guy named Carl who plays the accordion. 

But what happens when the music stops and somebody drops dead? Tenancy in common does not include the right of survivorship. There is no magical transfer of power. Upon the death of a tenant in common, their ownership interest passes to their heirs according to their will or state law. If Tech Bro A kicks the bucket, his 90% doesn’t go to his roommate. It goes to his mother, his sister, or his prized golden retriever if the trust is set up right. 

Now, if you want to bypass the probate court bouncers entirely, you have to form a blood pact. We call this a joint tenancy. Joint tenancy is a concurrent form of ownership that includes the right of survivorship. It’s the ultimate "Last Man Standing" reality show. The right of survivorship means that a deceased joint tenant's interest automatically passes to the surviving joint tenants. It happens instantly. Because this legal magic is so powerful, a joint tenant's property interest cannot be passed to heirs through a will. You can write down that you want your daughter to get your share of the ski lodge, but if you die in a joint tenancy, the surviving owners absorb your share like the Blob. Your will is useless against it. 

Because this overrides standard inheritance laws, New York State does not let you stumble into a joint tenancy by accident. In New York, a deed must explicitly state the intention to create a joint tenancy with the right of survivorship. It has to be spelled out in black and white. 

And to forge this blood pact, you must perfectly align the stars. Creating a valid joint tenancy requires the four unities of time, title, interest, and possession. If you miss even one, the pact fails and you’re just tenants in common. 
First, the unity of possession requires that all joint tenants have an equal right to possess the entire property—just like the chili pot. 
Second, the unity of interest requires that all joint tenants hold equal ownership shares in the property. No 90/10 splits allowed here. If there are four owners, everyone owns exactly 25%. 
Third, the unity of time requires that all joint tenants acquire their ownership interest at the exact same moment. 
And fourth, the unity of title requires that all joint tenants acquire their interest from the exact same deed or legal document. Time, Title, Interest, Possession. Memorize that.

But what if a joint tenant wants to escape the blood pact? A joint tenant can sell their individual ownership interest in the property without the consent of the other joint tenants. But wait! If Owner A sells to a new guy, Buyer B, that new guy didn’t buy in at the same moment or on the original deed. Selling a joint tenant's interest severs the unity of time and title for the new owner. Because those unities are shattered, the buyer of a joint tenant's interest automatically becomes a tenant in common with the remaining joint tenants. But here’s the plot twist: the remaining original joint tenants maintain their joint tenancy relationship with each other after another co-owner sells their share. So the original crew is still playing Last Man Standing, while the new guy is just hanging out with his separate, inheritable slice of the pie.

If the drama gets too hot—whether you’re a joint tenant or a tenant in common—and you want out entirely but the others refuse to sell, you push the big red nuclear button. A partition suit is a legal action used to force the division or sale of a co-owned property. Any joint tenant or tenant in common can file a partition suit to legally terminate the co-ownership. You drag everyone to court, the judge slams the gavel, forces the property to be liquidated, and hands out the cash. Boom. Finished.

Now, take everything we just talked about and put a wedding ring on it. Our final form of ownership is the two-headed marital dragon. Tenancy by the entirety is a specialized form of co-ownership available exclusively to legally married couples. The law looks at a married couple not as two roommates, but as one single, indivisible entity. Because of this, in New York, property conveyed to a married couple automatically creates a tenancy by the entirety unless the deed specifies otherwise. 

Just like the blood pact, tenancy by the entirety includes an automatic right of survivorship for the surviving spouse. If one spouse passes away, the other instantly owns the whole dragon. But here is the massive difference that protects the family home from catastrophic late-night decisions: the vault is locked from the inside. In a tenancy by the entirety, neither spouse can sell their property share without the explicit consent of the other spouse. You can’t secretly sell your half of the marital home to pay off a gambling debt. Furthermore, in a tenancy by the entirety, neither spouse can partition the property without the explicit consent of the other spouse. The nuclear button is disabled. 

So how do you slay the two-headed dragon if things go south? Well, altering the marriage alters the title. A legal divorce automatically terminates a tenancy by the entirety. The moment the gavel falls on the divorce decree, the legal fiction of the single marital entity is destroyed. Following a divorce, former spouses who owned property as tenants by the entirety automatically become tenants in common. They are now just two incredibly bitter roommates who have the power to sell their individual shares or file that nuclear partition suit. 

But it doesn’t always have to end in tears and court dates! Let’s say the couple is just doing some savvy estate planning. A married couple can mutually agree to terminate a tenancy by the entirety through a legally signed agreement, seamlessly converting their ownership to move the asset into a trust. 

So, next time you have a client sitting across your desk trying to list a property, before you even look at the comps, you better look at the deed. Find out if they are a lone wolf, a roommate eating from the chili pot, a survivor in a blood pact, or half of a two-headed dragon. Now, finish that drink, because your real estate exam isn’t going to pass itself.
