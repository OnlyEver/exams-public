When someone buys a piece of land or a house, they usually think they own it completely, just like when you buy a brand-new video game console. You think it’s 100% yours to play whenever you want! But in the real world, ownership is rarely that simple. 

Almost every house or piece of land has invisible strings attached to it. Other people or banks might have special rules, money claims, or rights to that property. These strings can change how much the house is worth or limit what the owner can do with it. Understanding who holds these invisible strings, how they take turns getting paid, and what power they have is the most important part of learning about real estate!

## The Anatomy of an Encumbrance

To make sure a house sale goes smoothly, you first have to understand the big category of rules that can stick to a property.

**An encumbrance is any claim, charge, or liability attached to real property.** Think of an encumbrance as any kind of string attached to the house. 

Encumbrances usually do one of two things:
1. **An encumbrance may restrict the physical use of a piece of real property.** This is a physical rule. For example, even if you own your yard, an encumbrance might say you aren't allowed to build a fence over a buried water pipe. It's like your parents telling you that you own your video game console, but you aren't allowed to put stickers on it.
2. **An encumbrance may lessen the financial value of a piece of real property.** This means because there are strings attached, the property might not be worth as much money.

Here is the really important part: **An encumbrance does not necessarily prevent the transfer of real property title.** This means you can still buy or sell a house even if it has encumbrances! The strings just travel with the house. If you buy the house, you have to follow the rules of the strings you inherited. 

## Liens: The Financial Anchors

While "encumbrance" is the big word for *any* string attached to a property, real estate helpers spend most of their time dealing with one specific kind of string: the lien.

**A lien is a specific type of financial encumbrance.** It is all about money. **A lien provides security for a debt or obligation of the property owner.** Imagine you borrow \$20 from a friend, and to make sure you pay them back, you let them hold onto your favorite skateboard. The skateboard is their security. A lien is just like that, but with a house!

If the owner pays back the money they owe, the lien goes away. But if they don't, there is a big consequence: **a lienholder can force the legal sale of a property to satisfy a debt if the property owner defaults.** That means the person owed money can legally force the owner to sell the house just to get their money back.

### The Genesis of a Lien: Voluntary vs. Involuntary

Liens don't just appear out of nowhere. They happen for specific reasons. We put them into two buckets based on whether the homeowner actually *wanted* the lien or not.

| Origin Type | What Does It Mean? | Real Estate Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Voluntary Lien** | **A voluntary lien is created intentionally by the actions of the property owner.** You asked for it! | **A real estate mortgage is an example of a voluntary lien.** When someone buys a house, they willingly offer the house as a promise to pay back the bank loan. |
| **Involuntary Lien** | **An involuntary lien is created by law without the property owner's consent.** You didn't ask for it, but you broke a rule or owe money, so it gets attached to your house anyway. | Things like unpaid taxes. The owner definitely didn't want this string attached, but the law did it anyway. |

Involuntary liens are broken down into two smaller groups based on who made the rule:
*   **A statutory lien is an involuntary lien created by state or federal legislation.** This means the government wrote a law in a rulebook, and the lien happens automatically if you don't pay your bills (like property taxes).
*   **An equitable lien is an involuntary lien created by a court order based on common law principles.** This happens when a judge looks at a situation, decides what is fair, and orders a lien to make sure someone doesn't get cheated out of their money.

## The Scope of the Net: Specific vs. General Liens

Besides knowing *how* a lien is made, you also need to know *what* it can grab. If someone owes money, can the creditor take their house, their car, AND their savings account? 

### Specific Liens

**A specific lien attaches only to one particular piece of real estate.** It is laser-focused. If you owe money on your bike, they can only take your bike—they can't touch your video games. 

*   **A property tax lien is an example of a specific lien.** If someone doesn't pay the taxes on their apartment, the city only puts a lien on that one apartment. 
*   **A mechanic's lien is an example of a specific lien.**

**What is a Mechanic's Lien?**
Let's say a carpenter builds a beautiful new deck on a house, but the owner refuses to pay them. **A mechanic's lien secures payment for labor or materials used to improve real property.** Because the house owner didn't happily agree to have a lien put on their house, **a mechanic's lien is classified as an involuntary lien.** It exists to protect workers so they don't do a bunch of hard work for free.

In New York, there is a ticking clock for workers to file this paperwork:
*   **In New York, a mechanic's lien on a single-family dwelling must be filed within four months of the completion of work.**
*   **In New York, a mechanic's lien on a commercial property must be filed within eight months of the completion of work.**

### General Liens

On the other hand, **a general lien attaches to all of a debtor's real and personal property.** It casts a giant net over everything the person owns! However, there is a geographic rule: **a general lien affects debtor property located in the county where the lien is officially recorded.**

There are four big general liens you should know about:

1.  **Judgment Liens:** **A judgment lien is a general lien.** It happens when someone gets sued and loses. **A judgment lien is created by a court decree finalizing a lawsuit.** **In New York, a judgment lien against real property remains valid for ten years.**
2.  **Estate Tax Liens:** **An estate tax lien is a general lien.** When a person passes away, **an estate tax lien is placed on a deceased person's property to secure the payment of estate taxes.**
3.  **Corporate Franchise Tax Liens:** **A corporate franchise tax lien is a general lien.** **A corporate franchise tax lien secures the payment of state taxes imposed on corporations** just for the privilege of doing business in the state. 
4.  **Internal Revenue Service (IRS) Tax Liens:** **An Internal Revenue Service tax lien is a general lien.** **An Internal Revenue Service tax lien results from a person's failure to pay federal income taxes.** If you don't pay your taxes to the big federal government, they cast a massive net over everything you own.

## The Hierarchy of Claims: Lien Priority

Imagine a house is sold at an auction because the owner owed a lot of people money. But what if the house doesn't sell for enough money to pay everyone back? Who gets the first slice of the pizza?

**Lien priority generally determines the order in which lienholders are paid if a property is sold.** The basic rule is "first in time, first in line." **Lien priority is typically established by the date and time a lien is recorded in the public records.** Whoever filed their paperwork first gets paid first!

Let's look at a step-by-step math example. Imagine a house is sold at a forced auction for \$400,000. 
The owner owes:
* \$10,000 to the government for property taxes.
* \$300,000 to Bank A for the first mortgage (recorded in 2015).
* \$150,000 to Bank B for a second mortgage (recorded in 2020).

Here is how the math breaks down based on who is in line:

1. **Step 1:** We start with the total money from the sale: \$400,000.
2. **Step 2:** We pay the government first (because of special tax rules). \$400,000 - \$10,000 = \$390,000 left over.
3. **Step 3:** We pay Bank A because they recorded their loan in 2015. \$390,000 - \$300,000 = \$90,000 left over.
4. **Step 4:** We try to pay Bank B. They are owed \$150,000, but there is only \$90,000 left! \$90,000 - \$150,000 = -\$60,000. The money ran out. Bank B simply loses \$60,000. 

As you saw in Step 2, the government always cuts to the front of the line! 
*   **Real estate property tax liens take priority over all other recorded liens.**
*   **Special assessment liens take priority over previously recorded mortgages.** (A special assessment is a charge from the city for things like putting in new sidewalks).

Sometimes, banks will agree to trade places in line. **A subordination agreement changes the legal priority of existing liens.** Think of it like giving your friend permission to cut in front of you in the lunch line. **A subordination agreement allows a later-recorded lien to take precedence over an earlier-recorded lien.**

## The Warning Bell: Lis Pendens

When you are looking at the history of a house, you might see a scary-sounding Latin phrase: *Lis Pendens*. It means "suit pending."

**A lis pendens is a recorded notice of a pending lawsuit affecting the title to a specific real property.** 

It is very important to remember: **a lis pendens is not a lien itself.** It is not a debt, and it doesn't mean someone can force the sale of the house today. Instead, **a lis pendens warns potential real estate buyers of a possible future lien against the property.** 

Think of a lis pendens like a big flashing yellow warning light. It tells anyone who wants to buy the house, "Hey! People are fighting over this house in court right now! If you buy it, you might get dragged into the mess!" Because of this warning, almost nobody will buy a house that has a lis pendens on it until the lawsuit is totally finished.
