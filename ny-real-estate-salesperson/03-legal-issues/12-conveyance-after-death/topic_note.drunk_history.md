Listen to me. Put down the overpriced IPA and look at me, because if you want to survive your first year as a real estate agent in New York, you need to understand the wildest, most absolute magic trick in property law: the exact second a heartbeat stops. 

Rookies think that when a homeowner kicks the bucket, their \$2 million Brooklyn brownstone just floats up into some ghostly legal purgatory, waiting for a gavel to bang. Wrong! Nature abhors a vacuum, and real estate law abhors an unowned building even more. Title to real property passes to the designated heirs or devisees immediately upon the death of the property owner. Boom. Instantaneous. The music stops, and someone is already sitting in the chair. But here is the catch, my friend: just because the universe instantly beamed the title into someone else's lap does not mean you can just slap a "For Sale" sign on the lawn and collect a commission.

If a grieving son walks into your brokerage with his late mother's keys and says, "List it," you stop him right there. You cannot just take his word for it. Every single dead person's property in this state has to get past the ultimate VIP bouncer of the afterlife. In New York State, the Surrogate's Court handles all probate and estate administration proceedings. That court is the central processing hub, the velvet rope. Until the Surrogate's Court gives someone the official wristband, nobody is signing your listing agreement.

Now, how that wristband gets handed out depends entirely on whether the deceased owner left a treasure map or just drove off a cliff. 

Let's talk about the map first. Testate succession occurs when a property owner dies leaving a valid legal will. But the Surrogate's Court doesn't just glance at a cocktail napkin with "I leave everything to my dog" scribbled on it and say "cool." They have to authenticate it. The legal process of proving the validity of a deceased person's will is called probate. It’s the court holding the map up to the blacklight, checking for watermarks, and making sure the person was of sound mind when they drew it.

If you want to pass the exam, you need to speak the language of the map. It's like a heist movie—you need to know the roles. First, you have the mastermind. A devisor is the deceased property owner who transferred real estate through a will. What did they transfer? The lair. A devise is the specific legal term for the transfer of real property made through a will. And the henchman who gets the lair? A devisee is a person who receives real property through a valid will. 

Do not mix up the dirt with the furniture! If the mastermind leaves behind a sweet vintage sports car or a velvet painting of Elvis, that is not a devise. A bequest is the legal term for a gift of personal property made through a will. You devise the brick and mortar; you bequeath the sofa inside it! Remember that or the exam will eat you alive.

So, who is actually running the heist from the ground now that the mastermind is gone? The inside man. An executor is a person explicitly named in a will to administer the deceased person's estate. This is your client. This is the person you are going to be texting at 2 AM about inspection contingencies. But wait—can they actually sell the house? You have to check the paperwork! An executor possesses the legal authority to sell the deceased person's real property if the will expressly grants that power. If the will says "hold onto the house forever," your executor client can't sell it to anyone. But if the power to sell is there, and you finally drag everyone to the closing table, the property is transferred using an executor's deed, which is the specific legal instrument used by an executor to convey real property from a deceased person's estate. It's the ultimate handoff of the briefcase.

But what if there is no map? What if the owner just checked out with zero planning? Pure chaos? No. Welcome to the state's unfeeling, algorithmic matchmaking service. Intestate succession occurs when a property owner dies without a valid will. Without instructions, the state takes the wheel. In intestate succession, real property passes to the deceased person's legal heirs according to state law. You didn't get chosen because you were loved; you got the house because you fell out of the right branch of the family tree! Acquiring real property through intestate succession is legally known as title by descent. 

Who gets what? You don't guess. The New York law of descent and distribution dictates the exact hierarchy of heirs entitled to receive an intestate decedent's property. It is a brutal flowchart. Spouses first, then kids. No kids? Parents. No parents? Siblings, then weird cousins you haven't seen since the Clinton administration. 

Because there’s no will, there’s no executor. So the Surrogate’s Court assigns a substitute teacher. An administrator is a person appointed by a court to manage the estate of a person who died intestate. They don't get to be creative. They can't decide your favorite niece deserves the house. A court-appointed administrator must distribute the deceased person's assets according to New York laws of descent and distribution. They are literally just reading off the state's menu. And when you, the brilliant agent, finally find a buyer for this intestate property, the closing is finalized with an administrator's deed, which is the legal instrument used by a court-appointed administrator to convey real property from an intestate estate.

Now, finish your drink, because here is the darkest, most existential edge-case of all. What if the algorithm runs dry? What if a person dies intestate, and the New York law of descent and distribution searches high and low—no spouse, no kids, no siblings, no cousins, no bloodlines left on planet Earth? The house doesn't just sit there waiting for a squatter. The state steps in and swallows it whole. Escheat is the legal process where real property reverts to the state government. Escheat occurs automatically if a property owner dies intestate and leaves no identifiable legal heirs. Think of it as Albany's giant vacuum cleaner. The state becomes the ultimate, inevitable house-flipper. 

So the next time a client calls you crying about a dead relative's house, you give them your condolences, and then you ask: "Are we doing testate or intestate? Do we have an executor with a devise, or an administrator dealing with title by descent?" You figure that out, you get the court's blessing, and you ride that listing all the way to the bank!
