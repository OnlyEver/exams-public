Imagine playing a multiplayer video game where a player suddenly logs off forever. What happens to their in-game house, gear, and gold? It doesn't just disappear. The game has specific rules for passing those items to other players. 

Real life real estate is exactly like that. When a person passes away, the ownership of their house has to go somewhere right away so the "game" of the real world keeps working smoothly. Knowing exactly how a house gets passed down—either because the owner left a set of written instructions (a will) or because the game's default rules kick in—is super important if you want to understand how buying and selling houses works!

## The Physics of Property After Death

There is a big myth that when someone passes away, their house just floats around in nowhere-land, owned by nobody, until a judge figures things out. That is not true at all! **Title to real property passes to the designated heirs or devisees immediately upon the death of the property owner.** The transfer happens the exact second they pass away.

But even though the *ownership* changes instantly, the new owners can't just sell the house the next day without making it officially recognized. Think of it like getting a high score on an arcade machine. You earned the score instantly, but you still need the arcade manager to verify it and put your initials on the screen. **In New York State, the Surrogate's Court handles all probate and estate administration proceedings.**

This means the Surrogate's Court is the official "manager" that processes everything when someone passes away. Before anyone can put a "For Sale" sign in the yard, this court has to officially say who is allowed to sign the papers. How the court does this depends entirely on whether the person left instructions (testate) or didn't leave instructions (intestate).

## Testate Succession: The Architecture of a Will

When someone plans ahead, they write down exactly what should happen to their stuff. **Testate succession occurs when a property owner dies leaving a valid legal will.** This is like leaving a written strategy guide for your team before you go offline.

But a will is just a piece of paper until a judge checks it out to make sure it's real and follows the rules. **The legal process of proving the validity of a deceased person's will is called probate.** During probate, the Surrogate's Court looks at the will, makes sure there are no fake signatures, and officially gives power to the people named in it.

### The Vocabulary of the Will

Real estate has its own special vocabulary. You wouldn't call a baseball a football, right? So, we have to use the exact right words for what is being given and who is getting it.

*   **Devisor:** A **devisor** is the deceased property owner who transferred real estate through a will. (This is the person giving the house).
*   **Devise:** A **devise** is the specific legal term for the transfer of *real property* made through a will. (This is the act of giving the house itself).
*   **Devisee:** A **devisee** is a person who receives real property through a valid will. (This is the person getting the house).

Notice how all those words are just for *real estate* (like land and houses)? What if the person leaves you their awesome comic book collection, a couch, or a cool bicycle? That is totally different! **A bequest is the legal term for a gift of personal property made through a will.**

> **Exam Tip:** Remember this rule: You get a *devise* if it's a house or land (real property). You get a *bequest* if it's movable stuff like furniture, money, or bikes (personal property).

### The Role of the Executor

When someone writes a will, they don't just say who gets what. They also pick a team captain to make sure their instructions are followed. **An executor is a person explicitly named in a will to administer the deceased person's estate.**

The executor is the main person in charge of handling the details. But can the executor just sell the house whenever they want? Not exactly. **An executor possesses the legal authority to sell the deceased person's real property if the will expressly grants that power.** If the will clearly says, "My executor is allowed to sell my house," then they are good to go once the court gives them the official thumbs up.

When the executor finally signs the house over to a new buyer, they use a special piece of paper to make it official. **An executor's deed is the specific legal instrument used by an executor to convey real property from a deceased person's estate.**

## Intestate Succession: The State's Default Protocol

What happens if a player leaves the game but never wrote a strategy guide? The game uses its default settings. **Intestate succession occurs when a property owner dies without a valid will.**

Since there are no written instructions from the owner, the state has to step in and use a strict set of rules to figure out who gets the house. **In intestate succession, real property passes to the deceased person's legal heirs according to state law.**

Getting a house this way has a special name. **Acquiring real property through intestate succession is legally known as title by descent.** You didn't get the house because you were picked in a document; you got it simply because you are family and "descended" from them—kind of like inheriting your older sibling's chores, but way better!

### The Law of Descent and Distribution

So, how does the state decide exactly who gets what? There is no guessing allowed. **The New York law of descent and distribution dictates the exact hierarchy of heirs entitled to receive an intestate decedent's property.**

It’s basically a giant family tree flowchart. First, the law looks for a husband or wife (a spouse) and kids. 

Let's do some math to see exactly how this works if someone passes away without a will and leaves behind a spouse, some kids, and cash worth \$150,000. New York law says the spouse gets the first \$50,000, and then the spouse and kids split whatever is left right down the middle.

Here is how you figure it out step-by-step:
1.  **Start with the total amount:** \$150,000.
2.  **Give the spouse the first cut:** Take away \$50,000 for the spouse. 
    \$150,000 - \$50,000 = \$100,000 remaining.
3.  **Divide the remaining amount in half:** 1/2 goes to the spouse, and 1/2 goes to the kids. 
    \$100,000 / 2 = \$50,000.
4.  **Calculate the spouse's total:** The first \$50,000 + the half-share of \$50,000 = \$100,000 total for the spouse.
5.  **Calculate the kids' total:** The other half-share = \$50,000 for the kids to share.

If there is no spouse and no kids, the law moves up the family tree to the parents, then sideways to brothers and sisters, and then outwards to cousins.

### The Role of the Administrator

Because there is no will to name a team captain (an executor), the court has to pick someone to do the job. **An administrator is a person appointed by a court to manage the estate of a person who died intestate.**

The administrator's job looks a lot like the executor's job, but their rulebook is different. Instead of following the owner's written wishes (since there aren't any), **a court-appointed administrator must distribute the deceased person's assets according to New York laws of descent and distribution.**

When an administrator sells the house to a new buyer, they use their own special piece of paper. **An administrator's deed is the legal instrument used by a court-appointed administrator to convey real property from an intestate estate.**

## The Void: Escheat

Let's think about a really rare "what if" scenario. What if a person passes away without a will (intestate), and the state's family tree flowchart looks for a spouse, kids, parents, siblings, and cousins, but finds absolutely zero living relatives?

The house can't just sit empty forever with nobody owning it. If nobody owns it, who mows the lawn or pays the taxes? So, the state government takes over. 

**Escheat is the legal process where real property reverts to the state government.** 

> **Escheat occurs automatically if a property owner dies intestate and leaves no identifiable legal heirs.**

Think of "escheat" like the game servers reclaiming an abandoned base. If there is no family left to claim it, the State of New York becomes the new owner so the land doesn't go to waste.

## Summary: A Comparative Breakdown

Let's put this all together! To really understand the rules, you need to be able to quickly tell the difference between having a will (testate) and not having a will (intestate). Think of this chart like your ultimate cheat sheet for comparing the two modes of the game:

| Concept | Testate Succession (With a Will) | Intestate Succession (Without a Will) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Starting Point** | Owner dies leaving a valid legal will. | Owner dies without a valid will. |
| **Court Process** | **Probate** (checking the will). | Administration (following default state law). |
| **How You Get Property**| Real property transfers through a **Devise**. | Real property transfers through **Title by Descent**. |
| **Who Gets It** | **Devisee** (receives the real property). | Legal Heirs (like kids or spouses). |
| **The Team Captain** | **Executor** (explicitly named in the will). | **Administrator** (appointed by the court). |
| **Special Paperwork** | **Executor's Deed**. | **Administrator's Deed**. |
| **Movable Stuff** | Passed on through a **Bequest**. | Given out by NY State Law. |
| **Worst Case Scenario** | Follows whatever the will says. | **Escheat** (goes back to the state if no family is found). |

When someone tells a real estate agent they want to sell a house that belonged to a family member who passed away, the agent's very first question should be: *"Did they leave a will, and did the Surrogate's Court put an executor or administrator in charge yet?"* 

If the court hasn't stepped in, nobody is allowed to play the game yet. Learn these terms, know the rules, and you'll understand exactly how real estate works!
