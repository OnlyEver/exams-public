Grab your drink, look me in the eye, and listen closely, because what they teach you in real estate school is far too polite. A closing table isn’t a peaceful boardroom negotiation; it is a high-stakes, sweat-inducing hostage exchange executed on a rainy tarmac. The property is the asset, the capital is the ransom, and you, the real estate agent, are the extraction coordinator desperately trying to keep the whole thing from blowing up. But before anyone gets to walk away with a set of keys or a briefcase of cash, the entire chaotic operation has to be balanced to the exact penny on a master script. We call this terrifyingly precise autopsy the Closing Disclosure form. This form is the ultimate itemized receipt of our glorious, destructive dinner party, as a Closing Disclosure form itemizes all closing costs and adjustments for the buyer and seller. On this paper, money is either bleeding out of a client or flowing into them to keep them alive. If you see a debit, that is a financial hemorrhage; a debit on a closing statement represents an amount owed by a party. It’s an outflow, a slash to their payout. Conversely, a credit is a lifesaving blood transfusion. A credit on a closing statement represents an amount owed to a party, meaning money is flowing directly into their pocket. 

If you understand the flow of that blood, you can survive the tarmac. Let’s look at the seller first. The seller is the operative desperately trying to flee the country, but they have to pay heavy tolls to escape the asset cleanly. Right out of the gate, they owe the extraction team—that’s us. In a New York real estate transaction, the seller typically pays the real estate broker's commission. That’s a massive debit right off the top. Furthermore, the seller can’t hand over the hostage if it’s still wearing a financial ankle monitor. The seller must pay off any existing mortgages on the property at or before closing. But quietly wiring the payoff money to the bank isn't enough; the seller has to prove to the world that the tracker has been completely destroyed. To do this, the seller pays a recording fee to officially record the satisfaction of the existing mortgage with the county clerk, legally scrubbing the debt from the public record. 

You think the government just lets a seller walk away with a bag of equity? Absolutely not. New York State acts like a bureaucratic mob boss demanding a cut of the action simply for letting the deal happen, which means New York State imposes a real estate transfer tax on the seller. The boss’s math is non-negotiable and brutally specific: the New York State real estate transfer tax rate is exactly two dollars (\$2) for every five hundred dollars (\$500) of the final purchase price. And if this hostage exchange is happening in Brooklyn, Manhattan, or anywhere in the five boroughs, the local precinct shakes the seller down too, because New York City imposes a local real property transfer tax on residential sales within the five boroughs. Sometimes, the asset itself is a trap. If the seller is trying to escape a cooperative apartment, it’s like trying to resign from a paranoid, elite cult. The cult demands a parting gift to fatten its own treasury, meaning cooperative apartments often charge a flip tax upon the sale of the unit. Because the seller is the one desperately trying to break out, a flip tax in a cooperative transaction is most commonly paid by the seller.

Now pivot to the buyer. The buyer is the poor fool willingly storming into the burning building, and they pay through the nose for the privilege of putting on the blindfold. First, they have to sweep the place for traps. The purchaser pays for any optional or required physical home inspections, making sure the floorboards aren't hiding termites, structural collapses, or ancient curses. Since the buyer is likely using the bank’s money to fund this reckless operation, the bank demands absolute proof the asset is actually worth the risk. Therefore, the purchaser typically pays for the property appraisal required by the mortgage lender. 

Once the value is proven, the buyer has to buy supernatural shields. The title of a property is its soul, and titles can be haunted by forged signatures, old contractor liens, or long-lost heirs showing up to claim the living room. To survive this, you buy a magical bulletproof vest: an owner's title insurance policy protects the purchaser against financial loss from defects in the property's title. But remember, the bank is terrified of ghosts too, so a lender's title insurance policy protects the mortgage lender against financial loss from defects in the property's title. Who buys these magical shields? The buyer is the one begging for the loan, so the purchaser pays the premium for the owner's title insurance policy at closing, AND the purchaser pays the premium for the lender's title insurance policy at closing. The buyer is just hemorrhaging debits!

Securing that leverage is a whole other extortion racket. A purchaser obtaining a mortgage must pay loan origination fees to the lender at closing, which is basically tipping the bank for the administrative agony of underwriting their loan. And the bank brings its own legal muscle to the table to draft the paperwork. Does the bank pay its own lawyer? Don't make me laugh. The purchaser typically pays the bank attorney fees in a financed real estate transaction. The buyer literally funds their own interrogator! The state then returns to tax the creation of this new debt, meaning New York imposes a mortgage recording tax on the purchaser when a new mortgage is created. Once they survive all that, the buyer has to carve their victory into public stone. The purchaser pays a recording fee to officially record the new deed with the county clerk, and if they borrowed money, the purchaser pays a recording fee to officially record the new mortgage with the county clerk. Oh, and if the property they just bought is a luxury compound? There's a velvet-rope cover charge. New York imposes a Mansion Tax on residential property sales of one million dollars (\$1,000,000) or more. Who gets slapped with this luxury toll? The purchaser traditionally pays the New York Mansion Tax. 

By the time we reach the end of the closing table, we hit the reality-TV elimination phase of the night: Proration. You can’t just freeze time. Proration is the mathematically brutal allocation of ongoing property expenses between the buyer and the seller at closing, slicing the timeline down to the exact day of transfer. Imagine the seller paid for a whole year of property taxes, but fled the country in June. The seller shouldn't pay for the buyer's future in the house! Thus, property taxes paid in advance by the seller are credited to the seller at closing—a sweet, sweet transfusion of cash back into their pocket to reimburse them for the time they won't be living there. At the exact same moment, those property taxes paid in advance by the seller are debited to the buyer at closing, because the buyer is inheriting that prepaid time and owes the seller for it. What about the literal fuel in the basement? If there is juice left in the tank, heating oil remaining in a property's tank at closing is typically credited to the seller. The buyer essentially buys that leftover oil right there at the table. On the flip side, what about the seller's dirty laundry? Utilities are usually billed in arrears. If the seller has been running the tap for months but the bill hasn't dropped, unpaid water bills are debited to the seller at closing. The seller owes that money, so it’s slashed right out of their payout to protect the buyer from inheriting a soggy tab.

At the end of this beautiful, chaotic disaster, there is exactly one thing both sides do equally. Because you need professional duelists to handle the actual legal blows in New York, both the buyer and the seller typically pay their own respective real estate attorney fees at closing. We orchestrate the heist, but the lawyers draft the truce. Master this ledger, understand who bleeds and who gets transfused, and you will walk out of that exam—and your first real closing—alive. Now finish your drink, we've got a deal to save.
