Buying a house isn't just handing over a giant bag of cash for a set of keys. It’s more like a massive multiplayer video game trade. When two players trade a huge item, there are all sorts of tiny fees, team rules, and extra items that have to be balanced out perfectly. As a real estate helper (agent), you have to know exactly who pays for what, like a scorekeeper tracking points. If you mess up the math, everyone gets mad! 

## The Ledger: Debits, Credits, and the Closing Disclosure

To understand who pays what, we use a special receipt called a **Closing Disclosure**. 

> **Closing Disclosure:** A Closing Disclosure form itemizes all closing costs and adjustments for the buyer and seller. Think of it as a final, super-detailed receipt. 

Think of the Closing Disclosure like the final scoreboard at the end of a long tournament. Every single dollar is tracked as either a debit or a credit. 

*   A **debit** on a closing statement represents an amount owed by a party. Think of this like spending your allowance—it is money going out of your pocket because you owe it.
*   A **credit** on a closing statement represents an amount owed to a party. Think of this like getting your allowance—it is money coming into your pocket!

If you understand how debits and credits work, you can figure out any closing math. Let’s look at what the seller and the buyer have to pay.

## The Seller’s Disentanglement: Exiting the Asset

For the seller, selling a house means packing up and paying to clear their name off the property.

### 1. The Cost of Representation
In a New York real estate transaction, the seller typically pays the real estate broker's commission. This means the person selling the house pays the agents for helping them find a buyer. It is kind of like paying the referee after a big game.

### 2. Clearing the Debt
A seller cannot give the buyer a house that still has an old loan (mortgage) attached to it. The seller must pay off any existing mortgages on the property at or before closing. 

But just paying the bank isn't enough. You have to prove it to the town! So, the seller pays a recording fee to record the satisfaction of the existing mortgage. It's like paying a small fee to have your name officially erased from the "I Owe You" board at school.

### 3. Transfer Taxes and the Flip Tax
New York charges a fee just for moving property to a new person. New York State imposes a real estate transfer tax on the seller. 

The math for this tax is very strict! The New York State real estate transfer tax rate is two dollars (\$2) for every five hundred dollars (\$500) of the final purchase price. 

Let's do the step-by-step math to see how that works:
1. First, take the total price of the house. For this example, let's say the house is a tiny toy house that costs \$1,000.
2. Divide that price by \$500 to see how many "chunks" of \$500 there are. (\$1,000 / 500 = 2 chunks).
3. Multiply that number of chunks by \$2. (2 chunks x \$2 = \$4).
4. The seller owes \$4 in tax!

If the house is in the city, there's an extra rule. New York City imposes a local real property transfer tax on residential sales within the five boroughs. This means sellers in places like Brooklyn or Queens pay both the state and the city.

Finally, some apartments in New York are called "co-ops." A co-op is a building where everyone owns shares in the whole property together, like a giant team. Cooperative apartments often charge a flip tax upon the sale of the unit. This is money that goes into a piggy bank for the building to fix the roof or paint the halls. A flip tax in a cooperative transaction is most commonly paid by the seller.

## The Purchaser’s Investment: Acquiring and Securing

While the seller pays to leave, the buyer (purchaser) pays to enter safely. A buyer's costs are all about checking the house for problems, borrowing money, and protecting their new home.

### 1. Due Diligence: Appraisals and Inspections
Before you buy a used bike, you’d probably check if the brakes work. With a house, the purchaser pays for any optional or required physical home inspections. This is to make sure there are no secret bugs or leaky pipes.

If the buyer is borrowing money from a bank to buy the house, the bank wants to know the house is actually worth the price. The purchaser typically pays for the property appraisal required by the mortgage lender. An appraisal is when an expert guesses the true value of the house. The bank forces the buyer to pay for this!

### 2. The Title Insurance Shield
Sometimes, a house has secret history problems, like someone else claiming they actually own it. We use "title insurance" as a magical shield to protect against these surprises.

There are two types of shields:
*   An owner's title insurance policy protects the purchaser against financial loss from defects in the property's title. 
*   A lender's title insurance policy protects the mortgage lender against financial loss from defects in the property's title.

Who buys the shields? The purchaser pays the premium for the owner's title insurance policy at closing. And, because the bank is bossy, the purchaser pays the premium for the lender's title insurance policy at closing too. The buyer buys both!

### 3. The Price of Leverage: Mortgage Fees
Borrowing money (getting a mortgage) costs extra money. A purchaser obtaining a mortgage must pay loan origination fees to the lender at closing. This is like a setup fee for the bank doing all the paperwork.

The bank also hires its own lawyer to make sure their money is safe. The purchaser typically pays the bank attorney fees in a financed real estate transaction. Yep, the buyer pays for the bank's lawyer!

Also, the state charges a fee for creating a brand-new loan. New York imposes a mortgage recording tax on the purchaser when a new mortgage is created. If you pay in all cash, you get to skip this fee!

### 4. Recording the Future
The buyer has to announce to the town that they are the new owner. The purchaser pays a recording fee to officially record the new deed with the county clerk. Also, if they borrowed money, the purchaser pays a recording fee to officially record the new mortgage with the county clerk. It's like paying to put your name on the high score screen so everyone knows the house is yours.

### 5. The Mansion Tax
New York has a special rule for really expensive homes. New York imposes a Mansion Tax on residential property sales of one million dollars (\$1,000,000) or more. Even though the seller usually pays taxes to transfer the house, the purchaser traditionally pays the New York Mansion Tax. 

## Proration: The Calculus of Time and Consumption

If you trade a giant pizza with a friend, but your friend already ate two slices, you shouldn't pay for the whole pizza! You only pay for what you eat. 

> **Proration:** Proration is the allocation of ongoing property expenses between the buyer and the seller at closing. It just means splitting bills fairly based on exactly what day the buyer moves in.

### Prepaid Expenses (The Seller's Future)
Imagine the seller paid for the whole year of town property taxes in January, but sells the house to the buyer in June. The seller paid for six months that the *buyer* will actually be living there! 

To make it fair, property taxes paid in advance by the seller are credited to the seller at closing. (The seller gets this money back in their pocket like a refund). At the exact same time, those property taxes paid in advance by the seller are debited to the buyer at closing. (The buyer owes that money to the seller to make up for those six months).

Another example is fuel. If the house uses oil for heat, the seller might leave a big tank full of oil they already bought. Heating oil remaining in a property's tank at closing is typically credited to the seller. The buyer basically buys that leftover oil from the seller at closing.

### Arrears (The Seller's Past)
Sometimes the seller hasn't paid a bill yet. If the seller took a lot of long showers before moving out, but the bill hasn't arrived, the new buyer shouldn't have to pay for that water! Therefore, unpaid water bills are debited to the seller at closing. The money is taken out of the seller's pile of cash to make sure their old bill gets paid.

## The Shared Expense: Legal Counsel

In New York, real estate agents don't write the heavy legal contracts. Lawyers do. Because of this, both the buyer and the seller typically pay their own respective real estate attorney fees at closing. The buyer pays the buyer's lawyer, and the seller pays the seller's lawyer. It’s totally fair.

## Conclusion

Figuring out closing costs is just about knowing the story behind the money. The seller pays to cleanly leave the game—paying their agents, taxes, and old loans. The buyer pays to start the game safely—checking the house, buying insurance, and setting up their new loan. By splitting the everyday bills right down the middle, nobody feels cheated. If you can remember these rules, you'll be a great real estate helper in New York!
