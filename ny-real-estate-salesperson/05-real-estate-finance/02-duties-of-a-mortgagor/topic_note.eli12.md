Imagine buying an incredibly expensive video game console, but you only have a little bit of your own allowance money. A friend lends you the rest. You get to keep the console in your room and play it, but your friend has some strict rules you have to follow until you pay them back. 

In New York real estate, when someone buys a house—like a cool Brooklyn apartment or a big house in Syracuse—they get the keys and own it. But the bank that lent them the money is like a powerful, silent partner. The rules the buyer must follow are called the duties of the mortgagor. Understanding these rules means learning how a promise to pay someone back turns into a real claim on a house. 

If you want to help people buy homes one day, you need to understand these rules perfectly so you can explain the paperwork they sign when they finally buy the house!

## The Anatomy of the Transaction: Who Holds What?

First, let's figure out who is who. The words might look a little funny, but they are easy once you see how they work.

*   The **mortgagor is the borrower in a real estate finance transaction**. (That is the person buying the house).
*   The **mortgagee is the lender in a real estate finance transaction**. (That is usually the bank).

Why is the borrower called the mortgag*or*? Because they are the one *giving* a promise. The **mortgagor pledges real estate as collateral for a mortgage loan**. "Collateral" is just a backup prize. It means, "If I don't pay you back, you can take my house." 

In return, the bank needs proof of this promise. The **mortgagee receives the mortgage instrument as security for the real estate debt**. Think of the "mortgage instrument" as a super-important contract.

In New York, the rules say that the **mortgagor retains legal ownership rights to the real estate while the mortgage remains active**. The bank does not own the house; the borrower does! You get to paint your room and live there. However, the **mortgage instrument creates a lien on the real estate to enforce the mortgagor's contractual duties**. A "lien" is like an invisible sticky note attached to the house that says, "You owe the bank money, and if you break the rules, the bank gets the house."

| Actor | Role | Action Provided | What They Hold |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Mortgagor** | Borrower | Pledges property as collateral | Legal title and possession of the property |
| **Mortgagee** | Lender | Provides the money | The mortgage instrument and a lien on the property |

## The Financial Engine: PITI and Beyond

The **specific legal duties of a mortgagor are explicitly detailed in the mortgage instrument**. These contracts are super long, like the terms and conditions you skip when downloading an app. But the **primary financial duties of a mortgagor are often summarized by the acronym PITI**. 

An acronym is a word made from the first letters of other words. The **acronym PITI stands for Principal, Interest, Taxes, and Insurance**. Let's break down these money rules and see what else the bank requires to keep their loan safe.

### 1. Repayment of the Secured Debt (Principal and Interest)
A **primary legal obligation of a mortgagor is to repay the debt secured by the mortgage**. Basically, you have to pay the money back! **Secured debt repayment typically involves making scheduled payments of principal and interest to the lender**. 

Think of it like this: The "principal" is the actual money you borrowed. The "interest" is the extra fee the bank charges for letting you use their money. Every month, you make a payment that covers a little bit of both.

### 2. Paying Taxes and Special Assessments
A **mortgagor has a legal duty to pay all property taxes levied against the mortgaged real estate**. Towns charge taxes to pay for schools and parks. Also, a **mortgagor has a legal duty to pay all special assessments levied against the mortgaged real estate**. A special assessment is an extra charge, like if the town builds a brand new sidewalk right in front of your house and asks you to pitch in.

Why does the bank care if you pay your town taxes? Because of who gets to be first in line. **Unpaid property taxes can result in a municipal tax lien on the real estate**. This is another invisible sticky note, but from the town. The crazy part is that a **municipal tax lien takes legal priority over a lender's mortgage lien**. 

This means the town cuts in front of the bank! If a homeowner owes \$18,000 in taxes and doesn't pay, the town can take the house. If that happens, the bank loses all the money they lent. The bank's invisible sticky note gets ripped up.

### 3. Maintaining Property Insurance
A **mortgagor must maintain adequate property insurance on the mortgaged real estate**. 

Imagine dropping your brand-new phone and shattering the screen. If you didn't buy phone insurance, you're out of luck. A house is the collateral (the backup prize) for the bank. If a fire burns the house down, the house is gone, and the bank has no backup prize left. **Property insurance protects the lender's financial interest against physical damage to the collateral**. If a disaster strikes, the insurance company pays to fix the house, which keeps the bank's money safe.

### The Escrow Solution
Because towns can take the house for unpaid taxes, and fires can destroy the house, banks don't like to just cross their fingers and hope you pay these bills. So, **lenders frequently require property taxes and insurance premiums to be paid into a dedicated escrow account**. 

An escrow account is like a piggy bank that the lender holds for you. Instead of trusting you to save up all year, the bank adds a little bit to your monthly bill to cover these costs. Let's look at how the math for this works:

1. **Find the total yearly cost.** Let's say your property taxes are \$2,400 a year, and your house insurance is \$1,200 a year.
2. **Add those two amounts together.** \$2,400 + \$1,200 = \$3,600 total for the year.
3. **Find the monthly cost.** Divide the yearly total by 12 months. \$3,600 / 12 = \$300 a month.
4. **Pay into the piggy bank.** The bank adds this \$300 to your normal monthly loan payment. The bank puts that \$300 into the escrow piggy bank every month and pays the town and the insurance company for you when the big bills are due!

## Preserving the Collateral: Maintenance, Alterations, and Waste

A home loan can last 15 to 30 years. That is longer than you've been alive! During that whole time, the bank needs the house to stay in great shape so it stays valuable.

### The Duty of Repair
A **mortgagor has a legal duty to keep the mortgaged property in good physical repair**. If a window breaks or the roof leaks, you have to fix it. Why? Because **proper property maintenance ensures the collateral retains sufficient value to cover the outstanding loan balance**. 

Imagine borrowing money to buy a bicycle, and you promise to give the bike to your friend if you can't pay them back. But then you let the bike rust in the rain and the tires pop. If you hand over a broken bike, your friend won't be able to sell it to get their money back! A house works the same way.

### The Prohibition of Waste
In real estate law, wrecking a house has a special name. 

> **Waste is a legal term for any destructive action or severe neglect that significantly diminishes a property's value.** 

A **mortgagor must avoid committing waste on the mortgaged real estate**. This means you can't do things that ruin the house on purpose (like smashing holes in the walls out of anger) or by being super lazy (like letting the bathtub overflow for days and rotting the floors). 

### Major Structural Alterations
Since you own the house, you might think you can knock down any wall you want. But wait! A **mortgagor must obtain the mortgagee's permission before making major structural alterations to the mortgaged property**. 

If you try to take out a wall to make your living room bigger, but it turns out that wall was holding up the ceiling, the roof might cave in! Since the bank is relying on the house as their backup prize, they want to make sure your big construction plans won't accidentally destroy it.

## The Consequences of Breach: Default and Foreclosure

The rules in the mortgage contract aren't just polite suggestions. They are strict promises. 

**Failing to fulfill any duty specified in the mortgage document constitutes a legal default**. Most people think a "default" only happens if you stop paying your monthly bill. But if you pay perfectly on time, and then secretly smash all your windows (committing waste) or build a huge crazy tower without permission (making major alterations), you are breaking the rules. You are in default! 

A **mortgagor default gives the lender the legal right to initiate foreclosure proceedings**. What does that big word mean? **Foreclosure allows the lender to pursue the sale of the mortgagor's real estate to satisfy the unpaid debt**. Because the mortgage contract put that invisible sticky note (the lien) on the house, the bank can step in, force the house to be sold, take back the money they lent you, and kick you out.

When you grow up and help people buy houses, you will be guiding them through a huge deal. Knowing these rules means you can tell your clients exactly how to take care of their new home and keep the bank happy for years to come!
