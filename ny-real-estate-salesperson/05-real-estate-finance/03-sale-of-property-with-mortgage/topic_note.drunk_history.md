Slide your glass over. You want to pass the New York real estate exam? You want to actually close deals in this town? Listen to me. You have to understand that when you sell a house, you aren't just handing over a nice set of brass keys and popping champagne. You are orchestrating a high-stakes hostage negotiation. 

Every property you will ever list is handcuffed to a wildly possessive, deeply paranoid financial entity called the Bank. The property is the hostage, the title is the physical body of the house, and the mortgage is the steel chain bolting them together. To get your buyer into that house, you have to deal with the chain. 

The cleanest, most beautiful way out of this hostage situation is what we call a sale free and clear. This is the Hollywood ending. In a sale free and clear, the seller pays off the existing mortgage balance prior to or right at the closing table. Usually, you take the buyer's shiny new cash, wire it directly to the old bank, and sever the chain. Why is this the dream? Because a sale free and clear transfers the property title to the buyer completely without the encumbrance of the seller's original mortgage! The buyer walks into a totally unburdened home. 

But you can't just throw money at the bank and whisper "we're good." The law demands a receipt, a magical piece of paper that proves the beast is slain. A Satisfaction of Mortgage document is recorded to officially prove a mortgage has been paid in full. Until that document is stamped by the county, the ghost of that debt still haunts the house. Recording a Satisfaction of Mortgage clears the specific mortgage lien from the property's public record, wiping the slate completely clean for your new buyer.

Now, what if the seller doesn't have the cash to kill the beast, and they just want to run? Enter the chaos of a sale subject to the mortgage. Picture this: your buyer gets the keys, they move their velvet sofa into the living room, but the seller's original mortgage remains attached to the real estate! The buyer receives title to the property, they physically own it, but the seller's heavy, rusted debt chain is still bolted to the front door. 

Here is the absolute wildest part of real estate physics: a buyer purchasing a property subject to the mortgage is not personally legally responsible for repaying the seller's loan debt. The buyer never signed a contract with that original bank! They just quietly slide checks under the door every month to keep the bank from throwing a tantrum. But make no mistake, the bank still holds the detonator. If a buyer takes a property subject to the mortgage and the payments cease, the lender can still foreclose on the property. The buyer will lose the house! But that's all they lose. 

The original seller, on the other hand, is sweating bullets in a dark room. Why? Because in a sale subject to the mortgage, the original borrower remains personally liable for the mortgage debt. Let's say the buyer stops paying, the bank forecloses, auctions the house, and the auction comes up short of what the bank is owed. The bank is going to go to court for a deficiency judgment to wring the remaining cash out of somebody's pockets. Because the buyer never signed the loan, a deficiency judgment resulting from foreclosure on a property sold subject to the mortgage applies only to the original seller! The buyer walks away whistling, and the seller's financial life goes up in flames.

If you're representing a seller, you'd be a lunatic to let them do that. You'd say, "Make the buyer take the heat!" That brings us to the assumption of a mortgage. This is a totally different reality TV drama. In an assumption of a mortgage, the buyer signs an ironclad agreement to become personally liable for the repayment of the seller's existing mortgage debt. They are officially stepping into the seller's financial shoes and looking the bank right in the eye. Because the buyer has accepted this burden, when a buyer assumes a mortgage, the lender can pursue the buyer for a deficiency judgment in the event of a foreclosure. 

Does this mean your seller can finally sleep at night? Absolutely not! Unless a specific legal blessing is granted, a seller remains secondarily liable for the mortgage debt even after a buyer assumes the mortgage. The seller is basically acting as an involuntary backup dancer for the buyer's financial performance. If the buyer defaults and skips town, the bank turns the spotlight right back onto the seller. 

To actually, truly cut the cord, the seller has to beg the bank for a novation. A novation is a legal agreement that completely releases the original borrower from mortgage liability. It is the ultimate brain-wipe. A novation replaces the original borrower with the new buyer on the mortgage contract, meaning the bank formally adopts the buyer and forgets the seller ever existed. 

Now, you might be asking, "Wait, with interest rates soaring, why wouldn't my buyer just assume the seller's ancient, insanely cheap mortgage or buy the house subject to it?" Because the bank isn't stupid! They have a tripwire buried in the paperwork. It is an alienation clause in a mortgage contract, and it allows the lender to demand the entire loan balance be paid immediately upon the sale of the property. The exact second ownership changes hands, the tripwire snaps, and the bank screams, "Give me my \$500,000 right now!" Because it detonates upon the transfer of the title, an alienation clause is also widely known as a due-on-sale clause. This due-on-sale clause prevents a buyer from assuming an existing mortgage without the lender's explicit permission. The bank controls the board.

Let's pivot to the arena where all this drama is actually documented: the public record. In New York, secret debts are useless debts. Mortgages in New York are recorded in the county clerk's office in the county where the real estate is physically located. If the house is in Brooklyn, you march to the Kings County Clerk. 

Why do we do this? Because recording a mortgage provides constructive notice to the public of the lender's lien against the specific real estate. Think of constructive notice as blasting a megaphone in Times Square. Constructive notice means the law presumes the public has knowledge of a recorded document regardless of actual knowledge. If a buyer purchases a house and later cries, "I didn't know there was a lien!" the judge will laugh them out of the room. The law says, "It was in the county records. You should have known." 

And let me tell you about the absolute track meet that happens at that clerk's office. New York uses a race-notice recording statute for real estate documents. It is exactly what it sounds like: a literal sprint to the finish line, with the added caveat that you can't have prior notice of someone else's claim. Under a race-notice statute, priority among multiple mortgage liens is generally determined by the date and time of recording. If Bank A gives a loan at breakfast, and Bank B gives a loan at lunch, but Bank B's lawyer runs a four-minute mile and files the paperwork first without knowing about Bank A, Bank B wins the priority! The golden, unbreakable general rule for mortgage lien priority is first in time equals first in right. 

What if a lender gets lazy and just shoves the paperwork in a desk drawer? An unrecorded mortgage is still valid between the borrower and lender. The borrower signed a promise; they still owe the money to that specific lender. However, it's a disaster for the bank because an unrecorded mortgage lacks priority against subsequent third-party claims or liens filed against the property. If the bank doesn't announce their hostage to the world, someone else can swoop in and claim the house first. Recording a mortgage protects the lender's security interest against subsequent purchasers or competing creditors. It is their armor. 

So next time you're sitting at that closing table, remember: you aren't just selling drywall and granite countertops. You are managing a treacherous, invisible web of debt, liability, and county clerk footraces. Now drink your ginger ale, we've got a showing in twenty minutes.
