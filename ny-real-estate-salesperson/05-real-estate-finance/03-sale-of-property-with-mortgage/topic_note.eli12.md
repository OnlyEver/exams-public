Selling a house is a bit like selling a used video game console, but with a lot more rules. When someone sells a house, they aren't just handing over the front door keys. Usually, the person selling the house borrowed a lot of money from a bank to buy it in the first place. That loan is called a mortgage. 

When it is time to sell the house, that big loan doesn't just magically disappear. The buyer, the seller, and the bank have to figure out what happens to the debt. Let's look at how the ownership of the house (called the "title") moves from one person to another, and what happens to the bank loan along the way!

## The Physics of Debt: Untangling Title from the Mortgage

Imagine a mortgage loan is like a heavy, locked steel chain tied around a bicycle. The bicycle is the property's title (the actual ownership of the house). You can sell the bicycle to a friend, but unless someone has the key to unlock the chain, the heavy lock stays on the bike. 

When a house with a mortgage is sold, the people involved usually handle that "chain" in one of three ways. The way they choose changes who has to pay the money, who gets in trouble if the payments stop, and who has to pay the bank if the house is taken away but still doesn't cover the whole loan.

### 1. Sale Free and Clear (Breaking the Chain)

Most of the time, people sell houses as a **sale free and clear**. This is the easiest and safest way to do it. 

In a sale free and clear, the seller pays off the existing mortgage balance prior to or at the closing table (the final meeting where the house is sold). Usually, the person handling the money takes the cash the buyer brought and uses it to pay the seller's bank first. Let's look at the math step-by-step:

1. The buyer brings \$100,000 to buy the house.
2. The seller still owes the bank \$40,000 for their mortgage.
3. The person handling the sale subtracts the loan from the purchase price: \$100,000 - \$40,000 = \$60,000.
4. The bank gets the \$40,000 to pay off the debt, and the seller keeps the remaining \$60,000.

Because the loan is paid off, a sale free and clear transfers the property title to the buyer without the encumbrance of the seller's original mortgage. The chain is completely removed!

But the law needs proof. To officially prove a mortgage has been paid in full, a special paper called a **Satisfaction of Mortgage** document is recorded with the local government. 

> **Satisfaction of Mortgage:** A receipt from the bank saying, "Thank you, the loan is 100% paid off, and we are taking off our lock."

Recording a Satisfaction of Mortgage clears the specific mortgage lien from the property's public record. Until that paper is filed, the public records still show a lock on the house, so this step is super important!

### 2. Sale Subject to the Mortgage (Ignoring the Chain)

What if they decide to just leave the lock on the bike? In a **sale subject to the mortgage**, the buyer receives title to the property while the seller's existing mortgage remains attached to the real estate. 

This sounds tricky, so let's break it down. If a buyer takes a property subject to the mortgage, the buyer is not personally legally responsible for repaying the seller's loan debt. It is like letting your friend play on your Xbox Live account; they might pay the monthly fee to keep it going, but the account is still in your name. 

However, because the bank's lock is still on the house, the bank still has power. If a buyer takes a property subject to the mortgage and the payments cease, the lender can still foreclose on the property. "Foreclose" means the bank takes the house away to sell it. The buyer loses the house, but they don't owe the bank any extra money.

The original seller is the one taking a huge risk. In a sale subject to the mortgage, the original borrower remains personally liable for the mortgage debt. If the new buyer stops making payments, the bank will take the house and sell it. If the house sells for less money than what is owed, the bank will ask a judge for a **deficiency judgment**—an order to pay the leftover missing money. Because the new buyer never signed the loan papers, a deficiency judgment resulting from foreclosure on a property sold subject to the mortgage applies only to the original seller. 

### 3. Assumption of the Mortgage (Passing the Chain)

Since leaving the lock on is scary for the seller, there is another option called an **assumption of a mortgage**. This is when the buyer agrees to take the heavy chain and lock onto their own shoulders. 

In an assumption of a mortgage, the buyer signs an agreement to become personally liable for the repayment of the seller's existing mortgage debt. They step into the seller's shoes. Because the buyer has agreed to this, when a buyer assumes a mortgage, the lender can pursue the buyer for a deficiency judgment in the event of a foreclosure. 

But is the original seller totally safe now? No! Unless a novation is granted, a seller remains secondarily liable for the mortgage debt even after a buyer assumes the mortgage. "Secondarily liable" means the seller is the backup. If the buyer runs out of money and can't pay the bank, the bank can turn around and demand the money from the original seller.

#### Novation: The Ultimate Release
If the seller wants to be 100% free and safe, they need something called a **novation**. 

> **Novation:** A fresh start. It is a legal agreement that completely releases the original borrower from mortgage liability. 

A novation replaces the original borrower with the new buyer on the mortgage contract. The bank checks to make sure the new buyer is good with money, and then completely cuts all ties to the original seller.

## The Lender's Veto: The Alienation Clause

You might be wondering: *If an older mortgage has super cheap monthly payments, why don't buyers just take over those old loans all the time?* 

The answer is a strict rule written into almost every loan contract. It is called the **alienation clause**. 

An alienation clause in a mortgage contract allows the lender to demand the entire loan balance be paid immediately upon the sale of the property. Because it happens the exact moment the house is sold, an alienation clause is also widely known as a **due-on-sale clause**.

Banks don't want to give their cheap loan rates to a total stranger. A due-on-sale clause prevents a buyer from assuming an existing mortgage without the lender's explicit permission. If a seller tries to sneakily sell the house and just pass the loan along, the bank will find out, point to this rule, and say, "Pay us everything you owe right now!" That is why most houses today must be sold "free and clear."

## The Public Ledger: The Mechanics of Recording

Imagine a giant bulletin board in your town where everyone posts important announcements. Real estate works the same way. When you buy a house, you need to know for sure that no hidden banks are going to pop out and say you owe them money. To keep things fair and visible, we use a recording system.

Mortgages in New York are recorded in the county clerk's office in the county where the real estate is physically located. If your house is in Queens County, the mortgage paper is pinned to the metaphorical bulletin board in the Queens County office. 

### The Power of Constructive Notice
Pinning that paper to the board does something very special. Recording a mortgage provides **constructive notice** to the public of the lender's lien against the specific real estate. 

Constructive notice means the law presumes the public has knowledge of a recorded document regardless of actual knowledge. If the bank puts the paper on the bulletin board, and a buyer buys the house without bothering to go look at the board, the buyer can't cry, "I didn't know!" The law says, "It was on the board. You should have checked." 

### New York's Race-Notice Statute
If two different banks are fighting over who gets paid first when a house is sold, New York has a specific way to decide the winner. New York uses a **race-notice** recording statute for real estate documents.

Under a race-notice statute, priority among multiple mortgage liens is generally determined by the date and time of recording. It is exactly what it sounds like: a literal race to the county office! If Bank A gives a loan on Monday, and Bank B gives a loan on Tuesday, but Bank B runs super fast to the office and puts their paper on the board first (without knowing about Bank A), Bank B wins. 

> **The Golden Rule:** The general rule for mortgage lien priority is first in time equals first in right. 

### The Fate of the Unrecorded Mortgage
What if a bank gives out a loan but forgets to run to the office and record it? Do they lose their money?

Not exactly. An unrecorded mortgage is valid between the borrower and lender. If you borrow money from the bank, you still have to pay it back because you signed a promise. However, an unrecorded mortgage lacks priority against subsequent third-party claims or liens filed against the property. 

That means if you borrow money from Bank A (who forgets to record it), and then borrow money from Bank B (who runs and records it), Bank B gets to go to the front of the line to get paid. So, recording a mortgage protects the lender's security interest against subsequent purchasers or competing creditors. It keeps the bank's spot safe!

## Summary Reference: Mortgage Transfer Liability

Here is a simple cheat sheet to help you remember who owes what when a house gets a new owner! 

| Transfer Type | Who Gets the House Title? | Who Owes the Loan Money? | Who Pays if the Bank Sues for Leftover Money (Deficiency)? | Does the Loan Stay on the House? |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Free and Clear** | Buyer | Nobody! (The debt is totally paid off) | Nobody | No (A Satisfaction of Mortgage is recorded) |
| **Subject To** | Buyer | Seller only | Seller only | Yes |
| **Assumption** | Buyer | Buyer (Main) & Seller (Backup) | Buyer (and Seller if Buyer fails) | Yes |
| **Assumption with Novation** | Buyer | Buyer only | Buyer only | Yes |
