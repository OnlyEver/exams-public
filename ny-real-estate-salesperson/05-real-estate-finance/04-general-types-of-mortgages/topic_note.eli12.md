When you want to buy an expensive video game console but only have a little bit of allowance saved up, what do you do? You might ask a friend or a parent to lend you the rest of the money. Buying a house works exactly the same way, just with much bigger numbers! The money you have saved up to pay on the very first day is called a "down payment." A bank will lend you the rest of the money, but they want to make sure they are safe in case you stop paying them back. To keep things safe, the world of borrowing money (which we call getting a "mortgage") is split into two paths: normal loans with strict rules, and special loans where the government helps you out. If you understand how these loans work, you can be an amazing helper when people are trying to buy their dream homes!

## Assessing Risk: The Loan-to-Value (LTV) Ratio

Before we talk about the different kinds of loans, we need to know how banks measure danger. Think of a house like a whole pizza pie. **Lenders use the Loan-to-Value (LTV) ratio to assess the financial risk of a mortgage loan.** The LTV is simply a way to compare the slices you didn't pay for yet (the loan) against the whole pizza (the value of the house). 

> **The LTV Baseline**
> Here is how you figure it out:
> 1. Imagine a house costs \$100,000 in total.
> 2. You give the bank a 20 percent down payment on the first day, which is \$20,000.
> 3. Subtract what you paid from the total price: \$100,000 - \$20,000 = \$80,000. This is the amount you still need to borrow.
> 4. Divide the loan amount (\$80,000) by the total house value (\$100,000). 
> 5. The math looks like this: 80,000 / 100,000 = 80/100, or 80 percent. 
> 
> Because of this math, **a conventional loan with exactly a 20 percent down payment represents an 80 percent Loan-to-Value (LTV) ratio.**

Why is this 80 percent number a big deal? Because if you have to give the house back to the bank, the bank knows they can sell it, even at a discount, and still not lose any of their own money. If your loan slices take up more than 80 percent of the pizza, the bank gets nervous!

## The Conventional Mortgage

Think of a conventional mortgage like riding a bike without training wheels. **A conventional mortgage is a home loan that is not insured or guaranteed by any government agency.** Because there is no government safety net catching them if you fall, the banks have to be super careful. That is why **conventional mortgages typically require higher credit scores and larger down payments compared to government-backed loans.** They want to make sure you are a really good bike rider before they let you borrow the money!

There are also rule-makers for these loans, sort of like referees in a sports game. Two famous groups named **Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac set the maximum loan limits for conforming conventional mortgages.** If your loan follows their rules and fits under their size limit, it "conforms." But what if someone wants to buy a giant, super expensive mansion? The loan would be too big for the normal rules. **Non-conforming conventional mortgages exceed the maximum loan limits established by Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac.** People sometimes call these massive loans "jumbo loans."

### The Cost of Low Equity: Private Mortgage Insurance (PMI)

What if someone is great at saving their allowance, but they still don't have enough for that big 20 percent down payment? The bank will still help them, but they make the buyer pay for a special shield. **Conventional mortgages require Private Mortgage Insurance (PMI) if the borrower's down payment is less than 20 percent of the purchase price.** 

It is super important to know what this shield actually does. Does it protect you, the buyer? Nope! **Private Mortgage Insurance (PMI) protects the mortgage lender against financial loss in the event of borrower default.** ("Default" is just a fancy word for when someone stops paying their loan). You pay for the shield, but it protects the bank. 

The good news is you don't have to pay for this shield forever! As you pay off your loan over time, the bank's danger shrinks. 
1. Let's say you bought a \$100,000 house.
2. Every month, you pay the bank a little bit of the money you owe them.
3. Eventually, the amount you owe drops down to \$78,000 (which is 78 percent of the starting price).
4. When that happens, a law called **the Homeowners Protection Act requires lenders to automatically cancel Private Mortgage Insurance (PMI) once a conventional loan balance drops to 78 percent of the original property value.** The shield goes away, and your monthly bill gets cheaper!

## Government-Backed Mortgages: Shifting the Risk

When the rules for a normal loan are just too tough for an everyday person, the government steps in to act like training wheels! **Government-backed mortgages include loans insured or guaranteed by the Federal Housing Administration, the Department of Veterans Affairs, the Rural Housing Service, and the State of New York Mortgage Agency.**

Because the government promises to step in if things go wrong, the banks feel totally safe. Because they feel safe, **government-backed loans generally allow for higher Loan-to-Value (LTV) ratios than standard conventional loans without mortgage insurance.** That means you can borrow a bigger chunk of the pizza!

However, there is one super strict rule. The government only wants to help you buy a house if you are actually going to live in it every day. **FHA, VA, and RHS mortgage programs only approve loans for primary residences.** That means if someone wants to buy a beach house for the summer, or a house just to rent out to other people to make money, they are out of luck. **Investment properties and vacation homes do not qualify for FHA, VA, or RHS government-backed loans.**

### The Federal Housing Administration (FHA)

A lot of people think the FHA is a building full of cash that they hand out to buyers. That's a myth! **The Federal Housing Administration (FHA) does not directly lend money to homebuyers.** Instead, it acts like a giant insurance company. **The Federal Housing Administration (FHA) provides mortgage insurance to approved private lenders to protect against borrower default.** 

If you are helping a normal family buy a house, you will probably see this specific program: **FHA Section 203(b) is the most widely used Federal Housing Administration mortgage program for purchasing one-to-four-family homes.** 

Why do so many people use it? Because it is incredibly easy to start! **FHA-insured loans allow a minimum down payment of 3.5 percent of the property purchase price.** 

But remember, a tiny down payment makes the bank nervous. To keep the FHA safety net strong, the FHA charges you fees.
First: **FHA-insured loans require the borrower to pay an upfront Mortgage Insurance Premium (MIP) at closing.** (Closing is the day you officially buy the house).
Second: **FHA-insured loans require the borrower to pay an annual Mortgage Insurance Premium (MIP) that is divided into monthly installments.** (You pay a little bit of this fee every single month with your normal bill).

### The Department of Veterans Affairs (VA) Loans

The VA loan is like a golden ticket, and it is a special reward for people who served in the military. **VA-guaranteed loans are available exclusively to eligible military veterans, active-duty service members, and certain surviving spouses.**

Notice the word difference here: the FHA *insures* loans, but the VA *guarantees* them. **The Department of Veterans Affairs (VA) guarantees a portion of the loan amount to protect private lenders against borrower default.** Because the VA makes such a huge, strong promise to the bank, the veterans get amazing perks!
* **VA-guaranteed loans allow eligible homebuyers to purchase a primary residence with zero down payment.** (They don't need any cash to start!)
* **VA-guaranteed loans do not require Private Mortgage Insurance (PMI) or monthly Mortgage Insurance Premiums (MIP).** (No annoying monthly shield fees!)

To get this awesome deal, you need to show the bank two special pieces of paper. First, to prove you actually earned the reward, **VA-guaranteed loans require a Certificate of Eligibility (COE) to verify the borrower's military entitlement.** Second, the VA wants to make sure you aren't paying too much for a junky house. So, **VA-guaranteed loans require a Certificate of Reasonable Value (CRV) to document the appraised value of the property.**

There is just one small catch. To keep the whole program running for future veterans, **VA-guaranteed loans require the borrower to pay a one-time funding fee based on the loan amount and military category.** But don't worry if the veteran doesn't have the cash for that fee in their pocket! **Borrowers can finance the VA funding fee into the total loan amount instead of paying the fee out-of-pocket at closing.** (They just add the fee to the giant loan pizza).

### The Rural Housing Service (RHS) Loans

What if you want to buy a house out in the country, where there are more cows than people? There is a program for that! **The Rural Housing Service (RHS) operates under the United States Department of Agriculture (USDA) to promote homeownership in rural areas.**

Just like the VA loan, this program is super generous. **Rural Housing Service (RHS) guaranteed loans offer 100 percent financing with zero down payment for eligible rural properties.** 

But this program is meant to help regular families afford a home, not rich people looking for giant farm estates. **Rural Housing Service (RHS) loans restrict eligibility to borrowers with low-to-moderate household incomes.** 

## The Empire State Special: SONYMA

If you live in New York, you have a secret power-up! **The State of New York Mortgage Agency (SONYMA) offers mortgage financing programs specifically for New York homebuyers.**

Where does a state get so much money to help people buy houses? They ask investors for it! **The State of New York Mortgage Agency (SONYMA) issues tax-exempt mortgage revenue bonds to fund SONYMA loan programs.** Because the investors don't have to pay taxes on these bonds, they charge the state less money, which means the loan becomes way cheaper for the person buying the house!

Who gets this cool New York loan? **The State of New York Mortgage Agency (SONYMA) primarily targets first-time homebuyers with low-interest-rate mortgages.** Also, since first-time buyers usually haven't had years to save up a giant piggy bank of cash, **the State of New York Mortgage Agency (SONYMA) offers down payment assistance programs to help cover upfront costs for eligible applicants.** It’s like getting a little extra allowance to help you cross the finish line.

### SONYMA Eligibility and Target Areas

To make sure this special money only goes to people who truly need it, SONYMA has some strict rules, kind of like a parent putting rules on what you can buy with your allowance.
* You can't be super rich. **The State of New York Mortgage Agency (SONYMA) establishes maximum household income limits for loan applicants.**
* The house can't be a fancy castle. **The State of New York Mortgage Agency (SONYMA) establishes maximum purchase price limits for eligible properties.**
* Just like the federal rules we talked about earlier, **the State of New York Mortgage Agency (SONYMA) requires the purchased property to be used as the borrower's primary residence.** (You have to actually live there).

But wait, there is a cool cheat code! What if you owned a house a long time ago, so you aren't technically a "first-time" buyer? You might still get to use SONYMA! **The State of New York Mortgage Agency (SONYMA) waives the first-time homebuyer requirement for properties located in designated target areas.** 

What is a target area? **Target areas are regions designated by the State of New York Mortgage Agency (SONYMA) as economically stressed.** This just means the neighborhood really needs nice people to move in and fix it up, so the government bends the rules to invite more buyers to that specific spot!
