Imagine four different people staring at a cool apartment building in New York: someone who figures out town taxes, someone who handles fire insurance, a rich person looking to buy it, and an official property checker. If you ask them, "How much is this building worth?" they will give you four totally different answers! The crazy part? Every single one of them is 100% correct.

How is that possible? It is like asking, "What is the value of a bicycle?" A kid who uses it to deliver newspapers values it completely differently than a collector who wants it to sit in a museum. A piece of property does not just have one magically floating price tag. Value changes based entirely on *why* you are measuring it and *how* you do it. Learning the differences between an appraisal, a valuation, and an evaluation is super important. It is the basic language used to help people figure out what to charge for a house, how to talk to clients, and how to follow the strict rules of buying and selling!

## The Hierarchy of Measurement: Valuation, Evaluation, and Appraisal

To get started, let's clean up our words. People often use "appraisal" and "valuation" like they mean the exact same thing. But in the rules of real estate, they are totally different!

Think of **valuation** as the big umbrella word. Valuation is the broad process of determining the financial worth of a property. If you are trying to stick a price tag on a building, you are doing valuation. Under this big umbrella, you will find a few different tools. The valuation process includes formal appraisals, comparative market analyses, and broker price opinions. 

However, not all valuation tools are the same.

### The Formal Appraisal
Let's say a bank is going to let someone borrow \$1,500,000 to buy an awesome house. The bank does not want a wild guess. They need the absolute facts. 

An **appraisal** is an objective, formal estimate of a property's value as of a specific given date. It is like taking a photograph of exactly what a house is worth on that exact day. 

In our state, formal appraisals in New York must be prepared by a state-licensed or state-certified real estate appraiser. A regular real estate agent cannot just type up a piece of paper and call it an appraisal. Furthermore, real estate appraisers must adhere to the Uniform Standards of Professional Appraisal Practice. This is the ultimate rulebook for how to play the game fairly, and the Uniform Standards of Professional Appraisal Practice is commonly abbreviated as **USPAP** (pronounced *yoo-spap*).

### The Evaluation
What if someone does not need to know the exact dollar amount a piece of empty dirt is worth? What if they just want to know if they are legally allowed to build an arcade there? That is when we switch from valuation to evaluation.

An **evaluation** is a study of the nature, quality, or utility of a property interest. Unlike an appraisal, an evaluation does not necessarily yield a specific monetary estimate of property value. It might not give you a dollar sign at all! Instead, evaluations focus on property utility and marketability rather than providing a strict numerical valuation. It tells you *how* you can use the property, not exactly *how much* it will sell for. 

Feasibility studies and highest-and-best-use analyses are common types of property evaluations. A feasibility study asks, "Will this project work?" A highest-and-best-use analysis asks, "What is the absolute best and most profitable thing we can legally do with this space?"

## The Licensee’s Toolkit: CMAs and BPOs

As a real estate salesperson in New York, you are not an appraiser. But you still help figure out what things are worth every day!

When a family asks you, "How much should we ask for when we sell our house?", you use a **comparative market analysis**. A comparative market analysis is a valuation tool used by real estate licensees to help sellers determine listing prices. It is like looking at how much your friends sold their old video games for, so you can figure out how much you should sell yours for!

New York real estate salespersons are legally permitted to perform comparative market analyses for clients. But there is a huge rule here! A comparative market analysis is not legally considered a formal appraisal. Because of this rule, New York real estate salespersons are legally prohibited from referring to a comparative market analysis as an appraisal. Calling it one is breaking the law because you would be pretending to have a special license you do not have.

You might also see something called a **broker price opinion**. A broker price opinion is a brief estimate of property value frequently requested by lenders for distressed properties. If a bank needs a really fast, cheap guess on what a house is worth—maybe because the owner stopped paying their loan—they ask a broker for a BPO instead of a full, expensive appraisal.

## The Great Illusion: Cost vs. Price vs. Value

One of the hardest things to explain to people is the difference between cost, price, and value. Most folks think these words mean the same thing. They definitely do not!

**Cost** represents the total past dollar expenditure required to construct a property improvement. Let's say someone spent \$20,000 putting a giant gold fountain in their front yard. That is their cost. 

But the monetary cost to construct a property does not necessarily equal the property's market value. A new buyer might think a giant gold fountain is tacky! Let's do some step-by-step math to see how cost and value can be completely different:

Step 1: Start with the original value of a normal house, which is \$200,000.
Step 2: Add the cost of a strange, custom improvement, like a \$20,000 gold fountain. So, \$200,000 + \$20,000 = \$220,000.
Step 3: A buyer actually lowers their offer because they have to pay \$5,000 to destroy and remove the ugly fountain! So the new market value is \$200,000 - \$5,000 = \$195,000.

Even though the owner's cost was really high, the actual value went down!

**Market price** is the actual amount of money a buyer pays and a seller accepts for a specific property. It is the number printed on the final receipt at the end. 

Wait, isn't that the same as market value? Nope! Market price can differ significantly from market value due to specific transaction circumstances like a forced sale. If a family has to move across the country tomorrow, they might accept \$300,000 just to be done fast. That is the *price*. But it does not mean the true *value* of the house is only \$300,000.

**Market value**, on the other hand, is a "perfect world" number. Market value is the most probable price a property will bring in a competitive and open market. For a sale to represent true market value, we have to assume a few things:
1. Market value assumes an arm's-length transaction between an unrelated buyer and seller. This means you are not giving your best friend a special discount.
2. Market value assumes neither the buyer nor the seller is acting under undue pressure. Nobody is rushing or being forced to sell.
3. Market value assumes both the buyer and seller are fully informed about the property's condition and uses. There are no secrets!

Price is what happens in the messy real world. Market value is what *should* happen if everything is perfectly fair and balanced.

## The Prisms of Worth: Specific Types of Value

Because value changes based on who is asking, real estate has a bunch of different ways to define it.

### The Investor’s Lens: Investment Value
While market value looks at a normal, everyday buyer, **investment value** is the specific worth of a property to a particular investor based on individual investment goals. 

Why would two investors value the same building differently? Because investment value calculations incorporate an individual investor's specific tax situation and required rate of return. Let's look at a step-by-step math example to see how an investor calculates their investment value based on the return they want:

Step 1: Figure out how much money the property makes in a year (let's say \$10,000).
Step 2: Figure out the investor's required rate of return (let's say they want a 10% return, which is 10/100 or 0.10).
Step 3: Divide the yearly income by the rate of return to find the investment value. \$10,000 / 0.10 = \$100,000.

If a different investor required a different rate of return, their investment value would completely change!

### The Insurer’s Lens: Insurable Value
If a house burns down, the insurance company does not care about what someone would pay to live there. They just want to know what it costs to rebuild the walls and roof. **Insurable value** is the monetary cost to replace the destructible parts of a property in the event of a total loss. 

You cannot burn dirt! Dirt is forever. Because of this, insurable value explicitly excludes the value of the underlying land.

### The Municipality’s Lens: Assessed Value
The local government has its own special number just to figure out how much you owe in taxes. **Assessed value** is the dollar amount assigned to a property by a municipality for the purpose of calculating property taxes. Think of it as your property's "tax score."

### The Crisis Lens: Liquidation Value
When you are in a huge rush, value drops. **Liquidation value** is the likely price a property will bring during a forced or rapid sale. It is like selling your favorite video game to a used game store because you need cash today—you get way less than it is really worth. When a bank takes back a house because the owner didn't pay, they want to sell it instantly. So, foreclosure sales are typical real estate scenarios that rely on liquidation value.

### The Current Operator's Lens: Value in Use vs. Going-Concern
Sometimes a building is super specific to one person. **Value in use** is the value of a property based strictly on its current specific use by the current owner. Let's say a family has a custom-built indoor skatepark. It has high value for them because they love skating! Value in use ignores any potential alternative highest and best use for a property. It does not matter if tearing down the skatepark to build a mall would make more money; we are only looking at what it is worth *right now, as a skatepark*.

Finally, sometimes a building is attached to a business that makes money. **Going-concern value** is the total financial value of an established, operating business. Going-concern value includes the business's real estate, tangible assets (like pizza ovens and chairs), and intangible assets like brand goodwill (the fact that everyone in town loves the pizza and knows its name). You aren't just selling the bricks; you are selling the popularity!

***

By knowing the difference between what a client *wants* and what the market actually *says*, you are doing a great job helping them. You now know exactly how to explain to someone why the money they spent (*cost*) does not match the final price tag (*market value*), why an *evaluation* is great for figuring out how to use land, and why your comparative market analysis is a super helpful tool, but totally different from a bank's official *appraisal*!
