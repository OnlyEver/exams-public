Slide that pint glass over, my friend, because you're looking at me like the New York State Department of State just asked you to value the moon. You're stressing over the state exam, specifically the appraisal section. Look, value isn't a magical aura hovering over a Bronx duplex. It is a cold, hard, psychological hallucination that we nail to the wall using three specific hammers. The three primary approaches to real estate valuation are the sales comparison approach, the cost approach, and the income approach. That's it. The holy trinity of pricing dirt and the bricks stacked on top of it.

Let's start with the one you'll be swimming in every single day: the sales comparison approach. The sales comparison approach estimates a property's value by comparing the subject property to recently sold similar properties. It’s exactly like the high school dating scene—you only know how out-of-your-league someone is by looking at who they just dated. These recently sold similar properties used in the sales comparison approach are known as comparables, or comps if you want to sound like you actually belong in the brokerage. Because we’re using actual, bleeding-edge neighborhood gossip—who bought what for how much—the sales comparison approach is also referred to as the market data approach. It is the undisputed king, the most reliable valuation method for single-family residential properties. And think about it, what about a patch of dirt? Because dirt doesn't pay rent or have a roof to price out, the sales comparison approach is the most reliable valuation method for vacant land, too.

Why does this work? Because buyers aren't idiots. The sales comparison approach relies heavily on the economic principle of substitution. The principle of substitution states that a buyer will not pay more for a property than the cost of acquiring an equally desirable alternative property. If I can buy a renovated brownstone for \$2,000,000, I am absolutely not paying you \$3,000,000 for the identical brownstone next door! The market creates a ceiling. 

Now, here is where the math gets delightfully unhinged. No two houses are identical twins. So, in the sales comparison approach, the appraiser makes mathematical adjustments to the sale prices of the comparable properties. Listen to me very closely, look me in the eyes before you take a sip: in the sales comparison approach, the appraiser never adjusts the value of the subject property! The subject property is a pristine, untouchable diva. You never change her! You only force the comparables to get plastic surgery on paper so they look exactly like her. If a comparable property has a superior feature compared to the subject property, the appraiser subtracts value from the comparable property's sale price. The comp was too pretty—it had a finished basement—so we chop its value down to simulate what it would have sold for without it. Conversely, if a comparable property lacks a feature present in the subject property, the appraiser adds value to the comparable property's sale price. The comp was inferior and had no garage, so we pump its price up with fake money to match our subject's garage. We are always manipulating the comps to match the diva!

But what happens when the neighborhood gossip runs dry? What if there are no comps? Imagine you get a listing for a cathedral in Manhattan, or a local high school, or a state-of-the-art medical center. Nobody is flipping hospitals on Zillow! For these weirdos, we use the second hammer. The cost approach estimates a property's value by calculating the cost to construct the improvements, subtracting depreciation, and adding the land value. Because you are literally playing Lego with construction costs, the cost approach is the most effective valuation method for special-purpose properties. Those special-purpose properties valued using the cost approach include churches, schools, and hospitals! It’s also brilliant for brand-new builds. Because there's no wear and tear yet, the cost approach is highly reliable for valuing newly constructed buildings.

To build this Frankenstein monster, you have to decide how you're building it. If you want to be a history nerd, you use reproduction cost. Reproduction cost is the estimated cost to construct an exact duplicate of the subject property using the exact same materials. Hand-carved 1800s mahogany gargoyles? You're hiring an artisan to carve new mahogany gargoyles. It’s expensive and insane. Most of the time, we use replacement cost. Replacement cost is the estimated cost to construct a building with the same utility as the subject property using modern materials. You just buy a fiberglass gargoyle from Home Depot—it holds the roof up just the same and serves the exact same architectural purpose. 

Once you build the fantasy structure, you have to place it on the earth. In the cost approach, land is valued separately using the sales comparison approach. And here is a rule you must tattoo on your brain: for real estate appraisal purposes, land is never depreciated. Buildings crumble, pipes rust, roofs leak. But dirt is eternal! Land simply exists, completely immune to the ravages of time.

Speaking of crumbling, let’s talk about the grim reaper of real estate: depreciation. Depreciation in an appraisal is defined as any condition that adversely affects the value of an improvement. It comes in three flavors of destruction. First, physical deterioration is a loss in property value caused by physical wear and tear or deferred maintenance. The roof is caving in, the paint is peeling—it’s the property physically dying of old age. 

Second, we have functional obsolescence. Functional obsolescence is a loss in property value caused by outdated design or inadequate internal facilities. Imagine a gorgeous, pristine five-bedroom mansion that only has one bathroom located in the basement. It’s in perfect physical shape, but its internal guts are hopelessly, functionally stupid for modern living. It's like wearing a velvet tracksuit to a marathon. 

Finally, there's the worst one: external obsolescence. External obsolescence is a loss in property value caused by negative factors outside the property boundaries. Because it’s about the neighborhood economy tanking or a toxic dump opening next door, external obsolescence is also known as economic obsolescence. And here's the tragedy: external obsolescence is generally considered incurable because the property owner cannot control the external factors causing the value loss. You can fix a roof, you can build a bathroom, but you cannot personally assassinate the city planner who decided to run a six-lane highway ten feet from your client's bedroom window!

Now, let’s leave the drama of the suburbs and head downtown to Wall Street. Enter the third hammer. The income approach estimates a property's value based on the property's ability to generate future income. These buyers don’t care about hand-carved gargoyles or matching paint; they are financial vampires who only drink cash flow. Thus, the income approach is the primary valuation method for commercial and investment properties. Because it transforms a stream of rent checks into a giant pile of upfront capital, the income approach is also referred to as the capitalization approach. 

It operates entirely on calculated greed, or, as economists politely call it, anticipation. The income approach is based on the economic principle of anticipation. The principle of anticipation states that value is created by the expectation of future financial benefits derived from a property. You aren't buying bricks; you are buying the right to harass tenants for checks in the year 2030.

But to figure out the value, we need the golden number. Net operating income is a required metric to calculate value using the income approach. You find this magical number by taking all the money you collected and paying the absolute bare essentials to keep the lights on. Net operating income is calculated by subtracting total operating expenses from the effective gross income of a property. But watch out, because the state exam is going to try to trick you like a shady accountant! Mortgage payments are not considered an operating expense when calculating net operating income. Your choice to take out a massive loan from Chase Bank is your personal debt problem, not the building's operating expense! Same goes for the IRS. Income taxes are not considered an operating expense when calculating net operating income. Your personal tax bracket is your business; the building's net operating income ignores your debt and your taxes entirely!

Once you have the pure net operating income, you introduce the investor's greed level. The capitalization rate represents the rate of return an investor expects to earn on an income-producing property. This gives us the ultimate formula: in the income approach, property value is calculated by dividing the net operating income by the capitalization rate. But here is the mind-bending reality of finance that you must understand to close deals: there is an inverse relationship between a property's capitalization rate and the property's estimated value. If investors are terrified of a neighborhood, they demand a higher capitalization rate to compensate for the risk, which drives the value of the property straight down into the gutter! High cap rate means low value. Inverse!

Now, what if you're not dealing with a 50-story commercial skyscraper? What if you're just selling a little duplex in Queens and doing a full capitalization analysis with operating expenses is like hunting a mosquito with a bazooka? We use a quick-and-dirty street shortcut. The gross rent multiplier is a simplified valuation method used for one-to-four unit residential rental properties. It completely ignores expenses and looks purely at top-line revenue. The gross rent multiplier is calculated by dividing a property's sale price by the property's gross monthly rent. Once you figure out the neighborhood's multiplier by looking at comps, it's easy. A property's value can be estimated by multiplying the market gross rent multiplier by the subject property's expected gross monthly rent. If the market multiplier is 100, and your duplex pulls in \$3,000 a month, boom, it's a \$300,000 property.

So now you’ve run all the math. You have a value from the comps, a value from the bricks, and a value from the rent checks. Are we done? No! We face the judge. The reconciliation process is the final step in the appraisal process where the appraiser weighs the findings from the three approaches to determine a final value. Notice I said weighs. I didn't say blends them in a smoothie! Listen to me, if you take away one thing tonight: in the reconciliation process, the appraiser does not simply average the values derived from the three approaches! Averaging is a mathematical crime committed by lazy people. Instead, an appraiser gives the most weight to the valuation approach that is most appropriate for the specific type of property being appraised. If it's a suburban house, the sales comparison approach wins. If it's a brand-new hospital, the cost approach wins. If it's a 50-unit apartment building, the income approach wins. You look at the evidence, pick the sharpest weapon, and you plant your flag. Now finish your drink, because you're going to ace this exam tomorrow.
