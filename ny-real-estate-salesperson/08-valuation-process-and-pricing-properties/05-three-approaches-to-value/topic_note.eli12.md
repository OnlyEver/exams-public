Have you ever wondered how people figure out the price of a house, an empty field, or a giant hospital? A building doesn't have a price tag naturally stuck to it. Figuring out what a piece of real estate is worth is a lot like figuring out the value of a rare trading card. You have to look at what other people are willing to pay, how much it costs to make, or how much allowance money it could earn you in the future. 

To solve this puzzle, real estate experts use three main tools. **The three primary approaches to real estate valuation are the sales comparison approach, the cost approach, and the income approach.** By learning these three methods, you won't have to guess what a property is worth—you can figure it out using simple math and logic!

## The Sales Comparison Approach

Imagine you want to sell your bicycle. How do you know what to charge? You would probably go online and see what other people recently charged for the exact same bike. Real estate works the exact same way. **The sales comparison approach estimates a property's value by comparing the subject property to recently sold similar properties.**

These recently sold similar properties used in the sales comparison approach are known as **comparables** (or "comps" for short). Because you are looking at real data from the actual market, **the sales comparison approach is also referred to as the market data approach.**

This method is the best way to price normal houses. **The sales comparison approach is the most reliable valuation method for single-family residential properties.** Also, because empty dirt doesn't cost anything to "build" and doesn't pay you rent, **the sales comparison approach is the most reliable valuation method for vacant land.**

### The Underlying Physics: The Principle of Substitution

Why do we look at comparables? Because humans like getting a good deal! **The sales comparison approach relies heavily on the economic principle of substitution.** 

> **The Principle of Substitution**
> **The principle of substitution states that a buyer will not pay more for a property than the cost of acquiring an equally desirable alternative property.**

Think of it like buying a slice of pizza. If two places next door to each other sell the exact same slice, you are not going to pay \$5 for one if you can substitute it with the \$3 slice. Buyers will always choose the cheaper, equal option. 

### The Mathematics of Adjustment

Here's the tricky part: no two houses are exactly the same. One might have a pool, and the other might not. Because of this, **in the sales comparison approach, the appraiser makes mathematical adjustments to the sale prices of the comparable properties.**

Imagine your property is a statue standing perfectly still on a pedestal. You never, ever touch it. **In the sales comparison approach, the appraiser never adjusts the value of the subject property.** You only ever add or subtract money from the *comparable* property to make it match yours on paper. 

*   **If a comparable property has a superior feature compared to the subject property, the appraiser subtracts value from the comparable property's sale price.** (The comp was "too good," so we take money away to see what it would be worth without that extra feature).
*   **If a comparable property lacks a feature present in the subject property, the appraiser adds value to the comparable property's sale price.** (The comp was missing something, so we pretend to add it by boosting its price).

**Step-by-Step Math Example:**
Let's pretend your house (the subject) does NOT have a pool. You find a comparable house that recently sold, but it DOES have a pool. Let's say a pool is worth \$10,000. 
1. Note the comparable property's sale price. Let's say it sold for \$250,000.
2. Notice the comparable has a superior feature (a pool) that your house lacks. 
3. Because the comparable is superior, subtract the pool's value from its price: \$250,000 - \$10,000 = \$240,000. 
Your house is worth \$240,000!

## The Cost Approach

Sometimes, you can't compare a building to anything else. Imagine trying to find the value of a high school or an old church. Those don't get sold very often, so there are no comparables! Instead, we build the value from scratch, like a giant Lego set.

**The cost approach estimates a property's value by calculating the cost to construct the improvements, subtracting depreciation, and adding the land value.** 

Because it focuses on how buildings are built, **the cost approach is the most effective valuation method for special-purpose properties.** What does that mean? **Special-purpose properties valued using the cost approach include churches, schools, and hospitals.** Also, because brand-new buildings haven't had time to get old and broken, **the cost approach is highly reliable for valuing newly constructed buildings.**

### Reproduction vs. Replacement

First, we figure out how much it costs to build the structure today. There are two ways to think about this:

1.  **Reproduction cost is the estimated cost to construct an exact duplicate of the subject property using the exact same materials.** (Like rebuilding an old 1800s mansion using expensive, hand-carved wood from the 1800s).
2.  **Replacement cost is the estimated cost to construct a building with the same utility as the subject property using modern materials.** (Like building a mansion that does the exact same job, but using normal wood from today's hardware store).

### Land Valuation and the Law of Dirt

After you price out the building, you have to add the value of the dirt underneath it. **In the cost approach, land is valued separately using the sales comparison approach.** 

Here is a super important rule: **For real estate appraisal purposes, land is never depreciated.** Buildings get old, paint peels, and roofs leak. But dirt is just dirt! It stays useful forever. 

### The Anatomy of Depreciation

Once you know the cost to build, you have to subtract value because the building is older and used. **Depreciation in an appraisal is defined as any condition that adversely affects the value of an improvement.** 

Things lose value in three specific ways:
1.  **Physical deterioration is a loss in property value caused by physical wear and tear or deferred maintenance.** This is just normal aging—like a cracked window or a leaky roof.
2.  **Functional obsolescence is a loss in property value caused by outdated design or inadequate internal facilities.** The house might not be broken, but it's weird or outdated. Imagine a huge 5-bedroom house with only one bathroom. That's bad design!
3.  **External obsolescence is a loss in property value caused by negative factors outside the property boundaries.** This is when something awful happens nearby, like someone building a smelly garbage dump next to your house. Because it is tied to the neighborhood or economy, **external obsolescence is also known as economic obsolescence.** 

Here is the really bad news about that last one: **External obsolescence is generally considered incurable because the property owner cannot control the external factors causing the value loss.** You can fix a broken window, but you can't force the city to move a garbage dump!

**Step-by-Step Math Example:**
1. Calculate the cost to construct the building brand new. Let's say it costs \$400,000.
2. Subtract the depreciation (let's say physical wear and tear cost \$50,000 in value). \$400,000 - \$50,000 = \$350,000.
3. Add the value of the land. Let's say the land is worth \$100,000. \$350,000 + \$100,000 = \$450,000. The total value is \$450,000.

## The Income Approach

When business people buy big buildings, they usually don't care what the bricks look like. They only care about one thing: how much money will this make me? 

**The income approach estimates a property's value based on the property's ability to generate future income.** Because it is all about making money, **the income approach is the primary valuation method for commercial and investment properties.** Also, because it turns a stream of income into a large pile of capital, **the income approach is also referred to as the capitalization approach.**

### The Underlying Physics: The Principle of Anticipation

**The income approach is based on the economic principle of anticipation.** 

> **The Principle of Anticipation**
> **The principle of anticipation states that value is created by the expectation of future financial benefits derived from a property.**

You aren't paying for what the building did in the past; you are paying because you are *anticipating* (looking forward to) getting rent money every month in the future. 

### The Core Metric: Net Operating Income (NOI)

To do the math, we need the right number. **Net operating income is a required metric to calculate value using the income approach.** 

How do we find it? **Net operating income is calculated by subtracting total operating expenses from the effective gross income of a property.** 

Think of it like running a lemonade stand. If you make \$100 selling lemonade (gross income), but you spent \$20 on lemons and cups (operating expenses), your net income is \$80. 

But you have to be careful about what counts as an "expense"! 
*   **Mortgage payments are not considered an operating expense when calculating net operating income.** A mortgage is a loan from a bank. It has nothing to do with running the building itself.
*   **Income taxes are not considered an operating expense when calculating net operating income.** Taxes depend on the owner's personal life, not the building.

### Capitalization Rate and Valuation

Once you know the net income, you use a special percentage to find the final value. **The capitalization rate represents the rate of return an investor expects to earn on an income-producing property.** Think of it as the "score" or interest rate an investor demands.

> **In the income approach, property value is calculated by dividing the net operating income by the capitalization rate.** 

**Step-by-Step Math Example:**
1. Find the Net Operating Income (NOI). Let's say the property earns \$20,000 a year after expenses.
2. Find the Capitalization Rate (Cap Rate). Let's say investors expect a 10% return (which is written as 0.10 in math).
3. Divide the NOI by the Cap Rate. \$20,000 / 0.10 = \$200,000. The property's value is \$200,000.

Also, remember this seesaw effect: **There is an inverse relationship between a property's capitalization rate and the property's estimated value.** "Inverse" just means opposite. If the cap rate goes UP, the value goes DOWN.

### The Gross Rent Multiplier (GRM) Shortcut

For smaller properties, doing all that deep math is exhausting. Sometimes, we use a fast shortcut! **The gross rent multiplier is a simplified valuation method used for one-to-four unit residential rental properties.**

Instead of calculating expenses, you just look at the total rent coming in. **The gross rent multiplier is calculated by dividing a property's sale price by the property's gross monthly rent.** 

**Step-by-Step Math Example for finding the GRM:**
1. Look at a similar house that just sold. It sold for \$300,000.
2. Look at how much rent it collects every month. It collects \$2,000.
3. Divide the price by the rent: \$300,000 / \$2,000 = 150. The market GRM is 150!

Once you have that magic multiplier, you can price your own property. **A property's value can be estimated by multiplying the market gross rent multiplier by the subject property's expected gross monthly rent.**

**Step-by-Step Math Example for finding value:**
1. Take the market GRM we just found: 150.
2. Find your property's expected gross monthly rent. Let's say your property will rent for \$2,500.
3. Multiply the GRM by your rent: 150 * \$2,500 = \$375,000. Your property's estimated value is \$375,000.

## The Reconciliation Process

If an appraiser tries all three methods on one building, they will get three different numbers! What do they do? 

**The reconciliation process is the final step in the appraisal process where the appraiser weighs the findings from the three approaches to determine a final value.** 

Notice the word *weighs*. **In the reconciliation process, the appraiser does not simply average the values derived from the three approaches.** Adding them all up and dividing by three is lazy math! 

Instead, **an appraiser gives the most weight to the valuation approach that is most appropriate for the specific type of property being appraised.** If it's a house, they trust the Sales Comparison approach the most. If it's a church, they trust the Cost approach. If it's an office building, they trust the Income approach. They think like a smart detective to declare the single, final worth of the property.
