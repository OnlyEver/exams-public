Slide your glass over here, my friend. You look like you've been staring at Splunk dashboards until your corneas bled. I know that thousand-yard SOC analyst stare anywhere. You’re elbow-deep in incident response, trying to figure out if your network is a crime scene or just a poorly configured dumpster fire. Let me tell you, an operating system is exactly like a chaotic reality TV show house—everyone has assigned drama, strict rules, and the second someone breaks character, you know the script has been hacked. Drink up, because tonight we are tearing apart exactly what it looks like when adversaries try to sneak onto your set.

Let’s start with the cast of characters, the running processes. An operating system tracks the genealogy of every app running on it, and abnormal parent-child process relationships serve as strong, screaming indicators of an operating system host compromise. It's a literal daytime TV paternity test. Think about the legitimate Windows process svchost.exe. It is the ultimate loyal child, and it always, without fail, executes as a child of the services.exe process. If you are looking at your endpoint logs and you see Microsoft Word or PowerShell suddenly giving birth to svchost.exe, you need to channel your inner talk-show host, point at Word, and yell, "You are NOT the father!" 

Adversaries know we look for weird names, so they slap a fake mustache on their malware. This is process masquerading, which involves renaming a malicious executable to perfectly match a legitimate operating system process name. They will name their keylogger svchost.exe and hope you scroll right past it. But you can rip that mustache off by looking at their home address. Legitimate svchost.exe processes only ever execute from the upscale, gated communities of the System32 or SysWOW64 operating system directories. If you see an svchost.exe running out of the digital trailer park of a Temp folder or the Public user directory, it’s a total fraud. And while you’re looking at it, check the power bill! Unusually high resource utilization by unfamiliar host processes frequently indicates unauthorized cryptojacking activity. If that fake process is sweating buckets and maxing out 99 percent of your CPU, they aren't running system tasks; they’ve hijacked your hardware to secretly mine cryptocurrency.

Once they get on stage, their first priority is stealing the VIP guest list so they can move freely around the house. In Windows, the bouncer holding that list is the Local Security Authority Subsystem Service, or lsass.exe. Because this process handles active user sessions, credential dumping attacks frequently target the lsass.exe process memory. It’s like a digital mugging. And the weapon of choice? Mimikatz. Mimikatz is an infamous, open-source post-exploitation tool primarily used for credential extraction straight from the lsass.exe process. If your security tools flag something reading the memory space of lsass.exe, it is almost guaranteed to be Mimikatz, or one of its cousins, rifling through the bouncer's pockets for password hashes.

But what if the attacker doesn't even want to bring a weapon onto the premises? That is the terrifying beauty of fileless malware. It leverages legitimate system tools, like PowerShell, to execute malicious code directly in memory. It’s pure mind control. No files touch the hard drive; they just hypnotize the bartender into doing their dirty work. To catch this, you have to watch who the hypnotized bartender talks to. Unexpected outbound network connections originating from scripting engines indicate potential command and control communication. If PowerShell is suddenly making HTTP calls to an unlisted IP address in a foreign country at three in the morning, your system is phoning the mothership.

Now, let's talk about survival. If a user reboots a compromised machine, it’s like the landlord turning off the lights and locking the doors. The adversary's campaign dies unless they have a spare key. To survive a reboot, adversaries frequently use Windows Registry run keys to achieve malicious payload persistence. They essentially glue their malware to the ceiling of the operating system so it drops down the second the lights come back on. Specifically, the Windows Registry path HKEY_LOCAL_MACHINE\Software\Microsoft\Windows\CurrentVersion\Run is a primary location monitored for malware persistence. Anything listed in that key launches automatically. 

And they won't stop there; they will use the Registry to blind you. Adversaries modify Windows Registry keys to intentionally disable native security solutions like Windows Defender. If you see a registry modification setting DisableAntiSpyware to a value of 1, the attacker just sneaked into the security room and snipped the wires to your cameras. They'll also mess with your daily habits. Unexpected changes to file extension associations in the Windows Registry indicate potential malware persistence techniques. It’s the equivalent of swapping the labels on the salt and sugar. They change the registry so that every time a user double-clicks a simple .txt file, it executes the malware instead of opening Notepad. 

If they don't want to mess with the Registry, they’ll just use the building's own alarm clocks. Scheduled tasks created by unknown user accounts indicate potential host-level persistence mechanisms. To do this, the schtasks command-line utility is frequently abused by threat actors to schedule malicious task execution on Windows systems. They just leave a digital post-it note telling the server, "Hey, run this sketchy backdoor script every Tuesday at midnight." 

And if they really want to mess with reality, they’ll alter your map. Before a computer asks DNS for directions to a website, it checks its local override map. The legitimate Windows hosts file is located at the absolute path %SystemRoot%\System32\drivers\etc\hosts. But unexpected modifications to the local hosts file indicate an attempt to redirect system network traffic to malicious infrastructure. An attacker can write a fake entry in that file, so when your system tries to reach a legitimate update server, it is silently rerouted straight into the attacker's evil lair. 

Moving away from the operating system, let’s look at the applications themselves. An application is just a storefront, and attackers love a smash-and-grab. When you look at authentication logs, repeated failed authentication attempts indicate a potential password spraying or brute-force attack. A brute-force attack is a guy kicking one door ten thousand times with different passwords, while password spraying is a guy walking down the hallway jiggling every single doorknob using one common password, hoping somebody was lazy enough to use "Password123." When someone finally breaks through, you’ll see it in the behavior. Application logs showing unauthorized privilege escalation indicate a successful bypass of application access controls. That’s the digital equivalent of a busboy suddenly opening the manager's safe. 

Sometimes, they don't pick the lock; they just blow up the building. Application crash logs containing unexpected buffer overflows often indicate an active memory exploitation attempt. Imagine trying to shove fifty pounds of potatoes into a five-pound sack. The sack violently explodes, the potatoes spill into the adjacent kitchen spaces, and suddenly the attacker who shoved the potatoes is the new head chef. By overflowing the memory buffer, they overwrite the application's instructions with their own malicious code.

For web applications, the logs are full of magic spells. Web application logs containing excessive single quotes indicate potential SQL injection attempts. The single quote is the ultimate monkey wrench thrown into a database query. And if you want the smoking gun, the presence of UNION SELECT statements in web application logs is a definitive indicator of SQL injection activity. It’s the attacker commanding the database to stitch the stolen, hidden data onto the bottom of a normal website search result. 

If they want to target the users instead of the database, Cross-Site Scripting, or XSS, attacks generate application logs containing unexpected HTML script tags. They slip a malicious script tag into a comment section, and when another user views it, their browser executes the code blindly. And if they want to rummage through your server's basement? Directory traversal attacks generate application access logs containing repeated dot-dot-slash character sequences. Every "../" is the attacker chewing through the drywall of the web directory to escape into the wider operating system to steal sensitive files.

But my friend, after all this technical wizardry, we have to talk about the weakest link: the wetware. The human beings. Social engineering attacks exploit human psychology rather than technical vulnerabilities to breach security boundaries. Why spend six months burning a zero-day exploit to bypass a firewall when you can just put on a fake uniform and ask the receptionist to hold the door?

This starts with email. Phishing campaigns rely on spoofed sender addresses to deceive victims into trusting malicious communications. It’s a digital disguise. But the real pros don't just guess; spear-phishing attacks utilize customized open-source intelligence, or OSINT, to target specific individuals within an organization. They stalk your LinkedIn, find out you went to a conference in Vegas last week, and send an email pretending to be the hotel billing department. The payload attached to these emails is rarely a glaring, scary .exe file. Instead, malicious Office document macros serve as common payload delivery mechanisms in initial access phishing vectors. You open a budget spreadsheet, click "Enable Content," and a hidden Visual Basic script silently downloads a backdoor while you’re looking at pie charts. 

When millions of dollars are on the line, attackers pivot to Business Email Compromise, or BEC. Business Email Compromise attacks focus on impersonating executives to authorize fraudulent financial transactions. It’s a purely psychological heist. The "CEO" emails an entry-level accountant on a Friday afternoon, screaming about a secret acquisition and demanding an urgent wire transfer to an offshore account. No malware needed. Just fear. 

And if they need to bypass your fancy multi-factor authentication? They will just annoy the user into submission. Multi-factor authentication fatigue attacks involve flooding a user with approval requests to bypass authentication controls. They trigger the login prompt fifty times at 3:00 AM until the exhausted, blurry-eyed victim finally hits "Approve" on their phone just to make the buzzing stop. 

They’ll even come straight at your phone. Smishing attacks utilize SMS text messaging protocols to deliver social engineering lures to mobile devices. People trust text messages way more than corporate email. A text claiming to be FedEx about a missing package is incredibly devastating. Or, they’ll just call you. Vishing attacks employ Voice over IP or traditional telephone calls to manipulate victims into divulging sensitive data. They spoof the IT helpdesk number, play typing sound effects in the background, and kindly ask you to verify your password for a "system migration." 

So there it is. The registry keys, the runaway processes, the exploding memory buffers, and the manipulated humans. As an analyst, you aren't just reading logs; you are reading the matrix. You are putting the narrative together. Now finish that drink, get back to your terminal, and go hunt some bad guys.
