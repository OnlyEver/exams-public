Look at you. Staring at the bottom of that glass like it’s a frozen Splunk dashboard at three in the morning. I know that look. You’ve been staring at the telemetry so long the IP addresses are starting to whisper to you. Drink up, and let me tell you the reality of the game we’re playing. We aren't IT workers. We are the exhausted, underpaid security guards locked in the basement of a massive casino that thousands of invisible thieves are constantly trying to rob. 

Right now, you are living in the second phase of the NIST incident response lifecycle: Detection and Analysis. This is the crucible. This is where we figure out if the building is actively on fire or if someone just burned popcorn in the breakroom. But before we even get there, what is an incident? NIST SP 800-61 Revision 2 defines an incident as a violation or imminent threat of violation of computer security policies, acceptable use policies, or standard security practices. That "imminent threat" part is the kicker. It’s not just when the vault is already empty; an incident is when a guy in a ski mask is walking through the revolving door holding a drill. 

Our entire job in this phase is reading the shadows on the wall, looking for two distinct signals. First, we look for a precursor, which is a sign that an attacker may launch an incident in the future. That’s the reconnaissance. That’s the thief sitting at the bar, snapping photos of our surveillance cameras. Then, we look for an indicator, which is a sign that an incident may have occurred or may currently be occurring on a network. That’s the alarm blaring or the smell of thermite melting the vault hinges. 

When we find the dust from that melted hinge, we’ve found gold. Indicators of Compromise (IoCs) are forensic artifacts that provide evidence of a potential security breach. They are the bloody fingerprints left at the scene. We look for these in three different flavors, starting with absolute certainty. Cryptographic file hashes matching known malware signatures serve as deterministic Indicators of Compromise. It’s a literal DNA match to a known criminal. If the hash matches, there’s no debate, no "maybe"—Danny Ocean is in the building. 

But sometimes they don't drop files; they just try to leave with the cash. That’s when you see unusual outbound network traffic to unexpected geographic locations, which acts as a network-based Indicator of Compromise. Why is the accounting server in Ohio suddenly streaming five gigabytes of encrypted data to a sketchy IP block in Eastern Europe at four in the morning? It’s not because accounting suddenly loves foreign cinema. They also try to disguise their getaway cars. A mismatched application protocol running on a non-standard port indicates potential command and control communication. It's like listening to the casino valet's walkie-talkie channel and suddenly hearing two guys speaking Russian over Port 80, a channel strictly reserved for unencrypted web traffic. They think we won't look closely at the valet channel. They are wrong.

When we find these clues, we have to broadcast the APB to the rest of the world so other casinos don't get hit. The OpenIOC framework is an extensible XML-based format used for describing Indicators of Compromise. It's the standard police sketch. But when we want to automate sharing this intel, we use STIX. STIX, or Structured Threat Information eXpression, is a standardized language used for conveying data about cybersecurity threats and Indicators of Compromise. And to understand *why* the thieves are doing what they do, we consult the grand master playbook. The MITRE ATT&CK framework is a globally accessible knowledge base of adversary tactics and techniques based on real-world observations. You find a weird registry key, you look at MITRE, and MITRE tells you exactly what heist movie the attacker thinks they are starring in.

But wait, what if they don't drop a known malware payload? What if they just steal the manager's keys? Behavioral Indicators of Compromise include abnormal user login times and unexpected privilege escalation events. A service account that only runs printer scripts suddenly trying to make itself a Domain Admin is a behavioral IoC. It’s the janitor suddenly demanding the combination to the high-roller safe. 

How do we even see all this happening? The eye in the sky. A Security Information and Event Management (SIEM) system centralizes log data from multiple sources for correlation and analysis. It is the massive wall of monitors in our basement. But the SIEM is useless without cameras on the casino floor. That’s why Endpoint Detection and Response (EDR) agents provide continuous monitoring and logging of endpoint system activities for incident detection. The EDR is a bodycam strapped to the chest of every single process running on your servers. 

When you dig into those endpoints, you have to speak the bouncer’s secret language: Windows Event IDs. You memorize these or you die out there. Windows Event ID 4624 indicates a successful account logon. The bouncer unhooked the velvet rope. Windows Event ID 4625 indicates a failed account logon attempt. The bouncer said no. If you see ten thousand 4625s in a minute, someone is violently trying to kick down the front door. Windows Event ID 4720 indicates the creation of a new user account. Someone just printed themselves a fake employee badge. And the absolute most terrifying one? Windows Event ID 1102 indicates the security audit log has been cleared. Someone just took a pair of wire cutters to the security cameras, because attackers deploy log clearing techniques to hide their tracks and disrupt subsequent incident analysis efforts. If you see an 1102, you panic, because the thieves are actively in the room.

Linux endpoints have their own guestbooks. The Linux /var/log/auth.log file stores system authorization information and user login attempts on Debian-based systems. If you're wandering through a Red Hat-based neighborhood instead, the Linux /var/log/secure file stores authentication and authorization events. Same bouncer, different clipboard. 

Now, step back from the endpoints and look at the network. This is where you see the escape routes. You have to understand the difference between the phone bill and a wiretap. Network flow data (NetFlow) provides metadata about network connections rather than the full packet payload. It tells you who called who, at what time, and for how long. It’s lightweight and great for spotting massive exfiltration. But full packet capture (PCAP) contains the complete payload and headers of network traffic for deep forensic analysis. PCAP is the actual audio recording of the phone call. You can rebuild the exact malicious file they downloaded from a PCAP.

Surrounding the whole property, firewall logs identify accepted and dropped network connections at the network perimeter or internal network segments. They show you exactly who the bouncers threw into the street. But when your internal machines reach out, proxy logs contain details of outbound web requests including requested URLs, user agent strings, and HTTP status codes. They tell you exactly what getaway cab the malware tried to hail. And if they hail a cab we know is driven by a mobster? DNS sinkholing logs reveal internal hosts attempting to resolve known malicious domains. We reroute their cab into a fake brick wall, and log the face of the infected machine sitting in the back seat.

But here is the absolute nightmare of modern detection: encrypted network traffic prevents traditional deep packet inspection without the deployment of TLS/SSL decryption proxies. The attackers are walking through the casino wearing lead-lined masks. Unless you have the decryption keys acting as an X-ray machine, your PCAP is just mathematical static. 

So you sit at the SIEM, and you try to make sense of the madness. Log correlation links multiple distinct log events to identify a broader attack sequence. You tie the failed login from the firewall to the weird proxy request to the new user creation on the endpoint. You become the TV detective tying red string between photographs on a corkboard. But listen to me very closely: you cannot tie those strings if your watches are lying to you! Clock synchronization across all network devices via Network Time Protocol (NTP) is strictly required to ensure accurate temporal correlation of logs. If your firewall thinks it’s Tuesday and your domain controller thinks it's 1998, reconstructing the attacker's timeline is a mathematically impossible temporal paradox!

When the clocks are synced, we add color to the raw data. Data enrichment adds contextual information to raw log events by appending details like geographic location or threat intelligence scores. We turn a meaningless string of numbers into a glaring, neon sign that reads "Known Ransomware Gang IP." 

To spot the thieves, you have to know what normal looks like. Baseline comparison involves measuring current system behavior against a known-good historical state to detect anomalies. If the server normally hums at five megabytes a day and suddenly it’s screaming at five terabytes, that baseline deviation is your alarm. But some thieves are patient. Trend analysis examines historical log data over time to identify slow-developing attacks or gradual changes in system behavior. That’s the crooked blackjack dealer pocketing one single chip every week for a year. A baseline won't catch it, but a trend will. And for the brand-new tricks? Heuristic analysis uses rules and algorithms to detect previously unknown threats based on suspicious characteristics. We don’t know who this guy is, and his face isn't in the database, but he's wearing a ski mask and holding a crowbar—heuristics tell us to tackle him anyway.

When the dust finally settles and you have to report to the board, you build the ultimate director's cut of the disaster. A super timeline aggregates log data from file systems, memory, and network devices into a chronological sequence of events. You merge everything into one massive script so you can replay the attacker's every move, second by second.

But man, this job eats your soul because the machines are stupid. False positives occur when an alerting system incorrectly identifies benign network activity as malicious. You tackle the pizza guy because he walked too fast. And worse, false negatives occur when an alerting system completely fails to identify actual malicious activity. The thieves walk right out the front door holding the bags of cash and the alarms sit dead silent. Because of the sheer volume of this chaos, a high volume of inaccurate security alerts can lead to alert fatigue for Security Operations Center analysts. You get so numb to the boy crying wolf that when the real wolf shows up, you just sip your coffee and let him bite you.

And time is always erasing your memory. Log retention policies dictate how long security event data must be stored for compliance auditing and historical incident analysis. If the attacker breached you 200 days ago, but your cheap boss only pays for 90 days of storage, the beginning of the heist is gone forever. Wiped from existence. 

So what do we do? We stop waiting for the alarms to tell us we're dying. Threat hunting is a proactive approach to finding undetected threats in a network by iteratively analyzing security data and logs. We assume the perimeter has already fallen. We grab our flashlights, we ignore the automated dashboards, and we stalk the halls of our own networks, looking for the subtle tremors the machines missed. We don't wait for the incident. We hunt it. Now buy me another drink, my shift starts in ten minutes.
