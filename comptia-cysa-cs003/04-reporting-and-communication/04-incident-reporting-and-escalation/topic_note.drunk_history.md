Look at that bartender right there. He just wiped down the counter. In our world, an event is any observable occurrence in a system or network. Just a thing that happened—a dropped packet, a logged-in user, a wiped counter. It’s reality happening in real-time. But watch this. What if he accidentally knocks over a glass and it shatters directly into the open ice bin? Suddenly, we’ve crossed a line. An adverse event is an event with a negative consequence. We now have glass in the ice, we can’t serve drinks, and the workflow is ruined. But let's escalate this reality TV drama. Imagine that bartender doesn't just drop a glass. Imagine he looks his manager dead in the eye, pulls a crowbar out from under the sink, and starts smashing the point-of-sale registers to steal customer credit card data. Boom! We have crossed from an accident into an attack. A computer security incident is a violation of computer security policies or acceptable use policies. It’s intentional, it’s policy-breaking, and it threatens the whole operation. 

Now, the floor staff can't just stare at him. Your Tier 1 Security Operations Center analysts perform initial triage to determine if an alert warrants formal incident declaration. They are the bouncers of the network, watching the chaos, grabbing the manager, and screaming, "Code Red!" That scream is vital because incident declaration is the formal process of classifying an observed adverse event as a security incident. You don't just whisper it; you declare it, because incident declaration triggers the formal incident response plan. The second that declaration happens, we stop pouring drinks and we go to war. And in this war, we don't improvise. NIST Special Publication 800-61 Revision 2 is the standard United States government guide for computer security incident handling. It is the absolute bible for keeping a bad day from becoming your company’s last day. 

But before you call in the SWAT team, you have to measure the heat of the fire. Incident severity classification determines the priority level of the incident response effort. Is the bartender stealing one tip jar, or is he burning down the whole block? Incident severity is typically calculated based on the technical information impact and the overall business impact. 

Once we know how bad it is, we have to pass the hot potato. Escalation is the process of transferring incident handling responsibility to a higher tier or specialized team. If the rogue bartender starts picking the vault lock and your bouncer doesn’t know how to stop him, you need the heavy hitters. Functional escalation involves transferring a security incident to personnel with more advanced technical skills. You call the master locksmith—the threat hunter who can reverse-engineer the attack. But what if the bartender has already set the roof on fire? Hierarchical escalation involves notifying management or executive leadership about a security incident. You don't call the CEO because a lock is tricky; hierarchical escalation is required when a security incident impacts critical business operations. When the money stops flowing, the big bosses need to be woken up.

And once management is awake, everyone demands a piece of the action. Your technical stakeholders require granular data such as IP addresses and hash values for incident remediation. They need to know the exact dimensions of the crowbar to stop the bleeding. On the flip side, management stakeholders require information regarding the financial impact of a security incident to make informed business decisions. They want to know if we are losing \$5,000 or \$5 million. Since our attacker is the bartender himself, human resources must be involved in an incident response if an internal employee is suspected of malicious activity. You can't just tackle an employee; HR has to suspend them legally! Then the suits walk in. Legal counsel must be involved in an incident response to guide regulatory compliance and assess potential litigation. Are we going to get sued by the customers? Meanwhile, the paparazzi are gathering outside, which means public relations teams manage external communication with the media during a high-profile security incident. They spin the disaster so our stock price doesn't plummet. And if that rogue bartender was part of a cartel? Law enforcement should be contacted if a security incident involves a clear violation of federal or state law. 

Now, here is the terrifying part of the heist. The rogue bartender has a buddy in the back who just cut the phone lines and bugged our radios. The primary network is compromised! Incident responders must use out-of-band communication to prevent adversaries from intercepting response plans. An out-of-band communication method is a separate communication channel used when the primary network is compromised. You can’t organize a counter-attack if the enemy is listening to your walkie-talkies. Today, secure messaging applications with end-to-end encryption are frequently used for out-of-band incident communication. But you can't be figuring out how to download Signal while the bar is burning down. Out-of-band communication systems must be established and tested before a security incident occurs! All of this structured paranoia is guided by a communication plan, which defines the specific channels to be used for different incident severity levels. To get everyone online without chaos, a call tree is a hierarchical communication model used to systematically notify incident response team members. I call two leads, they call four analysts. But beware the social engineering trap—the attacker might call you pretending to be the CEO asking for an update. Incident response team members must verify the identity of individuals before discussing sensitive incident details over the phone. 

While we are fighting this war, the timeline matters. An incident timeline provides a chronological sequence of events related to a security incident. But what if one of our servers is in Tokyo and the other is in New York? You can't match up the logs if the timestamps are in local time! To prevent temporal insanity, incident timelines must use a standardized time zone to ensure chronological accuracy across distributed systems. The absolute king of time here is UTC; Coordinated Universal Time is the standard time zone used in cybersecurity incident timelines. We also have to bag and tag the evidence, like the bartender’s laptop. The chain of custody documents the chronological history of digital evidence handling. Who touched it, when, and where? If you break that chain, the lawyers will eat you alive, because proper chain of custody maintenance is required for digital evidence to be admissible in a court of law.

While you are wrangling evidence, a massive countdown timer is ticking above your head. Regulatory reporting requirements dictate the timeframe for notifying affected individuals of a data breach. You cannot hide this. For the big leagues, the Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency provides a central incident reporting point for federal agencies and critical infrastructure entities. In fact, the Cyber Incident Reporting for Critical Infrastructure Act of 2022 mandates reporting of covered cyber incidents to the Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency. And they do not give you a leisurely deadline. This Act mandates reporting of covered cyber incidents within 72 hours. And if your executives panic and pay off the attacker to unlock the registers? The Cyber Incident Reporting for Critical Infrastructure Act of 2022 mandates reporting of ransomware payments within 24 hours. Outside of critical infrastructure, external breach notification requirements vary significantly based on regional data privacy laws. If your bar has European customers, the General Data Protection Regulation mandates data breach notification to supervisory authorities within 72 hours of awareness. If this was a hospital and medical records were stolen, the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act mandates breach notification to the Secretary of Health and Human Services within 60 days for breaches affecting 500 or more individuals. 

When the fire is finally out, you have to write the ultimate receipt for the disaster. A comprehensive incident response report documents the entire lifecycle of a security incident. The front page of this document is written strictly for the people who sign your paychecks. An executive summary provides a high-level overview of an incident for non-technical stakeholders. It has to translate the nerd-speak into dollars, meaning an executive summary must clearly outline the business impact of the security incident. Crucially, an executive summary should omit granular technical details like specific memory addresses or firewall rules. The CEO does not care about your hex offsets or port numbers; they care about the bottom line. 

The back half of the report is the autopsy for the engineers. An incident response report must include the specific Indicators of Compromise associated with the attack, like the malicious domains or file hashes the rogue bartender used. Furthermore, an incident response report must describe the Tactics, Techniques, and Procedures used by the adversary. We don't just invent names for how he hacked us. The MITRE ATT&CK framework provides a standardized vocabulary for documenting adversary Tactics, Techniques, and Procedures in incident reports. We also have to document our heroics. Incident containment strategies implemented during the response must be documented in the final incident response report. How did we stop him from getting into the vault? Following that, incident eradication steps taken during the response must be documented in the final incident response report. How did we permanently kick him out and scrub his malware from the servers?

You'd think we're done, right? Grab a beer, go home? Wrong. If you don't learn from the fire, you're going to burn down again. A root cause analysis identifies the underlying vulnerability or process failure that allowed a security incident to occur. Did the bartender get access because nobody changed the default password on the cash register? Once we discover the fatal flaw, we draft our final document. The lessons learned report identifies improvements for future incident response efforts. Maybe we need better security cameras, or multifactor authentication. But memory fades quickly when the adrenaline wears off. Because of this, the lessons learned report must be completed during the post-incident activity phase. You strike while the iron is hot, you fix the broken doors, and you pray the next shift is just a little bit quieter.
