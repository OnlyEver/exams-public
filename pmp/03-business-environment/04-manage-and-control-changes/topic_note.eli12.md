Imagine you and your friends are building a massive, complicated treehouse. You drew up the exact plans—which we will call your baseline—and figured out exactly how many boards and nails you needed. But once you start building, someone says, "Let's add a slide!" or "We should paint it neon green!" If you ignore them, they might get mad. But if everyone just grabs a hammer and starts adding whatever they want without telling anyone, the treehouse might collapse! 

Managing changes in a big project is just like managing your treehouse build. It is not about saying "no" to every fun new idea. Instead, it is about having a clear, step-by-step way to look at ideas, decide if they work, and safely add them without ruining what you are already making.

## The Physics of Project Change Control

In the project management world, this step-by-step way to handle new ideas is called the **Perform Integrated Change Control** process. This whole process is a big part of the **Project Integration Management** knowledge area. Think of Integration Management like the brain of your project—it makes sure that changing one thing (like taking extra time to add a slide) doesn't ruin everything else (like running out of wood for the roof).

To keep everything organized, we use a rulebook called the **change management plan**. This plan is super important because it writes out the exact rules for getting an idea looked at:
*   **The change management plan defines the procedure for submitting project change requests.** 
*   **The change management plan defines the procedure for evaluating project change requests.** 
*   **The change management plan defines the procedure for approving project change requests.** 

At the heart of all this is your baseline. A **project baseline** is the approved version of a work product. It is like your ultimate saved game file; it is the official starting point for your schedule, your budget, or your blueprint. Because it is the official plan everybody agreed to, **a project baseline can be changed only through formal change control procedures.** 

## The Genesis of a Change Request

Ideas for changes can come from anywhere. The rule is that **any project stakeholder may submit a formal change request to the project.** A stakeholder is just anyone who cares about the project—like your friends building the treehouse, the younger siblings who will play in it, or your parents who are paying for the wood.

But we have to be careful about passing comments. If a friend yells across the yard, "Make the window bigger!" you cannot just start cutting wood. A super important rule is that **a verbal change request must be documented in writing before the change request can be processed.** Writing it down ensures everyone knows exactly what is being asked for so there are no mix-ups.

Usually, these written requests fall into one of three buckets:

| Type of Change Request | What it Does | Real-World Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Corrective Action** | **Corrective action is a type of change request intended to realign the performance of project work with the project management plan.** | Your team was supposed to finish homework before gaming, but got distracted. The corrective action is turning off the TV right now to get back on schedule. |
| **Preventive Action** | **Preventive action is a type of change request intended to ensure the future performance of the project work aligns with the project management plan.** | Putting a waterproof tarp over your treehouse roof *before* it rains tomorrow, so the inside floor doesn't get ruined later. |
| **Defect Repair** | **Defect repair is a type of change request to modify a nonconforming product component.** | Fixing a broken wheel on a skateboard you just built because it is stuck and doesn't spin right. |

## The Project Manager's Impact Assessment

When you get a written change request, you can't just say "yes" because it sounds cool. You have to be a detective. **The project manager evaluates the impact of every change request on the overall project constraints.** You have to figure out how this one change will mess with everything else you planned. 

You have to ask four big questions for every single change:
1. **The project manager must assess the impact of a proposed change on the project schedule.** (If we add a slide, will it take three extra days to finish the treehouse?)
2. **The project manager must assess the impact of a proposed change on the project budget.** (Do we have enough money for this?) Let's do a quick calculation if someone wants to buy a slide:
   *   Step 1: Start with our total remaining budget. Let's say we have \$50 left.
   *   Step 2: Find out the cost of the new slide. The slide costs \$35.
   *   Step 3: Subtract the cost from the budget: \$50 - \$35 = \$15.
   *   Step 4: Check if we still have enough money left for our required paint, which costs \$20. Since \$15 is less than \$20, we don't have enough money for the slide!
3. **The project manager must assess the impact of a proposed change on project risks.** (Will adding a second floor to the treehouse make it too heavy and dangerous in the wind?)
4. **The project manager must assess the impact of a proposed change on project quality requirements.** (If we build the roof super fast just to get it done, will it be leaky?)

You also need a way to track what the project actually looks like and how it works. That is where a configuration management system helps out. 
*   **A configuration management system tracks changes to the physical characteristics of the project deliverables.** (Like recording that we changed from a red door to a blue door).
*   **A configuration management system tracks changes to the functional requirements of the project deliverables.** (Like making sure the door actually locks, instead of just pushing open).

## The Authority to Alter Reality: The CCB

Even if you are the leader (the project manager), you don't get to make all the big decisions on your own. **The project manager cannot unilaterally approve changes that affect the established project baselines.** "Unilaterally" just means "by yourself." 

Because the baselines are the big, official promises you made, **changes to project baselines can only be approved through the Perform Integrated Change Control process.** 

So, who actually gets to say yes or no? In traditional projects, there is a special team called the Change Control Board. 

> **Change Control Board (CCB):** **A Change Control Board is a formally chartered group responsible for reviewing changes to the project.** Think of them like the referees in a sports game.

The CCB holds the real power. 
*   **A Change Control Board has the authority to approve proposed project changes.** (Thumbs up!)
*   **A Change Control Board has the authority to reject proposed project changes.** (Thumbs down!)
*   **A Change Control Board has the authority to defer proposed project changes.** (Saying "Let's pause and decide on this next month.")

What if a change is so massive that even the CCB isn't allowed to decide? Like what if someone wants to tear down the treehouse and build a swimming pool instead? In that case, **the project sponsor is typically the final escalation point for change requests that exceed the authority of the Change Control Board.** The sponsor is the person paying for everything (like your parents!).

## Execution, Communication, and Documentation

Once the CCB or the sponsor makes a decision, you have to tell everyone and update your paperwork.

### The Change Log
To keep track of all these ideas, you use a list called the change log. **The change log tracks the current status of all submitted change requests.** 

As the leader, **the project manager uses the change log to communicate the current status of proposed changes to project stakeholders.** Everyone needs to know what is happening. 
*   **The project manager must communicate the approval of a change request to the relevant stakeholders.** 
*   **The project manager must communicate the rejection of a change request to the relevant stakeholders.** (Even if the news is bad, you have to tell them so they aren't waiting around!)

If an idea gets approved, people need details. **The project manager must inform stakeholders about the schedule impact of an approved change** (like telling them the project will take two extra weeks). And **the project manager must inform stakeholders about the cost impact of an approved change** (like telling them everyone needs to chip in an extra \$5).

### Updating the Baselines
If a change is approved, your old plans are out of date. You have to fix them right away!
*   **The project manager must update the project management plan to reflect approved change requests.**
*   **The project schedule baseline must be updated following the approval of a schedule-altering change request.**
*   **The project cost baseline must be updated following the approval of a cost-altering change request.**
*   **The project scope statement must be updated if an approved change request alters the project scope.** (Scope means "the total amount of work we are supposed to do.")
*   Also, **a work breakdown structure must be updated when a change request adds new deliverables to the project scope.** (A work breakdown structure is just your massive list broken down into smaller, bite-sized chores).

### The Rule of Implementation
Here is a massive rule you can never break: **Only approved change requests can be implemented by the project team.** 

If your friend is holding a hammer and wants to smash a new window into the wall right now, you have to stop them. **A project team member cannot implement a change request before receiving formal approval.** You must wait for the official "yes."

## The Spectrum of Change: Predictive, Agile, and Hybrid

Everything we just talked about—written forms, rulebooks, and CCBs—is for traditional projects where everything is planned up front. But what about projects that are meant to be fast and flexible, like designing a fun new video game?

### The Agile Approach to Change
In "Agile" projects, we expect things to change all the time! We don't use a slow Change Control Board for everyday things. Instead, we use a simple to-do list called a "product backlog," and a team leader called the "Product Owner."

*   **In Agile projects, the Product Owner manages project changes by reprioritizing items in the product backlog.** If a new idea is great, it gets moved to the top of the list. If it's bad, it moves to the bottom.
*   Instead of filling out confusing forms, **in Agile projects, new requirements are incorporated into the product backlog.** 
*   Because the Product Owner manages this list directly, **in Agile projects, execution-level changes bypass the formal Change Control Board process.** 

But Agile isn't total chaos. The team works in short bursts called "sprints" (usually a couple of weeks long). Once they pick the chores for their current sprint, they lock it in. **Agile teams typically freeze changes to the sprint backlog once a sprint begins.** This way, the team can just focus on working without people yelling new ideas at them every five minutes.

### The Hybrid Reality
Sometimes, a project is a mix of both styles. This is called a Hybrid project. In these projects, you split the big decisions from the little decisions:
*   Big, giant changes are handled the traditional way: **In a hybrid project, strategic project changes often require formal Change Control Board approval.** (For example, deciding to change a racing video game into a puzzle game).
*   Small, everyday tweaks are handled the Agile way: **In a hybrid project, operational execution-level changes are often managed dynamically via the product backlog.** (For example, changing the color of the race car from red to purple).

Managing changes is really just about keeping things from spinning out of control. Whether you are using a super strict Change Control Board, or an Agile to-do list, your job is to make sure every new idea is looked at, decided on, and safely added without blowing up all the hard work you've already done!
