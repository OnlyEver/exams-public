Imagine you are planning a massive outdoor pizza party. You have a great plan, but the moment you start setting up, things change. The wind blows away the napkins, the pizza delivery is late, or your friends decide they want to play a different game. Managing a project is exactly like that! Uncertainty isn't a mistake; it's just a normal part of life that we have to handle. 

Having a plan for risks means we stop running around panicking when surprises happen. Instead, we look ahead, spot the storm clouds early, and grab an umbrella before it starts raining. 

## The Engine of Risk Monitoring

Risk monitoring is like having a radar in a video game. **Risk monitoring requires tracking identified project risks continuously throughout the project lifecycle.** This means keeping an eye on the obstacles you already know about from the very beginning to the very end. But you also have to watch the horizon, because **risk monitoring involves identifying new project risks continuously as the project progresses.** You always have to watch out for new surprises!

How do we spot these new surprises? **Project teams compare work performance information against baseline metrics to identify potential new risks.** Think of "baseline metrics" as your original plan. If you planned to build 10 Lego sets in 5 days, but you are taking way longer, comparing your real work to your original plan tells you there might be a problem soon.

We have two special ways to check our radar carefully:

1. **Technical performance analysis compares technical accomplishments during execution to the schedule of technical achievement.** This is checking if the cool features of your project actually work as well as you promised they would by a certain date. (Like promising your science fair volcano will erupt 5 feet high, but testing it and seeing it only erupts 1 foot).
2. **Reserve analysis compares the amount of remaining contingency reserves to the amount of remaining project risk.** Think of "contingency reserves" as your emergency allowance. Let's look at how to calculate if your emergency allowance is safe:
    *Step 1:* Find out how much emergency money you have left. Let's say you started with \$100 and spent \$80. You have \$20 left.
    *Step 2:* Look at the remaining risks that might cost you money. Let's say there are 4 risks left, and each might cost \$10.
    *Step 3:* Calculate the total potential risk cost: 4 * 10 = \$40.
    *Step 4:* Compare your remaining money to the total risk cost. You only have \$20 left, but you might need \$40! Because 20 is less than 40, your reserve analysis tells you that you are running out of emergency money and need to be careful.

### The Agile Rhythms of Vigilance
In Agile projects (where teams work in quick, flexible bursts), checking for risks is a daily habit. 
* **Agile project teams continuously monitor risks during daily stand-up meetings.** A stand-up meeting is a quick daily huddle. If a teammate says, "My computer broke and I can't do my part today," they are spotting a risk right then and there.
* When they look at the big picture, **Agile project teams continuously monitor risks during sprint retrospectives.** A retrospective is a meeting at the end of a work cycle to talk about what went right or wrong. Here, teams can say, "We almost ran out of time, let's fix our process so we don't risk being late next time!"

## Maintaining the Living Ledger: The Risk Register

If risk monitoring is your radar, the **risk register** is your notebook. It isn't just a list you write once and throw away. **A risk register is a living document updated regularly throughout the project lifecycle.** 

**The risk register records the detailed characteristics of all identified individual project risks.** It holds every little detail about what could go wrong or right. But since the real world changes, your notebook has to change, too. When new things happen, **updating a risk register involves modifying the probability of existing risks based on new execution data.** For example, if you worried it might rain on your pizza party, but the weather forecast changes to clear skies, the chance (probability) of rain goes down. 

Also, **updating a risk register involves modifying the impact of existing risks based on new execution data.** If your parents buy indoor pizza ovens, the damage (impact) of a rainstorm isn't a big deal anymore!

Most importantly, **updating a risk register involves formally closing out outdated risks.** If winter is over, you must cross out the risk of a snowstorm. Don't let old worries clutter up your notebook!

### Ownership and Activation
Every risk needs a boss. **A risk owner is the individual responsible for monitoring a specific assigned risk.** At the same time, **a risk owner is the individual responsible for implementing the agreed-upon risk response for an assigned risk.** If you are the owner of the "dog eats the pizza" risk, it is your job to watch the dog and take action to stop it!

But how do you know when to take action? You look for clues. **Risk triggers are specific events indicating that a risk is about to occur** (like the dog drooling while staring at the table). Also, **risk triggers are specific events indicating that a risk has occurred** (like the dog jumping onto a chair). 

## Executing Risk Responses: The Anatomy of Action

When a risk happens, it is time to make your move! **Executing a risk management plan requires implementing planned risk responses upon the activation of risk triggers.** This just means that when you see the clue, you do the thing you planned to do.

Here is how we respond to surprises:

| Response Type | Characteristics & Triggers |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Contingency Plans** | **Contingency plans are predefined risk responses executed exclusively upon the activation of specific risk triggers.** (For example: *If* the dog jumps on the chair, *then* move the pizza to the counter.) |
| **Fallback Plans** | **Fallback plans are executed when a primary contingency plan fails to effectively mitigate a risk.** This is your backup to the backup! (For example: *If* moving the pizza to the counter doesn't work because the dog is super tall, *then* lock the dog in the bedroom.) |
| **Workarounds** | **A workaround is an unplanned response to an unexpected negative project event.** This is when a surprise happens that you never planned for, so you just make up a fix on the spot using whatever you have! |

> **The Cost of Action: Baselines and Change Control**
> Fixing a problem usually costs time or money. **Executing a risk response may require submitting a formal change request to the change control board.** The "change control board" is like the group of parents running the party. You have to ask permission to change the big rules. **A change request is mandatory if a risk response alters the project cost baseline** (like if you need to spend an extra \$20 to buy more pizza). Also, **a change request is mandatory if a risk response alters the project schedule baseline** (like if the party needs to last two hours longer). You cannot just change the main budget or schedule without asking!

### The Ripple Effects: Secondary and Residual Risks
When you try to fix one problem, sometimes you create a new one. 
* **Secondary risks are new risks created as a direct outcome of implementing a risk response strategy.** If you lock the dog in the bedroom to save the pizza, the *secondary risk* is that the dog gets bored and chews your favorite sneakers.
* **Residual risks are remaining risks expected to persist after planned risk responses have been taken.** If you wear knee pads while skateboarding to stay safe, the chance that you might still scrape your elbow is the *residual risk*. It’s the leftover risk that you couldn't completely get rid of.

## Domain-Specific Execution: IT Security and Sustainability

Sometimes projects involve computers or the planet. We have special ways to handle those!

**Information Technology (IT) Security Risks**
Hackers on the internet move fast. Because of this, **information technology security risks require specific documented warning triggers in the risk register.** You need a specific clue, like a user typing the wrong password 5 times in a row, to know a hacker is trying to break in. 
To handle these tech dangers, **information technology security risk responses often involve transferring risk liability through cyber insurance policies.** This is like buying insurance so that if a hacker does break in, the insurance company pays for the damage instead of you. Also, **information technology security risk responses often involve mitigating risk probability through enhanced network protocols.** This is a fancy way of saying you lower the chance of getting hacked by using stronger digital locks!

**Sustainability Risks**
Projects also affect the world around us. **Sustainability risk management addresses the long-term environmental impacts of project deliverables.** This means making sure your project doesn't hurt nature, like checking that you aren't creating too much trash. Plus, **sustainability risk management addresses the long-term social impacts of project deliverables.** This means making sure your project treats people fairly, like buying supplies from companies that pay their workers fairly.

## Auditing the Strategy: Are We Actually Safe?

You might follow your safety plan perfectly, but what if the plan itself is bad? We need to test our plans like a teacher grades a test. 
* **Risk audits evaluate the effectiveness of the overall risk management process.** An audit asks, "Are our team meetings actually helping us find problems, or are we just wasting time talking?"
* On top of that, **risk audits evaluate the effectiveness of specific risk response strategies.** This asks, "We put a lock on the door to keep the dog out, but did it actually stop the dog?"

## Communicating the Reality

If you know about a danger but don't tell your friends, it doesn't help anyone! **Risk status communication ensures project stakeholders remain aware of current project threat levels.** "Stakeholders" are all the people who care about the project, like your team or your parents. They need to know if danger is high or low. And remember, **risk status communication ensures project stakeholders remain aware of current project opportunity levels.** Good things (opportunities) are risks too! Everyone needs to know if there's a chance to finish early or get extra pizza!

### The Architecture of Risk Reporting
You can't just yell out warnings randomly. **Risk impact communication must align perfectly with the stakeholder communication requirements defined in the communications management plan.** This means you need to share news exactly how everyone agreed to get it. You wouldn't send a giant book of computer code to your parents; you would just tell them, "The video game works!"

We use special tools to share this news:
* **A risk report summarizes critical information regarding individual project risks** (like listing the top three things that could go wrong). Also, **a risk report summarizes the level of overall project risk exposure** (like giving a score for how risky the whole project is right now). Here is how you might calculate that overall score step-by-step:
    *Step 1:* Give each risk a danger score from 1 to 5. Let's say Rain is 3, and Dog Eating Pizza is 4.
    *Step 2:* Add the scores of all active risks together: 3 + 4 = 7.
    *Step 3:* Compare it to yesterday. If yesterday's total score was 10, your overall project risk exposure has gone down!
* For normal, step-by-step projects, **project managers typically communicate ongoing risk status through regular project status reports.** This is like bringing home a weekly report card.
* In fast-moving Agile projects, teams like to see things right away. **Agile teams often use visual information radiators to communicate current risk status to all stakeholders.** An "information radiator" is just a big, colorful poster on the wall. Anyone walking by can look at it and instantly know what's going on!
* A super helpful poster is a chart. **A risk burndown chart tracks the cumulative severity of identified project risks over time.** Imagine drawing a line on a graph. As you solve problems day by day, the line goes down, showing everyone that the project is getting safer!

### Escalation and Reassessment
Projects change fast. When you sit down to double-check the risks (which is called a reassessment), you have to share what you find. **Project managers must communicate the outcomes of periodic risk reassessments to the project sponsor.** The sponsor is the person paying for the project. They really need to know if their money is safe!

If you discover a giant new problem, you cannot keep it a secret. **Project managers must promptly communicate any significant changes in overall project risk exposure to relevant stakeholders.**

By watching out for problems, updating your notebook, taking smart actions, and telling your team what is happening, you take the scary parts out of a project. Instead of being afraid of surprises, you turn them into a game you know exactly how to win!
