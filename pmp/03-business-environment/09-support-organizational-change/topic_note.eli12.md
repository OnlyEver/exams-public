Imagine if your favorite video game completely changed its rules overnight. The new rules might make the game better, but if the players don't understand them or don't want to play the new way, they will just quit! In the professional world, getting people to accept a new way of doing things is a huge part of a project manager's job. 

## The Physics of Organizational Culture

Every school, team, or company has a unique "vibe." In the project world, organizational culture is classified as an Enterprise Environmental Factor. You can think of an Enterprise Environmental Factor (or EEF for short) like the invisible rules of a house or the weather outside. Specifically, organizational culture consists of the shared values among an organization's members. It also means that organizational culture includes the behavioral norms accepted by an organization's members, like whether people usually work together as a team or compete against each other.

Because these invisible rules are so powerful, Enterprise Environmental Factors directly influence project execution strategies. You wouldn't use the same game plan for a fun, friendly group as you would for a super strict group. Because every group is different, project managers must assess organizational culture to tailor project communication strategies. If the culture is relaxed, you might send quick text messages; if it is formal, you might write official letters.

## The Intersection of People and Process

When building something new, there are two different jobs. First, project management focuses on the processes required to deliver a specific change (like buying new computers and plugging them in). But getting people to actually use them is different! Organizational Change Management focuses primarily on the people side of change (like helping people not feel scared of the new computers).

Because making something new means dealing with people, project managers frequently act as change agents within their organizations. A change agent is an individual who promotes the execution of organizational change. They are the cheerleaders and coaches helping everyone adapt. However, a change agent needs backup from someone with a lot of authority. The project sponsor plays a critical role in championing organizational change to executive stakeholders. The sponsor is like the school principal who convinces all the other adults that this change is a great idea.

## Measuring the Terrain: Readiness and Risk

Before you introduce a massive update, you need to know if the players are ready. A change readiness assessment evaluates an organization's preparedness to undergo a transformation. Project managers use change readiness assessments to identify potential resistance to project deliverables. "Resistance" just means people pushing back, getting frustrated, or saying, "I don't want to do this!"

If people refuse to play the new way, it’s a big problem. In fact, resistance to change constitutes a common organizational project risk. Since it is a danger to the project's success, project managers must log identified resistance to change in the project risk register so the team can figure out how to solve it.

Also, when big shifts happen, your best helpers might get busy with other things. Because of this, organizational changes frequently alter project resource availability. The people you need to help you might suddenly be assigned to a different task!

## The Change Management Toolkit

Just like having a playbook in sports, developing an organizational change management plan helps structure the approach to transitioning individuals so they don't get lost or upset along the way. We have a few famous toolkits to help us do this.

### Frameworks for Organizational Transition

*   **ADKAR Model:** The ADKAR model is an organizational change management framework designed to help one person change at a time. The acronym ADKAR stands for Awareness, Desire, Knowledge, Ability, and Reinforcement. To start, Awareness in the ADKAR model represents an individual's understanding of the need for change. If they know why it’s happening, next comes desire. Desire in the ADKAR model represents an individual's willingness to support a change.
*   **Kotter's 8-Step Process:** If you need the whole company to change at once, John Kotter created the 8-Step Process for Leading Change. The first step in Kotter's change model is creating a sense of urgency—making everyone feel like we have to do this right now! Building a guiding coalition is the second step in Kotter's change model, which means getting the most popular or influential people on your team to help lead the way.
*   **Lewin’s Change Management Model:** Think of changing a company like changing the shape of an ice cube. Lewin's Change Management Model consists of three distinct stages. The three stages of Lewin's Change Management Model are Unfreeze, Change, and Refreeze. The Unfreeze stage of Lewin's model involves preparing the organization to accept necessary change (melting the ice). Then, the Refreeze stage of Lewin's model aims to solidify the new state after implementing a change (freezing the water into its new shape).
*   **Bridges Transition Model:** Change can be highly emotional. The Bridges Transition Model focuses on the psychological transitions people experience during a change. Bridges Transition Model includes three phases named Ending, The Neutral Zone, and The New Beginning. People have to say goodbye to the old way before they can truly accept the new way.
*   **McKinsey 7-S Framework:** When looking at the whole picture, McKinsey's 7-S Framework analyzes organizational design through seven key internal elements. If you change one thing, it affects the rest! The McKinsey 7-S Framework elements include Strategy, Structure, Systems, Shared Values, Style, Staff, and Skills.

## Adjusting to the Tectonic Shifts

Sometimes the company's main goals totally change. If that happens, organizational strategy changes require a re-evaluation of the project business case. For example, if your school suddenly decides saving money is more important than a \$500 pizza party, your pizza project might get canceled! Project managers must align project goals with newly updated organizational strategic objectives to make sure the project is still helpful.

When the vibe or culture changes, you also have to change how you talk to people. Cultural shifts during a project execution require updating the project stakeholder engagement plan. In the same way, project teams must update communication management plans to reflect organizational culture shifts so everyone stays perfectly in the loop.

## Matching Methodology to Organizational Culture

How a project runs depends on where it lives. Assessing organizational culture helps determine the most appropriate project management methodology. 

In highly flexible groups, Agile project management frameworks support organizational change through iterative delivery (doing things in small, quick chunks instead of all at once). Short iterations in Agile allow teams to adjust quickly to shifting organizational priorities. 

But not every place is flexible. A highly rigid organizational culture will likely struggle to adopt pure Agile methodologies. In strict places, predictive projects manage organizational change impacts through formal change control processes. This means you have to ask for official, written permission before you change anything. 

If a company is somewhere in the middle, hybrid project approaches combine predictive change control with agile adaptability to manage organizational shifts smoothly.

## Sustaining the Change: Capability, Metrics, and Fatigue

To make sure the change actually sticks, training programs act as a required project action to build organizational change capability. You have to teach people how to play the new game! 

After teaching them, project managers measure change adoption metrics to evaluate the success of an organizational transformation. Are people actually using the new tools? 

Here is how a project manager calculates a change adoption metric if they bought new computers for the school:
1. Count the total number of people who are supposed to use the new item (let's say 200 students).
2. Count how many of those people are actually using the computers every day (let's say 150 students).
3. Divide the number of daily users by the total number of students to get a decimal (150 / 200 = 0.75).
4. Multiply that decimal by 100 to find the percentage (0.75 x 100 = 75%).

So, your change adoption metric is 75%!

Finally, you can't ask people to change too much at once. Change fatigue occurs when an organization implements too many concurrent transformations. Imagine getting a new phone, moving to a new house, and starting a new school all on the exact same day! It’s exhausting. To keep people from getting totally overwhelmed, project managers mitigate change fatigue by staggering the rollout of major project deliverables. This means giving out the changes one small, easy piece at a time.
