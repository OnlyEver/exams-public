Before we even picked this target, we had to run the math. We use Net Present Value, which expresses the current monetary value of expected future cash flows minus the initial investment cost. Because inflation eats cash, a million dollars next year is worth less than a million today, so we discount it back. The rule of the underworld is absolute: a project with a Net Present Value strictly greater than zero is considered financially profitable. If it's zero or negative, we are risking federal prison for charity. But say we have three rival casinos we could hit. How do we choose? We calculate the Internal Rate of Return, which finds the specific interest rate at which the Net Present Value of a project equals exactly zero. It's our ultimate metric of efficiency. Because our capital and manpower are finite, organizations generally prioritize competing projects that offer a higher Internal Rate of Return. Once the target is locked, we have to decide how to breach it. In the old days, crews relied purely on predictive delivery approaches, where you release the final product to the customer only at the end of the project life cycle. That’s digging a tunnel for six months. You spend millions on shovels, and you see absolutely zero payoff until you finally breach the vault floor on the very last day. If the cops find your tunnel on month five, you lose everything. 

Screw that. We survive on value-based delivery, which prioritizes project work that yields the highest business value early in the project lifecycle. We want cash in hand tonight. We achieve this through incremental delivery, which provides functional portions of a product to customers in successive phases rather than a single deployment. Instead of one massive heist, we knock over the armored trucks week by week! If we need constant market intel, we shift to agile delivery approaches, where we deploy working products frequently to allow stakeholders to evaluate real-world value and provide immediate feedback. We steal a handful of diamonds, run them to our fence, and if he says the cut is outdated, we immediately pivot to stealing gold bullion instead. Sometimes, we need a masterpiece of both worlds: hybrid project delivery. This combines predictive upfront planning—mapping the laser grid and timing the guard patrols—with agile incremental execution, where we adaptively loot the safety deposit boxes based on whatever we find inside. Balance the predictability of the infiltration with the early value realization of the loot.

When we start pulling things out of the vault, do not confuse testing the waters with securing the bag. But what happens when we only have three minutes left, and two safes remain? We use Weighted Shortest Job First to sequence project work by dividing the cost of delay by the job size to maximize economic value delivery. Safe A holds \$100,000 but takes 1 minute to crack. Now, if we are running a classic, heavy-duty predictive tunnel operation, we rely on Earned Value Management, which integrates project scope, schedule, and cost to measure objective performance and value realization in predictive projects. Speed is life in this game. If we are too slow, the market shifts, or the SWAT team arrives. We use Value Stream Mapping to visualize the entire production process to identify activities that add customer value and eliminate activities that create waste. If we map out the getaway route and realize taking the scenic bridge adds ten minutes of pure waste without evading more cops, we cut it! We measure this flow with two distinct stopwatches. Lead time measures the total elapsed time from a customer request—the boss saying "Get me that casino's money"—to the delivery of the final product value. But Cycle time measures the total time a project team spends actively working on a specific task to deliver incremental value. That’s the exact stopwatch time from the moment the thermal drill touches the titanium door until the lock melts. Optimize the cycle time of the drill, eliminate the waste in the value stream, and your overall lead time shrinks.
