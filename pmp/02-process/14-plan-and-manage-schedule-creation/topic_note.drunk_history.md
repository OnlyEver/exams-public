On that software, I’ve planted a massive red flag at midnight labeled "Vault Open." That is a project milestone, which represents a significant point, event, or major deliverable completion in a project lifecycle. It is a threshold we cross, an achievement unlocked. Because it is merely a marker in time, a project milestone has a scheduled duration of exactly zero days. You don't "do" a milestone; you survive it.

To reach that milestone, every action must be linked by the iron laws of sequence, which we call dependencies. First, we face mandatory dependencies, which are legally or physically required relationships between project activities. You physically cannot reach your hands into the vault before the titanium door is blown off! Then we have discretionary dependencies, which are established based on industry best practices or specific project team preferences. We *could* put our ski masks on while running through the casino lobby, but best practice dictates we put them on inside the van so we aren't caught on the security cameras early. We are also at the mercy of external dependencies, which involve relationships between project activities and non-project activities outside the team's control. We literally have to wait for the city garbage truck to block the alleyway before we move. Finally, internal dependencies involve relationships between project activities completely within the project team's control. Our hacker waiting for our inside-man to plug a USB drive into the mainframe? We own both sides of that equation.

How do these actions connect? The backbone of our vault team relies on the Finish-to-Start dependency, which requires a predecessor activity to finish before a successor activity can start. The alarm system finishes shutting down, and only then does the drilling start. Because it’s the most logical way the physical world works, a Finish-to-Start dependency is the most commonly used logical relationship in predictive project scheduling. 

But a heist requires parallel chaos. A Start-to-Start dependency requires a predecessor activity to start before a successor activity can start. As soon as the distraction explosion on the casino floor starts, the hacking of the security cameras starts. We also have a Finish-to-Finish dependency, which requires a predecessor activity to finish before a successor activity can finish. The uploading of the fake camera loop must finish before the system reboot can finish, or we are exposed. And the weirdest one? A Start-to-Finish dependency requires a predecessor activity to start before a successor activity can finish. Our imposter night guard must start his shift before the legitimate day guard can finish his shift and go home!

We bend these relationships using time offsets. A lead allows an acceleration of a successor activity based on the finish date of a predecessor activity. We start revving the getaway van's engine two minutes *before* the team finishes loading the cash. Conversely, a lag directs a mathematical delay in the successor activity relative to a predecessor activity. We pump sleeping gas into the air vents, but we mathematically enforce a three-minute lag before entering the room to ensure the guards are actually unconscious!

But how do we know how long any of this takes? We don't guess; we calculate! For the vault, we use analogous estimating, which uses historical data from a similar past project to estimate duration or cost for a current project. "Drilling this safe is like the Monaco job." But since vaults upgrade their tech, analogous estimating relies on expert judgment to adjust historical data for current project conditions. If we want pure algorithm-driven math, we use parametric estimating, which uses an algorithm to calculate activity duration based on historical data and project parameters. If it takes one minute to cut through one inch of steel, a ten-inch steel door takes exactly ten minutes. When we have the time to be meticulous, we use bottom-up estimating. This aggregates the duration estimates of lower-level components of the Work Breakdown Structure into a total project estimate. I corner every single thief and ask them exactly how many seconds their specific micro-task takes, and I add it all up.

But human behavior is risky! Will a guard drop his coffee and delay his patrol? We mitigate risk with three-point estimating, which uses optimistic, most likely, and pessimistic values to calculate an expected activity duration. Let's say optimistic is 5 minutes, most likely is 7, and pessimistic is 20. The Triangular Distribution formula for three-point estimating adds the optimistic, pessimistic, and most likely values, then divides the sum by three. It's unweighted, treating disaster and perfection as equally likely. If we want to bet our lives on reality, we use the Beta Distribution formula for three-point estimating, which adds the optimistic, pessimistic, and four times the most likely value, then divides the sum by six. That formula anchors our escape plan directly to the highest probability!

Our agile grifters on the floor don't use minutes and hours. Story points are a relative measure of effort used in agile estimating rather than an absolute measure of time. Pickpocketing a drunk tourist might be a 2, but seducing a pit boss for his security codes is a massive 13! Story point estimates account for the complexity, risk, and volume of work associated with an agile user story. To size these jobs without the veteran con-artists intimidating the rookies, the crew sits in the hideout playing Planning Poker. This is a consensus-based agile estimating technique designed to avoid the anchoring bias of individual estimates. Everyone throws their cards down at the exact same time! Planning Poker utilizes a modified Fibonacci sequence—1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, 21—to assign story point values to product backlog items, making sure there's a huge, undeniable difference between a small job and a massive headache. And if they have a giant list of marks, affinity estimating categorizes product backlog items into groups of similar size or effort, rapidly sorting marks into T-shirt sizes like Small, Medium, or Extra-Large problems.

Once the entire matrix is built, the software performs a schedule network analysis, which identifies early start, late start, early finish, and late finish dates for all project activities. It reveals the very heartbeat of the heist! The crown jewel of this analysis is the Critical Path Method, which calculates the longest sequence of dependent activities in a project schedule network diagram. Think about that paradox: the longest sequence of dependent actions actually dictates the shortest possible physical duration for completing the entire project! If the critical path to breach the vault takes 45 minutes, it is physically impossible to finish the heist in 44 minutes.

This calculation reveals our float, our breathing room. Total float is the amount of time a schedule activity can be delayed without delaying the project finish date. Free float is the amount of time a schedule activity can be delayed without delaying the early start date of any immediate successor activity. But listen to me carefully: the guys drilling the vault have no slack! Activities situated directly on the critical path typically possess zero total float. If the driller sneezes and loses thirty seconds, the entire midnight escape is delayed by thirty seconds!

How do our agile grifters know if they can steal enough keycards before midnight? They look at their historical pacing. Agile velocity measures the exact number of story points an agile team successfully completes within a single iteration. Project managers utilize historical team velocity to mathematically predict the volume of work an agile team can complete in future iterations. If our grifters historically clear 30 story points an hour, I can confidently forecast exactly when they'll have all the keys.

Suddenly, disaster strikes! The casino owner decides to move the diamond shipment out an hour early. We have to shrink the timeline! We trigger schedule compression techniques, which aim to shorten the overall project duration without reducing the original project scope. We are still stealing everything, we just have to bend time. Option one: Crashing is a schedule compression technique involving adding additional resources to critical path activities. I hire a second safecracker to drill the hinges while the first drills the lock. Because I have to pay this mercenary double for the short notice, crashing a project schedule almost always results in increased total project costs. Option two: Fast-tracking is a schedule compression technique involving performing normally sequential activities in parallel. We start carrying bags to the van while the vault is still actively being drilled. But because we are moving blindly before things are finished, fast-tracking a project schedule often increases overall project risk due to the potential for rework—like dropping the bags and having to scramble to pick up loose cash in the hallway!

Wait, we have another crisis. Our master hacker is scheduled to disable the laser grid and loop the cameras at the exact same minute. He is only one man! We have to fix this resource overallocation. Resource leveling adjusts start and finish dates of activities to balance resource demand with available resource supply. We tell him to hack the cameras first, then do the lasers. But beware: applying resource leveling to a project schedule frequently alters the original critical path, and it usually pushes the final escape out by ten minutes. We can't afford that! So instead, we use resource smoothing, which adjusts activity dates exclusively within the mathematical limits of free float and total float. We only shift his secondary tasks into his downtime, never touching the critical path. Because of this strict limitation, resource smoothing guarantees that the original project completion date remains entirely unaffected.
