Slide that drink over here and listen to me very carefully, because you are looking at procurement all wrong. You’re looking at it like a boring paperwork exercise, when in reality, it is a high-stakes, international heist. You are the mastermind. You have a blueprint for the most complex piece of machinery on the planet—let’s say a global, deep-space satellite array. 

Before we even start turning wrenches, you have to look yourself in the mirror and do a make-or-buy analysis. This is where your master plan begins, because a make-or-buy analysis determines whether work should be accomplished by your own internal project team or if you need to go out and purchase it from outside sources. You realize your internal team is great at software, but they can't build a titanium thruster. You have to buy it. You need mercenaries.

Welcome to the Executing Process Group. The Project Management Body of Knowledge categorizes the 'Conduct Procurements' process entirely within this Executing Process Group. Why? Because we are taking action! The 'Conduct Procurements' process involves obtaining formal responses from prospective sellers, selecting a qualified seller to perform the project work, and then awarding a binding contract to a selected seller. 

But you don’t just walk out into the street and yell, "Who wants to build a thruster?!" You host a bidder conference. Picture a dimly lit warehouse filled with rival aerospace contractors glaring at each other. Bidder conferences ensure all prospective sellers share a clear understanding of the procurement requirements at the exact same time. It’s an ethical firewall; bidder conferences prevent any single prospective seller from receiving preferential informational treatment. No whispers in the hallway. No secret advantages.

When their proposals slide across your desk, you better not be guessing if their price is fair. You need your own guy on the inside giving you an independent estimate. These independent estimates serve as an objective benchmark to evaluate the reasonableness of proposed seller prices. If your baseline says it costs \$500,000 and some hotshot bids \$3 million, you immediately know someone is trying to take you for a ride.

But how do you pick the winner without letting your personal bias—or the fact that one vendor bought you a really nice steak—ruin the project? You lean on your procurement management plan, which defines the specific metrics used to evaluate a prospective seller's proposal. We use cold, hard source selection criteria to objectively evaluate prospective sellers during the Conduct Procurements process. To make it totally bulletproof, we use weighting systems. Weighting systems assign numerical values to multiple evaluation criteria, which provides an objective mathematical method to score vendor proposals. 

You aren't just scoring their price tag, though! Your proposal evaluation techniques include reviewing the management approach presented by the prospective seller—how are they going to run their crew?—and reviewing the technical approach presented by the prospective seller—what actual engineering wizardry are they using? All of this ruthless math leads to shortlisting, a process that narrows down the pool of potential vendors to those most capable of fulfilling the project requirements. 

Now, you have your vendor. It’s time for the sit-down. 

You go into negotiations, but do not go in acting like a mob boss trying to squeeze them for every penny. The primary goal of procurement negotiation is to reach a mutual agreement satisfying both the buyer and the seller. If you bankrupt them, they'll build you a garbage thruster. You want a 'win-win' negotiation approach that focuses on satisfying the underlying interests of both the buyer and the seller. 

In that room, you, the project manager, have a very specific job: the project manager clarifies project requirements and clarifies technical scope limits during procurement negotiations. You define the physics and the boundaries. But here is the massive plot twist—do not sign that paper! The project manager frequently lacks the legal authority to sign a binding contract on behalf of the organization. If you scribble your name on that dotted line, HR will have your head on a spike. Instead, the legal department typically holds the explicit authority to sign binding organizational contracts, or a designated contract manager typically holds the explicit authority to sign binding organizational contracts. Let the suits handle the ink.

Let's talk about that ink, because contracts are layered like an onion. At the top, you have the Master Services Agreement. A Master Services Agreement establishes the overarching legal terms and conditions between a buyer and a seller. It’s the grand treaty. Beneath that, you attach Statements of Work to the Master Services Agreement to define the specific scope for individual project engagements. And to make sure they don’t give you shoddy work, you slap down a Service Level Agreement. A Service Level Agreement defines the specific performance metrics expected from a service provider, and it defines the specific quality levels expected from a service provider. 

But here is where the blood pressure spikes: who pays when things go horribly wrong? That is determined by the contract type. 

If you are terrified of budget overruns, you use Fixed-Price contracts, which transfer the majority of the financial risk to the seller. If they mess up, they eat the cost. The tightest version of this is a firm-fixed-price contract, which explicitly defines the total price for a precisely defined product or service. You build it for \$1 million, and if it costs you \$2 million, sucks to be you! 
Sometimes you want to give them a little flexibility, so you use a Fixed-Price Incentive Fee contract. This explicitly includes a maximum price ceiling. However, there is a terrifying threshold in a Fixed-Price Incentive Fee contract called the Point of Total Assumption. The Point of Total Assumption is the exact cost point in a fixed-price incentive contract where the seller assumes responsibility for all additional costs. Once they hit that mathematical wall, the seller absorbs all costs above the negotiated price ceiling, and you can bet their priority will instantly shift from quality to pure survival.

On the flip side, what if the tech is so new nobody can predict the cost? You use Cost-Reimbursable contracts. But beware, cost-reimbursable contracts transfer the majority of the financial risk to the buyer—you! The most common is a Cost Plus Fixed Fee contract. Cost Plus Fixed Fee contracts reimburse the seller for all mutually allowable project costs, and they provide the seller with a predetermined fixed profit amount. The beauty of this is that the fixed profit amount in a Cost Plus Fixed Fee contract does not change with actual cost variations. They don't get richer just by spending more of your money. Alternatively, you could use a Cost Plus Award Fee contract, which bases the final profit on subjective performance evaluations conducted by the buyer. "Impress me, and I'll tip you well."

And what if you are running an agile software team, flying by the seat of your pants? Agile procurement approaches favor collaborative supplier relationships over rigid adversarial contracts. Time and material contracts share financial risk relatively evenly between the buyer and the seller. Because of this flexibility, a time and materials contract is frequently used to acquire staff augmentation in agile projects. But to keep them from treating you like an endless ATM, a time and materials contract often includes a 'not-to-exceed' clause to limit the buyer's maximum financial exposure. 
Agile contracts also often employ a fixed-price structure for small incremental deliverables—you buy a two-week sprint at a time. If the market shifts, dynamic scope contracts allow new requirements to replace old requirements within a fixed total budget. And if you realize you don't even need the rest of the software? Early cancellation clauses in agile contracts allow the buyer to terminate the agreement once sufficient business value is achieved. Bam. Clean break.

Alright, the ink is dry. Work begins. We now rip ourselves out of Executing and dive into the Monitoring and Controlling Process Group! The Project Management Body of Knowledge categorizes the 'Control Procurements' process within the Monitoring and Controlling Process Group. The whole 'Control Procurements' process involves monitoring ongoing contract performance. You are the watchtower now. Control Procurements ensures that both the buyer's and seller's performance meets the project's explicit legal requirements.

You keep them honest by running procurement performance reviews. These brutal little check-ins evaluate a seller's progress against the established statement of work, evaluate a seller's progress against the contract quality requirements, and evaluate a seller's progress against the established schedule baseline. 
You pull out your spreadsheets and run trend analysis. Trend analysis determines whether a vendor's performance is improving over time, or whether a vendor's performance is deteriorating over time. You overlay this with Earned Value Analysis, which compares the vendor's actual schedule performance against the expected schedule baseline, and compares the vendor's actual cost performance against the expected cost baseline. Are we behind? Are we bleeding cash? 

You don’t just look at spreadsheets, though. You go down to the factory floor and run inspections. Inspections verify that the seller's deliverables meet the specific standards outlined in the procurement contract. Based on what you see, vendor performance evaluations provide formal documented feedback to the seller regarding work execution. If they passed the test, your payment system ensures the seller receives compensation in accordance with the exact contract terms. Keep in mind, payments to the seller are typically tied to the verified completion of specific project deliverables. No completed thruster, no cash.

But here comes the chaos. Something always changes. You realize you need the thruster to be painted neon pink. 
Any modification to an existing contract must be formally documented in writing, and any modification to an existing contract must be approved by a legally authorized representative. You process this through the Contract Change Control System. The Contract Change Control System defines the formal process by which a procurement contract can be modified. It includes the specific paperwork required for authorizing contract changes, the tracking systems required for authorizing contract changes, and it dictates the approval levels required for authorizing contract changes.

But what if you accidentally caused a change? What if you forgot to give them the pink paint on time, so they had to buy their own and work overtime? That is a constructive change. Constructive changes occur when buyer actions unintentionally alter the seller's required scope of work. Worse, constructive changes occur when buyer inactions unintentionally alter the seller's required scope of work. 

When you and the vendor stare at each other and cannot agree on who pays for that mistake, you have a contested change. Contested changes occur when the buyer and seller cannot agree on compensation for modified work. Now you are in dangerous waters, and claims administration handles contested contractual changes. 
If claims administration fails, do not go to court! Court is a black hole of time and money. You turn to Alternative Dispute Resolution. Alternative Dispute Resolution includes the technique of mediation, and it includes the technique of arbitration. Mediation and arbitration are used to resolve contract disputes without formal litigation.

And if a literal meteor strikes their factory? That’s where force majeure saves you. Force majeure clauses relieve both contractual parties from legal liability in the event of unforeseen extraordinary circumstances. Unforeseen extraordinary circumstances triggering force majeure clauses include natural disasters and wars. The universe exploded; nobody gets sued.

Finally, the smoke clears. The thruster is built. The heist is a success. But you can't just walk away from the explosion in slow motion. The project manager must verify that all contract terms and conditions are satisfied before closing a procurement. 
You run procurement audits. Procurement audits review the entire procurement process from initial planning through final administration. You dissect the corpse of the project because procurement audits identify successes to improve future procurement processes within the performing organization, and they identify failures to improve future procurement processes within the performing organization. 

The last thing you do is hand over the goods. A delivery solution outlines the specific methodology for transferring the completed product to the end customer. Furthermore, a delivery solution outlines the specific sequence for transferring the completed product to the end customer. First we hand over the keys, then we migrate the data, then we pop the champagne. 

That’s how you run procurement, my friend. Not with paper pushers, but with surgical, tactical execution. Now finish your drink, you’ve got an exam to pass and a vendor to negotiate with.
