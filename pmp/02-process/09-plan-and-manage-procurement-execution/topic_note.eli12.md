When a team of game developers decides to make a massive new video game, they don't do everything themselves. They might not record their own sound effects or draw every single background tree. Instead, they team up with outside artists or musicians. The line where your own team stops and outside helpers begin is called procurement (which is a fancy word for buying the things you need). At the very beginning, you have to make a big choice. **Make-or-buy analysis** determines whether work should be accomplished by the internal project team or purchased from outside sources. But once you decide to buy, you have to figure out how to actually go out and shop for the right helpers!

## Conducting Procurements: The Execution Phase

In the official rulebook of project management, the Project Management Body of Knowledge categorizes the **'Conduct Procurements'** process within the Executing Process Group. This is the action phase where you actually get things done! 

The 'Conduct Procurements' process involves obtaining formal responses from prospective sellers. It also involves selecting a qualified seller to perform the project work, and finally awarding a binding contract to a selected seller. 

Before you pick a seller, you might invite all the possible helpers to a big meeting called a **bidder conference**. Imagine inviting five different pizza shops to your school gym to explain exactly how you want pizzas made for a huge party. Bidder conferences ensure all prospective sellers share a clear understanding of the procurement requirements. It's super important to be fair. Bidder conferences prevent any single prospective seller from receiving preferential informational treatment. Everyone hears the exact same rules and questions at the exact same time.

### The Physics of Proposal Evaluation

When the companies (like those pizza shops) send you their ideas and prices, you can't just guess who is best. You need a secret weapon. **Independent estimates** serve as an objective benchmark to evaluate the reasonableness of proposed seller prices. If you figured out beforehand that cheese and dough for 10 pizzas should cost \$100, and a shop asks for \$1,000, you know right away their price is way too high.

Your **procurement management plan** defines the specific metrics used to evaluate a prospective seller's proposal. To do this fairly, we use **source selection criteria**, which are used to objectively evaluate prospective sellers during the Conduct Procurements process. 

Sometimes it's hard not to pick your favorite shop just because you like their logo. To stop this, we use **weighting systems**. Weighting systems assign numerical values to multiple evaluation criteria. By doing this, weighting systems provide an objective mathematical method to score vendor proposals. 

Let's do a quick math example of a weighting system. Suppose we give "Taste" a weight of 5 points, and "Speed" a weight of 2 points.
Step 1. Give the pizza shop a score out of 10 for Taste (Let's say they score 8).
Step 2. Multiply the Taste score by its weight: 8 * 5 = 40.
Step 3. Give them a score out of 10 for Speed (Let's say they score 9).
Step 4. Multiply the Speed score by its weight: 9 * 2 = 18.
Step 5. Add the totals together: 40 + 18 = 58 total points!

But what exactly are we looking at when we score them? Proposal evaluation techniques tell us to look at a few specific things. Proposal evaluation techniques include reviewing the **management approach** presented by the prospective seller (how they will organize their workers) and reviewing the **technical approach** presented by the prospective seller (what kind of ovens or secret recipes they will use). 

After all this grading, a step called **shortlisting** narrows down the pool of potential vendors to those most capable of fulfilling the project requirements. You take the top scorers and move forward with them!

## The Art and Boundaries of Contract Negotiation

Once you have your shortlist, you sit down to talk details. The primary goal of procurement negotiation is to reach a mutual agreement satisfying both the buyer and the seller. We don't want anyone to feel tricked or cheated. Instead, a **"win-win" negotiation approach** focuses on satisfying the underlying interests of both the buyer and the seller.

During these talks, the project manager has a very specific job. The project manager clarifies project requirements during procurement negotiations. The project manager also clarifies technical scope limits during procurement negotiations. This means you tell them exactly what game levels they need to design or exactly how spicy the pepperoni should be.

But there is a very important rule: the project manager frequently lacks the legal authority to sign a binding contract on behalf of the organization. You might be the boss of the project, but you aren't the lawyer! Instead, the **legal department** typically holds the explicit authority to sign binding organizational contracts, or a designated **contract manager** typically holds the explicit authority to sign binding organizational contracts. 

### The Architecture of the Agreement

When companies work together, they use special papers to organize their rules:
1.  **Master Services Agreement (MSA):** A Master Services Agreement establishes the overarching legal terms and conditions between a buyer and a seller. Think of it like the basic rules for all the sports you play in gym class (like "no cheating" and "listen to the coach").
2.  **Statements of Work (SOW):** Statements of Work attached to a Master Services Agreement define the specific scope for individual project engagements. If the MSA is the gym class rules, the SOW is the exact rules for today's basketball game.
3.  **Service Level Agreement (SLA):** A Service Level Agreement defines the specific performance metrics expected from a service provider, and a Service Level Agreement defines the specific quality levels expected from a service provider. This guarantees the basketballs will be pumped full of air and the court will be swept.

## Financial Risk and Contract Typology

Different contracts handle money in different ways. Sometimes you take the risk (if things get too expensive, you pay), and sometimes the seller takes the risk. Think of "risk" like dropping your ice cream cone. Who has to pay to buy a new one?

### Fixed-Price Contracts (Seller Risk)
Fixed-price contracts transfer the majority of the financial risk to the seller. 
*   **Firm-fixed-price:** A firm-fixed-price contract explicitly defines the total price for a precisely defined product or service. If you offer a kid \$10 to mow your lawn, and it takes them 5 hours and breaks their mower, they still only get \$10. They took the risk!
*   **Fixed-Price Incentive Fee (FPIF):** Sometimes we give a little bonus if they do great, but we set a limit. A Fixed-Price Incentive Fee contract explicitly includes a maximum price ceiling. In a Fixed-Price Incentive Fee contract, the seller absorbs all costs above the negotiated price ceiling. 
*   **Point of Total Assumption (PTA):** The Point of Total Assumption is the cost point in a fixed-price incentive contract where the seller assumes responsibility for all additional costs. This is the exact dollar amount where the seller realizes, "Uh oh, every extra penny we spend now comes right out of my own piggy bank."

### Cost-Reimbursable Contracts (Buyer Risk)
Cost-reimbursable contracts transfer the majority of the financial risk to the buyer. This means you promise to pay the seller for all the materials they buy, plus a little extra for their hard work.
*   **Cost Plus Fixed Fee (CPFF):** Cost Plus Fixed Fee contracts reimburse the seller for all mutually allowable project costs, and provide the seller with a predetermined fixed profit amount. So, if they spend \$100 on wood for a treehouse, you pay them \$100 for the wood, plus a \$20 profit fee. The fixed profit amount in a Cost Plus Fixed Fee contract does not change with actual cost variations. If they accidentally spend \$200 on wood, they still only get the \$20 profit!
*   **Cost Plus Award Fee (CPAF):** A Cost Plus Award Fee contract bases the final profit on subjective performance evaluations conducted by the buyer. This means you decide how much bonus profit to give them based on if you feel like they did an awesome job.

### Time and Materials (Shared Risk)
Time and material contracts share financial risk relatively evenly between the buyer and the seller. You pay them an hourly allowance plus materials. But to make sure they don't just work super slow on purpose to get more allowance, a time and materials contract often includes a **'not-to-exceed' clause** to limit the buyer's maximum financial exposure. This puts a cap on the total allowance you will pay.

### Agile Procurement Strategies
Sometimes projects are "Agile," which means we change our plans a lot as we learn. Agile procurement approaches favor collaborative supplier relationships over rigid adversarial contracts. We want to be teammates, not enemies!
*   A time and materials contract is frequently used to acquire staff augmentation in agile projects (which means hiring extra helpers for a little while to boost your team).
*   Instead of one giant project, agile contracts often employ a fixed-price structure for small incremental deliverables (like paying for just one level of a video game at a time).
*   **Dynamic scope contracts** allow new requirements to replace old requirements within a fixed total budget. If you want a dragon in your game instead of a robot, you swap them out without changing the total price!
*   **Early cancellation clauses** in agile contracts allow the buyer to terminate the agreement once sufficient business value is achieved. If your video game is already super fun and ready to sell, you can stop paying for extra features you don't really need.

## Controlling Procurements: Ensuring Reality Matches the Contract

Once the project starts, you have to watch carefully to make sure the work is actually getting done right. The Project Management Body of Knowledge categorizes the **'Control Procurements'** process within the Monitoring and Controlling Process Group. The 'Control Procurements' process involves monitoring ongoing contract performance. Control Procurements ensures that both the buyer's and seller's performance meets the project's explicit legal requirements. It's like checking the scoreboard during a game to make sure everyone is playing by the rules.

How do you check? We use **procurement performance reviews**. Procurement performance reviews evaluate a seller's progress against the established statement of work, evaluate a seller's progress against the contract quality requirements, and evaluate a seller's progress against the established schedule baseline. 

We also look for patterns:
*   **Trend analysis** determines whether a vendor's performance is improving over time, and trend analysis determines whether a vendor's performance is deteriorating over time. Are they getting faster, or are they slowing down?
*   **Earned Value Analysis** compares the vendor's actual schedule performance against the expected schedule baseline. Earned Value Analysis also compares the vendor's actual cost performance against the expected cost baseline.

Let's show a quick math example for checking the schedule with Earned Value Analysis:
Step 1. Find the Expected Schedule Baseline (What you planned to happen). Let's say by today, they should have built 10 treehouse stairs.
Step 2. Find the Actual Schedule Performance (What they actually did). Let's say they only built 8 stairs.
Step 3. Subtract the Expected from the Actual to find the difference: 8 - 10 = -2.
Since the number is negative (-2), you know they are 2 stairs behind schedule!

Next, we check the actual items they made. **Inspections** verify that the seller's deliverables meet the specific standards outlined in the procurement contract. Do the stairs hold your weight? After looking, **vendor performance evaluations** provide formal documented feedback to the seller regarding work execution (like giving them a report card).

Finally, when they do a good job, they need to get paid! A **payment system** ensures the seller receives compensation in accordance with the exact contract terms. Payments to the seller are typically tied to the verified completion of specific project deliverables. If they finish the stairs, they get the stair money!

## Navigating Turbulence: Changes, Claims, and Disputes

Sometimes, things don't go as planned. If you need to change the rules of the project, you have to be super careful. Any modification to an existing contract must be formally documented in writing, and any modification to an existing contract must be approved by a legally authorized representative. 

We don't just change things randomly. The **Contract Change Control System** defines the formal process by which a procurement contract can be modified. The Contract Change Control System includes the specific paperwork required for authorizing contract changes, it includes the tracking systems required for authorizing contract changes, and it dictates the approval levels required for authorizing contract changes.

But sometimes accidents happen without paperwork. **Constructive changes** occur when buyer actions unintentionally alter the seller's required scope of work. Also, constructive changes occur when buyer inactions unintentionally alter the seller's required scope of work. For example, if you forgot to unlock the yard, the lawnmower kid has to wait around, and your "inaction" just made their job way harder!

When accidents like this happen, people might argue about money. **Contested changes** occur when the buyer and seller cannot agree on compensation for modified work. To fix this, **claims administration** handles contested contractual changes.

If you really can't agree, you don't want to go to a giant, expensive courtroom. Instead, we use **Alternative Dispute Resolution (ADR)**. Alternative Dispute Resolution includes the technique of mediation, and Alternative Dispute Resolution includes the technique of arbitration. Mediation and arbitration are used to resolve contract disputes without formal litigation. It's like having a wise teacher step in to settle a recess argument fairly without having to go to the principal's office.

Sometimes, massive surprises ruin the project, and it's nobody's fault. **Force majeure** clauses relieve both contractual parties from legal liability in the event of unforeseen extraordinary circumstances. Unforeseen extraordinary circumstances triggering force majeure clauses include natural disasters and wars. If a hurricane blows away the treehouse, the force majeure rule says neither side gets in trouble.

## Closing the Loop and Delivering the Solution

Before you can officially say the project is 100% finished, you have to do a final check. The project manager must verify that all contract terms and conditions are satisfied before closing a procurement. 

Once it's closed, smart teams like to look back and learn. **Procurement audits** review the entire procurement process from initial planning through final administration. Like reviewing game tape after a sports match, procurement audits identify successes to improve future procurement processes within the performing organization. Procurement audits also identify failures to improve future procurement processes within the performing organization.

Ultimately, the best part of the whole journey is handing over the awesome thing you built! A **delivery solution** outlines the specific methodology for transferring the completed product to the end customer. A delivery solution also outlines the specific sequence for transferring the completed product to the end customer. This means planning exactly how and when you will hand over the keys to the new treehouse or the download code for the new video game. 

By learning all these steps, you don't just buy things—you learn how to team up with people everywhere to build amazing stuff!
