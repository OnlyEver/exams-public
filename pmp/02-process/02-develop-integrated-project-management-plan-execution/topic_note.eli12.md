Building a giant Lego castle comes with a step-by-step instruction booklet. You know exactly how many pieces you need and what the castle will look like at the end. But imagine playing a video game like Minecraft, where you want to build a cool treehouse but have to explore the map to see what blocks you can find first. The goal is clear, but how you get there is a guessing game. 

As the leader of a big project (the project manager), your first job is to figure out what kind of game you are playing. Are you following a strict set of rules, or are you exploring into the unknown? The answer changes how you make your plan and get the job done.

## Choosing the Right Path: Project Execution Strategies

Before you can start handing out chores or spending money, you have to decide on the big-picture plan. A **project execution strategy** defines the overall approach a team will use to deliver project outcomes and business value. Think of it as deciding the rules of the game before you start playing. You can't just guess; a project manager must assess project complexity and organizational culture before recommending an execution strategy. (You wouldn't try to play a quiet game of chess in the middle of a loud, messy pizza party!)

To figure out the best strategy, we use a tool called the **Stacey Matrix**. The Stacey Matrix is a tool used to determine the appropriate execution strategy by mapping requirements uncertainty (how much we don't know about *what* we are building) against technical uncertainty (how much we don't know about *how* to build it). Based on this matrix, you will pick one of three paths:

1. **Predictive Strategy:** Imagine you are planning a massive school dance. You know exactly what night it is, what the budget is, and what decorations you need. A predictive project execution strategy requires defining all scope, time, and cost parameters *before* execution begins. You figure out everything first, then do the work.
2. **Agile Strategy:** Now imagine you are inventing a brand new flavor of soda. You aren't sure what people will like. An agile project execution strategy focuses on iterative delivery and frequent feedback to manage high levels of uncertainty. This means you make a little bit of the soda, let your friends taste it, and keep making small changes until it's perfect.
3. **Hybrid Strategy:** Sometimes you need a mix of both! A hybrid project execution strategy combines elements of both predictive and agile methodologies to suit specific project needs. For example, building the hard plastic shell of a video game console might use a predictive strategy, but creating the new video game software inside it might use an agile strategy.

## The Blueprint: Creating the Integrated Project Management Plan

Once you pick your strategy, you need a master rulebook. The **project management plan** is a comprehensive document detailing how the project will be executed, monitored, and closed. 

This isn't just a simple calendar on the wall. The project management plan integrates all **subsidiary management plans** and performance baselines into one giant package. **Subsidiary management plans** dictate how specific knowledge areas like scope, cost, and schedule will be handled throughout the project. It's like having a special mini-rulebook for what to do if you run out of money, and another mini-rulebook for what to do if a team member gets sick.

All these mini-rulebooks help build the most important measuring stick for your project: the **performance measurement baseline**. 

> **The Performance Measurement Baseline**
> A performance measurement baseline integrates the approved scope, schedule, and cost baselines. It is basically the ultimate scoreboard. The performance measurement baseline acts as the standard against which project execution is compared. If you want to know if you are winning or losing your project game, you look at this baseline.

Because this scoreboard is so important, the person paying for the project (the project sponsor) must formally approve the project management plan before baseline execution begins. Once they say "Yes," you host a big gathering called a **project kickoff meeting**. The project kickoff meeting formally communicates project objectives to stakeholders and signifies the start of the execution phase. It's the official starting whistle!

### The Agile Counterpart
If you are making something super new and uncertain, a giant printed rulebook might be useless by tomorrow. So, in agile projects, the overarching project plan frequently takes the form of a **prioritized product backlog** (a ranked wish list of everything you want to build) and a **high-level release plan** (a rough map of when you will hand over the finished pieces). 

## Calculating the Effort: Resource and Estimation Techniques

To make a great plan, you need to know what you need and how long it will take. **Resource estimation** involves identifying the type and quantity of materials, people, and equipment required for project activities. Do you need three painters, ten cans of blue paint, and two ladders?

To keep track of all this stuff, managers use a **resource breakdown structure**. A resource breakdown structure is a hierarchical representation of project resources categorized by resource type (like people vs. tools) and class (like beginners vs. experts). It looks like a family tree for your supplies.

Once you know *what* you need, how do you guess the time and money it will take? 

### Traditional Estimation Techniques

| Technique | How it Works | Accuracy vs. Effort |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Analogous Estimating** | Analogous estimating uses historical data from a similar past project to estimate duration or cost for a current project. (Like guessing it will take 2 hours to mow your neighbor's lawn because it took 2 hours to mow yours last week). | Quick, but analogous estimating is considered a less accurate estimation technique compared to bottom-up estimating. |
| **Parametric Estimating** | Parametric estimating uses statistical relationships between historical data and project variables to calculate an estimate. (For example, if you know one slice of pizza costs \$2, you can do the math to guess that 10 slices will cost \$20). | Very accurate if your starting numbers are good! |
| **Bottom-up Estimating** | Bottom-up estimating aggregates the detailed estimates of lower-level components within the work breakdown structure. (This means breaking a giant chore into tiny pieces, guessing the time for every single piece, and adding them all up). | Super accurate, but bottom-up estimating requires the greatest amount of time and effort to complete among all traditional estimation techniques. |

Sometimes guessing just one number is risky. What if everything goes wrong? What if everything goes perfectly? That is why we use **three-point estimating**. Three-point estimating uses optimistic (best case), pessimistic (worst case), and most likely (normal case) scenarios to calculate an expected duration or cost. 

There are two common ways to do the math. Here is how you calculate them step-by-step:

> **Triangular Distribution:** 
> Formula: (Optimistic + Most Likely + Pessimistic) / 3
> The triangular distribution formula for three-point estimating is the simple average of the optimistic, pessimistic, and most likely estimates.
> 
> **Step-by-Step Math Example:**
> Let's say your Optimistic guess is 2 days. Your Most Likely guess is 4 days. Your Pessimistic guess is 6 days.
> 1. Add the three numbers together: 2 + 4 + 6 = 12.
> 2. Divide that total by 3: 12 / 3 = 4.
> 3. Your triangular estimate is 4 days!
>
> **Beta (PERT) Distribution:** 
> Formula: (Optimistic + [4 x Most Likely] + Pessimistic) / 6
> The beta distribution formula for three-point estimating gives the most likely estimate a weight of four in its calculation. This means you count the "normal" guess four times so it has the biggest impact on the final answer, keeping the guess realistic.
> 
> **Step-by-Step Math Example:**
> Using the exact same numbers (Optimistic = 2, Most Likely = 4, Pessimistic = 6):
> 1. Multiply the Most Likely number by 4: 4 x 4 = 16.
> 2. Add the Optimistic number, your new Most Likely number, and the Pessimistic number together: 2 + 16 + 6 = 24.
> 3. Divide that total by 6: 24 / 6 = 4.
> 4. Your beta estimate is 4 days!

### Agile Estimation Techniques
In an agile project (like inventing a brand new video game), asking a computer programmer exactly how many hours a brand new idea will take is super difficult. Because of this, agile work effort is frequently estimated using **story points** instead of absolute time measurements. 

**Story points** represent a relative measure of effort, complexity, and risk associated with completing a single user story (a single feature of the project). It's like saying a dragon boss fight is an "8" in difficulty, while a simple jumping puzzle is just a "2". 

To figure out these points together as a team, they play a game! **Planning Poker** is a consensus-based agile estimation technique utilizing physical or virtual numbered cards. Everyone picks a card with a number secretly and reveals them all at once. If everyone picks a "5", great! If someone picks a "1" and someone else picks a "20", the team has to talk about why they disagree.

## Action and Adaptation: Executing and Maintaining the Plan

With a plan in place, the real action begins. **Direct and Manage Project Work** is the formal process of leading the work defined in the project management plan to achieve project objectives. It simply means being the team captain and getting the tasks done. **Work performance data** is a primary output of the Direct and Manage Project Work process. This is the raw information about what happened today—like "we laid 500 bricks" or "we spent \$50."

### Progressive Elaboration and Rolling Wave Planning
You rarely know everything on the very first day. **Progressive elaboration** is the iterative process of increasing the level of detail in a project plan as more information becomes available. It’s like drawing a sketch with a pencil, and then going back later to fill in the colors once you know exactly what they should be. 

**Rolling wave planning** is a specific technique of progressive elaboration. **Rolling wave planning** involves detailing near-term work comprehensively while keeping long-term work at a higher, less detailed level. Imagine driving a car at night. You can only see the next 50 feet perfectly in your headlights (near-term work), but you still know you are driving all the way to grandma's house (long-term work). 

In the agile world, things change even faster. **Agile methodologies** maintain the project plan continuously through routine sprint planning and backlog refinement ceremonies. This means the team gets together regularly (like every two weeks) to adjust their plans based on what they just learned.

### Change Control and Configuration Management
Things will always change. But, maintaining the project management plan requires formal processes to handle any deviations from the performance measurement baseline. You can't just secretly change the rules of the game!

The **Perform Integrated Change Control** process handles all formal requests to modify the project management plan. If someone wants to add a swimming pool to the house you are building, they have to ask permission first. A **change control board** is a formally chartered group responsible for reviewing and approving or rejecting project change requests. Think of them as a panel of judges. 

If the judges say "Yes," approved change requests frequently require direct updates to the integrated project management plan and baselines. You have to rewrite the rulebook to include the new changes!

Because the rulebook changes, you must use **configuration management**. Configuration management ensures that the project team is always referencing the correct and most current version of the project management plan. It stops people from accidentally following the old rules instead of the new ones.

### Closing the Loop
Finally, doing the work isn't just about finishing tasks; it is about getting smarter. A project manager must capture lessons learned during execution to continuously update organizational process assets and ongoing project plans. This just means writing down what went well and what went wrong, so that the next time your team does a project, you are even faster and wiser!
