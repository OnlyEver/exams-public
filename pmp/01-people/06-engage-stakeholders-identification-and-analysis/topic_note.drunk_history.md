Listen to me, slide that coaster over and pay attention, because what I’m about to tell you is the difference between surviving a project and waking up with a horse's head in your inbox. You think project management is just drawing little boxes on a Gantt chart? It’s not. It’s a heist. It’s a psychological thriller. And your survival depends entirely on understanding the people in the room, outside the room, and the people who just think they own the room. 

Let's get the absolute bedrock truth out of the way: project stakeholders are individuals, groups, or organizations that may affect a project decision, activity, or outcome. But that's only a third of the equation. You also have to realize that project stakeholders are individuals, groups, or organizations that may be affected by a project decision, activity, or outcome. They are the collateral damage of your grand plans. And here is where it gets truly psychotic: project stakeholders are individuals, groups, or organizations that *perceive* an impact from a project decision, activity, or outcome. Let that sink in. If Gladys in accounting merely hallucinated that your new \$5 million software rollout will steal her beloved stapler and ruin her legacy process, she is a stakeholder, and she will actively try to assassinate your project. Perception is reality in this game.

You can't wait until the building is on fire to figure out who holds the matches. That’s why the identification of project stakeholders begins during the project initiation phase. You start before the real work even begins. You look at your foundational documents, because a project charter is a key input to the Identify Stakeholders process. Why? Because the project charter identifies the project sponsor and initial key stakeholders. It’s the mob boss handing you the initial hit list of who to care about. And if you are bringing in outside mercenaries, procurement documents are an input to the Identify Stakeholders process when the project involves external vendors or contractors. 

But you can't just stop with the obvious names. You need a dragnet. Brainstorming and brain writing are data gathering techniques used to identify the initial list of project stakeholders. Brainstorming is you and the team shouting names in a room, but brain writing lets the quiet paranoid folks independently write down the threats before the loudmouths take over. Once you have a pile of names, you need to see the web they form. Mind mapping visually organizes information about stakeholders to identify relationships among different stakeholder groups. It’s exactly like a detective’s corkboard with pushpins and red string—you realize the compliance department is secretly married to the primary vendor, and suddenly everything makes sense. If you are dealing with a massive corporate populace, questionnaires and surveys gather quantitative and qualitative data regarding stakeholder expectations from large audiences. It's like dropping thousands of psychological leaflets from an airplane to see who shoots back. 

And don't you dare think you can do this once and walk away. This ecosystem breathes and mutates. Stakeholder identification is an iterative process requiring continuous updates throughout the entire project lifecycle. People get fired, people get promoted, allegiances shift. Especially when you pivot! Changes in project scope or schedule require a reassessment of the stakeholder register to identify new stakeholders or changes in stakeholder impact. You change the heist target from a bank to a casino mid-stride, you suddenly have to worry about the casino manager, not the bank teller. 

Now, you have a massive list of people, but you can’t treat them all equally or you’ll burn out in a week. You must profile your marks. Stakeholder analysis involves gathering and evaluating quantitative and qualitative information to determine whose interests should be prioritized. We do this by throwing them onto a Power/Interest grid, which is a glorious little tool that groups stakeholders based on stakeholder authority levels and stakeholder concern levels regarding project outcomes. Picture this: who has the guns (power) and who actually cares about the loot (interest)? 

If you have stakeholders with high power and high interest, they require a manage-closely engagement strategy. This is your volatile getaway driver. You hold their hand, you text them back immediately. Stakeholders with high power and low interest require a keep-satisfied engagement strategy. This is the sleeping dragon—the CEO who can cancel your project with a pen stroke but doesn't care about the daily mechanics. Give them their executive summaries, keep them happy, and do not wake them up with trivial nonsense! Conversely, stakeholders with low power and high interest require a keep-informed engagement strategy. These are the nosy neighbors or dependent teams. They can't fire you, but if you don't communicate proactively, they will panic and create a PR nightmare. Finally, stakeholders with low power and low interest require a monitor-only engagement strategy. Just watch them from across the street.

When the corporate politics get completely Byzantine, the simple grid isn't enough, so we upgrade to the Salience Model. The Salience Model categorizes project stakeholders based on power, urgency, and legitimacy. Think of it like triage at an emergency room or a velvet rope at a nightclub. In the Salience Model, power is the ability of the stakeholder to impose a specific will or outcome on the project—can they force you to do something? In the Salience Model, urgency is the degree to which a stakeholder claim requires immediate attention—is their hair on fire? And in the Salience Model, legitimacy refers to the legal or moral appropriateness of the stakeholder involvement in the project—do they actually have a right to be screaming at you? 

You can also look at the battlefield geographically. Directions of influence classify stakeholders based on hierarchical or functional position relative to the project manager. Upward influence refers to stakeholders such as senior management, the project sponsor, or the steering committee—the gods living above you. Downward influence refers to stakeholders such as the project team or specialists contributing to the project—the troops in your trenches. Outward influence refers to stakeholders outside the project team such as suppliers, government departments, or the public—the civilian casualties. And sideward influence refers to stakeholders such as project manager peers sharing resources or information—the other captains you are fighting with for budget scraps. If you want to melt your brain and do 3D chess, you use a Stakeholder Cube. A Stakeholder Cube is a three-dimensional model used to map stakeholders based on power, interest, and attitude, giving you the total volume of their political weight. 

Every single bit of this terrifying intelligence goes into one place. The primary output of the Identify Stakeholders process is the Stakeholder Register. This is your Burn Book. The Stakeholder Register records stakeholder identification details such as name, organizational position, and role in the project. But it goes deeper. The Stakeholder Register records stakeholder assessment information such as major requirements and expectations. And crucially, the Stakeholder Register records stakeholder classifications based on influence and impact. Now listen to me very carefully: because you are writing down that the VP of Marketing is a "high-power, low-interest obstacle," the stakeholder register contains sensitive information requiring the project manager to exercise discretion regarding document access. You do not leave the Burn Book on the conference room table! 

Next, you need to play reality TV producer and map their emotional arcs. We use the Stakeholder Engagement Assessment Matrix. The Stakeholder Engagement Assessment Matrix compares current stakeholder engagement levels with desired engagement levels required for successful project delivery. You look at where they are, versus where you need them to be, categorizing them into five distinct psychological states. An Unaware stakeholder in the engagement matrix is oblivious to the project and potential project impacts—the husband watching football while the kitchen is on fire. A Resistant stakeholder in the engagement matrix is aware of the project and opposed to the changes or outcomes the project will bring—the mother-in-law actively trying to sabotage the wedding. A Neutral stakeholder in the engagement matrix is aware of the project and neither supportive nor unsupportive of the project—the bored teenager. A Supportive stakeholder in the engagement matrix is aware of the project and in favor of the project outcomes—the loyal bridesmaid. And finally, a Leading stakeholder in the engagement matrix is aware of the project and actively engaged in ensuring project success—that’s the manic wedding planner. 

If you find a massive gap between current and desired states, you have to act. Highly influential stakeholders with negative attitudes toward the project require immediate and targeted communication strategies to mitigate resistance. You are defusing a bomb; you don't just send an automated newsletter! Which brings us to the actual transmission of data. Project managers must tailor communication methods and frequencies based on the results of the stakeholder analysis. Tailoring communication involves adjusting the format, language, and medium to match the preferences and cultural backgrounds of specific stakeholders. You speak raw data to the engineers, and you speak glossy visualizations to the executives. 

You have three weapons here. Interactive communication is a multi-directional exchange of information such as meetings, phone calls, or video conferences. It’s a screaming match or a therapy session, designed to build consensus. Then there is push communication. Push communication involves sending information directly to specific recipients who need the information. Push communication methods include emails, memos, and project reports. It’s firing a t-shirt cannon of facts into the crowd—you know you shot it, but you don't know if they caught it. Finally, pull communication requires stakeholders to access project information at the stakeholder's own discretion. Pull communication methods include intranet sites, shared network drives, and knowledge repositories. It’s laying out a massive buffet and telling everyone to eat when they get hungry. And you better keep adjusting the menu, because the Communications Management Plan and the Stakeholder Engagement Plan are iteratively updated to reflect changing stakeholder needs.

Now, take everything I just said, and throw it into a chaotic, screaming kitchen. That’s Agile. In agile environments, stakeholder identification and engagement are continuous processes driven by frequent collaboration. You aren’t relying on static, dusty registers. Agile projects favor transparent, frequent, and interactive communication methods over formal push communication. No one wants your 50-page memo; they want to see the working software. Agile frameworks utilize mechanisms like sprint reviews to directly engage stakeholders. Instead of guessing what the customer wants, agile frameworks utilize sprint reviews to gather immediate feedback from stakeholders on project deliverables. You put the meal in front of the food critic right now and watch their face! To make sure they are always cooking for the right palate, agile teams often create stakeholder personas to represent the needs, goals, and characteristics of different user groups. They literally invent imaginary friends to argue with during development so they never lose sight of the end-user.

But out there in the real world? You're usually stuck in the purgatory between the two. Hybrid projects combine formal stakeholder documentation with frequent, iterative stakeholder feedback loops. You are wearing a corporate tuxedo, but you are doing agile parkour. You keep your Stakeholder Register locked in your desk to satisfy the PMO, but you drag the clients into sprint reviews every two weeks to make sure they aren't perceiving an impact that’s going to derail your life. Identify them, map their power, lock up the Burn Book, and talk to them in the language they understand, or they will bury you! Now, finish your drink, we’ve got a project to plan.
