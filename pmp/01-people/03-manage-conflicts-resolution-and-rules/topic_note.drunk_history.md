When the sirens start wailing, you do not sit around and wait. Project managers must address conflicts early in the project lifecycle before a minor disagreement becomes a blood feud. Conflict resolution should focus on the present rather than past events. We are surviving today!

Now, how do you actually defuse the bomb? The PMBOK Guide recognizes five general techniques for resolving project conflict. These aren't just random guesses; they are surgically extracted from behavioral science where the Thomas-Kilmann Conflict Mode Instrument defines five specific conflict resolution strategies. You are the mastermind. You have to pick the exact right tool for the exact level of chaos:

First, you have the holy grail. The collaborating conflict resolution strategy involves incorporating multiple viewpoints to reach a consensus. The hacker and the muscle map out a completely new wiring grid so both the data stream and the gold vault pop open simultaneously. Because it fixes the root cause, the collaborating conflict resolution strategy is frequently referred to as problem-solving. It takes time, but it’s glorious because the collaborating conflict resolution strategy leads to a win-win outcome.

But what if the cops are three blocks away and you don't have time to engineer a miracle? You pivot. The compromising conflict resolution strategy requires all conflicting parties to concede something. The hacker gets half the data, the muscle gets half the gold. Nobody is thrilled, but nobody is empty-handed. The compromising conflict resolution strategy results in partial satisfaction for all involved parties. 

Now, what if the SWAT team is actively kicking down the door? You don't ask for opinions! You rip the USB drive out of the hacker's hand, toss it to the muscle, and scream, "Gold only, we're leaving!" The forcing conflict resolution strategy involves pushing a single viewpoint at the expense of other viewpoints. You just broke the hacker's heart, but the forcing conflict resolution strategy is typically used when an emergency requires an immediate decision. It's brutal, and the forcing conflict resolution strategy leads to a win-lose outcome, but you all live to steal another day.

Let's say the stakes are lower. They are arguing over what radio station to play in the getaway van. You use the Jedi mind trick. The smoothing conflict resolution strategy emphasizes areas of agreement rather than areas of difference. "Hey, guys, we all love 80s synth-pop, right?" The smoothing conflict resolution strategy is alternatively known as the accommodating strategy. You intentionally surrender your own preference to let the driver pick the music. Why? Because the smoothing conflict resolution strategy maintains team harmony by conceding one position to the needs of others. You suffer through a terrible playlist, but the smoothing conflict resolution strategy results in a lose-win outcome, and the driver stays loyal to you.

Finally, there is the tactical retreat. The withdrawing conflict resolution strategy involves retreating from an actual or potential conflict situation. You see two devs looking like they are about to throw monitors at each other, and you literally step out to get coffee. It’s not cowardice! The withdrawing conflict resolution strategy is alternatively known as the avoiding strategy. By stepping back, the withdrawing conflict resolution strategy postpones a conflict issue to allow parties to be better prepared or for their adrenaline to drop.

But listen to me! *Taps the bar violently.* You wouldn't be playing peacemaker so often if you had built the guardrails on day one! I am talking about establishing the laws of physics for your team. Ground rules establish clear expectations regarding acceptable behavior by project team members. But you cannot just dictate them like a dictator. Team members must collaboratively develop the project team ground rules. Why? Because human psychology dictates that people will fiercely defend the laws they helped write! Collaborative development of ground rules increases team commitment to the ground rules. 

Once agreed upon, you carve them into stone. Ground rules are formally documented in the project team charter. The project team charter is an agreement outlining team values and working guidelines. It is the constitution of your heist! When enforced, clear ground rules significantly decrease the risk of interpersonal conflict. 

You must establish dominion immediately. The project manager must review ground rules during project kickoff meetings to ensure stakeholder alignment. But a project is a living beast. The project manager must periodically review ground rules to ensure continued relevance to the project environment. And the rules aren't just for the crew in the van. If the shadowy mob boss funding the operation drops by, he has to wipe his boots at the door! External stakeholders interacting with the project team must adhere to the established ground rules. 

What happens when someone goes rogue? The getaway driver starts vaping in the server room? The project manager and the project team share responsibility for enforcing ground rules. If it happens again, the project manager should address repeated ground rule violations through private feedback. Just you and the driver in the alleyway. If he still refuses to comply? We escalate through corporate channels. Unresolved ground rule violations must be escalated according to the formal resource management plan.

Now, take everything I just said and throw it into a blender, because if you are running an Agile project, the entire paradigm flips! Agile methodologies view conflict as an opportunity for continuous improvement. The friction is the fuel! Instead of a massive, heavy charter, agile teams document conflict resolution approaches in team working agreements. They don't let tension hide in the shadows. Agile teams use daily standup meetings to quickly identify emerging conflicts. Every morning: "What did you build, what are you building, and who is standing in your way?" Bam! Friction surfaced.

In Agile, you don't play the overbearing parent. Agile teams are expected to self-manage minor ground rule violations. They police their own ranks! A Scrum Master facilitates conflict resolution only when an agile team cannot resolve the conflict internally. 

But when they fail to resolve it... oh, the horror. The descent into madness is measurable! The Speed Leas conflict model defines five distinct levels of team conflict. You must diagnose exactly which circle of hell your team is currently occupying. 
It starts peacefully. Level one of the Speed Leas conflict model is characterized by collaborative problem solving. They are talking about the facts, trying to fix the code together.
Then egos get bruised. Level two of the Speed Leas conflict model is characterized by active disagreement. They stop sharing information and start protecting their own departments.
Then it becomes a sport. Level three of the Speed Leas conflict model is characterized by a contest. They don't even care about the code anymore; they just want to win the argument. The front-end team vs the back-end team.
If you don't stop it, it becomes ideological! Level four of the Speed Leas conflict model is characterized by a crusade. They want the other side completely removed from the project! They are drafting emails to HR!
And finally, the nuclear winter. Level five of the Speed Leas conflict model is characterized by world war. They will intentionally crash the entire multi-million dollar project just to assure mutual destruction!

Your sole purpose as an Agile leader at that point is extreme de-escalation. Agile coaches aim to de-escalate conflicts down to level one of the Speed Leas conflict model. You drag them kicking and screaming away from their crusades and world wars, forcing them to look at the objective reality of the project schedule, the priority, and the resources, until they are back to collaborative problem solving.
