Whenever people work together on a big project—like a group science presentation or building a fort in the backyard—disagreements are going to happen. Just like rubbing your hands together creates heat and friction, **conflict is an inevitable consequence of organizational interactions.** But friction isn't always a bad thing; after all, friction is what helps your sneakers grip the basketball court so you don't slip! When people argue respectfully, **constructive conflict can lead to better decision-making and innovation within a project team.** 

Sometimes, looking at the raw facts can solve an argument instantly. Let's say your group has a total budget of \$20 for science fair supplies, and two teammates are fighting over what to buy. We can use math to solve this conflict peacefully!
Step 1: Write down the total budget (\$20).
Step 2: Add up the cost of what Teammate A wants (glue for \$5) and what Teammate B wants (special markers for \$10). 5 + 10 = 15.
Step 3: Subtract the total needed from your budget (20 - 15 = 5).
Step 4: Notice that you have \$5 left over! Because you did the math, you see that you can afford both items, and the argument is solved.

## The Anatomy of Project Conflict

Why do teammates fight? You might think it is just because someone is acting annoying, but usually, the real reason is about the project itself. If we look at the history of group projects, we see a clear pattern of what causes the most trouble:

1.  **Project schedules are historically the most frequent source of conflict in project management.** This is like arguing over whose turn it is to use the video game console before bedtime because time is running out.
2.  **Project priorities are historically the second most frequent source of conflict in project management.** This happens when people argue over what is most important, like choosing whether to finish your homework first or do your chores.
3.  **Human resources are historically the third most frequent source of conflict in project management.** This is like two different baseball teams fighting because they both want the exact same star pitcher.
4.  **Personality differences are historically the least frequent source of conflict in project management.** It is actually really rare for people to fight just because their personalities don't mix!

Because the true cause is usually time or resources, **conflict resolution should focus on project issues rather than individual personalities.** Never attack the person; fix the problem! 

To keep things from getting worse, **project managers must address conflicts early in the project lifecycle.** When a fight does happen, **project managers must address conflicts in private whenever possible**, so nobody gets embarrassed in front of the whole group. Also, **project managers must address conflicts using a direct and collaborative approach**, meaning they speak honestly and work together. Finally, **conflict resolution should focus on the present rather than past events.** Don't bring up who stole whose pencil last week! Focus on right now.

## The Five Strategies for Conflict Resolution

Did you know there is actually a rulebook for how to handle disagreements? **The PMBOK Guide recognizes five general techniques for resolving project conflict.** These come from a special tool called the Thomas-Kilmann Conflict Mode Instrument. **The Thomas-Kilmann Conflict Mode Instrument defines five specific conflict resolution strategies.** You need to know what they are and when to use them!

| Strategy | Synonyms | Outcome | Mechanics & Application |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Collaborating** | Problem-Solving | Win-Win | **The collaborating conflict resolution strategy is frequently referred to as problem-solving.** How does it work? **The collaborating conflict resolution strategy involves incorporating multiple viewpoints to reach a consensus.** Because everyone works together to find the best answer, **the collaborating conflict resolution strategy leads to a win-win outcome.** |
| **Compromising** | Reconciling | Partial Satisfaction | **The compromising conflict resolution strategy requires all conflicting parties to concede something.** This means everyone gives up a little bit of what they want. Think of splitting the last slice of pizza right down the middle (1/2 for you, 1/2 for your friend). **The compromising conflict resolution strategy results in partial satisfaction for all involved parties.** |
| **Forcing** | Directing | Win-Lose | **The forcing conflict resolution strategy involves pushing a single viewpoint at the expense of other viewpoints.** This is like a parent yelling "Stop!" when you run toward a busy street. It isn't fun, but **the forcing conflict resolution strategy is typically used when an emergency requires an immediate decision.** Because one person gets their way and the other doesn't, **the forcing conflict resolution strategy leads to a win-lose outcome.** |
| **Smoothing** | Accommodating | Lose-Win | **The smoothing conflict resolution strategy is alternatively known as the accommodating strategy.** With this choice, **the smoothing conflict resolution strategy emphasizes areas of agreement rather than areas of difference.** Basically, **the smoothing conflict resolution strategy maintains team harmony by conceding one position to the needs of others.** You let your friend pick the movie just to be nice. Because you give in, **the smoothing conflict resolution strategy results in a lose-win outcome.** |
| **Withdrawing** | Avoiding | No immediate resolution | **The withdrawing conflict resolution strategy is alternatively known as the avoiding strategy.** Sometimes you just need to walk away! **The withdrawing conflict resolution strategy involves retreating from an actual or potential conflict situation.** This is smart because **the withdrawing conflict resolution strategy postpones a conflict issue to allow parties to be better prepared** or to cool down. |

## The Speed Leas Conflict Model

Arguments aren't just "on" or "off." They can grow step-by-step from a tiny disagreement to a massive fight. To measure this, **the Speed Leas conflict model defines five distinct levels of team conflict.** 

*   **Level 1:** **Level one of the Speed Leas conflict model is characterized by collaborative problem solving.** People are friendly and just trying to fix a puzzle together.
*   **Level 2:** **Level two of the Speed Leas conflict model is characterized by active disagreement.** People start getting annoyed and protective of their own ideas.
*   **Level 3:** **Level three of the Speed Leas conflict model is characterized by a contest.** Now, people don't care about fixing the puzzle; they just want to win the argument.
*   **Level 4:** **Level four of the Speed Leas conflict model is characterized by a crusade.** People start thinking their side is perfectly good and the other side is totally bad.
*   **Level 5:** **Level five of the Speed Leas conflict model is characterized by world war.** The only goal left is to completely destroy the other side, even if the whole project gets ruined in the process.

When things get heated, it is time for a coach to step in. **Agile coaches aim to de-escalate conflicts down to level one of the Speed Leas conflict model.** They want to calm everyone down so they can go back to happily solving problems together.

## Architecting the Environment: Ground Rules

The best way to stop a fight is to prevent it before it even starts. We do this by setting up the rules of the game first. **Ground rules establish clear expectations regarding acceptable behavior by project team members.**

Instead of the teacher or boss making all the rules alone, **team members must collaboratively develop the project team ground rules.** Why? Because when you help build something, you want to protect it. **Collaborative development of ground rules increases team commitment to the ground rules.** 

Once everyone agrees on the rules, **ground rules are formally documented in the project team charter.** You can think of the charter like a club's official rulebook. **The project team charter is an agreement outlining team values and working guidelines.** When everyone knows exactly how to act, **clear ground rules significantly decrease the risk of interpersonal conflict.**

### Enforcement and the Project Lifecycle

Rules are totally useless if everyone forgets them. That's why **the project manager must review ground rules during project kickoff meetings to ensure stakeholder alignment.** As the project keeps going, things might change, so **the project manager must periodically review ground rules to ensure continued relevance to the project environment.** 

Even guests have to follow your club's rules! **External stakeholders interacting with the project team must adhere to the established ground rules.** 

If a rule is broken, who acts like the referee? **The project manager and the project team share responsibility for enforcing ground rules.** If someone keeps acting out, **the project manager should address repeated ground rule violations through private feedback.** Just pull them aside for a quiet chat. If they *still* won't listen and keep messing up the project, **unresolved ground rule violations must be escalated according to the formal resource management plan.** This is like taking a problem to the principal if it can't be solved in the classroom.

## Predictive vs. Agile Conflict Management

Different types of teams handle fights in different ways. Some teams work in a very strict, step-by-step way. But "Agile" teams—which move fast and change plans easily—look at fights in a highly positive way. **Agile methodologies view conflict as an opportunity for continuous improvement.** They think arguments help them learn!

To make sure everyone plays nice, **agile teams document conflict resolution approaches in team working agreements.** Because Agile teams love talking to each other constantly, **agile teams use daily standup meetings to quickly identify emerging conflicts** before they turn into huge problems. 

If a tiny argument happens, the boss doesn't jump in right away. **Agile teams are expected to self-manage minor ground rule violations.** They fix it themselves! A special team leader called a Scrum Master just watches and waits. **A Scrum Master facilitates conflict resolution only when an agile team cannot resolve the conflict internally.**

### The Bottom Line for the PMP

As a project manager, your job isn't to pretend fights will never happen. Disagreements over schedules and toys are going to happen! Your job is to set up amazing ground rules. That way, when fights do happen, they stay focused on fixing the problem instead of being mean to people. You will figure out how bad the fight is, pick the perfect Thomas-Kilmann strategy to calm it down, and safely guide your whole team back to solving problems together.
