Imagine building the perfect treehouse exactly to the measurements in a book, only to find out your friends wanted an underground fort, your parents expected a neat little shed, and your neighbors are angry about the noise. The boards of the treehouse might be nailed together flawlessly, but the project is still a huge failure. In managing a project, success isn't just about how well a thing is built. Success is about making sure what you build matches what people expect. To be a great project manager, you have to be really good at understanding what everyone wants.

People's expectations are all very different. What your parents want is different from what your friends want. If you don't figure out these hopes and rules early on, it's like trying to play a video game blindfolded! 

## The Anatomy of a Stakeholder

Before we can manage what people want, we have to figure out who these people are. Anyone who cares about your project is called a "stakeholder." We usually split them into two main teams: people inside your group and people outside your group.

**Internal customers are individuals or groups within the performing organization.** This means the people on your own team or in your own school, like the teacher giving you a grade or the friends helping you build the project. On the flip side, **external customers are individuals or groups outside the performing organization.** These are people totally outside your group, like the person who buys a product or the cashier at the craft store. 

When figuring out who these people are, a project manager has to dig deep to find out what they really care about. **Project managers identify stakeholder expectations through direct interviews.** This means sitting down and talking to someone one-on-one, like asking your mom exactly how she wants her car washed. Other times, **project managers identify stakeholder expectations through focus groups.** This means bringing a bunch of people together in a room to talk about ideas, like asking your whole gaming squad what game you should all buy next.

Finding out what people really want takes a lot of focus. **Active listening is an interpersonal skill used by project managers to fully understand stakeholder expectations.** This means really paying attention to the person talking, not just waiting for your turn to speak, so you can understand their true feelings.

Once you know everyone's expectations, you have to write them down. **A stakeholder register documents the identification, assessment, and classification of project stakeholders.** Think of this like a giant master list for a birthday party that shows everyone you invited, how you know them, and exactly what kind of cake they like!

## Mapping the Ecosystem: Stakeholder Classification

You only have so much time and energy. If you try to talk to every single person every single day, you will run out of time and fail the people who matter most. So, we have to sort our stakeholders into groups.

### The Power/Interest Grid
The easiest way to sort people is by using a simple chart. **The Power/Interest grid categorizes stakeholders based on the stakeholder's level of authority and the stakeholder's level of concern regarding project outcomes.** "Authority" is their power (like a principal), and "concern" is their interest (how much they care). If someone has a lot of power and cares a lot about your science fair project, you need to pay very close attention to them!

### The Salience Model
Sometimes a simple chart isn't enough for complicated projects. **The Salience Model classifies stakeholders based on power, urgency, and legitimacy.** Let's look at what those big words mean:
1. **Power:** **In the Salience Model, power represents a stakeholder's ability to influence the project.** Can they shut your project down? Can they force you to change your design?
2. **Urgency:** **In the Salience Model, urgency represents the need for immediate attention to a stakeholder's claim.** Is this an emergency right now, or can their problem wait until tomorrow?
3. **Legitimacy:** **In the Salience Model, legitimacy represents the appropriateness of a stakeholder's involvement in the project.** Does this person actually have a good reason to be involved, or are they just a random kid trying to boss your team around?

### Directions of Influence
We can also group stakeholders by where they stand compared to you, the project manager. **Directions of influence classify stakeholders as upward, downward, outward, or sideward.**
*   **Upward influence refers to stakeholders such as senior management or project sponsors.** These are the adults in charge, like parents or the school principal.
*   **Downward influence refers to stakeholders such as the project team or temporary specialists.** These are the people working under your direction, like the helpers on your group project.
*   **Outward influence refers to stakeholders outside the project team such as suppliers or the public.** These are people completely outside your bubble, like the people who live in your neighborhood.
*   **Sideward influence refers to peers of the project manager.** These are people on your same level, like other kids in your grade who are also leading their own projects.

## The Architecture of Engagement

Knowing who everyone is is only half the battle. Now we have to plan exactly how we will work with them. This plan is built using two different guides.

**The Stakeholder Engagement Plan defines the strategies and actions required to promote productive involvement of stakeholders.** This asks: *How do we get a grumpy teammate to actually want to help us?*

**The Communication Management Plan details the specific methods and frequency for distributing information to stakeholders.** This is the specific schedule of how you talk. It asks: *Should I text my teammate every day, or just give them one big phone call on Friday?*

### Anchoring Expectations to Deliverables
As a project gets built, people sometimes forget what they asked for. Someone might look at your project and say, "This isn't what I wanted!" To stop this, we use a tool called the RTM. **The Requirements Traceability Matrix links product requirements from their origin to the deliverables that satisfy those requirements.** It is basically a checklist that matches "You asked for extra cheese" directly to "Here is the extra cheese on your pizza." Because of this, **the Requirements Traceability Matrix ensures that project outcomes align with documented customer expectations.**

Of course, people will still argue sometimes. **Conflict resolution techniques are applied when internal and external stakeholder expectations contradict each other.** This is how you solve arguments—like if your internal project team wants to spend your whole allowance on fancy markers, but the teacher (an external stakeholder) expects you to buy poster board too!

## The Spectrum of Delivery: Predictive, Agile, and Hybrid Approaches

How we handle changes depends on the style of project we are running. 

In really strict projects, things don't change easily. **Predictive projects use formal change control processes to manage modifications to stakeholder expectations.** This means if a stakeholder wants a change, they have to write it out, get it studied, and get official permission before you can do it.

Other projects are much more flexible. **Agile projects align stakeholder expectations through frequent delivery of working software increments.** Instead of making you wait a whole year to play a video game, agile teams give you a small, working piece of the game every few weeks! To show off this work, **agile projects utilize sprint reviews to demonstrate working increments directly to stakeholders.** It's like inviting your friends over to play-test a new level you just finished. 

To keep everyone informed in these fast projects, teams use giant charts. **Information radiators display continuous, updated project metrics to stakeholders in agile environments.** Think of a huge scoreboard at a basketball game. **Information radiators manage stakeholder expectations by providing transparent access to project progress**, which completely removes the mystery of what the team is doing.

Many projects today use a mix of both styles. **A hybrid project approach combines predictive formal documentation with agile frequent feedback loops to manage stakeholder expectations.** You might use strict rules for building the hard plastic shell of a robot, but use quick, flexible software tests to program the robot's brain.

## Measuring the Invisible: Monitoring and Responding

What people expect can change over time. Because of this, project managers have to constantly check in on their stakeholders and respond if things go wrong.

### The Stakeholder Engagement Assessment Matrix (SEAM)
To keep track of how involved people are, project managers use a special chart. **The Stakeholder Engagement Assessment Matrix compares the current engagement levels of stakeholders with the desired engagement levels.** It shows where a person is right now versus where we want them to be.

> **The Five States of the SEAM:**
> **The Stakeholder Engagement Assessment Matrix defines five specific states of stakeholder engagement:**
> 1. **Unaware:** **The Unaware state in the Stakeholder Engagement Assessment Matrix indicates a stakeholder has no knowledge of the project.** (They don't even know you are building a treehouse).
> 2. **Resistant:** **The Resistant state in the Stakeholder Engagement Assessment Matrix indicates a stakeholder is aware of the project and opposes the changes the project will bring.** (Your little brother knows about the treehouse and hates it because it blocks his swing).
> 3. **Neutral:** **The Neutral state in the Stakeholder Engagement Assessment Matrix indicates a stakeholder is aware of the project and neither supports nor opposes the project.** (They know about it, but they don't care either way).
> 4. **Supportive:** **The Supportive state in the Stakeholder Engagement Assessment Matrix indicates a stakeholder is aware of the project and desires the project to succeed.** (Your mom wants the treehouse to be safe and awesome).
> 5. **Leading:** **The Leading state in the Stakeholder Engagement Assessment Matrix indicates a stakeholder is aware of the project and is actively engaged in ensuring the project's success.** (Your best friend is literally helping you hammer the nails).

The golden rule here is to take action. **Project managers adjust engagement strategies when a stakeholder's actual engagement level falls below the desired engagement level.** If you need someone to be "Leading" but they are currently "Neutral," you have to change your plan to get them excited again!

### Tracking Deviations and Satisfaction
Day to day, little problems will pop up. **An issue log documents and tracks problems that currently affect stakeholder expectations.** It is simply a notebook where you write down every glitch or problem so you don't forget to fix it.

To figure out if our project is mathematically off-track, we look at the difference between what we planned and what actually happened. **Variance analysis compares actual project results to planned expectations.** 

Here is exactly how you use math to figure out a budget variance:
1. Identify your planned expectation. (Example: You expected to spend \$15 on supplies).
2. Identify your actual project result. (Example: You actually spent \$20 at the store).
3. Set up your math formula: Actual Result - Planned Expectation = Variance.
4. Do the simple math: 20 - 15 = 5.
5. See what the answer means. Your variance is \$5. This tells you that you went \$5 over your budget!

Because it works so well, **project managers use variance analysis to identify deviations from customer expectations** before those tiny differences turn into giant disasters.

Finally, we have to ask people if they are happy. **Project managers use surveys and questionnaires to measure customer satisfaction quantitatively.** This means using math and numbers, like asking someone to give a 5-star rating. 

When asking outside customers, one metric is super famous. **Net Promoter Score is a metric used to measure external customer satisfaction and loyalty.** It asks one simple question: "On a scale of 0 to 10, how likely are you to recommend our product to a friend?" It gives you a crystal-clear number showing if people loved your project.

In the end, managing stakeholder expectations is about making sure people's hopes match reality. By figuring out who people are, putting them in the right categories, and constantly checking in, you make sure that when your project is finally done, it is exactly what everyone was waiting for.
