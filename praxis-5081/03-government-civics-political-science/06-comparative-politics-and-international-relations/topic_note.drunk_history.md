Let's start with how these kids organize their own little domestic clubs. Look at the Student Council. That right there is your classic American setup. In a presidential system of government, the executive and legislative branches operate as distinct and separate entities. The Yearbook Committee making the rules and the Student Body President executing them do not mix. Why? Because in a presidential system, the chief executive is elected by the citizens independently of the legislature. The whole school voted for Kevin; the Yearbook nerds didn't pick him. Because Kevin got his mandate from the masses, the chief executive in a presidential system serves a fixed term in office. You're stuck with Kevin for the whole academic year, even if he burns the homecoming float. And poor Kevin has to do everything. In a presidential system, the president typically serves simultaneously as both the head of state and the head of government. He has to do the grueling spreadsheet math for the budget—the government part—and he still has to wear the giant foam bulldog mascot head to wave at the pep rally, acting as the symbolic head of state. 

Now, cross the hallway to the Drama Club. They run things totally differently. In a parliamentary system of government, the executive branch derives its democratic legitimacy directly from the legislature. The actors are the legislature, and they just look at each other and say, "Sarah, you're the boss." In a parliamentary system, the head of government is usually a prime minister selected by the majority party or coalition in the legislature. The stagehands and the chorus kids formed an alliance and gave Sarah the clipboard. But notice the vibe shift: in a parliamentary system, the head of state and the head of government are distinct roles filled by different individuals. The diva lead actress stands in the spotlight accepting the flowers—she's the ceremonial head of state. Sarah is backstage in a headset, sweating bullets, acting as head of government. But if Sarah orders the wrong pizzas for cast party? Boom. A prime minister in a parliamentary system can be removed from office before the end of a term through a legislative vote of no confidence. The cast gets mad on a Tuesday, they vote, and Sarah is out by Wednesday. No waiting till the end of the year! 

Sometimes, a school tries to do both, creating a chaotic hybrid where a semi-presidential system features both a popularly elected president and a prime minister who is responsible to the legislature. Think of France, where you have a big-picture class president elected by everyone, but also a prime minister chosen by the council to scrub the cafeteria floors.

But what if the kids don't get to vote at all? Enter the substitute teachers from hell. Authoritarian regimes concentrate political power in a single leader or a small elite group not constitutionally accountable to the public. That's the rogue gym teacher who changes the dodgeball rules mid-game and doesn't care what the school handbook says. But he still lets you go home at 3 PM. Now, the principal who demands access to your social media, dictates your haircut, and makes you chant the school motto in your sleep? That's worse. Totalitarian regimes attempt to control all aspects of public and private life through extreme state centralization and mass surveillance. 

Maybe power isn't held by one psycho, but by the varsity cheer squad. An oligarchy is a system of government where political power rests with a small, elite group of individuals. Or maybe the purity-ring youth group takes over the math department and teaches algebra based on divine scripture—a theocracy is a form of government in which religious leaders hold primary political power.

Alright, so that's how the clubs are governed. But what happens when the 12:00 bell rings and everyone spills into the cafeteria? Welcome to International Relations, my friend. Welcome to the jungle. 

If you ask the cynical kids, the cafeteria is a death match. Realism in international relations posits that states act primarily to maximize their own power and national security. Every table is just trying not to get their lunch money stolen. Why? Because realism views the international system as inherently anarchic due to the absence of a global governing authority. There are no lunch monitors! The teachers are hiding in the lounge! Because no one is coming to save you, in realist theory, the anarchic nature of international relations forces states to rely entirely on self-help for survival. You better pack your own lunch and watch your own back. 

But this paranoia breeds chaos. It creates the ultimate lunchroom tragedy: the security dilemma occurs when a state's actions to increase its own security inadvertently cause other states to feel threatened. Imagine you start carrying a heavy geometry textbook on your tray to block flying baby carrots. The chess club at the next table sees your heavy book, gets paranoid you're going to throw it at them, and they start bringing metal compasses to defend themselves. Now you feel threatened by the compasses, so you bring a pair of scissors. Nobody actually wanted a fight, but the system forced an arms race!

The optimistic kids, however, disagree completely. Liberalism in international relations emphasizes the potential for cooperation, absolute gains, and peace among states. They look at the cafeteria and say, "Hey, we can trade!" Liberal theory asserts that economic interdependence creates disincentives for war between states. Think about it: if your entire social survival relies on trading your Dunkaroos to the kid next to you for his Capri Sun, you are not going to punch that kid. You need his Capri Sun! Furthermore, democratic peace theory claims that mature democratic states rarely engage in armed conflict with one another. If two different tables both elect their table-captains through fair votes, they're going to spend all their time having committee meetings instead of throwing mashed potatoes.

Then you have the artsy kids who think reality is just a vibe. Constructivism argues that international relations are primarily shaped by shared ideas, norms, and identities rather than material forces. Why is everyone utterly terrified of the 90-pound goth girl, but totally ignores the 250-pound offensive lineman? The lineman has the material power! But constructivism claims that state interests are socially constructed over time rather than biologically or geographically fixed. Over three years, the rumor mill collectively *constructed* the idea that the goth girl is a powerful witch. The reality is what we all agree it is!

And lurking in the corner are the radical kids. Marxist international relations theory focuses on global economic inequalities and the exploitation of developing nations by capitalist states. They see the AP students harvesting the labor of the freshmen to make their flashcards. The rich get richer, the freshmen stay poor. It's a class war. 

So, with all these motivations, how do these cafeteria tables actually execute their game plans? That's foreign policy! The kid sitting alone with noise-canceling headphones? Isolationism is a foreign policy approach in which a state avoids entering into political and military alliances with other nations. The nosey kid who butts into a couple's breakup shouting unsolicited advice? Interventionism is a foreign policy approach where a state actively interferes in the domestic affairs or foreign conflicts of another state. When it's time to build a mega-volcano for the science fair, multilateralism is a foreign policy strategy that involves three or more countries working together cooperatively on a common goal. But the kid who goes totally rogue and presents his own slideshow while ignoring his assigned group? Unilateralism occurs when a state acts independently in foreign affairs without seeking the support or agreement of other nations. 

To manage all this drama, kids pass meticulously folded notes. Diplomacy is the professional practice of conducting negotiations and managing relations between sovereign states. But when notes fail, they hit you where it hurts. Economic sanctions are commercial and financial penalties applied by one or more countries against a targeted sovereign state. The whole table agrees to financially freeze out Chad by refusing to trade him any snacks until he stops beatboxing.

Now, how do we keep the whole school from burning down without a principal? We make up rules. International law consists of a body of rules and principles that govern the relations and dealings of sovereign nations with each other. Sometimes kids write these down in blood—treaties are formally concluded, written, and ratified binding agreements between sovereign states. Other times, it's just an unwritten vibe. Customary international law derives from the consistent and general practice of states followed out of a sense of legal obligation. Nobody sits at the senior table. Nobody signed a document saying that, but it is custom treated as absolute law.

But here is the massive, glaring problem with all of it. A major functional limitation of international law is the lack of a centralized global executive authority to enforce compliance. Who is going to arrest the senior who steals a freshman's seat? Nobody! And it's all because of lockers. Your locker is your domain. State sovereignty is the fundamental legal principle that a state has absolute authority over its own territory and domestic affairs. Your locker, your rules. Because of this, the principle of state sovereignty inherently complicates the mandatory enforcement of international law and human rights treaties. If a kid is hoarding moldy sandwiches in his locker, the principal can't force him to clean it without violating his sacred locker sovereignty! It's a jurisdictional nightmare!

To herd these sovereign teenagers, we build super-clubs. Intergovernmental organizations are entities created by formal treaty and composed primarily of sovereign member states. Think of the official "Alliance of Hallway Monitors." Do not confuse them with the rogue parent volunteers who just show up uninvited with clipboards and orange slices—nongovernmental organizations are non-profit, voluntary citizens' groups operating independently of any national government.

The biggest super-club of them all? Built right after the Great Cafeteria Food Fight of '45. The United Nations was established in 1945 to promote international peace, security, and cooperation. It's got different rooms. There's the giant all-school assembly where everyone yells at once: the United Nations General Assembly provides a deliberative forum for all member states to debate international issues. And the beauty of the assembly is that each member state in the United Nations General Assembly receives one equal vote on resolutions. The squeaky-voiced freshman's vote counts exactly the same as the starting quarterback's.

But we all know the assembly doesn't have the real teeth. The real power is the hardcore discipline committee. The United Nations Security Council holds the primary responsibility for maintaining international peace and security. Now, this room is exclusive. The United Nations Security Council is composed of fifteen member states. Ten of them are just rotating tourists. The real gods of the hallway are the untouchables. Five permanent members of the United Nations Security Council hold veto power over substantive resolutions. Just one of them says "no," and the whole suspension is canceled. Who are these five terrifying elite kids? The five permanent members of the United Nations Security Council are the United States, the United Kingdom, France, Russia, and China. 

When there's a serious beef, the school relies on courts. The International Court of Justice serves as the principal judicial organ of the United Nations. But listen to me carefully—this court only deals with club-versus-club drama. The International Court of Justice settles legal disputes submitted by sovereign member states. It's Table A versus Table B. 

But what if one specific, deranged kid goes completely off the deep end and pours bleach into the prom punch bowl? You don't sue his whole table. You go after the maniac himself. That's a different tribunal entirely! The International Criminal Court is an intergovernmental organization that prosecutes individuals for genocide, crimes against humanity, and war crimes. It pierces the veil of the table and drags the actual human being into the light!
