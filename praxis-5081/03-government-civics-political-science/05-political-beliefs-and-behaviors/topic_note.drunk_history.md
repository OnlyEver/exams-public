Listen to me. Pull up a stool and slide that pint glass over. You want to teach high school civics? You’re about to walk into a classroom filled with thirty hormonal, sleep-deprived teenagers and try to explain the American electoral process. You can't just read them a textbook. You have to make them see that Washington D.C. is just a macro-level, multi-billion-dollar version of their own vicious, cafeteria survival-of-the-fittest reality show.

Let's start with where these kids even get their ideas. When they walk through your door, they aren't blank slates. The family is the most significant factor in shaping an individual's political socialization. They are essentially downloading their parents' dinnertime rants. And that right there—political socialization is the process by which individuals develop political beliefs and values. But who actually shows up to vote in this giant nationwide student council election? Demographics dictate everything. Age is a strong predictor of voter turnout. Your high school seniors barely care enough to vote for prom king, and statistically, older citizens vote at higher rates than younger citizens in the United States. Furthermore, the AP and Honors kids? They’re running the board. Education level strongly correlates with political participation rates. It’s a proven fact that individuals with higher levels of education are statistically more likely to vote in United States elections. Then you have the gender dynamic, which is massive. The gender gap refers to the statistical difference in voting behavior and political preferences between men and women. Look at any electoral map and you'll see it: women in the United States have statistically favored Democratic candidates more often than male voters since the 1980s.

But why do some kids raise their hand and others put their head on the desk? It’s all about whether they think the game is rigged. Political efficacy is the belief that an individual's political participation makes a meaningful impact on government. Naturally, high political efficacy correlates with increased likelihood of participating in elections. If they think their vote counts, they show up. Historically, the principal's office locked half the school out of the voting booth. We had to literally amend the rulebook. The Fifteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution prohibited the denial of voting rights based on race. Later, the Nineteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution granted women the right to vote. Then we had to stop the administration from demanding a cover charge for democracy, so the Twenty-fourth Amendment to the United States Constitution eliminated poll taxes in federal elections. Finally, because we were drafting eighteen-year-olds to fight in Vietnam, the Twenty-sixth Amendment to the United States Constitution lowered the federal voting age from 21 to 18. That’s your seniors! Even with constitutional amendments, local administrators still played games, which is why the Voting Rights Act of 1965 banned literacy tests as a prerequisite for voting, absolutely torching those rigged reading quizzes. Later, to make signing up as easy as breathing, the National Voter Registration Act of 1993 requires states to offer voter registration opportunities at motor vehicle departments. We tied democracy directly to getting a driver's license! But even today, friction exists. Structural barriers such as strict voter identification laws can decrease voter turnout. If you make it harder to get into the stadium, fewer people watch the game. That lower energy is why voter turnout in United States midterm elections is historically lower than turnout in presidential elections. People only show up for the championship match.

Once inside the booth, how do they choose? Some look at the menu and ask, "What gets me a bigger slice of pizza?" Rational-choice voting occurs when a voter selects a candidate based on individual self-interest. Some look backward: retrospective voting involves evaluating a candidate's past performance in office to make a voting decision. Some look forward: prospective voting involves selecting a candidate based on predictions about the candidate's future policy actions. And some just blindly wear their clique's colors: party-line voting involves casting a ballot for candidates affiliated with a single political party across all offices.

Speaking of those cliques, the mega-factions running the hallways? Political parties serve as linkage institutions connecting citizens to the government. They bridge the gap between the screaming student body and the principal's office. To tell everyone what they stand for, political parties draft platforms to articulate the party's policy goals and ideological stances. Once they win, they take over the student council room. Political parties organize government operations within the United States Congress by determining committee leadership. They literally hand out the gavels. 

But why are there only two mega-cliques? Because the math forces them into it. The United States utilizes a winner-take-all plurality electoral system for most congressional elections. If you get the most votes, you take the whole seat, and second place gets absolutely nothing. This brutal math leads to Duverger's Law, which posits that winner-take-all plurality systems naturally favor a two-party political structure. You either merge into a giant coalition, or you fade into irrelevance. In other countries, they use different math. Proportional representation systems allocate legislative seats based on the percentage of the total vote a political party receives. Because a tiny fraction of the vote actually wins you a tiny fraction of power, proportional representation systems generally encourage the proliferation of multi-party systems. But here? The weird fringe kids—the third parties—are treated like trespassers. Sure, third parties often introduce new policy issues to the national political discourse. They bring up stuff nobody else wants to talk about. But electorally? Third parties can act as spoilers by drawing votes away from major party candidates with similar ideologies. They accidentally burn down the campaign of the candidate they actually agree with most!

To pick their champion, the cliques hold massive tryouts. Sometimes it's a free-for-all: an open primary allows any registered voter to cast a ballot in either party's primary election. Chaos! The drama club kids can vote in the jock's election! Other times, they lock the doors. A closed primary restricts voting to individuals formally registered with that specific political party. Or they skip the secret ballots entirely and get in a room to yell at each other. A caucus is a local gathering where voters openly discuss and vote for political party candidates. It’s sweaty, loud, and public. Because early momentum is everything, everyone tries to cut the line. Front-loading is the practice of states scheduling presidential primaries early in the election year to increase political influence. 

Over time, the cafeteria map shifts. Party realignment occurs when a demographic group significantly shifts long-term allegiance from one political party to another. Imagine all the band geeks suddenly deciding to permanently sit with the jocks. On the other hand, some kids just get sick of the drama entirely. Party dealignment occurs when an increasing number of voters identify as political independents, refusing to wear anybody's team jacket.

What about the kids who only care about one hyper-specific thing, like banning plastic straws in the cafeteria? Those are the interest groups. Interest groups are organizations seeking to influence public policy on specific issues. Some are super broad, but single-issue interest groups focus advocacy efforts on one narrow policy area. How do they get what they want? They corner the principal in the hallway. Interest groups utilize lobbying to directly persuade lawmakers to support or oppose legislation. If that fails, they whip up a mob. Grassroots lobbying relies on mobilizing the general public to contact elected officials regarding specific policy issues. Send a thousand emails! Blow up the switchboard! If the rule is already passed, they take it to the student court. Interest groups frequently use litigation to challenge the constitutionality of laws or executive agency actions. And if they aren't directly involved in the lawsuit, they slide notes to the judge under the door. An amicus curiae brief is a legal document filed by an interest group to influence a Supreme Court decision. But these groups constantly struggle with the slacker problem. The free-rider problem occurs when individuals benefit from an interest group's actions without contributing to the organization. Everybody enjoys the newly allowed off-campus lunch, but nobody helped pay for the petition!

When the AV club gets too powerful, they form an unholy alliance. An Iron Triangle is a mutually beneficial policy-making relationship among a congressional committee, an interest group, and a bureaucratic agency. They trade favors, lock the doors, and write the rules for themselves. On a messier scale, you have issue networks, which include diverse policy experts, media figures, and interest groups debating a specific policy area. And the people running these groups? They never leave the building. The revolving door refers to the movement of personnel between roles as legislators and jobs as lobbyists. The student body president graduates on Friday and comes back on Monday as the guy selling the school its vending machines!

Overseeing all this drama is the school newspaper—the media. The mass media functions as a gatekeeper by determining which issues receive national attention and shape the political agenda. If they don't print it, it didn't happen. They also supposedly act as the hallway monitors. The mass media functions as a watchdog by investigating and exposing government corruption or inefficiency. But let's be real, they mostly just care about the drama of who is winning. Horse-race journalism emphasizes candidate polling numbers rather than detailed candidate policy positions. To make matters worse, there used to be dozens of little indie zines, but now? Media consolidation reduces the diversity of independent news sources available to the public. And they figured out that making you angry keeps you reading. Narrowcasting involves targeting media programming at specific segments of the population. By feeding you a constant diet of your own opinions, narrowcasting contributes to political polarization by reinforcing pre-existing ideological beliefs.

Now grab a napkin, we need to draw the map. The physical space of the school dictates who has power. Every ten years, after we count the students, we do reapportionment, which is the redistribution of United States House of Representatives seats among states based on decennial census data. Once a state knows how many slices of the pie they get, they have to draw the lines. Redistricting is the process of redrawing state legislative and congressional district boundaries. If you let a vicious partisan draw the lines, they will carve up the map to protect their friends. Gerrymandering involves manipulating district boundaries to favor a specific political party or demographic group. The Supreme Court had to blow the whistle on this cartographic cheating. The Supreme Court case Baker v. Carr established the one person, one vote principle for legislative redistricting, meaning you can't have a district of ten people and a district of ten thousand people. Furthermore, the Supreme Court case Shaw v. Reno ruled that racial gerrymandering is subject to strict scrutiny under the Equal Protection Clause, making it incredibly hard to draw bizarrely shaped districts based predominantly on race. 

Because of the way these maps are drawn, once a kid gets elected, they basically become royalty. The incumbency advantage refers to the extremely high reelection rate of politicians currently holding political office. Statistically, members of the United States House of Representatives have historically experienced higher incumbency reelection rates than United States Senators. Why? Because House members represent smaller, often gerrymandered districts, and they have insane perks like the franking privilege, which allows incumbent members of Congress to send mail to constituents at the government's expense. Free marketing, paid for by the taxpayers!

But the Presidency? The Presidency is decided by the most insane board game ever invented. The Electoral College is the formal mechanism for electing the President of the United States. Here is the math: the total number of Electoral College votes is 538. To win the crown, a presidential candidate must secure at least 270 Electoral College votes to win the presidency. How do you get your state's points? A state's Electoral College vote allocation equals the state's number of United States Representatives plus the state's two United States Senators. And here is where the game gets brutal. Forty-eight states and Washington D.C. award Electoral College votes on a winner-take-all basis. If you win Florida by one single vote, you get 100% of Florida's electoral points. The only exceptions are the hipsters up north. Maine and Nebraska allocate Electoral College votes using a congressional district method, splitting their points up. Because of the brutal winner-take-all math everywhere else, the Electoral College system encourages presidential candidates to concentrate campaign efforts on competitive swing states. You ignore the deep red and deep blue states and spend a billion dollars fighting over Pennsylvania! What if there's a crazy three-way tie and the board game breaks? If no presidential candidate reaches 270 electoral votes, the House of Representatives chooses the president from the top three candidates. And in this doomsday scenario—a contingent presidential election in the House of Representatives—each state delegation casts exactly one vote. California gets one vote. Wyoming gets one vote. It’s mathematical anarchy!

Finally, we have to talk about the shadow economy. The bake sales that fund the war. The referee trying to track the cash is the FEC. The Federal Election Commission is an independent regulatory agency responsible for enforcing federal campaign finance laws. For a long time, there were two piggy banks. Hard money refers to campaign contributions strictly regulated by the Federal Election Commission and given directly to candidates. It's highly tracked. But then there was soft money, which refers to unregulated campaign contributions given to political parties for party-building activities. It was a massive loophole. So, Congress tried to slam the loophole shut. The Bipartisan Campaign Reform Act of 2002 attempted to ban soft money contributions to national political parties. Because nobody wants to say that mouthful, you just need to know that the Bipartisan Campaign Reform Act of 2002 is commonly known as the McCain-Feingold Act. 

During this era, if a group of kids wanted to pool their lunch money to buy ads, they formed PACs. Political Action Committees are organizations collecting political donations from members to distribute to candidate campaigns. But traditional Political Action Committees have strict legal limits on the amount of money allowed to be donated directly to a candidate. 

Then, the Supreme Court dropped a nuke on the bake sale. The Supreme Court case Citizens United v. Federal Election Commission ruled that corporate political spending is a form of protected free speech. Money became legally synonymous with shouting your opinion! The Citizens United decision led to the creation of Super PACs. These are the Godzilla of campaign finance. Super PACs can raise and spend unlimited amounts of money on political advocacy. Billionaires, corporations, unions—they can dump \$10,000,000 from a helicopter. There is only one tiny, flimsy string attached: Super PACs are legally prohibited from coordinating directly with a political candidate's official campaign. They can't text the candidate and say 'Where do you want these ads?' Instead, they have to shout into the void through independent expenditures, which are campaign communications not coordinated with any candidate's campaign organization. 

So there you have it. The whole beautiful, terrifying, multibillion-dollar high school election. Teach them how the gears mesh, and they just might survive the real thing.
