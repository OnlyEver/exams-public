Listen to me, slide that pint over here and look at this napkin. You’re about to walk into a secondary school classroom and govern thirty feral teenagers. They are going to test every single boundary you have, and to survive, you need to understand the absolute chaos of American constitutional law, because your high school is a microcosm of the entire United States government! You think you’re just teaching history? You’re teaching the rules of survival! 

First, we have to separate the shield from the sword. Imagine your classroom door has a giant, impenetrable lock on it. Civil liberties are constitutional protections against arbitrary government interference. They are your shield. They tell the overbearing Principal—the government—"You may not cross this line into my room!" And where does this shield come from? Civil liberties are primarily derived from the Bill of Rights. But once you leave your classroom and step into the lunchroom, it’s a totally different game. Civil rights are legal protections against discrimination based on characteristics like race or gender. They say, "If the school is serving tater tots, every kid gets an equal scoop regardless of who they are." Civil rights are primarily derived from the Equal Protection Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment. Remember that! Liberties keep the government out; rights force the government to play fair when it steps in.

Now, let's open the Pandora’s box of the First Amendment, which is basically the engine of all teenage drama. First, dealing with religion. The First Amendment restricts the national government from establishing an official religion—meaning you can’t force the pep rally to worship the school mascot! The Supreme Court proved this in Engel v. Vitale (1962), which ruled that mandatory prayer in public schools violates the Establishment Clause of the First Amendment. But flip the coin! The First Amendment also prohibits the national government from infringing on the free exercise of religion. You can’t crush their faith, either. Wisconsin v. Yoder (1972) ruled that compelling Amish students to attend school past the eighth grade violates the Free Exercise Clause. You can't force them to consume your curriculum if it destroys their soul!

Then we get to the loudmouths. The First Amendment prohibits the national government from abridging the freedom of speech, but this isn't a blank check! Schenck v. United States (1919) established the clear and present danger test for restricting freedom of speech. A kid can't yell "Fire!" in the auditorium just to watch everyone trample each other! But what if they just sit there, silently protesting your homework policy with a symbolic gesture? Texas v. Johnson (1989) ruled that burning the American flag is protected symbolic speech under the First Amendment. And if you think they lose that right when the morning bell rings, think again! Tinker v. Des Moines (1969) ruled that students retain First Amendment rights to freedom of speech while on public school property. Black armbands, protest shirts—as long as it doesn’t cause a massive disruption, they can wear it!

And what about the school newspaper trying to expose the cafeteria meatloaf? The First Amendment restricts the national government from censoring the press. In New York Times Co. v. United States (1971), the Court established a heavy presumption against government prior restraint of the press. You can’t stop them from publishing just because it’s embarrassing! Finally, the First Amendment protects the right of individuals to peacefully assemble, and it protects the right of individuals to petition the government for a redress of grievances. They can gather by the lockers and hand you a petition demanding better bathroom soap, and you just have to take it!

But what happens when things get dangerous? The Second Amendment limits the national government by protecting an individual right to keep and bear arms. And if the administration goes on a witch hunt? The Fourth Amendment restricts the national government from conducting unreasonable searches and seizures. You can't just upend a kid's backpack because you feel like it. If you drag them to the Dean’s office, the Fifth Amendment prevents the national government from compelling individuals to testify against themselves in criminal cases. They have the right to sit there and plead the Fifth! Plus, the Fifth Amendment prohibits the national government from subjecting a person to double jeopardy for the same offense. You can't suspend them twice for the same spitball! If it goes to a real trial, the Sixth Amendment requires the national government to provide criminal defendants with a speedy and public trial, and the Sixth Amendment guarantees criminal defendants the right to legal counsel. They get a buddy in their corner. And if they are guilty? The Eighth Amendment restricts the national government from imposing cruel and unusual punishments. No locking children in the boiler room!

Take a sip of your drink, because here comes the plot twist that will blow your students' minds. For the longest time, the Bill of Rights only applied to the big boss in Washington, D.C. The state governments—like the rogue hall monitors of the country—ran wild! They could search your house or ban your speech! But then, the Supreme Court pulled off the ultimate constitutional heist. The Supreme Court uses the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment to selectively incorporate the Bill of Rights to the states. Selective incorporation is the legal doctrine applying provisions of the Bill of Rights to state governments, piece by painstaking piece. 

And they did this using two types of grading rubrics. First, procedural due process requires the government to follow fair legal procedures before depriving a person of life, liberty, or property. Did you give them a hearing before you expelled them? But then there is substantive due process, which protects certain fundamental individual rights from government interference regardless of the procedures used. Even if you use the perfect procedural rubric, some parts of a citizen's life are just off-limits! 

Through selective incorporation, the Court finally leashed the states. McDonald v. Chicago (2010) incorporated the Second Amendment right to keep and bear arms to the state governments. What if the state police search you illegally? Mapp v. Ohio (1961) applied the exclusionary rule to state governments. And what is that? The exclusionary rule prohibits the government from using illegally obtained evidence in a criminal trial. If you find a stolen test in a locker without a warrant, you can't use it! If the local cops arrest someone, Miranda v. Arizona (1966) required police to inform criminal suspects of the right to remain silent and the right to an attorney before custodial interrogation. And if the suspect is dead broke? Gideon v. Wainwright (1963) required state courts to provide legal counsel to indigent criminal defendants. 

And what about the secrets kids write in the margins of their notebooks? The Constitution doesn't even say the word "privacy," but substantive due process says it's there, hiding in the shadows! Griswold v. Connecticut (1965) established that the United States Constitution implies a fundamental right to privacy. This implied right shook the earth when Roe v. Wade (1973) extended the constitutional right to privacy to protect a woman's decision to have an abortion. But remember, the Supreme Court can always rewrite the ending of the story. Decades later, Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health Organization (2022) overturned Roe v. Wade by ruling that the Constitution does not confer a right to abortion. 

Alright, wipe down this table, we need to map out the fight for Civil Rights. When the government decides to discriminate, how do the courts grade that action? They use a three-tiered rubric called judicial scrutiny. The top tier is brutal. Strict scrutiny is the highest standard of judicial review used to evaluate the constitutionality of governmental discrimination. It almost always results in a failing grade for the government. Courts apply the strict scrutiny standard to evaluate government actions that classify individuals based on race or national origin. The middle tier? Courts apply intermediate scrutiny to evaluate government actions that classify individuals based on gender. And the bottom tier? The rational basis test is the lowest level of judicial scrutiny used to evaluate laws involving non-suspect classifications like age or wealth. If the school says only seniors can park in the front lot, rational basis says, "Sure, makes sense, you pass."

Let me draw you a timeline on this napkin of the catastrophic failures and hard-won victories in the cafeteria of civil rights. At the darkest bottom, Dred Scott v. Sandford (1857) ruled that enslaved people and descendants of enslaved people were not United States citizens. A complete moral collapse. Then, even after the Civil War, Plessy v. Ferguson (1896) upheld the constitutionality of racial segregation under the separate but equal doctrine. Decades later, panic and prejudice led to another disaster when Korematsu v. United States (1944) upheld the constitutionality of Japanese-American internment camps during World War II—applying strict scrutiny but letting the discrimination pass under the guise of military necessity!

But then, the map turns! The Supreme Court finally looked at the lunchroom and snapped. Brown v. Board of Education (1954) ruled that racial segregation in public schools violates the Equal Protection Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment. It tore Plessy to shreds! Then, Loving v. Virginia (1967) invalidated state laws prohibiting interracial marriage as violations of the Equal Protection Clause. The march for equality continued into the modern era, where Obergefell v. Hodges (2015) ruled that the Fourteenth Amendment guarantees the fundamental right to marry to same-sex couples.

But what about using race to fix the damage of the past? It’s a tightrope walk! Regents of the University of California v. Bakke (1978) upheld the constitutionality of affirmative action programs in college admissions, arguing that diversity is a brilliant educational goal. But in the exact same breath, Regents of the University of California v. Bakke (1978) ruled that the use of strict racial quotas in college admissions is unconstitutional. You can look at the whole student, but you can't just hold a rigid number of seats! And what about drawing political maps? Shaw v. Reno (1993) ruled that racial gerrymandering is subject to strict scrutiny under the Equal Protection Clause. You can't just draw bizarre, squiggly district lines solely based on race!

Finally, my friend, look at the school board—Congress! While the Court interpreted the rules, Congress and the states had to actually write them into the rulebook. After the Civil War, the Thirteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution formally abolished slavery across the United States. To secure the ballot box, the Fifteenth Amendment prohibited the denial of voting rights based on race, color, or previous condition of servitude. Later on, the Nineteenth Amendment granted women the constitutional right to vote in the United States. And because states kept trying to charge a cover fee to vote, the Twenty-fourth Amendment prohibited the use of poll taxes in federal elections. 

But amendments weren't enough, because private businesses and local governments were still finding loopholes! So in the 1960s, Congress dropped the hammer. The Civil Rights Act of 1964 prohibited discrimination based on race, color, religion, sex, or national origin in public accommodations. No more segregated hotels or restaurants! Inside the workplace, Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 prohibited employment discrimination based on race, color, religion, sex, or national origin. At the ballot box, the Voting Rights Act of 1965 banned the use of literacy tests as a requirement for voter registration. And my absolute favorite piece of trivia for the PE teachers: Title IX of the Education Amendments of 1972 prohibited sex-based discrimination in any education program receiving federal financial assistance. That’s why the girls’ volleyball team gets the same quality buses as the boys’ football team!
