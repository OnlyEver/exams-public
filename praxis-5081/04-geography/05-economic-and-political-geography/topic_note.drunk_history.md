Slide that bowl of peanuts over here, grab a coaster, and listen to me, because you are about to walk into a high school to student-teach social studies, and you need to understand that the entire planet Earth is exactly like a chaotic, unsupervised 9th-grade cafeteria. Geography isn't just coloring inside the lines on a map; it is the ultimate reality-TV survival show where humanity figures out how to hoard the tater tots, steal the lunch money, and build literal walls to keep the freshmen out. Let’s trace how humanity went from wandering the hallways to drawing laser-lines in the ocean.

We have to start with the lunchroom, because before humans could build empires, they had to stop starving. Picture this: it’s approximately 10,000 BCE in the Fertile Crescent. This is where the First Agricultural Revolution began. The First Agricultural Revolution marks the transition from human hunting and gathering to intentional plant cultivation. We finally stopped running around the prehistoric cafeteria trying to tackle our food, sat down at a table, and figured out how to grow it. Fast forward a few thousand years to when some kid brings a literal power tool to lunch. The Second Agricultural Revolution historically coincided with the European Industrial Revolution. The Second Agricultural Revolution involved the introduction of mechanization to farming practices. Suddenly, human muscle was replaced by steam and steel plows, and crop yields exploded. But the dramatic mid-century sequel is the real blockbuster. The Third Agricultural Revolution is widely known as the Green Revolution. Norman Borlaug is widely considered the central figure of the Green Revolution—he is the ultimate VIP lunch-lady of the planet who saved a billion people from starvation. How? The Green Revolution involved the genetic development of high-yielding varieties of cereal grains, engineering dwarf wheat that wouldn't fall over when it grew massive heads of grain. To feed these new super-crops, the Green Revolution caused a massive expansion in the use of artificial chemical fertilizers. 

But how we farm today is still pure high school clique logic. Subsistence agriculture involves growing food primarily to feed the farmer's immediate family—you brought a brown paper bag just for you, survival mode. On the flip side, commercial agriculture involves growing food primarily for profitable sale off the farm—you’re the kid selling full-size candy bars out of your locker for pure profit. If you don't have a lot of space at the lunch table, you use intensive subsistence agriculture, which involves cultivating small plots of land with significant inputs of human labor. You are aggressively guarding and maximizing every square inch of your tiny corner of the table. But if you have the whole cafeteria to yourself, extensive agriculture requires large areas of land with relatively minimal labor input per acre. In tropical areas with awful soil, farmers use shifting cultivation, which involves clearing agricultural land by slashing vegetation and burning the debris, using the ash for a quick burst of nutrients before moving on to burn the next spot. But when we push the environment too far, the land completely breaks. Desertification is the severe degradation of land in arid and dry sub-humid geographical areas. And what ruins the land? Livestock overgrazing is a primary human cause of desertification. Imagine a hundred teenagers trampling the courtyard grass until there’s nothing left to hold the dirt down, and it all just blows away in the wind. 

Back in the 1820s, a German guy named Johann Heinrich von Thünen figured out the absolute mathematical seating chart for this agricultural lunchroom. Von Thünen's model predicts the spatial layout of agricultural land use around a central city. How does a farmer decide what to plant? Von Thünen's model relies primarily on the cost of transportation to determine agricultural land use. He drew a target with the market city in the middle. In Von Thünen's model, highly perishable goods are produced in the ring immediately surrounding the market. The milk and the berries sit right next to the city center because without refrigeration, they’ll rot before they arrive. Ring two is for forestry, because wood is incredibly heavy and you don't want to drag it for miles. Ring three is for grain, which is lighter and doesn't spoil. But my absolute favorite piece of spatial logic? Von Thünen's model places extensive livestock grazing in the outermost ring of agricultural production. Why? Because cows have legs! They literally walk themselves to the market, so transportation costs drop to zero! Today, of course, the corporate overlords have taken over. Agribusiness is the corporate integration of various steps of production within the food processing industry. It’s the whole supply chain, from the patented seed in a lab to the frozen chicken nugget on your tray, operating as one massive corporate machine.

Once we mastered the food, humanity moved to the shop class and the science fair. The Industrial Revolution began in Great Britain in the late eighteenth century, and it shattered the economy into four distinct cliques. Primary economic activities involve the direct extraction of natural resources from the physical environment—the miners, the drillers, the kids digging in the dirt. Secondary economic activities involve processing raw materials into finished manufactured goods—the factory kids assembling the parts. Tertiary economic activities involve the provision of commercial services to businesses and individual consumers—the retail workers and cashiers serving the finished goods. And sitting in the ivory tower of the school library, quaternary economic activities strictly involve specialized information processing, academic research, and corporate management. 

To figure out where to put the factories, Alfred Weber became the hall monitor of economics. Weber's Least Cost Theory predicts the optimal location of manufacturing plants by treating factory owners like misers trying to save every penny of their allowance. Weber's Least Cost Theory focuses on minimizing the transportation costs of raw materials to the factory, AND Weber's Least Cost Theory focuses on minimizing the transportation costs of finished products to the market. So, in Weber's Least Cost Theory, a bulk-reducing industry optimally locates near the source of raw materials. Think of smelting copper like whittling a giant tree trunk down to a toothpick; you don't drag the massive heavy trunk to the store, you whittle it down where you found it! Conversely, in Weber's Least Cost Theory, a bulk-gaining industry optimally locates near the consumer market. Think of soda bottling—water is heavy and available everywhere, so you just ship the lightweight syrup and add the heavy water right next to the thirsty people! The only exception is a footloose industry, which can locate virtually anywhere without being financially constrained by transportation costs, like a software coder whose product weighs absolutely nothing.

Now, why are some nations rolling in cash while others are broke? Rostow's Stages of Economic Growth model posits that all national economies pass through five distinct developmental stages. It's a very optimistic idea that everyone eventually graduates to being rich. But Wallerstein's World Systems Theory is the cynical reality-TV producer. He divides all global economies into core, semi-periphery, and periphery categories. Core countries in Wallerstein's World Systems Theory focus on high-skill, capital-intensive economic production—the popular kids designing iPhones. Periphery countries in Wallerstein's World Systems Theory focus on low-skill, labor-intensive economic production—the kids stuck digging the cobalt out of the ground to make the core's batteries. To track who is actually living a decent life, we check the global report card: The Human Development Index is a composite metric measuring a country's overall level of human development. The Human Development Index incorporates a country's average life expectancy at birth, the Human Development Index incorporates a country's average educational attainment levels, and the Human Development Index incorporates a country's gross national income per capita. 

Lately, the global economy has been hijacked by neoliberal economic policies, which act like a principal who wants to privatize the entire school. Neoliberal economic policies strongly promote international free trade. Furthermore, neoliberal economic policies strongly advocate for the sweeping deregulation of private industries, and neoliberal economic policies heavily encourage the privatization of government-owned enterprises. The result? A massive spatial heist! Corporations engage in outsourcing, which is the corporate practice of moving industrial production jobs to geographic regions with lower labor costs. Look right next door: Maquiladoras are foreign-owned manufacturing factories located in Mexico. Thanks to free trade, maquiladoras are heavily concentrated along the geographical border between Mexico and the United States, importing components, exploiting cheaper labor, and shipping the products right back across the line. We also see Special Economic Zones, which are designated national areas where business and trade laws differ from the rest of the country to lure in foreign cash. And to save money on storage lockers, companies use just-in-time delivery, which reduces corporate inventory costs by scheduling parts to arrive at a factory moments before required assembly. But the losers of this global game face a nightmare. Deindustrialization is the sustained decline of secondary economic activity in a specific geographic region. When the factories flee, you get the aftermath. The Rust Belt is a geographic region in the midwestern United States that experienced severe deindustrialization—a whole landscape of empty, rusting factories that got left out of the global clique.

Now we reach the final boss battle: Political Geography. The Principal's office. Let’s get the vocabulary straight, because if you misuse these words, the geography teachers will hunt you down. A state is a politically organized territory that possesses ultimate internal sovereignty. To be real, a state must possess a defined physical territory to be recognized under modern international law, and a state must possess a permanent human population to be recognized under modern international law. A nation, however, is a cohesive group of people sharing a common cultural heritage—it's a shared identity, an ethnic or linguistic bond. When the band geeks get their own dedicated, legally recognized school district, you get a nation-state. A nation-state is a politically organized area where a single nation and a state occupy the exact same geographic space. Japan is frequently cited by geographers as a prime modern example of a nation-state. But sometimes, a cohesive group is left wandering the halls without a home. A stateless nation is a culturally unified national group lacking its own politically organized sovereign state. The Kurdish people represent the largest recognized stateless nation in the modern Middle East, split across several different state borders.

And speaking of borders, let’s draw some lines! The timing of a border changes everything. An antecedent boundary is a political boundary drawn before a geographic area experiences heavy human settlement. The political border between the United States and Canada along the 49th parallel is an antecedent boundary—they just drew a straight line on a map before anyone lived out there. A superimposed boundary is a political boundary forcefully placed by outside powers, and a superimposed boundary typically ignores existing cultural or ethnic territorial patterns. It's like the administration imposing a new seating chart that forces rival gangs to sit together. The political borders established in Africa during the 1884 Berlin Conference are superimposed boundaries, drawn by arrogant Europeans committing a massive continental heist. A subsequent boundary is drawn after the development of a cultural landscape, and a subsequent boundary is typically drawn to formally accommodate existing religious or linguistic territorial differences. The political border drawn between India and Pakistan in 1947 is a subsequent boundary, carefully drawn to separate Hindu and Muslim populations. Finally, a relic boundary is a political boundary that no longer legally functions as an international border, but a relic boundary leaves a visible historical imprint on the modern cultural landscape. The former Berlin Wall is a prominent European example of a relic boundary—the guards are gone, but the scar on the city remains.

What keeps the drama from boiling over? Centripetal forces serve to socially unify a state's human population—like a killer pep rally. Conversely, centrifugal forces serve to socially divide a state's human population—like extreme wealth inequality tearing the student body apart. If the centrifugal forces get too hot, a country might use devolution, which is the formal transfer of political power from a central government to regional governments, just to let the angry factions govern themselves a bit. If that fails, the school explodes into balkanization. Balkanization is the violent fragmentation of a geographic region into smaller, hostile units along ethno-linguistic lines. The 1990s geopolitical breakup of Yugoslavia is a historical example of balkanization, tearing a country to shreds in a bloody civil war.

To hold onto power, politicians design the internal geometry of the state. Unitary states centralize the vast majority of political power in the hands of national government officials. Federal states, like the US, formally allocate significant autonomous political power to local government units within the country. But the sneakiest trick in the book is how they draw the voting districts. Gerrymandering is the deliberate redrawing of legislative territorial boundaries. Gerrymandering is explicitly utilized to benefit the political party currently in power. It’s like drawing the dodgeball boundaries by zig-zagging a chalk line across the gym just so your side gets all the varsity pitchers and the other side gets the kids with asthma. 

On the global stage, countries are terrified of being picked off alone, so they form massive super-cliques. Supranationalism is a formal alliance involving three or more national countries for mutual political or economic benefit. The European Union is a modern economic and political supranational organization. Geographically, states also obsess over physical quirks that give them leverage. An enclave is a political territory completely surrounded geographically by another single country. The nation of Lesotho is a geographic enclave completely surrounded by South Africa. An exclave is a geographic portion of a state separated from the main territory by surrounding alien territory. The state of Alaska is a geographic exclave of the United States. And you absolutely must control the doors to the cafeteria. A geographic chokepoint is a strategic narrow route providing maritime passage to another vital region. The Strait of Hormuz is a critical global geographic chokepoint for international oil transportation; if you plug that strait, the global economy has a heart attack. If you live in the crossfire of the big kids, you are in a shatterbelt. Shatterbelts are geographic regions heavily caught between stronger colliding external cultural or political forces. Eastern Europe operated as a geopolitical shatterbelt region throughout the Cold War, getting crushed between the Soviets and the West. Historically, geographers have tried to crack the code for ultimate power. Mackinder's Heartland Theory argues that control of Eastern Europe and Russia guarantees global geopolitical domination. He thought whoever held the massive interior fortress of the world would win. But Spykman's Rimland Theory argues that control of the coastal fringes of Eurasia guarantees global geopolitical domination, because the coasts have the ships, the trade, and the money!

And finally, when the kids spill out into the parking lot—the ocean—there are still rules to this heist! Under the UN Convention on the Law of the Sea, a state's territorial waters extend 12 nautical miles from its baseline. That is your absolute sovereign bubble. But the real money is further out. Under the UN Convention on the Law of the Sea, an Exclusive Economic Zone extends 200 nautical miles from a state's baseline. Within an Exclusive Economic Zone, a sovereign state retains the sole legal right to exploit oceanic natural resources. Other countries can sail their ships through those 200 miles, but if they drop a fishing net or find a billion-dollar offshore oil deposit, too bad! Only the sovereign state gets to keep the treasure!
