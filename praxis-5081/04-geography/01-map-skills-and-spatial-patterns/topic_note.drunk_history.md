You’re stressing over how to teach geography, treating maps like they are just innocent pictures of the Earth. They aren't! First thing you must understand to pass this test and survive your future classroom: all map projections introduce distortion to the spherical Earth when represented on a two-dimensional plane. Think about trying to flatten a grapefruit rind onto this bar table without tearing it. It is physically, mathematically impossible! The rind snaps. Because of this geometric nightmare, map projections inevitably distort shape, area, distance, or direction. You cannot have it all! 

Every projection is like a contestant on a reality TV dating show—they all have a fatal flaw. Take the Mercator projection. He’s the old-school sailor, the sea captain of the bunch. The Mercator projection preserves accurate compass direction, so if you are trying to navigate a pirate ship across the Atlantic, he’s your guy. But his Botox is completely out of control. To keep those compass lines straight, the Mercator projection severely distorts the relative size of landmasses near the poles. He makes Greenland look like a bloated whale, bigger than Africa, which is a total lie! 

Then there’s the Gall-Peters projection. He came on the show to protest the Mercator. He’s all about fairness. The Gall-Peters projection preserves the accurate relative area of landmasses. If a country is twice as big in real life, it’s twice as big on the map. But what’s the trade-off? He stretched everyone out like a funhouse mirror! The Gall-Peters projection distorts the geometric shape of landmasses, making the continents look like melted wax dripping down the page. 

Then we have the ultimate middle-child people-pleaser. The Robinson projection minimizes extreme spatial distortions by compromising on perfect shape preservation, and, because it just wants everyone to get along, the Robinson projection minimizes extreme spatial distortions by compromising on perfect area preservation. It just curves the edges to make everything look "good enough." But the absolute prom king? The Winkel Tripel projection minimizes distortion in area, direction, and distance simultaneously. It is a mathematical masterpiece of juggling, which is why National Geographic uses it.

But wait, imagine you’re planning a bank heist. Or, same energy, managing a middle school cafeteria. You need different camera angles. If you place a security camera directly over the ceiling fan in the center of the room, that’s an azimuthal projection. Azimuthal projections display the Earth from a single central point. Because you are looking straight down, azimuthal projections represent accurate distances from the central point of the map to any outward edge, making them absolutely essential for plotting polar flight routes. But what if you just want to focus on the United States? You put a party hat on the globe! Conic projections project the surface of the Earth onto a cone. Because that cone sits most snugly right across the middle of the globe before flaring out, conic projections provide the highest accuracy at the mid-latitudes. 

Okay, so we have our flattened, slightly flawed maps. What are we putting on them? Think of a map as a theater stage. Reference maps display the boundaries and names of geographic areas without focusing on specific data sets. They just show you where the stage props are. Political maps display human-created boundaries, like the arbitrary masking tape lines a substitute teacher puts on the floor to separate desks. Physical maps display natural geographic features of the Earth, like the mountains, the rivers, or the fact that your classroom borders the loud band room. 

But reference maps are boring. You want the drama! You want the plot! That’s what thematic maps are for. Thematic maps display specific types of data related to a geographic area. They are the gossip column. Imagine a map of your classroom showing which rows bombed the midterm. A choropleth map uses different colors or shading to represent the statistical value of a specific variable across predefined areas—dark red for the failing rows, light pink for the passing ones. 

What if you want to track every time a student secretly checks their phone under the desk? A dot density map uses uniform dots to represent a specific quantity of a given geographic feature. You place one dot for every phone check. Suddenly, a massive clump of dots appears in the back corner of the room, because dot density maps visually communicate the spatial concentration of a phenomenon! 

Now, what if you want to show how badly they are distracted? A proportional symbol map uses symbols of different sizes to represent the magnitude of a specific variable. A giant circle for the kid watching full movies, a tiny circle for the kid just checking the time. Or maybe you want to map the terrible airflow in the school. An isoline map connects points of equal value with continuous lines, linking all the 85-degree spots together. The ultimate version of this is for hikers: topographic maps use contour lines to represent changes in land elevation, so you don't accidentally walk off a cliff. And finally, the absolute hallucination of cartography: a cartogram distorts the physical size of geographic regions to reflect the statistical value of a specific variable. If you map the number of tardy slips, the back row swells up to the size of a blimp, while the front row shrinks to a toothpick.

Now, why does that back row act like that? Grab a napkin, we are doing human geography. Spatial patterns describe the arrangement of geographic features across the surface of the Earth. To figure out the drama, we measure two things. Density measures the frequency of a specific feature occurring within a defined geographic area. Literally, how many kids are crammed into a 10-foot space? Meanwhile, dispersion describes the extent to which geographic features are spread out across an area. 

Think of a middle school dance. When the slow song plays, a clustered spatial pattern exists when geographic features are grouped closely together—that’s the brave kids huddled in the center. But when the lights turn on at the end of the night, a dispersed spatial pattern exists when geographic features are spread far apart, as everyone flees to the perimeter walls. 

Why do they flee? Physics and laziness! The friction of distance refers to the cost and effort required to overcome the physical space between two locations. It takes sweating, walking, and social awkwardness to cross that gym floor. Because of this friction, distance decay states that interaction between two locations declines as the physical distance between those locations increases. You aren't going to yell across the gym to ask someone out; you're just going to talk to the person next to you! 

And speaking of distance, pay attention to this next part because it breaks everyone's brain on the exam: scale. Map scale is the mathematical relationship between the distance on a map and the corresponding distance on the ground. Think like a math teacher looking at fractions. Which is a bigger slice of pizza: 1/10th or 1/1,000,000th? 1/10th! It’s a larger fraction! Therefore, a large-scale map shows a small geographic area in great detail. It is the zoomed-in security camera catching the heist up close! Conversely, a small-scale map shows a large geographic area with minimal detail. It’s the zoomed-out camera showing the whole city grid. Do not confuse this with geographic scale! Geographic scale ranges from local to global levels of analysis. A school board meeting is local geographic scale; climate change is global geographic scale.

Okay, final stretch. Where is all this gossip and data coming from? The government! The United States Constitution mandates a national census every ten years. It’s literally in the founding document. To pull this off, the United States Census Bureau collects demographic and geographic data about the American population. You, me, our incomes, our commutes. Geographers use census data to analyze spatial demographic shifts over time. They can literally see the hipsters moving into the old warehouse district before the hipsters even realize they are doing it!

But to track everyone, they need the ultimate digital skeleton. Topologically Integrated Geographic Encoding and Referencing is a digital database of geographic features. TIGER! It digitizes every street, river, and zip code in the nation. And who built this beast? The United States Census Bureau produces the Topologically Integrated Geographic Encoding and Referencing database so they can actually route the census workers to our doors. 

Once you have TIGER, you use the three infinity stones of modern geography. First, Global Positioning Systems use a network of satellites to determine exact mathematical locations on the surface of the Earth. That’s the blue dot on your phone. Second, remote sensing involves gathering data about the Earth from an elevated distance using satellites or aircraft. It’s the spy plane! Because it’s up high, remote sensing monitors environmental changes such as deforestation or urban sprawl without anyone ever touching the ground. 

And finally, the mastermind: GIS. Geographic Information Systems manage spatially referenced data. Imagine a clear plastic overhead projector sheet. Geographic Information Systems layer multiple types of spatial data to identify complex geographic relationships. You layer the TIGER road map, then you layer the census income data, then you layer the elevation isolines, and suddenly you know exactly where to build a new boutique coffee shop so rich hikers can buy a \$8 latte without driving too far!
