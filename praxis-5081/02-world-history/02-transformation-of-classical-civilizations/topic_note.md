When a massive physical structure collapses, the [kinetic energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_energy) does not simply vanish; it displaces outward, altering everything in its proximity. The same principle applies to human institutions. When **the [Western Roman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_Roman_Empire) collapsed in [476 CE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fall_of_the_Western_Roman_Empire)**, it did not mean the end of history in [Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Europe); rather, it meant the sudden evaporation of centralized authority. **The fall of the Western Roman Empire created a [political vacuum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_vacuum) in [Western Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_Europe).** The vast, interconnected infrastructure of the [Pax Romana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pax_Romana) gave way to localized [survivalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Survivalism). For the next millennium, the story of [global history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_history) is the story of how invasions, the spread of brilliant new ideas (both scientific and religious), and the relentless human desire for trade forged a totally new, interconnected world. 

![Map showing the political division of the Roman Empire into Western and Eastern halves by 476 CE. The collapse of centralized authority in the West created a vast power vacuum across Europe.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/628px-Western_and_Eastern_Roman_Empires_476AD(3).PNG)

As a future [social studies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_studies) teacher, your task is to show your students that the period from [300 to 1400 CE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_Ages) is not a disjointed list of random kings and wars. It is an era defined by *networks*. Ideas, [trade goods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trade), armies, and even [microbes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism) traveled along the exact same routes. Let’s break down the mechanics of this transformation.

## 1. The Post-Roman Vacuum and the Fortress of Europe

Nature abhors a vacuum, and so does [geopolitics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geopolitics). Without [Roman legions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_legion) to patrol the frontiers, Western Europe became highly vulnerable. Between the eighth and eleventh centuries, **[Viking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vikings) invasions targeted coastal and river settlements throughout Europe**. The physics of [Scandinavian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scandinavia) engineering made this terror possible: **Scandinavian Vikings utilized shallow-draft [longships](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Longship) to navigate both open oceans and inland rivers**, allowing them to strike deep into the European interior without warning. 

![Schematic diagram of a Viking longship. Its shallow-draft design allowed Scandinavian raiders to navigate both deep oceans and shallow inland rivers, striking deep into the European interior.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Viking_longship.png)

Simultaneously, Europe faced pressure from [Magyars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hungarians) to the east and [Muslim](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muslims) forces to the south. How does a society respond when the central government cannot protect it? It [decentralizes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decentralization). **[Feudalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feudalism) emerged in [medieval Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_Ages) as a decentralized system of mutual obligation.** Land was exchanged for military service and labor. This was not a primitive regression; it was a highly logical adaptation. **European feudalism provided local defense against invasions by Vikings, Magyars, and Muslims.** 

![A medieval manuscript illustrating the three rigid classes of feudal society: those who prayed (clergy), those who fought (knights), and those who worked (peasantry). This decentralized system provided necessary local defense and economic stability.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Cleric-Knight-Workman.jpg)

Amidst this political fragmentation, institutions of continuity were required. 
*   In the West, **the [Roman Catholic Church](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catholic_Church) served as the primary unifying institution in medieval Western Europe**, providing shared cultural identity, administration, and [literacy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Literacy).
*   In the East, the [Roman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Empire) never actually fell; it simply became [Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greeks). **The [Byzantine Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantine_Empire) preserved [Roman legal traditions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_law) through the compilation of [Justinian's Code](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corpus_Juris_Civilis).** Crucially for the trajectory of the West, **the Byzantine Empire acted as a geographical and military buffer between Western Europe and the [Islamic world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muslim_world).**

> **Teacher's Framing Tip:** Have your students imagine Feudalism as a [neighborhood watch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neighborhood_watch) program that mutated into a government. When the local police ([Rome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rome)) disappear, you rely on the guy down the street with a high wall and weapons (the [Lord](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lord)). You farm his land; he keeps you alive. It is an economic and military transaction.

## 2. The Rise of Islam and the Unification of the Middle East

While Europe was decentralizing, the [Arabian Peninsula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula) was experiencing a profound unification. **The [Prophet Muhammad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muhammad) founded [Islam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islam) in the early [seventh century](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/7th_century) CE in the Arabian Peninsula.** Unlike earlier religions that spread slowly over centuries, Islam expanded with breathtaking speed. **The [spread of Islam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spread_of_Islam) unified diverse cultural groups across the [Middle East](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_East) and [North Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Africa) under a common religious framework**, creating a vast zone of shared language ([Arabic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabic)), law, and commerce.

![Map detailing the rapid territorial expansion of the Islamic Caliphates between 622 and 750 CE, which unified diverse cultural groups from the Middle East to the Iberian Peninsula under a common framework.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Map_of_expansion_of_Caliphate.svg)

However, rapid expansion inherently brings friction. A permanent [political schism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schism) emerged almost immediately. **The [Sunni](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunni_Islam) and [Shia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shia_Islam) branches of Islam split over the [succession of leadership after the death of Muhammad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Succession_to_Muhammad) in [632 CE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/632).** 

Despite internal divisions, the political states representing the Islamic world—the [Caliphates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caliphate)—became the global centers of gravity. 
*   **The [Umayyad Caliphate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Umayyad_Caliphate) established [Damascus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Damascus) as an imperial capital** and pushed the borders of the empire into Europe. In fact, **Muslim forces conquered the [Iberian Peninsula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula) in [711 CE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Umayyad_conquest_of_Hispania)**, transforming modern-day Spain into a flourishing hub of Islamic culture ([Al-Andalus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Andalus)).
*   They attempted to push further north into [Francia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Francia) (modern [France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/France)), but **[Charles Martel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Martel) defeated an Umayyad army at the [Battle of Tours](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Tours) in [732 CE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/732).** This is a massive turning point in world history, as **the Battle of Tours halted the northward expansion of Islam into Western Europe.**

Following the Umayyads, the center of Islamic power shifted east. **The [Abbasid Caliphate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abbasid_Caliphate) established the city of [Baghdad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baghdad) as a new imperial capital in [762 CE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/762).** If you want to show your students the pinnacle of medieval intellectual achievement, look at Abbasid Baghdad. 

During the [Islamic Golden Age](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic_Golden_Age), Baghdad was the [Silicon Valley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley) of its day. **The Abbasid Caliphate sponsored the [translation of ancient Greek and Indian texts into Arabic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graeco-Arabic_translation_movement).** Through an institution known as **the [House of Wisdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/House_of_Wisdom) in Baghdad, which functioned as a major intellectual center**, scholars preserved and advanced [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics_in_the_medieval_Islamic_world), [astronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy_in_the_medieval_Islamic_world), and [medicine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medicine_in_the_medieval_Islamic_world).

![A page from Al-Khwarizmi's influential 9th-century manuscript on algebra. The Abbasid Caliphate's sponsorship of scholarship at the House of Wisdom preserved and significantly advanced global mathematics and science.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Image-Al-Kit%C4%81b_al-mu%E1%B8%ABta%E1%B9%A3ar_f%C4%AB_%E1%B8%A5is%C4%81b_al-%C4%9Fabr_wa-l-muq%C4%81bala.jpg)

## 3. Clashes and Exchanges: The Crusades

By the 11th century, religious boundaries were hardening. In [1054 CE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1054), [Christianity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christianity) suffered its own massive fracture: **The [Great Schism of 1054 CE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East%E2%80%93West_Schism) divided Christianity into the Roman Catholic Church and the [Eastern Orthodox Church](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church).** 

Shortly after, the Byzantine Empire (Eastern Orthodox) found itself under intense pressure from [Seljuk Turks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seljuk_Empire) (Islamic) and appealed to the West for help. **[Pope Urban II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pope_Urban_II) initiated the [First Crusade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_Crusade) at the [Council of Clermont](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Council_of_Clermont) in [1095 CE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1095).** **The [Crusades](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crusades) aimed to reclaim control of the [Holy Land](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holy_Land) from Islamic rule.**

The timeline of the Crusades is a pendulum of conquest and [reconquest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reconquista):
*   **Christian [Crusaders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crusades) captured [Jerusalem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerusalem) during the First Crusade in [1099 CE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siege_of_Jerusalem_%281099%29).**
*   A century later, the brilliant **Muslim commander [Saladin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saladin) recaptured Jerusalem from Christian control in [1187 CE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siege_of_Jerusalem_%281187%29).**
*   The [holy wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holy_war) eventually devolved into naked [political opportunism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Opportunism). **The [Fourth Crusade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourth_Crusade) resulted in the [sack of the Christian city of Constantinople](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sack_of_Constantinople) in [1204 CE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1204).** Fellow Christians burning a Christian capital exposes the economic and political motivations hiding beneath religious rhetoric. Consequently, **the sack of Constantinople during the Fourth Crusade severely weakened the Byzantine Empire**, leaving it vulnerable to its [eventual collapse in 1453](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fall_of_Constantinople).

![A 15th-century manuscript depicting the Crusader sack of Constantinople in 1204. The attack on a fellow Christian capital severely weakened the Byzantine Empire and exposed the political opportunism underlying the holy wars.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/ConquestOfConstantinopleByTheCrusadersIn1204.jpg)

### The Great Irony of the Crusades
Here is the core [cause-and-effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Causality) relationship you must drill into your students: the Crusades were a military failure for Europe, but an *economic and intellectual triumph*. 
1.  **The Crusades stimulated demand in Europe for Middle Eastern and Asian trade goods** ([spices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spice), [silks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silk), [perfumes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perfume)). European palates had been awakened. 
2.  **The Crusades facilitated the transfer of [Greco-Roman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greco-Roman_world) and Islamic knowledge to Western Europe.** The Crusaders went to conquer, but they came back with [Arabic numerals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hindu%E2%80%93Arabic_numeral_system), [algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_algebra), translated [Greek philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek_philosophy), and [medical treatises](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medical_literature). This intellectual injection directly laid the groundwork for the [European Renaissance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Renaissance).

## 4. The Mongol World Engine

In the 13th century, the geopolitical map was violently redrawn by a new variable from the [Asian steppes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eurasian_Steppe). **[Genghis Khan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genghis_Khan) united the nomadic [Mongol tribes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongols) in [1206 CE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1206).** Through terrifyingly effective [cavalry tactics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cavalry_tactics) and [siege engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siege_engine), **the [Mongol Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongol_Empire) became the largest contiguous land empire in human history.**

Their expansion was devastating to the existing Islamic power structures. **Mongol forces [sacked Baghdad in 1258 CE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siege_of_Baghdad_%281258%29).** The destruction was absolute—rivers reportedly ran black with the ink of the books from the House of Wisdom and red with blood. **The Mongol sack of Baghdad ended the Abbasid Caliphate.**

![A 14th-century illustration depicting the Mongol siege and sack of Baghdad in 1258, a devastating military event that permanently ended the Abbasid Caliphate.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Fall_Of_Baghdad_(Diez_Albums).jpg)

Yet, the [Mongol conquests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongol_invasions_and_conquests) are a paradox. Once the bloodshed stopped, the sheer scale of Mongol control created unprecedented stability. **The [Pax Mongolica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pax_Mongolica) facilitated safe travel and trade along the [Silk Road](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silk_Road).** For the first time, a [merchant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Merchant) could walk from [Italy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italy) to [China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China) under the protection of a single empire. 

> **A Principle of Networks:** Trade routes never carry *only* goods. They are arteries for ideas. Just as Islam spread along trade routes, centuries earlier **[Buddhism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buddhism) [spread from India to China along the Silk Road trade networks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silk_Road_transmission_of_Buddhism).** 

## 5. Harnessing Geography: African and Indian Ocean Trade

While the Silk Road dominated [Eurasia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eurasia), two other massive trade networks reshaped the [Southern Hemisphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere). 

### Trans-Saharan Trade
Imagine trying to cross an ocean of sand. You need the right "ship." **[Trans-Saharan trade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trans-Saharan_trade) routes relied on [camel caravans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camel_train) for transportation.** The economics of this route were based on a perfectly balanced geographical equation: **Trans-Saharan trade networks primarily exchanged [West African](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/West_Africa) gold for [North African](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Africa) [salt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_salt).** (Salt was essential for preserving food and retaining water in human bodies).

As Arab traders crossed the desert looking for gold, they brought their faith. **Muslim merchants introduced Islam to West Africa via Trans-Saharan trade routes.** This was largely a peaceful, economically driven religious conversion. The immense wealth of these West African Islamic kingdoms was showcased when **the [Mali Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mali_Empire) ruler [Mansa Musa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mansa_Musa) completed a highly publicized [pilgrimage to Mecca](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hajj) in [1324 CE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1324),** dispensing so much gold along the way that he temporarily collapsed the economy of [Cairo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cairo).

![Mansa Musa, ruler of the Mali Empire, depicted holding a gold nugget in the 1375 Catalan Atlas. His famously lavish pilgrimage to Mecca highlighted the immense wealth generated by exchanging West African gold for North African salt.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Catalan_Atlas_BNF_Sheet_6_Mansa_Musa.jpg)

Further south, demographic shifts were fundamentally altering the continent. **[Bantu migrations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bantu_expansion) spread [agriculture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_agriculture) and [ironworking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron_metallurgy_in_Africa) technology throughout [sub-Saharan Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa).** When these [Bantu peoples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bantu_peoples) reached the East Coast and interacted with Arab traders, a beautiful linguistic and cultural [synthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syncretism) occurred: **[Swahili culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swahili_culture) emerged on the East African coast from the blending of Bantu and Arab Islamic influences.**

### Indian Ocean Trade
The [Indian Ocean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_Ocean) was the world's most dynamic maritime highway. It was governed by [meteorology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteorology): **[Indian Ocean trade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_Ocean_trade) relied on the seasonal patterns of [monsoon winds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monsoon).** Merchants knew that the winds blew northeast for half the year and southwest for the other half. 

![Diagram of the seasonal monsoon wind patterns in the Indian Ocean. Maritime merchants relied heavily on these predictable seasonal winds to time their trade voyages between Africa, India, and Southeast Asia.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Indian_Ocean_Monsoon.jpg)

Technological innovations optimized this travel:
*   **Arab merchants utilized the [astrolabe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrolabe) for [maritime navigation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Navigation) in the Indian Ocean**, allowing them to calculate [latitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latitude) using the stars.
*   **The [lateen sail](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lateen) allowed merchant ships to sail against the wind in the Indian Ocean.** (This triangular sail acts like an [airplane wing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wing), creating [lift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lift_%28force%29) that pulls the ship forward even when sailing into a [headwind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Headwind_and_tailwind)).

![Diagram of a lateen sail. This triangular aerodynamic design acts like a wing, allowing maritime merchants to sail effectively against headwind currents.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Rigging-lateen-sail.svg)

Just like the Sahara, this network moved more than spices. **Indian Ocean trade networks facilitated the spread of Islam to coastal East Africa and [Southeast Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southeast_Asia).** 

Simultaneously, Islam was moving overland into [South Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Asia). **The [Delhi Sultanate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Delhi_Sultanate) established Islamic rule over large parts of the [Indian subcontinent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_subcontinent) starting in the thirteenth century.** The collision of Islamic [monotheism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monotheism) and [Hindu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hinduism) [polytheism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polytheism) was complex, but it ultimately **resulted in the creation of a [syncretic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syncretism) Indo-Islamic culture**, blending [architecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indo-Islamic_architecture), art, and administrative practices.

## 6. The Unintended Consequence of Connection: The Black Death

We end this era with a stark lesson in the dangers of hyper-connectivity. The Pax Mongolica made trade seamless, but microbes do not care if a host is a merchant or an emperor. **The [Bubonic Plague](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bubonic_plague) spread across Eurasia in the [fourteenth century](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/14th_century) along established trade routes.** 

![Map showing the rapid spread of the Bubonic Plague across Eurasia and into Europe. The hyper-connectivity of the Pax Mongolica allowed the pathogen to travel swiftly along established trade routes.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Bubonic_plague_map_2.png)

The impact was apocalyptic. **The [Black Death](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_Death) drastically reduced the European population in the mid-fourteenth century**, killing roughly a third to half of the continent. 

But as a social studies teacher, you must focus on the *economic fallout* of this tragedy. Think of this as a [supply and demand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supply_and_demand) graph. The supply of labor suddenly plummeted. **The massive [population loss from the Black Death](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consequences_of_the_Black_Death) caused severe [labor shortages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shortage) in medieval Europe.** 

When labor is scarce, its value goes up. [Peasants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peasant) who survived suddenly realized they had leverage. They could demand wages or better conditions from their lords. Therefore, **labor shortages from the Black Death accelerated the decline of the [manorial system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manorialism) in Western Europe.** The decentralized, rigid system of feudalism began to unravel, paving the way for the rise of centralized [nation-states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nation-state) and the [early modern](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early_modern_period) economy.

### Final Study Summary for the 5081 Exam
To ace the exam, remember these cause-and-effect pairings:
*   **[Fall of Rome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fall_of_the_Western_Roman_Empire)** $\rightarrow$ [Power vacuum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_vacuum) $\rightarrow$ Rise of [Feudalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feudalism) & the unifying power of the [Catholic Church](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catholic_Church).
*   **Invasions ([Vikings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vikings)/[Magyars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hungarians))** $\rightarrow$ Need for local defense $\rightarrow$ Solidification of the [manorial system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manorialism).
*   **[Spread of Islam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spread_of_Islam)** $\rightarrow$ Preservation of Greek/Indian texts ([Abbasids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abbasid_Caliphate)) $\rightarrow$ Eventual transfer back to Europe via [Crusades](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crusades).
*   **The [Crusades](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crusades)** $\rightarrow$ Military failure / Economic success $\rightarrow$ High demand for Eastern goods.
*   **[Mongol Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongol_Empire)** $\rightarrow$ Sack of [Baghdad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baghdad) (ends Abbasids) AND [Pax Mongolica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pax_Mongolica) $\rightarrow$ Trade flourishes.
*   **Trade Routes** $\rightarrow$ Spread of Islam to [West Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/West_Africa) (Camels) and [SE Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southeast_Asia) ([Monsoons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monsoon)) $\rightarrow$ Also spreads the [Bubonic Plague](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bubonic_plague).
*   **[Black Death](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_Death)** $\rightarrow$ Labor shortages $\rightarrow$ End of European Feudalism.
