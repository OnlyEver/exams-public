Imagine walking into your classroom. The desks face the front, you raise your hand when you want to speak, and a bell tells you exactly when it is time to pack up your backpack. Nobody is born knowing how to do this. It is like the rules of a video game that you have to learn by playing. **Culture is the complex system of meaning and behavior that defines the way of life for a given group or society.** It works like an invisible operating system running in the background of your life, telling you how to act, what things mean, and how to build your world.

To understand how the world works, you have to understand how people learn and change the rules of this big game. 

## The Architecture of Reality: Elements of Culture

If culture is like an operating system, how does it get installed in your brain? You are not born knowing how to wait in line for lunch or what your favorite food is. **Culture is learned through social interaction rather than being biologically inherited.** You pick it up by hanging out with family, friends, and neighbors.

Scientists divide culture into two main buckets:

1.  **Material culture consists of the tangible physical objects that members of a society create, use, and share.** "Tangible" just means things you can actually touch. Think of smartphones, skateboards, pizza boxes, and your school building. 
2.  **Nonmaterial culture includes the abstract creations of society such as beliefs, values, rules, and language.** You cannot touch these. A \$10 bill is a piece of material culture (paper), but the *belief* that you can trade it for two slices of pizza is nonmaterial culture.

At the very bottom of this system is the idea of a symbol. **A symbol is anything that carries a specific culturally recognized meaning.** A thumbs-up, a red stop sign, or a sports team logo are all symbols. When you organize these symbols using rules, you get language. **Language is a set of symbols and rules that provides a complex communication system.** 

Language does more than just help us talk; it actually shapes how our brains see the world! **The Sapir-Whorf hypothesis states that the language a person speaks determines or heavily influences the speaker's perception of reality.** (You should also know that **the Sapir-Whorf hypothesis is also known in linguistic anthropology as the principle of linguistic relativity.**) If your language has many different words for different types of rain, you will actually notice the differences in the weather much better than someone whose language only has one word for rain.

## The Rules of the Game: Values, Norms, and Sanctions

Every society has rules so that everything runs smoothly. 

> **Values are abstract standards in a society or group that define ideal principles of what is considered good, right, or desirable.** 
> **Norms are the specific cultural expectations and rules that govern behavior in particular situations.**

For example, if your community *values* honesty, the *norm* might be "do not cheat on your math test." 

Not all rules are equally serious. **Sociologist William Graham Sumner categorized cultural norms into folkways, mores, and laws.** This helps us see which rules are just polite habits and which are serious crimes.

| Type of Norm | What is it? | Everyday Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Folkways** | **Folkways are informal norms that dictate everyday behavior and carry minor sanctions if violated.** | Wearing your shirt inside out. It is weird, but you will not get in trouble with the police. |
| **Mores** | **Mores are strict norms that control moral and ethical behavior and carry severe sanctions if violated.** | Bullying someone or lying to your best friend. People will get very angry because it breaks serious trust. |
| **Taboos** | **Taboos are the most strongly ingrained mores within a society. Violating a taboo causes extreme disgust or expulsion from the social group.** | Eating human flesh. Just the thought of doing this completely grosses people out. |
| **Laws** | **Laws are formal, standardized norms that have been enacted by a legislative body to regulate behavior.** | Stealing a bicycle. The government writes these rules down and uses police to stop people from breaking them. |

How does society make sure people follow the rules? Through a system of rewards and punishments called sanctions. **Sanctions are the social reactions of approval or disapproval used to enforce cultural norms.** 

*   **Positive sanctions are rewards given for conforming to cultural norms.** This could be a high-five, a gold star on homework, or earning an allowance. Let's look at how positive sanctions can add up with a quick math example:
    1. Suppose you earn \$5 every week as a positive sanction for doing your chores.
    2. You keep up this good behavior for 4 weeks.
    3. Multiply your weekly reward by the number of weeks: 5 x 4 = 20.
    4. You earned \$20 in positive sanctions!
*   **Negative sanctions are punishments issued for violating cultural norms.** This could mean losing your video game time, getting grounded, or sitting in the penalty box during a hockey game.

## The Factory of the Self: Socialization

Because we are not born knowing our culture, we have to be taught it. **Socialization is the lifelong social experience by which individuals develop their human potential and learn culture.** 

When we look at the whole society passing down its rules, we call it cultural transmission. **Cultural transmission is the process by which one generation passes societal culture to the next generation.** When scientists look at *you* specifically learning your culture, they use another word: **enculturation is the specific anthropological term for the process of learning the traditional content and norms of one's own primary culture.**

You learn culture from "agents." **Agents of socialization are the individuals, groups, and institutions that create the social context in which socialization takes place.** Here are the four main ones:

1.  **The Family:** This **is typically the most significant agent of socialization during an individual's early childhood.** Your parents or guardians are the first ones to teach you how to talk and how to behave.
2.  **Schools:** **Schools act as an agent of socialization by teaching formal academic knowledge and the hidden curriculum of broader cultural values.** They teach you spelling, but the "hidden curriculum" teaches you to share, follow schedules, and listen to teachers.
3.  **Peer Groups:** These are kids your own age. **Peer groups act as an agent of socialization by allowing children to form relationships and escape the direct supervision of adults.** With friends, you figure out your own hobbies and personality outside of your family.
4.  **Mass Media:** **Mass media acts as a major agent of socialization by transmitting standardized cultural messages and norms to a large, diverse audience.** Think of YouTube, movies, and social media. They show millions of people what is "cool" all at the same time.

## Navigating Differences: Subcultures and Relativism

Sometimes, smaller groups inside a big society make their own mini-rules. This is called a subculture. **A subculture is a group of people whose values and norms of behavior differ to some degree from those of the dominant culture.** Skateboarders, theater kids, and serious gamers are subcultures. But remember, **members of a subculture still participate in and share the broader values of the dominant culture.** A skateboarder still uses regular money and goes to the local grocery store.

On the other hand, **a counterculture is a subculture created as a direct reaction against the values of the dominant culture.** In this case, **members of a counterculture actively reject and oppose the broader values of the dominant culture.** It is like deciding to play a totally different game. People who quit modern society to live totally off the grid are acting as a counterculture.

When humans meet people from completely different cultures, it can sometimes feel strange. People often default to **ethnocentrism**, which **is the practice of judging another culture entirely by the standards and values of one's own culture.** For instance, if you think eating insects is "wrong" just because people in your town only eat chicken, that is ethnocentrism. Usually, **ethnocentrism often involves the belief that one's own culture is superior to all other cultures.**

To be fair and open-minded, scientists use a different method. **Anthropologist Franz Boas popularized the concept of cultural relativism in the early twentieth century.** **Cultural relativism is the practice of evaluating a culture by its own standards rather than through the lens of another culture.** Instead of saying "their food rules are weird," you try to understand *why* those rules make perfect sense for the place where they live.

## The Colliding World: Cultural Change and Globalization

Culture never stays still. **Cultural diffusion is the process by which a cultural item or idea spreads from one group or society to another.** Think about how pizza came from Italy but is now loved all over the world! 

Today, things spread faster than ever because of globalization. **Globalization refers to the worldwide integration of international trade, finance, and culture into a highly interconnected network.** Thanks to the internet and airplanes, the world is super connected. **Globalization accelerates cultural diffusion by drastically increasing contact between distinct, geographically separated societies.** 

This massive mixing leads to some big changes:
*   **Cultural leveling is the process by which different cultures approach each other as a result of extensive travel and communication.** This is why you can find a McDonald's or people wearing jeans in almost every major city on Earth. Things start to look similar.
*   **Cultural imperialism is the extensive infusion of one powerful nation's culture into other nations.** This is when a really wealthy, powerful country dominates a smaller country's movies, music, and businesses, pushing the local culture out of the way.

When groups of people move to a new place, they have to adapt. There are three ways this happens:

| Adaptation Process | What does it mean? | Everyday Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Assimilation** | **Assimilation is the process by which a minority individual or group takes on the characteristics of the dominant culture.** | Moving to a new country and completely stopping the use of your old language to only speak the new one. |
| **Acculturation** | **Acculturation is the process of adopting the cultural traits of another group while still retaining distinct elements of the original culture.** | Eating American hot dogs on the 4th of July, but still speaking your native language at home with your family. |
| **Syncretism** | **Syncretism is the blending of traits from two different cultures to form a new, original cultural trait or practice.** | Mixing traditional Mexican recipes with American fast-food styles to invent a totally new "Tex-Mex" taco! |

### The Speed of Change: Cultural Lag

Change does not always happen evenly, which can cause problems. **Cultural lag occurs when a society's material culture changes faster than its nonmaterial culture.** (Make sure you remember that **Sociologist William F. Ogburn coined the term cultural lag in his 1922 book Social Change.**)

Why does this cause trouble? Because **cultural lag creates social disruption because technological advancements outpace the development of corresponding social norms and laws.** 

Let's use some step-by-step math to see exactly how this "lag" adds up over time:
1. Suppose a brilliant new material invention (like artificial intelligence) is created in the year 2020.
2. It takes society 5 years to figure out the nonmaterial rules (new laws and safety guidelines) to control it.
3. We add the years together to find when the laws finally exist: 2020 + 5 = 2025.
4. That means there is a 5-year gap (from 2020 to 2025) where people have the powerful new tool but no rules for it. That 5-year gap is the cultural lag!

During those 5 years, things can get pretty chaotic. Learning about culture helps us understand these gaps so we can figure out the rules together and make the world a better place to live!
