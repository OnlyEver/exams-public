Have you ever wondered why your friends act the way they do, why you can memorize an entire video game map but forget your homework, or why toddlers love playing peek-a-boo? If you want to understand how people think and learn, you need to look at psychology. Psychology is basically the user manual for the human brain. By figuring out how our minds grow, how we remember things, and what makes our personalities unique, you can understand yourself and the people around you a whole lot better!

## The Foundations of Psychology: Mapping the Human Mind

To understand how the brain works today, we have to look back at the people who started studying it. A long time ago, people just guessed why we act the way we do. But eventually, people started treating the mind like a real science. 

**Wilhelm Wundt opened the first experimental psychology laboratory in 1879**, which was kind of like building the first-ever testing center for human thoughts in Germany! Soon after that, the science made its way to America, where **William James wrote the first major psychology textbook in the United States**. 

As more people studied the brain, they started looking at it through different "lenses" or viewpoints. 

### The Seven Major Approaches

Psychologists use seven main ways to explain why we do what we do:

| Approach | What It Means | Example in Real Life |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Behavioral** | **The behavioral approach in psychology focuses strictly on observable actions** (things you can actually see someone do). It **emphasizes the role of the environment in shaping human actions.** A scientist named **John B. Watson established the psychological school of behaviorism**. | Earning points in a classroom game to get a pizza party. |
| **Cognitive** | **The cognitive approach in psychology studies internal mental processes.** Basically, it **examines processes like thinking, memory, and problem-solving.** | Figuring out a tricky puzzle in a video game by remembering a clue from earlier. |
| **Biological** | **The biological approach in psychology examines how genetics influence behavior** and also **examines how brain chemistry influences behavior.** | Realizing someone has trouble paying attention because their brain chemicals are a bit different. |
| **Psychodynamic** | **The psychodynamic approach in psychology emphasizes the influence of unconscious drives on behavior** (hidden feelings you don't even realize you have). **Sigmund Freud founded the psychodynamic approach to psychology.** | Getting grumpy because of a hidden, ignored worry. |
| **Humanistic** | **The humanistic approach in psychology emphasizes human free will** (making your own choices) and **emphasizes an individual's inherent drive toward self-actualization** (wanting to be the absolute best version of yourself). | A coach reminding you that you have the power to achieve your dreams. |
| **Sociocultural** | **The sociocultural approach in psychology analyzes how cultural context shapes human behavior** and **analyzes how social interactions shape human behavior.** | Not talking out of turn because your culture teaches strict respect for adults. |
| **Evolutionary** | **The evolutionary approach in psychology explains behavior through the principles of natural selection.** | Being naturally afraid of snakes because our ancient ancestors needed to avoid them to survive! |

## Learning: The Mechanics of Behavioral Change

How do we actually learn new things? In psychology, "learning" just means a long-lasting change in your behavior because of something you experienced. 

### Classical Conditioning: Learning by Association

**Classical conditioning is a learning process that pairs a biologically potent stimulus with a previously neutral stimulus.** That sounds complicated, but it just means linking a natural reaction to something that didn't matter before. **Ivan Pavlov first described classical conditioning through experiments with dogs.** 

Imagine smelling fresh pizza.
* **An unconditioned stimulus naturally and automatically triggers a physiological response without prior learning.** (The pizza).
* **An unconditioned response is an unlearned reaction to an unconditioned stimulus.** (Your mouth watering at the smell of the pizza—you don't have to practice this!).
* Now, imagine an oven timer dings right before the pizza comes out. This ding becomes a **conditioned stimulus: a previously neutral stimulus that triggers a response after being paired with an unconditioned stimulus.** (The timer sound).
* Finally, you have the **conditioned response: a learned reaction to a previously neutral stimulus.** (Your mouth watering just from hearing the timer ding, even if you can't smell the pizza yet).

> **When Conditioning Changes**
> What happens if the timer dings, but there is never any pizza? Eventually, you stop caring. **Extinction in classical conditioning occurs when a conditioned stimulus is repeatedly presented without the unconditioned stimulus.** 
> Sometimes, brains get confused. **Stimulus generalization occurs when a response conditioned to one stimulus is elicited by similar stimuli.** (If your mouth waters at *any* bell sound, not just the oven timer). On the flip side, **stimulus discrimination is the learned ability to distinguish between a conditioned stimulus and other irrelevant stimuli.** (You only drool for the oven timer, and you ignore the doorbell).

### Operant Conditioning: Learning by Consequence

**Operant conditioning is a learning process where behavior is controlled by consequences.** A famous psychologist named **B.F. Skinner developed the fundamental principles of operant conditioning.** It’s all about rewards and punishments!

1. **Positive reinforcement involves adding a desirable stimulus to increase the frequency of a behavior.** (Getting a \$5 allowance for cleaning your room).
2. **Negative reinforcement involves removing an undesirable stimulus to increase the frequency of a behavior.** (Your mom says you don't have to do the dishes because you got an A on your math test).
3. **Positive punishment involves presenting an unfavorable outcome to decrease the frequency of a behavior.** (Having to run extra laps at practice because you were goofing off).
4. **Negative punishment involves removing a favorable stimulus to decrease the frequency of a behavior.** (Losing your phone for a day because you broke a rule).

You don't usually learn a huge task all at once. Instead, we use **shaping**, which **is an operant conditioning procedure that reinforces successive approximations toward a target behavior.** This means rewarding tiny steps—like giving a dog a treat for just looking at a skateboard, then for touching it, and eventually for riding it!

### Observational Learning: The Power of Modeling

You also learn just by watching people. **Observational learning occurs by watching the behaviors of others and the consequences of those behaviors.** **Albert Bandura demonstrated observational learning through the Bobo doll experiment**, where kids watched adults act mean to a blow-up doll, and then the kids copied that exact same mean behavior! 

## Memory: Information Processing in the Human Mind

Think of your brain like a computer. For you to remember something, three steps have to happen:
1. **Encoding is the process of converting information into a form usable in memory.** (Typing the info on a keyboard).
2. **Storage is the process of retaining encoded information over time.** (Saving the file to your hard drive).
3. **Retrieval is the process of bringing stored information out of memory and into conscious awareness.** (Clicking the file later to open it back up).

### The Architecture of Memory

**Richard Atkinson and Richard Shiffrin proposed the multi-store memory model in 1968.** This famous map shows how memories travel. **The multi-store memory model divides memory into sensory, short-term, and long-term storage systems.**

* First is the loading screen: **Sensory memory holds briefly presented information for a fraction of a second.** (Like the quick flash of lightning).
* If you pay attention, it moves on: **Short-term memory holds a small amount of information in an active state for a brief period.** 
* How much can it hold? **Short-term memory has a typical capacity of about seven items.** Let's calculate the range of things most people can hold in their short-term memory (this is known as the "7 plus or minus 2" rule):
  1. Start with the average number of items: 7.
  2. Add 2 to find the maximum average amount: 7 + 2 = 9 items.
  3. Subtract 2 from the average to find the minimum amount: 7 - 2 = 5 items.
  So, your short-term memory can hold between 5 and 9 things at once!
* If you study the information, it goes to the vault: **Long-term memory is the relatively permanent storage system with a practically limitless capacity.**

### Types of Long-Term Memory

Long-term memory is split into two big categories.

**Explicit memory involves conscious recollection of factual information or past experiences.** (Things you can say out loud).
* **Episodic memory is a type of explicit memory containing personal experiences and specific events.** (Remembering your 10th birthday party).
* **Semantic memory is a type of explicit memory containing general knowledge and facts.** (Knowing that the capital of France is Paris).

**Implicit memory involves unconscious retention of learned skills or conditioned associations.** (Things your body just knows how to do).
* **Procedural memory is a type of implicit memory responsible for knowing how to perform motor skills.** (Riding a bike without having to think about how to balance).

### Why We Forget

Why do we forget the math formula we learned last week? A scientist named **Hermann Ebbinghaus discovered the forgetting curve**, which proves that if we don't practice new information, we forget most of it super fast. 

Also, memories bump into each other!
* **Proactive interference occurs when older memories disrupt the recall of newer information.** (You try to learn a new video game's controls, but your fingers keep pressing the buttons from your old favorite game).
* **Retroactive interference occurs when new learning disrupts the recall of older information.** (You memorize a new locker combination, and suddenly you completely forget your bike lock's old combination).

## Personality: The Enduring Traits of the Individual

Have you ever noticed that the funny kid in class is usually funny everywhere? **Personality psychology studies the enduring patterns of thoughts, feelings, and behaviors that distinguish individuals.** 

### Psychodynamic Perspectives
A very famous (and sometimes strange) psychologist named **Sigmund Freud divided the human personality into the id, ego, and superego.**

* **The id operates on the pleasure principle.** It is the wild part of your brain! **The id seeks to satisfy basic survival and aggressive desires**—it wants what it wants right now.
* **The superego represents internalized moral standards and societal ideals.** This is like the strict rule-follower in your head trying to be perfect.
* Stuck in the middle is the boss: **The ego operates on the reality principle to mediate between the id and external reality.** It tries to find a fair compromise.

When the ego gets stressed out trying to balance things, it uses tricks. **Defense mechanisms are unconscious psychological strategies used by the ego to cope with anxiety.** A big one is repression. **Repression is a defense mechanism that banishes anxiety-arousing thoughts from consciousness**, almost like sweeping dirt under a rug so you don't have to look at it.

### Trait Theory
Today, psychologists prefer looking at personality like a recipe. **Trait theory focuses on identifying and measuring persistent characteristics that determine behavior.** 

The most popular recipe is the Big Five. **The Big Five personality model identifies openness, conscientiousness, extraversion, agreeableness, and neuroticism as core traits.** You might be super high in "extraversion" (meaning you love talking to big groups) but low in "conscientiousness" (meaning your backpack is a total mess!).

### Humanistic Perspectives
Some psychologists look at the bright side of humanity. 
* **Carl Rogers proposed that individuals require unconditional positive regard from others to develop a healthy self-concept.** That just means you need people to love and support you no matter what, so you can feel good about yourself.
* **Abraham Maslow proposed a hierarchy of human needs culminating in self-actualization.** He said you can't reach your highest potential (self-actualization) if you are hungry or feel unsafe. You have to build up from the basics!

## Human Development: The Moving Target

You aren't the same person you were when you were a baby! **Developmental psychology studies physical, cognitive, and social change throughout the human life span.**

### Cognitive Development (Jean Piaget)

A researcher named **Jean Piaget formulated a four-stage theory of cognitive development in children.** He figured out that kids' brains actually work differently at different ages!

1. **The sensorimotor stage is the first phase of Jean Piaget's theory of cognitive development** (from birth to 2 years old). Babies learn by touching and tasting. Most importantly, **infants develop object permanence during Jean Piaget's sensorimotor stage.** What's that? **Object permanence is the understanding that objects continue to exist even when out of sight.** (This is why peek-a-boo is so mind-blowing for babies!).
2. Next is the preoperational stage (ages 2 to 7). Here, **children use language but lack complex logical thought during Jean Piaget's preoperational stage.**
3. Then comes the concrete operational stage (ages 7 to 11). This is when **children gain the mental operations to think logically about physical events during Jean Piaget's concrete operational stage.** They can do math, but they need real-life examples to help them.
4. Finally, the formal operational stage (age 12 and up). At your age, **adolescents develop the capacity for abstract and hypothetical reasoning during Jean Piaget's formal operational stage.** You can now imagine "what if" scenarios!

### Sociocultural Cognitive Development (Lev Vygotsky)

Another expert, **Lev Vygotsky's sociocultural theory emphasizes the role of social interaction in cognitive development.** He believed we learn best by teaming up with others. 

He talked about a sweet spot for learning. **The zone of proximal development describes the difference between what a learner can do independently and what a learner can do with guidance.** It's like playing a video game level that is too hard to beat alone, but perfectly beatable if an older sibling gives you a few hints.

### Psychosocial Development (Erik Erikson)

While Piaget looked at how we think, **Erik Erikson developed a theory of psychosocial development containing eight stages across the lifespan.** He believed that as we grow up, **each stage in Erik Erikson's psychosocial theory centers on resolving a specific social crisis.**

It all starts when we are babies: **The trust versus mistrust stage is the first crisis in Erik Erikson's psychosocial theory.** If a baby is fed and hugged, they learn to trust the world! 

### Moral Development (Lawrence Kohlberg)

How do you know what is right and wrong? **Lawrence Kohlberg developed a three-level stage theory of moral reasoning.** 

1. **Preconventional Level:** 
   * Sometimes, **individuals in the preconventional stage of moral development obey rules primarily to avoid punishment.** (You don't steal a cookie because you don't want to get grounded).
   * Other times, **individuals in the preconventional stage of moral development obey rules primarily to gain concrete rewards.** (You clean up only to get your allowance).
2. **Conventional Level:** 
   * As we grow, **conventional morality upholds laws and rules to maintain social order.** (We follow the speed limit so no one crashes).
   * Also, **conventional morality upholds laws and rules to gain social approval.** (You act nice so people will think you are a good person).
3. **Postconventional Level:** 
   * Finally, the highest level! **Postconventional morality involves actions judged by self-defined ethical principles.** (You stand up against a bully, even if it gets you in trouble, because you know deep down that bullying is wrong).
