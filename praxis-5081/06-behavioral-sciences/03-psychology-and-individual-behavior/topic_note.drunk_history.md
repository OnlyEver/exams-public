Now, let's talk about the domestic economy of your classroom: how do you get these kids to actually learn? Think of learning as the currency exchange of the mind. Ivan Pavlov first described classical conditioning through experiments with dogs. Imagine Pavlov as a central banker who figured out how to peg a totally fake fiat currency to the gold standard. Classical conditioning is a learning process that pairs a biologically potent stimulus with a previously neutral stimulus. The gold standard in this economy is the unconditioned stimulus—it naturally and automatically triggers a physiological response without prior learning. The market's immediate, instinctual reaction is the unconditioned response, which is an unlearned reaction to an unconditioned stimulus. Now, introduce your fiat currency, like a ringing bell or a teacher clapping three times. A conditioned stimulus is a previously neutral stimulus that triggers a response after being paired with an unconditioned stimulus. Because of this artificial pairing, the market suddenly treats the paper money like real gold! The result is a conditioned response, which is a learned reaction to a previously neutral stimulus. 

But watch out for market crashes. Extinction in classical conditioning occurs when a conditioned stimulus is repeatedly presented without the unconditioned stimulus. If you clap three times but never actually start the fun activity, the currency loses all its value. Sometimes, the market gets confused and panics over anything that looks vaguely similar. Stimulus generalization occurs when a response conditioned to one stimulus is elicited by similar stimuli—like a kid fearing all authority figures because of one mean principal. But smart traders figure out the nuances: stimulus discrimination is the learned ability to distinguish between a conditioned stimulus and other irrelevant stimuli.

That’s classical conditioning—the involuntary stuff. But what about passing laws and enforcing them? B.F. Skinner developed the fundamental principles of operant conditioning, realizing that you have to actively legislate behavior. Operant conditioning is a learning process where behavior is controlled by consequences. You have four main tax brackets to work with here. Positive reinforcement involves adding a desirable stimulus to increase the frequency of a behavior—a tax rebate for raising their hands! Negative reinforcement involves removing an undesirable stimulus to increase the frequency of a behavior—canceling a pop quiz because they worked hard, removing a heavy tax burden to stimulate the local economy. Then come the penalties. Positive punishment involves presenting an unfavorable outcome to decrease the frequency of a behavior—slapping a massive tariff, like detention, on a kid who throws a paper airplane. Negative punishment involves removing a favorable stimulus to decrease the frequency of a behavior—an immediate embargo, confiscating their cell phone. You can't expect them to build Rome in a day, though, so you use municipal grants. Shaping is an operant conditioning procedure that reinforces successive approximations toward a target behavior. You reward the thesis statement, then the paragraph, then the whole essay. All the while, the citizens are watching the leadership. Albert Bandura demonstrated observational learning through the Bobo doll experiment, proving beyond a shadow of a doubt that observational learning occurs by watching the behaviors of others and the consequences of those behaviors. Now, what good are all these laws if the National Archives burn down? You have to understand how records are kept. Richard Atkinson and Richard Shiffrin proposed the multi-store memory model in 1968, outlining the bureaucratic pipeline of the brain. The multi-store memory model divides memory into sensory, short-term, and long-term storage systems. Let's trace a document through the bureaucracy. First, encoding is the process of converting information into a form usable in memory, like a clerk frantically translating a spoken word into official shorthand. Next, storage is the process of retaining encoded information over time, filing it away. Finally, retrieval is the process of bringing stored information out of memory and into conscious awareness, pulling that file back up for a pop quiz.

The front desk of this bureaucracy is absolute chaos. Sensory memory holds briefly presented information for a fraction of a second. It's the sights and sounds hitting the lobby and vanishing instantly if no one pays attention. If the clerk grabs it, it goes to the active bullpen. Short-term memory holds a small amount of information in an active state for a brief period. But the clerks only have so many hands; short-term memory has a typical capacity of about seven items. Give them an eight-step instruction, and they drop the files all over the floor. If the file is properly processed, it goes down to the vaults. Long-term memory is the relatively permanent storage system with a practically limitless capacity. 

Inside the vault, the files are color-coded by department. Explicit memory involves conscious recollection of factual information or past experiences—these are the documents you can read out loud. In that department, episodic memory is a type of explicit memory containing personal experiences and specific events, like your personal diary. Semantic memory is a type of explicit memory containing general knowledge and facts—this is the Constitution, the historical dates, the capital of France. Completely separate from the reading room is the motor pool: implicit memory involves unconscious retention of learned skills or conditioned associations. Its main sub-department is procedural memory, which is a type of implicit memory responsible for knowing how to perform motor skills, like riding a bike or typing an essay without looking at the keys. 

But the Archives are plagued by sabotage! Hermann Ebbinghaus discovered the forgetting curve, mapping exactly how fast these documents decay into dust if you don't actively review them. Worse, the files attack each other. Proactive interference occurs when older memories disrupt the recall of newer information, like an outdated colonial law blocking a modern civil rights bill. Conversely, retroactive interference occurs when new learning disrupts the recall of older information, like a brand new constitutional amendment completely overwriting your memory of the previous laws.
