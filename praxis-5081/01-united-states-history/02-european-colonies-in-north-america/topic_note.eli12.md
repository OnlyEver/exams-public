When European countries started sending people to North America hundreds of years ago, it wasn't just a random camping trip. It was a massive, highly organized project driven by a desire for extreme wealth and intense religious changes back home. To understand this time period, we have to look past the stories of simple pioneers. Instead, think of history as a giant row of dominoes. When something happened in a city like London or Paris—like a population boom or a new law—it tipped over a domino that eventually caused a war, a new way of farming, or a brand-new government across the ocean.

## The Engine of Colonization: Economics and Exile

To understand *why* Europeans crossed a dangerous ocean, we have to look at what pushed them out of Europe and what pulled them toward America. The biggest reason was a popular idea about money.

> **Mercantilism is an economic theory driving European colonization to increase national wealth through favorable trade balances.** Back then, people thought the world’s wealth was like a single pizza—if someone else got a slice, that meant one less slice for you. 

Because of mercantilism, **European nations sought North American colonies to extract raw materials.** If a country like England could get its own wood, fur, or gold from its own colonies, it didn't have to buy those things from a rival country. 

But starting a whole new town across the ocean was incredibly expensive and risky. Instead of kings and queens paying for it all, they used **joint-stock companies**. These companies **pooled investor capital to fund risky colonial ventures in North America.** This was a lot like modern crowdfunding (like Kickstarter). Dozens of people would chip in a little bit of money. That way, if a colony failed, no single person went completely broke. 

While rich investors provided the money, problems in Europe provided the workers. In England, rich landowners started putting fences around open public land so they could raise sheep for the wool business. **The enclosure movement in England displaced agricultural workers.** This meant farmers were suddenly kicked off the land and had no jobs. Facing poverty, **displaced English agricultural workers sought economic opportunities in North America.**

At the same time, **religious persecution in Europe motivated various Christian dissenting groups to establish colonies in North America.** If you didn't practice the exact same religion as the king or queen, you could be bullied, thrown in jail, or worse. Moving to America gave these groups a place to practice their beliefs safely.

## The Rival Empires: Spain, France, and the Netherlands

Before England took over the eastern coast, three other European countries built their own empires in America. They all had different ways of making money and treating the Native Americans.

### The Spanish: Extraction and Conversion
Spain got here first and built a massive empire. **Spain established St. Augustine in Florida in 1565.** Because it was built so early, **St. Augustine is the oldest continuously occupied European settlement in the continental United States.** 

The Spanish cared mostly about getting rich quick. They used **the Spanish encomienda system, which granted colonists control over Native American labor for agriculture and mining.** This meant the Spanish forced Native Americans to work in gold mines and on farms. Also, **Spanish colonizers sought to convert Native Americans to Catholicism through the mission system.** 

This forced labor and attempt to erase Native American culture caused major fights. **The Pueblo Revolt of 1680 temporarily expelled the Spanish from the province of New Mexico.** The Native Americans fought back so fiercely that the Spanish had to retreat for a while! 

When the Spanish did settle down, **Spanish colonial society developed a rigid caste system based on race and geographic origin.** Think of a caste system like a ladder you can't ever climb. If you were born in Spain, you were at the top of the ladder. Native Americans and enslaved African people were stuck at the very bottom.

### The French: The Fur Trade and Cooperation
The French had a totally different strategy. **Samuel de Champlain founded Quebec in 1608 for France**, building a fort up in the freezing, snowy north. 

Because it was too cold for giant farms, **the French colonial economy heavily relied on the fur trade.** They hunted beavers to make fancy hats for people in Europe. You can't hunt animals if you stay inside a fort all day, so the French had to travel deep into the woods. Because of this, **French colonists cultivated cooperative trade relationships with Native American tribes.** They treated Native Americans like important business partners instead of forcing them to work. 

**French Jesuit missionaries attempted to convert Native Americans to Catholicism without forcing complete cultural assimilation.** This means they shared their religion but let the Native Americans keep their own languages and traditions. Because the fur trade required people to be spread out and moving around, **French colonies in North America experienced lower European population density compared to English colonies.** (In other words, their towns were way less crowded).

### The Dutch: Commerce and Diversity
The Dutch just wanted to do business. **The Dutch West India Company established New Netherland in 1624.** Just like the French, **the primary economic focus of New Netherland was the fur trade.** 

Because life in the Netherlands was pretty great, not many Dutch people wanted to leave home. To get enough workers, the Dutch let anyone from anywhere move to their colony. Because of this open-door policy, **New Netherland possessed a highly diverse demographic population compared to other European colonies.** It was a bustling mix of different languages and cultures!

But because it was situated right between England's other colonies, England wanted the land. **England conquered New Netherland in 1664** without even firing a weapon. Right after they took it, **England renamed New Netherland to New York.**

## The English Colonies: A Study in Regionalism

England's colonies weren't all the same. Depending on where you lived—the South, New England, or the Middle Colonies—your climate, your job, and your government looked completely different. 

### The Chesapeake and the Deep South (Virginia, Maryland, Carolinas, Georgia)
The Southern colonies were all about massive farms called plantations. **The Virginia Company founded Jamestown in 1607.** This is a huge date because **Jamestown was the first permanent English settlement in North America.** 

At first, Jamestown was a disaster. People starved. But then, **the introduction of Caribbean tobacco cultivation by John Rolfe made the Virginia colony economically viable.** Growing tobacco finally gave them a way to make money. 

Virginia also created a brand new way to govern. **The Virginia House of Burgesses convened in 1619.** Just like your school might have a student council where kids vote on things, **the Virginia House of Burgesses was the first representative legislative assembly in English North America.** 

Because tobacco was super hard to grow, they needed a lot of workers. **The headright system incentivized migration by granting land to individuals paying for the passage of new settlers.** 

Here is a step-by-step example of how a wealthy farmer could use the headright system to get huge amounts of land:
1. The rule was that you get 50 acres of land for every person's boat ticket you buy.
2. A wealthy farmer decides to spend \$100 to buy boat tickets for 4 poor workers in England.
3. You multiply the 4 workers by the 50 acres.
4. The farmer gets exactly 200 acres of new land, plus 4 people who now have to work for him!

Because of this system, **early Chesapeake tobacco planters relied heavily on the labor of European indentured servants.** These were poor workers who agreed to work for free for several years to pay off their boat ticket. 

Sadly, a darker form of labor also began. **The first enslaved Africans arrived in the Virginia colony in 1619.** At first, the rules around slavery weren't strict, but a major event changed that. **Bacon's Rebellion occurred in Virginia in 1676.** A group of angry, poor workers and former indentured servants rebelled against the rich leaders. Because the rich leaders were terrified of future rebellions from poor workers, **Bacon's Rebellion accelerated the colonial transition from indentured servitude to enslaved African labor.** 

**Southern enslavers used chattel slavery to establish permanent ownership over human beings.** This meant treating people like property that could be bought and sold forever. Moving forward, **the Southern colonies relied heavily on enslaved African labor to cultivate labor-intensive cash crops.**

Other southern colonies popped up for different reasons:
*   **Maryland:** **Lord Baltimore founded Maryland as a safe haven for English Catholics.** To protect them, **the Maryland Toleration Act of 1649 guaranteed freedom of worship to all Trinitarian Christians.**
*   **The Carolinas:** **The Carolina colonies developed economies centered on the cultivation of rice and indigo** (a plant used to make blue dye). 
*   **Georgia:** **James Oglethorpe founded Georgia in 1732.** It had two main jobs: **Georgia was established as a military buffer zone against Spanish Florida** (to act like a shield), and **Georgia was initially established as a refuge for English debtors** (people who owed money and needed a second chance).

### New England (Massachusetts, Rhode Island, Connecticut)
Up north, the climate was cold and the soil was terrible for farming. **English Separatists founded the Plymouth Colony in 1620.** We usually call them the Pilgrims! Before getting off their boat, **the Pilgrims drafted the Mayflower Compact before landing at Plymouth.** 

> **The Mayflower Compact established a framework for self-government based on majority rule.** It meant they all agreed to vote on the rules and follow what the majority wanted.

Ten years later, **Puritans founded the Massachusetts Bay Colony in 1630.** Their leader was very strict. **Puritan leader John Winthrop envisioned the Massachusetts Bay Colony as a model Christian community called a City upon a Hill.** He wanted the whole world to look at them as a perfect example. 

Because **the rocky soil of New England hindered the development of large-scale plantation agriculture**, they couldn't grow cash crops. Instead, **the New England colonial economy relied on mixed agriculture, fishing, lumbering, and shipbuilding.** Because they lived close together in tight-knit towns, **New England towns utilized town hall meetings for local democratic decision-making.** Everyone would gather in one room to argue and vote on town rules!

But the Puritans were incredibly strict, and some people got kicked out for disagreeing:
*   **Rhode Island:** **Roger Williams founded Rhode Island after being banished from Massachusetts Bay.** He thought people should believe whatever they want, so **Rhode Island established the principle of complete religious toleration.**
*   **Connecticut:** **Thomas Hooker founded the Connecticut colony.** In 1639, **settlers in Connecticut drafted the Fundamental Orders of Connecticut in 1639.** This is a huge deal in history because **the Fundamental Orders of Connecticut is considered the first written constitution in the American colonies.** 

Eventually, the colonists pushing into the forests caused a massive war. **King Philip's War erupted in 1675 between New England colonists and an alliance of Native American tribes.** The war was horrific, and **King Philip's War resulted in the devastating defeat of Native American resistance in southern New England.** 

### The Middle Colonies (Pennsylvania, New York, New Jersey, Delaware)
The Middle Colonies sat right in between New England and the South. **William Penn founded Pennsylvania in 1681 as a refuge for Quakers.** The Quakers were a religious group with a very special rule: **Quakers practicing in the American colonies adhered strictly to pacifism.** Pacifism means they refused to fight in wars or use violence.

The Middle Colonies had great soil and mild weather. Because of this, **the Middle Colonies developed an economy focused on exporting cereal crops** like wheat, oats, and barley. They grew so much food that **the Middle Colonies were known as the breadbasket of colonial North America.** 

Because they were so welcoming to different types of people, **the Middle Colonies possessed greater ethnic and religious diversity than the New England and Southern colonies.** People from all over Europe lived there together.

## The Empire Matures: Trade, Neglect, and Awakening

As the colonies got bigger, they became part of a giant trading network. **The Triangular Trade linked the economies of North America, Europe, and Africa.** Imagine a giant triangle drawn across the ocean: Europe sent tools to Africa, Africa sent enslaved people to America, and America sent crops back to Europe. 

The worst part of this triangle was the journey from Africa. **The Middle Passage was the brutal sea journey of enslaved Africans across the Atlantic Ocean to the Americas.** It was a terrifying, cruel, and deadly trip. 

England wanted to make sure they were the only ones getting rich from the colonies. So, **the English Parliament passed the Navigation Acts beginning in 1651.** These were strict rules because **the Navigation Acts legally restricted colonial trade to exclusively benefit the English economy.** The colonists weren't allowed to trade with France or the Netherlands. 

But the ocean is huge, and it was hard for England to watch the colonies all the time. For many years, England used a strategy called **salutary neglect, which describes the period when England weakly enforced trade regulations in the American colonies.** 

> Think of salutary neglect like a babysitter who falls asleep on the couch and lets you play video games and eat junk food all night. Because England wasn't watching them, **salutary neglect allowed the American colonies to develop independent political and economic practices.** They got used to making their own rules!

Finally, the colonies were brought together by a massive cultural event. **The First Great Awakening was an evangelical religious revival movement in the American colonies during the 1730s and 1740s.** Traveling preachers gave exciting, emotional speeches to huge crowds. 

**The First Great Awakening challenged established religious authorities.** It taught people that you didn't need a fancy priest or king to tell you how to be a good person. Even more importantly, **the First Great Awakening fostered a sense of inter-colonial unity.** For the very first time, people from Massachusetts all the way down to Georgia were sharing the exact same experience. It made them feel like they were on the same team, which was super important for the American Revolution that was coming soon!
