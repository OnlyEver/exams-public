Listen to me, future Teacher of the Year, because if you’re going to pass this exam and stand in front of thirty feral teenagers, you need to understand that the colonization of North America wasn’t a charming Thanksgiving pageant. It was a cutthroat, high-stakes reality show driven by money, religious exiles, and absolute sheer panic. I want you to imagine the European economy like a deeply toxic school district budget. That’s because mercantilism is an economic theory driving European colonization to increase national wealth through favorable trade balances. It was a total zero-sum game; if the English department got funding, the art department had to bleed for it. Because of this mindset, European nations sought North American colonies to extract raw materials. Why buy lumber and gold from your rival when you can just rip it out of the ground yourself across the ocean? 

But moving an entire civilization across the Atlantic is like trying to fund a multi-million-dollar heist out of your own pocket—it’s going to bankrupt you if it fails. To fix this, joint-stock companies pooled investor capital to fund risky colonial ventures in North America. They essentially crowdfunded the apocalypse so no single king or merchant took the whole financial hit if the ship sank. Now, who exactly was getting on these terrifying boats? In England, landlords started kicking peasants off public lands just to raise sheep for the lucrative wool trade. This was a total eviction notice; the enclosure movement in England displaced agricultural workers by the thousands. Facing absolute starvation, these displaced English agricultural workers sought economic opportunities in North America. They were desperate gig-workers looking for anything. Add in the fact that getting banned from the state church was basically a death sentence, meaning religious persecution in Europe motivated various Christian dissenting groups to establish colonies in North America just so they could practice in peace without getting thrown in a dungeon.

Now, let’s look at the players. Spain rolls up first like the terrifying veteran teacher who claims the biggest classroom and rules it with an iron fist. Spain established St. Augustine in Florida in 1565. To this day, St. Augustine is the oldest continuously occupied European settlement in the continental United States. They didn’t come to make friends; they came for a hostile takeover. The Spanish encomienda system granted colonists control over Native American labor for agriculture and mining, basically forcing the locals to do all the grueling manual labor for free. At the same time, Spanish colonizers sought to convert Native Americans to Catholicism through the mission system, demanding total spiritual submission. You push people that hard, they push back. The Pueblo Revolt of 1680 temporarily expelled the Spanish from the province of New Mexico—a massive indigenous victory that forced the Spanish to back off for years. Inside their empire, it was an incredibly strict social hierarchy; Spanish colonial society developed a rigid caste system based on race and geographic origin, meaning your entire life’s trajectory was determined by exactly who your parents were and where they were born. 

Meanwhile, France was playing an entirely different game. They were like the hipster outdoor-ed sub who just wants to camp. Samuel de Champlain founded Quebec in 1608 for France. It was freezing, so large-scale farming was out of the question. Instead, the French colonial economy heavily relied on the fur trade. You can’t put beavers on a plantation; you have to track them through the wilderness. Because they needed expert hunters, French colonists cultivated cooperative trade relationships with Native American tribes, treating them like vital business partners rather than a disposable workforce. Even their religious approach was softer; French Jesuit missionaries attempted to convert Native Americans to Catholicism without forcing complete cultural assimilation. They just wanted to sprinkle a little Jesus into the existing vibe. Because they were constantly moving through the woods chasing pelts rather than building massive cities, French colonies in North America experienced lower European population density compared to English colonies. 

Then you have the Dutch, who were pure Wall Street crypto-bros just setting up a pop-up shop to get rich quick. The Dutch West India Company established New Netherland in 1624. Just like the French, the primary economic focus of New Netherland was the fur trade. They honestly did not care who you prayed to as long as your money was green, so New Netherland possessed a highly diverse demographic population compared to other European colonies. But sitting right in the middle of the Atlantic seaboard made them a massive target. England pulled off a completely bloodless corporate takeover; England conquered New Netherland in 1664 without firing a single shot, and England renamed New Netherland to New York.

Now, let’s talk about the English colonies, which were incredibly split by region. Down south, it was all about the cash crops. The Virginia Company founded Jamestown in 1607, and it was a total nightmare of disease and starvation, but Jamestown was the first permanent English settlement in North America. They were eating their own shoes until a guy literally hacked the local agriculture; the introduction of Caribbean tobacco cultivation by John Rolfe made the Virginia colony economically viable. But tobacco is a brutally demanding plant. To get enough workers, they rolled out a referral bonus program: the headright system incentivized migration by granting land to individuals paying for the passage of new settlers. This brought over a massive wave of desperate people, meaning early Chesapeake tobacco planters relied heavily on the labor of European indentured servants who signed away years of their lives to work the fields. 

Politically, they started organizing the chaos. The Virginia House of Burgesses convened in 1619, creating the ultimate student council because the Virginia House of Burgesses was the first representative legislative assembly in English North America. But 1619 is a deeply dark year, too, because the first enslaved Africans arrived in the Virginia colony in 1619. For a while, the labor force was a mix of enslaved people and indentured servants, but then the unpaid interns realized they were getting scammed. Bacon's Rebellion occurred in Virginia in 1676 when frustrated, landless former servants violently revolted against the wealthy elite. The ruling class panicked. They realized poor white servants and enslaved Africans might team up again, so Bacon's Rebellion accelerated the colonial transition from indentured servitude to enslaved African labor. To ensure total control over their workers, Southern enslavers used chattel slavery to establish permanent ownership over human beings, legally reducing them to property. From then on, the Southern colonies relied heavily on enslaved African labor to cultivate labor-intensive cash crops. 

The rest of the South had its own niche drama. Lord Baltimore founded Maryland as a safe haven for English Catholics who were tired of getting harassed back home, and they passed the Maryland Toleration Act of 1649, which guaranteed freedom of worship to all Trinitarian Christians. Further down the coast, the Carolina colonies developed economies centered on the cultivation of rice and indigo, requiring highly specific agricultural expertise imported directly from West Africa. Finally, you had the bouncer at the door of the English empire: James Oglethorpe founded Georgia in 1732. Georgia was established as a military buffer zone against Spanish Florida, to keep the Spanish from marching up the coast, and Georgia was initially established as a refuge for English debtors to get a fresh start instead of rotting in prison.

Now whip your head back up North to New England, where things were entirely driven by the soul and the sea. English Separatists founded the Plymouth Colony in 1620. But before they even unlocked the door to their new life, they drafted a roommate agreement. The Pilgrims drafted the Mayflower Compact before landing at Plymouth, and it was revolutionary because the Mayflower Compact established a framework for self-government based on majority rule. Ten years later, their slightly less extreme cousins showed up when Puritans founded the Massachusetts Bay Colony in 1630. Their leader was the ultimate strict HOA president; Puritan leader John Winthrop envisioned the Massachusetts Bay Colony as a model Christian community called a City upon a Hill. 

But their geography was deeply stubborn. The rocky soil of New England hindered the development of large-scale plantation agriculture. You just couldn't grow massive fields of tobacco there. Consequently, the New England colonial economy relied on mixed agriculture, fishing, lumbering, and shipbuilding. Because they lived in incredibly tight-knit, densely packed communities, New England towns utilized town hall meetings for local democratic decision-making. Everybody just gathered in a room and yelled at each other until a consensus was reached. But the Puritans were deeply intolerant of anyone breaking their rules. Roger Williams founded Rhode Island after being banished from Massachusetts Bay for his radical ideas, and because of his experience, Rhode Island established the principle of complete religious toleration. Meanwhile, Thomas Hooker founded the Connecticut colony. These guys were incredibly organized; settlers in Connecticut drafted the Fundamental Orders of Connecticut in 1639, and this document is a massive deal for your civics exams because the Fundamental Orders of Connecticut is considered the first written constitution in the American colonies. Naturally, all this rapid expansion enraged the locals. King Philip's War erupted in 1675 between New England colonists and an alliance of Native American tribes. It was arguably the most brutal war per capita in American history, and King Philip's War resulted in the devastating defeat of Native American resistance in southern New England.

Smack in the middle of the seaboard were the Middle Colonies—the golden children of agriculture. William Penn founded Pennsylvania in 1681 as a refuge for Quakers. Now, the Quakers practicing in the American colonies adhered strictly to pacifism, meaning they refused to fight in wars and generally pushed for fair dealings with Native Americans. Geographically, they struck the jackpot. The Middle Colonies developed an economy focused on exporting cereal crops like wheat and oats. Because they were producing insane amounts of food, the Middle Colonies were known as the breadbasket of colonial North America. And because they were so economically booming and generally tolerant, the Middle Colonies possessed greater ethnic and religious diversity than the New England and Southern colonies.

Eventually, all these distinct regions got tied into a massive, terrifying global supply chain. The Triangular Trade linked the economies of North America, Europe, and Africa in a continuous loop of raw materials, manufactured goods, and human lives. The darkest leg of this system was the Middle Passage, which was the brutal sea journey of enslaved Africans across the Atlantic Ocean to the Americas. 

Back in London, the English government saw how wealthy these colonies were becoming and decided they wanted a bigger cut. The English Parliament passed the Navigation Acts beginning in 1651. Think of this like the principal saying you are only legally allowed to buy food from the school cafeteria—the Navigation Acts legally restricted colonial trade to exclusively benefit the English economy. But here’s the kicker: they didn't enforce it! They took a massive nap. Salutary neglect describes the period when England weakly enforced trade regulations in the American colonies. It’s like when the substitute teacher puts on a movie and falls asleep, so the students just learn to run the class themselves. Salutary neglect allowed the American colonies to develop independent political and economic practices. 

Finally, right before everything exploded into the American Revolution, the colonies experienced a massive cultural earthquake. The First Great Awakening was an evangelical religious revival movement in the American colonies during the 1730s and 1740s. It was basically the first viral trend in American history. Fiery preachers traveled from town to town, entirely bypassing the old, stuffy church structures. Because anyone could experience this emotional salvation, the First Great Awakening challenged established religious authorities. But more importantly for the future of the country, the First Great Awakening fostered a sense of inter-colonial unity. For the first time, a farmer in Georgia and a shipbuilder in Massachusetts were experiencing the exact same cultural phenomenon, laying the psychological groundwork for them to eventually unite, look across the ocean, and tell the King of England exactly where he could shove his taxes.
