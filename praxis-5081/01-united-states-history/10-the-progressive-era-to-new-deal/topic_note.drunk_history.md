Hey, grab a drink, future teacher. You want to understand the Progressive Era through the New Deal for this Praxis exam? I need you to picture the worst-run, most chaotic middle school in existence. I’m talking a total Lord of the Flies scenario where the kids are running a black market out of the lockers, the cafeteria is serving literal poison, and the student council is just shaking everyone down for lunch money. The United States at the end of the 19th century was basically this feral school. The Progressive Era occurred from approximately 1890 to 1920 in the United States, and it was the frantic, desperate attempt by the staff to take back control. Progressivism was a broad political and social response to the problems caused by rapid industrialization, urbanization, and political corruption. It’s when everyone finally looked around at the absolute mess of the building and said, "We have to fix this before the whole thing burns down."

The first people to sound the alarm weren’t the principals; they were the school paper nerds. Muckrakers were reform-minded journalists who exposed corruption and social injustices during the Progressive Era. They were the ones slipping flash drives of evidence to the PTA. First, you had Jacob Riis, the guy who realized nobody believes how bad the locker rooms smell until you flash-bang them with a camera. Jacob Riis published the photojournalism book 'How the Other Half Lives' in 1890 to document the squalid living conditions in New York City slums. Then there’s Ida Tarbell. She went after the apex predator, the kid who was cornering the playground economy. Ida Tarbell wrote 'The History of the Standard Oil Company' in 1904 to expose the monopolistic and predatory business practices of John D. Rockefeller.

But the absolute reality TV kitchen-nightmare moment came from Upton Sinclair. Upton Sinclair published the novel 'The Jungle' in 1906 to expose the harsh conditions and exploited lives of immigrants in the industrialized cities. He wanted people to cry for the poor, exhausted kitchen staff, but instead, everyone just threw up in their mouths when they realized what was in the mystery meat. The publication of 'The Jungle' directly led to public outcry about unsanitary practices in the meatpacking industry. The higher-ups instantly intervened. That same year, they slapped down the Meat Inspection Act of 1906, which established federal inspection of meat products and set strict sanitary standards for slaughterhouses. They paired it with the Pure Food and Drug Act of 1906, which forbade the manufacture, sale, or transportation of adulterated or mislabeled food and drug products. Boom. No more sawdust in the tater tots.

While the muckrakers were screaming about the mess, others were building safe havens for the overwhelmed transfer students. Settlement houses provided social services, healthcare, and education to the urban poor and recently arrived European immigrants. The most famous was Jane Addams, who co-founded Hull House in Chicago in 1889 as a prominent and highly influential settlement house. It was the ultimate cool-teacher sanctuary where kids could go to get a band-aid, learn English, and hide from the dodgeball bullies.

Eventually, the principals themselves got involved. President Theodore Roosevelt rolled in like a substitute teacher who used to be a cage fighter. President Theodore Roosevelt's domestic program was known as the Square Deal. He believed the administration needed to be the referee, so the Square Deal focused on the conservation of natural resources, government control of corporations, and consumer protection. Teddy didn't just give detentions; he expelled the massive cliques. Theodore Roosevelt earned the nickname 'Trust Buster' for his use of the Sherman Antitrust Act to break up large corporate monopolies. 

Then you get President Woodrow Wilson, the hyper-organized math teacher. President Woodrow Wilson's domestic policy agenda was known as the New Freedom. He hated that the popular kids controlled the hallway currency, so the Federal Reserve Act of 1913 created the central banking system of the United States to provide a more elastic currency and regulate the money supply. He also tightened the rules on bullying when the Clayton Antitrust Act of 1914 strengthened the Sherman Antitrust Act by explicitly prohibiting specific anti-competitive practices like price discrimination. But here’s the kicker: the Clayton Antitrust Act explicitly exempted labor unions and agricultural organizations from antitrust litigation. He basically said, "If the giant monopolies team up, that's illegal, but if the nerds band together for protection, that's completely fine."

During this era, they actually rewrote the school’s constitution four times in a row! The Sixteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution, ratified in 1913, allowed the federal government to levy a national income tax—they finally started skimming off the rich kids' allowances. The Seventeenth Amendment to the United States Constitution, ratified in 1913, established the direct election of United States senators by popular vote, completely bypassing the corrupt backdoor student council appointments. The Eighteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution, ratified in 1919, established the prohibition of the manufacture, sale, and transportation of alcoholic beverages—the ultimate administrative ban on all fun juice. And finally, the Nineteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution, ratified in 1920, prohibited the denial of the right to vote on the basis of sex. The girls finally got a say in how the school was run.

So, they fixed the school, right? Wrong. Welcome to the 1920s, where the school’s token economy looks like a massive party but is actually a catastrophic math error waiting to happen. 

The physics of the Roaring Twenties economy were totally cursed. First, the unequal distribution of wealth in the 1920s left many working-class Americans without the purchasing power to consume the massive volume of goods produced by domestic industries. It’s like the art club churning out five thousand macaroni necklaces, but only three kids in the whole school actually have the lunch money to buy them. Second, agricultural and industrial overproduction during the 1920s created massive surpluses that severely drove down prices and corporate profits. Because there was a macaroni necklace surplus, their value plummeted to zero. 

Then you add the casino element. Buying stocks 'on margin' allowed investors to purchase shares by paying a small upfront percentage of the price and borrowing the rest from a broker. Imagine a sixth-grader putting down a single penny, borrowing 99 cents from the class treasurer, and buying stock in a rumor. It’s a great idea until the rumor turns out to be fake. Buying on margin fueled rampant stock market speculation and created a fragile financial system highly vulnerable to sudden market downturns.

When the illusion shattered, it was brutal. The Wall Street Crash of 1929 occurred in late October and signaled the start of the Great Depression in the United States. The absolute breaking point was Black Tuesday, which occurred on October 29, 1929, when panicked sellers traded nearly 16 million shares on the New York Stock Exchange and the market collapsed. The token economy imploded in an afternoon.

But the crash was just the spark. The Great Depression was the result of a horrific domino effect. You have to understand that banks don't keep all your money in a vault; they lend it out to the kid making macaroni necklaces. So, bank runs occurred when large numbers of anxious customers withdrew their deposits simultaneously out of fear that the financial institution would become insolvent. Because everyone panicked at once, frequent bank runs in the early 1930s forced thousands of banks to completely collapse because institutions did not hold enough cash in reserve to cover all immediate withdrawals. The banks literally ran out of physical cash. 

Meanwhile, the government panicked and tried to wall off the playground. The Smoot-Hawley Tariff Act of 1930 significantly raised United States import duties to protect domestic businesses and farmers from foreign competition. But this is like a kid refusing to trade their snacks, so everyone else boycotts them. The Smoot-Hawley Tariff Act triggered rapid retaliatory tariffs from foreign nations and drastically reduced the volume of international trade. Global trade basically suffocated.

As if the math wasn't bad enough, the earth itself decided to quit. The Dust Bowl was a period of severe dust storms that greatly damaged the ecology and agriculture of the American prairies during the 1930s. This wasn't just bad weather. Severe ongoing drought and decades of extensive deep plowing without crop rotation or windbreaks caused the ecological disaster of the Dust Bowl. They literally farmed the dirt to death until it turned into a blinding, suffocating powder.

And how did the principal at the time handle this apocalypse? President Herbert Hoover's initial response to the Great Depression relied on voluntary cooperation from businesses and a staunch belief in rugged individualism. He essentially looked at a burning cafeteria and said, "You kids need to use your character-building skills to put this out yourselves." The students mutinied. Franklin D. Roosevelt defeated incumbent Herbert Hoover in a landslide during the 1932 United States presidential election. 

FDR walked in, grabbed a megaphone, and unleashed an absolute avalanche of policies. The New Deal was a massive series of public work projects, financial reforms, and economic regulations enacted by President Franklin D. Roosevelt. He structured his entire playbook around the 'Three Rs': The New Deal focused on the 'Three Rs': Relief for the unemployed, Recovery of the economy, and Reform of the financial system. 

First up: stop the bleeding in the banks. Franklin D. Roosevelt declared a four-day national bank holiday in 1933 to halt the disastrous series of bank runs and stabilize the banking system. He literally hit the pause button on the entire economy. While the doors were locked, the Emergency Banking Act of 1933 allowed the federal government to inspect and reorganize national banks before permitting them to reopen. He audited the bankers. To make sure nobody ever panicked like that again, the Glass-Steagall Act of 1933 legally separated commercial banking activities from investment banking activities. You can't use the lunch money fund to gamble on dice games anymore! Crucially, the Glass-Steagall Act created the Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation to insure individual bank deposits and restore public trust in the banking system. Finally, he brought in hall monitors for the trading floor: the Securities and Exchange Commission was established in 1934 to regulate the stock market and prevent corporate abuses relating to the sale of securities. 

With the bleeding stopped, FDR unleashed his Alphabet Soup of federal programs to get people moving again. The Civilian Conservation Corps provided manual labor jobs related to the conservation and development of natural resources for unemployed, unmarried young men. He looked at the darkest, most impoverished corner of the map and ensured the Tennessee Valley Authority was created in 1933 to provide navigation, flood control, electricity generation, and regional economic development to the impoverished Tennessee Valley. 

To fix the broken economy, the National Industrial Recovery Act of 1933 authorized the President to regulate industry for fair wages and prices in an effort to stimulate economic recovery. Then came the absolute behemoth: the Works Progress Administration was an ambitious federal employment and infrastructure program created by presidential order in 1935. The Works Progress Administration employed millions of job-seekers to carry out massive public works projects, including the construction of public buildings and roads. But it didn't just hire ditch diggers; the Works Progress Administration included the Federal Art Project to provide federally funded employment to musicians, artists, writers, and actors. 

And what about the farmers growing too many crops? The Agricultural Adjustment Act of 1933 offered American farmers financial subsidies in exchange for strictly limiting their production of certain crops. It sounds crazy to pay people NOT to work, but the Agricultural Adjustment Act intended to eliminate agricultural overproduction so that market crop prices and farm incomes could organically increase. 

Then FDR fundamentally rewrote the social contract between the administration and the students. The National Labor Relations Act of 1935 is commonly known as the Wagner Act. The Wagner Act formally guaranteed the right of private sector employees to organize into trade unions, engage in collective bargaining, and execute strikes. You could finally stand up to the bullies without getting suspended. Next, he built the ultimate safety net: the Social Security Act of 1935 created a federal social insurance program designed to pay retired workers age 65 or older a continuing income after retirement. It didn't stop there. The Social Security Act established a national system of unemployment insurance, and the Social Security Act provided direct federal aid to dependent children and the physically handicapped. 

Now, when you change all the rules of the school, the veteran teachers are going to freak out. The United States Supreme Court declared key New Deal legislation, including the National Industrial Recovery Act and the Agricultural Adjustment Act, unconstitutional. They told FDR he was acting like a dictator. 

FDR, high on his own supply of popularity, tried to pull a completely unhinged power move. Franklin D. Roosevelt proposed the Judicial Procedures Reform Bill of 1937 to allow the president to appoint an additional justice to the U.S. Supreme Court for every sitting member over age 70. He essentially tried to hire six new substitute teachers who would just rubber-stamp his ideas! It was a total heist movie scheme gone wrong. Franklin D. Roosevelt's Judicial Procedures Reform Bill of 1937 was widely criticized as a 'court-packing' scheme to bypass judicial checks and balances. The backlash was so fierce that the Judicial Procedures Reform Bill of 1937 ultimately failed to pass in the United States Congress. 

So, what's the takeaway for your test? Did the New Deal work? Well, it completely rewired the politics of the country. The New Deal Coalition was a powerful political alignment of diverse voting groups, including labor unions, minorities, and Southern whites, who consistently supported the Democratic Party for decades. Administratively, the New Deal established a lasting legacy by permanently and significantly expanding the size, scope, and regulatory power of the federal government in the United States economy. The days of the administration letting the kids run the room were over forever. 

Economically, the New Deal stabilized the American financial system and provided critical relief to millions of citizens. However—and this is the trick question they love to throw at you on the Praxis—the New Deal programs alone did not completely end the Great Depression or eliminate mass unemployment in the United States. It patched the ship, but it didn't pull it out of the storm.

What finally ended the Great Depression? A massive, existential, life-or-death school rivalry that forced every single person in the building into around-the-clock overdrive. The massive industrial mobilization and military expenditures associated with the United States' entry into World War II definitively ended the Great Depression.
