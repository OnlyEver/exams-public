Imagine a giant game of capture the flag where the teams stay exactly the same for thirty years. But slowly, players get frustrated. Suddenly, half the players trade jerseys, and the whole map changes! For a long time in America, a massive team of voters called the New Deal Coalition kept the Democratic Party winning. But big arguments over civil rights and money caused the map to break apart. Over a few decades, Southern states switched sides, millions of people moved to sunnier states, and the government's job changed completely. Let's look at how the United States changed its teams at home, and how it dealt with being the biggest kid on the global playground.

## The Apex of the Federal Engine: LBJ and the Great Society

To understand why things changed, we have to look at what started it all. The Great Society was a set of domestic programs launched by President Lyndon B. Johnson in 1964. The primary goal of the Great Society was the elimination of poverty and racial injustice in the United States. Think of it like a president trying to fix every unfair glitch in a video game so everyone has a chance to win.

### Legislative Cornerstones
President Johnson passed a ton of new rules. We can group them into three main buckets:

1. **Civil Rights:**
   * The Civil Rights Act of 1964 outlawed discrimination based on race, color, religion, sex, or national origin. This meant you couldn't be banned or treated unfairly just because of who you are.
   * The Voting Rights Act of 1965 prohibited racial discrimination in voting practices, making sure everyone got an equal turn at the ballot box.

2. **Helping People in Need (The Safety Net):**
   * President Johnson's War on Poverty created the Office of Economic Opportunity to administer federal anti-poverty programs. 
   * The Medicare program was established in 1965 to provide federal health insurance for Americans aged 65 and older. 
   * The Medicaid program was established in 1965 to provide health insurance coverage for low-income individuals.

3. **Immigration:**
   * The Immigration and Nationality Act of 1965 abolished the national origins quota system, which used to strictly limit where immigrants could come from. 
   * Instead, the Immigration and Nationality Act of 1965 prioritized family reunification and the admission of skilled immigrants.

> **The Big Idea:** Because of all these new rules and helpers, Great Society programs significantly expanded the size and scope of the United States federal government. The government went from just being a referee to being a highly involved coach.

### The Backlash
Not everyone thought this bigger government was a good idea. Conservatives criticized Great Society programs for creating government dependency and increasing federal spending. They worried that if the government handed out too much help, people wouldn't try to succeed on their own—kind of like if your parents did your homework for you, you'd never learn the material yourself. They also didn't like how incredibly expensive it was getting.

## Tectonic Shifts: Fracturing the New Deal Coalition

For a long time, the New Deal Coalition consisted of labor unions, minority groups, and Southern white Democrats. It was an odd team-up—like putting the chess club, the football team, and the band all in one group—but they stuck together because they cared about the same money issues.

However, teams fall apart when players completely disagree on what is right and wrong. The New Deal Coalition began to fracture in the 1960s over civil rights legislation and cultural issues. When the Democratic Party started passing laws to help minorities, Southern white voters got very angry and felt betrayed.

### The Southern Strategy and the Silent Majority
The Republican Party saw this fight and made a clever move. The Southern Strategy was a Republican electoral tactic designed to gain political support among white voters in the South. 

How did they do it? The Southern Strategy capitalized on white resentment toward federal civil rights legislation and desegregation. By talking about things like "states' rights," the Republicans won them over. The result was massive: The American South transitioned from a predominantly Democratic stronghold to a Republican stronghold during the late twentieth century.

Around the same time, President Richard Nixon appealed to the Silent Majority of Americans who opposed the counterculture and anti-war protests. If you were someone who just wanted peace, quiet, and traditional rules—and you disliked loud protests—Nixon invited you to join the Republican team.

## The 1970s Catalyst: Stagflation and Demographics

### The Economic Engine Overheats: Stagflation
In the 1970s, the economy got super sick with something called stagflation. Stagflation is defined by the simultaneous occurrence of high inflation, high unemployment, and slow economic growth.

Here is how stagflation hurts your wallet using step-by-step math:
1. Imagine your weekly allowance is \$10. Your favorite pizza costs \$10, so you can buy exactly 1 pizza.
2. Then, high inflation hits. The price of that same pizza shoots up to \$15!
3. Because there is slow economic growth and high unemployment, the economy is stuck. You can't get a raise on your allowance or find extra chores to do. You still only have \$10.
4. You do the math: 10 / 15 = 2/3. Now, you can only afford 2/3 of a pizza with your exact same allowance. 

Because everything was so expensive and people couldn't find jobs, economic stagflation in the 1970s undermined public confidence in liberal Keynesian economic policies. People stopped trusting the government's ability to fix the economy.

### The Power Shift: The Sunbelt Migration
People also started moving. The Sunbelt refers to the southern and southwestern states that experienced significant population growth in the late twentieth century. Thanks to things like cheap air conditioning, millions of people packed up and moved to warm states like Texas and Florida.

In the United States, states get more points in presidential elections (called Electoral College votes) if they have more people. Because so many people moved, the migration of populations to the Sunbelt increased the Electoral College power of conservative-leaning states. The scoreboard shifted!

## The Conservative Ascendancy: Values and Supply-Side Economics

### The Moral Majority
By the late 1970s, a new group of voters got very active. The Moral Majority was a prominent American political organization associated with the Christian right and the Republican Party. Reverend Jerry Falwell founded the Moral Majority organization in 1979. 

Before this, a lot of religious people ignored politics. But they didn't like the new cultural changes happening in the country. The Christian right mobilized evangelical voters around social issues like opposition to abortion and support for school prayer. They brought a huge amount of energy to the Republican team.

### The Reagan Revolution
All these changes came together in 1980. The election of Ronald Reagan to the presidency in 1980 marked a definitive conservative realignment in American politics. The team change was officially complete!

Reagan brought a totally different plan for money. Reaganomics refers to the economic policies promoted by President Ronald Reagan during the 1980s. Reaganomics relied on supply-side economics to stimulate economic growth. 

What does that mean? Supply-side economics postulates that lowering taxes and decreasing business regulations increases production and economic growth. Think of it like running a lemonade stand. If the neighborhood stops charging you fees and making you fill out ten forms just to sell a cup (deregulation and lower taxes), you can afford to buy more lemons, make more lemonade, and even hire your friend to help!

### The 1994 Culmination
The Republican team's ideas kept growing. The Contract with America was a 1994 Republican legislative agenda promising to reduce the size of the federal government. They basically made a huge promise to voters to shrink the government.

It worked perfectly. The 1994 midterm elections resulted in Republicans gaining control of both the House and the Senate for the first time in forty years.

| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **New Deal/Great Society (1930s-1960s)** | Liberalism / Keynesianism | The main helper to manage money and treat people fairly. | Labor unions, minorities, Southern Democrats. |

## The Global Chessboard: Unipolarity to Multipolarity

While all this was happening at home, America's role in the world was totally shifting, too. 

### The Brief Unipolar Moment
For a long time, the US and the Soviet Union were like two rival school captains constantly facing off. But then, the dissolution of the Soviet Union in 1991 left the United States as the sole global superpower.

Because America was the only giant left, the post-Cold War era of the 1990s is often described as a unipolar international system dominated by the United States. "Unipolar" just means "one pole"—like a game of tug-of-war where only one side is pulling all the weight. 

Everyone saw how strong the US was right away. The 1991 Gulf War demonstrated overwhelming American military superiority following the end of the Cold War. When the US military went to the Middle East to push Iraqi forces out of Kuwait, they won incredibly fast.

### The Post-9/11 Paradigm
But that feeling of being an untouchable superpower didn't last. The terrorist attacks of September 11, 2001, fundamentally shifted American foreign policy toward global counterterrorism. 

Instead of waiting to be attacked again, the US made a new rule. The Bush Doctrine asserted the right of the United States to launch preemptive military strikes against perceived foreign threats. It's like in dodgeball: if you think someone is *about* to throw a ball at you, you hit them first so they can't.

This led to two major actions:
1. The United States invaded Afghanistan in 2001 to dismantle Al-Qaeda and remove the Taliban from power. 
2. The United States invaded Iraq in 2003 based on intelligence claiming the presence of weapons of mass destruction. 

### The Return of Multipolarity
Unlike the super-fast war in 1991, these new wars dragged out for years and years. Prolonged military engagements in Iraq and Afghanistan led to domestic war weariness in the United States. Americans were just exhausted, like trying to beat an impossibly hard, endless video game level.

While America was focused on those long wars, other countries were leveling up. In the twenty-first century, the global power structure shifted toward multipolarity due to the economic and military rise of China. "Multipolarity" means there are now *multiple* big bosses on the server again, not just one. The world is back to being a complicated place with many strong players.
