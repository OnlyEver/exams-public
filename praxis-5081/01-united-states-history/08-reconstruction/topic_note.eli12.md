The Civil War decided that the United States would stay one single country, but it left a huge mess behind. Imagine trying to rebuild a giant Lego city after someone smashed it, but all the builders are arguing over the instructions, the pieces, and who even gets to live in the city. This time of rebuilding was known as Reconstruction. **The Reconstruction era in the United States lasted from 1865 to 1877.** The U.S. government had to figure out how to let the eleven Southern states back into the country while also figuring out how to protect the rights of four million newly freed African Americans. 

Learning about this time is like learning about the rules of a complex multiplayer game. It shows us how power works, what happens when rules are broken, and why civil rights are so important.

## The Competing Blueprints for Reunion

When the war was ending, the President and Congress had completely different ideas on how to fix the country. 

During the war, **Abraham Lincoln proposed the Ten Percent Plan in 1863**. His goal was to end the war quickly by making it easy for the South to surrender. **The Ten Percent Plan required ten percent of a state's 1860 voters to take an oath of allegiance to the United States.** 

Here is how the math for that plan worked:
1. Imagine a Southern state had 1,000 voters in the year 1860.
2. To find ten percent, we multiply 1,000 by 0.10.
3. 1,000 * 0.10 = 100.
4. This means only 100 out of 1,000 people needed to promise loyalty.

Along with this promise, **the Ten Percent Plan required Southern states to formally abolish slavery to be readmitted to the Union**. 

Congress felt this was way too easy. To them, the South had broken the biggest rules of the country and needed a timeout and a major reset, not just a slap on the wrist. So, **Congress passed the Wade-Davis Bill in 1864 to counter Abraham Lincoln's Ten Percent Plan**. This new plan was much stricter. **The Wade-Davis Bill required a majority of a state's white male citizens to take an Ironclad Oath of absolute loyalty to the Union.** (A majority means more than half, which is way harder to get than 10%). Knowing this would make it almost impossible for states to rejoin, **President Abraham Lincoln pocket-vetoed the Wade-Davis Bill**, which means he basically ignored it until it expired, like leaving a text message on "read."

Things got even messier when **Andrew Johnson assumed the presidency after the assassination of Abraham Lincoln in 1865**. Johnson started a period called Presidential Reconstruction. **Presidential Reconstruction aimed for the rapid readmission of Southern states to the Union with lenient terms.** He wanted to move fast and be super forgiving. Without asking Congress, **President Andrew Johnson freely issued pardons to most former Confederates**, which was like handing out free "get out of jail" cards to the people who started the war.

## Action and Reaction: The Radical Pivot

Because President Johnson was so easy on them, Southern leaders thought they could go back to the way things were. **Southern state legislatures passed Black Codes immediately following the Civil War.** 

> **Black Codes**
> **Black Codes legally restricted the freedom of African Americans and forced them into low-wage labor contracts.** It was like making up fake rules in a game so that certain players could never win or have freedom.

Northern lawmakers in Congress were furious. This anger led to the rise of the Radical Republicans. **Radical Republicans in Congress sought to aggressively punish former Confederate leaders.** More importantly, **Radical Republicans sought to use federal power to actively enforce civil rights for formerly enslaved individuals.** 

Congress stepped in to help. **Congress established the Freedmen's Bureau in 1865.** This was a huge deal because **the Freedmen's Bureau provided food, housing, medical aid, and schooling to newly freed African Americans**. 

Congress also wanted to create permanent laws to protect people. So, **Congress passed the Civil Rights Act of 1866 over a veto from President Andrew Johnson**. This amazing law was simple: **The Civil Rights Act of 1866 declared that all people born in the United States were citizens regardless of race.** 

The fight between the President and Congress got so bad that Congress took over. **Congress passed the Military Reconstruction Act of 1867.** To make sure the South played by the new rules, **the Military Reconstruction Act divided the former Confederacy into five military districts governed by Union generals**. Imagine putting giant referees on the field to make sure nobody cheats.

Eventually, Congress had enough of the President blocking them. **The House of Representatives voted to impeach President Andrew Johnson in 1868.** Impeachment is like putting the President on trial. **President Andrew Johnson was impeached by the House of Representatives for violating the Tenure of Office Act** (a rule about firing government workers). But the trial was really about him getting in the way of Reconstruction. In the end, **the United States Senate failed to convict President Andrew Johnson by a margin of a single vote**. He stayed in office, but he lost most of his power.

## The Constitutional Revolution

Three huge changes were made to the U.S. Constitution during this time. They are called the Reconstruction Amendments.

| Amendment | Year Ratified | Core Function and Nuance |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Thirteenth** | **1865** | **The Thirteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution was ratified in 1865.** It was huge because **the Thirteenth Amendment completely outlawed the institution of slavery in the United States.** However, there was a catch: **the Thirteenth Amendment permitted involuntary servitude exclusively as a punishment for a legally convicted crime.** Southern states would later use this loophole to unfairly arrest people and force them to work. |
| **Fourteenth** | **1868** | **The Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution was ratified in 1868.** It is the ultimate rulebook for fairness. **The Fourteenth Amendment guaranteed birthright citizenship to all persons born or naturalized in the United States.** It also **guaranteed equal protection under the law for all American citizens.** |
| **Fifteenth** | **1870** | **The Fifteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution was ratified in 1870.** It gave Black men a voice. **The Fifteenth Amendment prohibited the denial of voting rights based on race, color, or previous condition of servitude.** |

Because of these new rules, Black citizens started participating in government. In an amazing historic moment, **Hiram Revels became the first African American to serve in the United States Senate in 1870**. 

## The Reality on the Ground

Life in the South was chaotic. People were divided into different groups with funny-sounding nicknames. 

For example, **Northerners who migrated to the South during Reconstruction to profit or promote social changes were known as carpetbaggers**. Southerners made up this insulting name, joking that these newcomers packed everything they owned into cheap suitcases made of carpet. At the same time, **White Southerners who openly supported Reconstruction policies and the Republican Party were derisively called scalawags**. Their neighbors hated them and thought they were traitors.

Because the war destroyed the South's economy, nobody had money to pay workers. A new system took over. **Sharecropping emerged as the dominant agricultural labor system in the South during the Reconstruction era.** A landowner would let a farmer use their land and seeds, but the farmer had to give back a huge "share" of the crops at the end of the year. 

Sadly, **the sharecropping system systematically trapped many African Americans and poor white farmers in inescapable cycles of debt**. Let's look at the math to see how unfair this trap was:
1. A farmer needs supplies to grow crops, so he borrows supplies worth \$50.
2. The landowner charges a crazy high interest fee of 50% for borrowing the supplies.
3. To calculate the fee, we multiply \$50 by 0.50, which equals \$25.
4. Now, the farmer owes the original amount plus the fee: \$50 + \$25 = \$75.
5. At harvest time, the farmer's share of the crop only earns him \$60.
6. Since \$60 - \$75 = -15, the farmer ends the year \$15 in debt. He has to work for the same landowner the next year just to try and pay it off, but the debt only grows.

## White Supremacist Backlash and Judicial Retreat

Whenever rules change to help people, some people fight back. As Black Americans gained rights, a violent group formed to stop them. 

**The Ku Klux Klan was founded by Confederate veterans in Pulaski, Tennessee, in 1865.** Acting like violent bullies, **the Ku Klux Klan utilized systematic violence and intimidation to suppress African American voting and Republican political power.** To stop these bullies, **Congress passed the Enforcement Acts between 1870 and 1871 to combat white supremacist violence in the South**. 

But soon, people in the North started getting tired of focusing on the South. They began backing away. First, **the Amnesty Act of 1872 restored voting and office-holding rights to the vast majority of former Confederates**. 

Then, the Supreme Court started breaking the "shield" of the 14th Amendment with two terrible decisions:
1. **The Supreme Court decided the Slaughterhouse Cases in 1873.** This decision **resulted in a very narrow judicial interpretation of the Fourteenth Amendment's privileges and immunities clause.** Basically, the court said the rulebook only protected a few tiny rights, not your everyday rights.
2. **The Supreme Court decided United States v. Cruikshank in 1876.** This case ruled that **the United States v. Cruikshank decision ruled that the Fourteenth Amendment only protected citizens against state government actions**. Because of this, **the United States v. Cruikshank decision limited federal power to prosecute anti-Black violence committed by private individuals.** This meant if an individual person or a mob attacked someone, the national government said they couldn't step in to help. 

## The Collapse of Reconstruction

Reconstruction finally fell apart because the country ran out of money and patience. 

First, **the Panic of 1873 triggered a major economic depression in the United States.** Banks failed and people lost their jobs. Because everyone was worried about money, **the Panic of 1873 diverted Northern political attention and financial resources away from Southern Reconstruction**. 

The final blow came during an election. There was a huge argument over who won the presidency in 1876. To avoid another war, politicians made a secret deal. **The Compromise of 1877 resolved the highly disputed 1876 United States presidential election.** Because of this deal, **Rutherford B. Hayes became president of the United States as a direct result of the Compromise of 1877**. 

But in exchange for letting Hayes be president, the South demanded a massive prize: **The Compromise of 1877 resulted in the total withdrawal of federal military troops from the Southern states.** 

Without the military there to enforce the rules and protect citizens, **the withdrawal of federal troops from the South marked the formal and practical end of the Reconstruction era.**

## The Era of the Redeemers and Jim Crow

With the troops gone, the old Southern leaders took over again. **Southern Democrats who aggressively regained control of state governments after Reconstruction were known as Redeemers**, because they believed they were "redeeming" or saving the South.

These Redeemers wanted to stop Black men from voting, but they couldn't just say "no voting" because the 15th Amendment still existed. Instead, they invented trick rules:
*   **Southern states implemented mandatory poll taxes to financially disenfranchise impoverished African American voters.** This was like forcing someone to pay \$100 just to cast a vote, which poor farmers couldn't afford.
*   **Southern states implemented subjective literacy tests as a prerequisite for voting to effectively disenfranchise African Americans.** They forced Black voters to take impossible reading and law tests before they were allowed to vote.
*   To make sure poor white voters didn't get blocked by these same traps, they made a VIP pass: **Grandfather clauses allowed white voters to bypass literacy tests and poll taxes if their ancestors possessed voting rights before the Civil War.** 

The Redeemers also forced people to stay apart in everyday life. **Jim Crow laws were state and local statutes that legally enforced strict racial segregation in public facilities across the Southern United States.**

The saddest moment for equality came at the end of the century. **The Supreme Court of the United States issued the Plessy v. Ferguson decision in 1896.** The court said it was perfectly fine to force people to use separate train cars or water fountains. **The Plessy v. Ferguson decision formally established the separate but equal constitutional doctrine.** They claimed everything was "equal," but in reality, things were incredibly unfair. This harmful rule would last until the 1950s.
