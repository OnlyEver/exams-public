Every group of people in the world faces the exact same problem: we want a lot of stuff, but there is only so much stuff to go around. A market is like a giant calculator that helps us trade and figure out who gets what. When people buy and sell things, prices act like secret messages that tell everyone how to best use materials, time, and money. To understand how the world works, you need to understand the basic rules of these trades!

## The Anatomy of Market Efficiency

Imagine you are trying to buy a used copy of your favorite video game. You really want it, and you are willing to pay \$40 for it. Your friend has a copy they don't play anymore, and they are willing to sell it for at least \$15. You both agree on a price of \$25. When this happens, both of you walk away feeling like you won!

Here is how economists look at this awesome trade. First, we look at the buyer's side. **Consumer surplus is the difference between the maximum price a consumer is willing to pay and the actual price paid.** 
Let's do the math step-by-step:
1. Find your maximum price: You are willing to pay \$40.
2. Find the actual price you paid: You paid \$25.
3. Subtract the actual price from the maximum price: \$40 - \$25 = \$15.
Your consumer surplus is \$15. You basically saved \$15!

Next, we look at the seller's side. **Producer surplus is the difference between the actual price received and the minimum price a seller is willing to accept.** 
Let's calculate this step-by-step:
1. Find the actual price your friend received: They got \$25.
2. Find the lowest price they were willing to accept: They would have taken \$15.
3. Subtract the lowest price from the actual price: \$25 - \$15 = \$10.
Your friend's producer surplus is \$10. They made \$10 more than they needed to!

Now we put them together. **Total economic surplus is the sum of consumer surplus and producer surplus.** 
Let's add them up step-by-step:
1. Take your consumer surplus: \$15.
2. Take your friend's producer surplus: \$10.
3. Add them together: \$15 + \$10 = \$25.
The total economic surplus for this trade is \$25.

When everything is working perfectly, **market efficiency occurs when total economic surplus is maximized.** This means the market gave the biggest possible win to everyone!

But sometimes, things mess up the market. When this happens, we get a "deadweight loss." **Deadweight loss is the reduction in total surplus resulting from a market distortion.** A "distortion" is just an outside rule that gets in the way. Deadweight loss means happiness and money basically vanished into thin air because a great trade wasn't allowed to happen.

## Government Intervention: Tinkering with the Engine

Sometimes the government steps in to act like a referee. They make rules to collect money, help certain businesses, or protect people. 

### Taxation and the Price Wedge
When the government charges a tax on an item, it messes with the price. **A tax on a good creates a wedge between the price buyers pay and the price sellers receive.** Imagine paying \$10 for a toy, but the toy store is only allowed to keep \$8 because the government took \$2. That \$2 gap is the wedge! 

Because the toy is now more expensive, fewer kids want to buy it. This means **a tax placed on a market decreases the equilibrium quantity traded.** (Equilibrium quantity is just a fancy term for the normal, balanced amount of stuff people usually buy). Because fewer toys are sold, taxes accidentally cause a deadweight loss.

Who actually feels the pain of the tax? It depends on how stubborn people are. **The burden of a tax falls more heavily on the side of the market that is more price inelastic.** "Inelastic" just means stubborn—like kids who refuse to stop buying candy even when the price goes up. Because they won't quit buying it, they end up paying most of the extra tax money.

### Subsidies: The Artificial Boost
Sometimes the government hands out free money to help businesses make more of something. This is called a subsidy. **A government subsidy shifts the supply curve to the right.** (Shifting the supply curve right is just how economists say, "Boom! Suddenly there is a whole lot more of this item available!").

Because the government is helping pay for it, two cool things happen:
1. **A government subsidy lowers the equilibrium price paid by consumers.** (Your pizza gets cheaper!)
2. **A government subsidy increases the effective price received by producers.** (The pizza restaurant gets extra money!)

It sounds amazing, right? But **subsidies create deadweight loss by encouraging the production of units where the cost of production exceeds the value to consumers.** Imagine society spends \$10 to bake a weird broccoli pizza that you only value at \$8. That is a \$2 waste of resources!

### Price Controls: Ceilings and Floors
Governments also make rules that say prices aren't allowed to go too high or too low. 

**A price ceiling is a legally established maximum price at which a good can be sold.** (It is a roof the price cannot break through!). To actually matter, **a binding price ceiling is set below the free-market equilibrium price.** Imagine if the normal price of a slice of pizza is \$5, but the government makes a rule saying, "Pizza can never cost more than \$1!"

Because pizza is now super cheap, everyone tries to buy it, but restaurants stop making it because they are losing money. Because of this, **a binding price ceiling causes a market shortage.** 
* **Rent control policies are standard examples of economic price ceilings.** When cities tell landlords they can't charge normal prices for apartments, landlords stop building them, and soon there aren't enough apartments for anyone.

On the flip side, **a price floor is a legally established minimum price at which a good can be sold.** (It is the absolute bottom limit). To matter, **a binding price floor is set above the free-market equilibrium price.** 

Because the price is forced to stay super high, companies make way too much stuff, but nobody wants to buy it. Therefore, **a binding price floor causes a market surplus.** 
* **The federal minimum wage is an example of an economic price floor.** If the law forces the minimum wage (the price of hiring a worker) too high, companies might stop hiring, which leaves a surplus of workers who want jobs but can't find them.

## The Inner Workings of the Firm: Costs and Profit

Let's imagine you quit a summer job where you earned \$500, just so you could start your own lemonade stand. You spend \$200 of your own cash on lemons, sugar, and cups. At the end of the summer, you make \$800 by selling lemonade. Did you make a profit?

Your accountant says yes! **Accounting profit is calculated as total revenue minus total explicit costs.** 
**Explicit costs are direct monetary out-of-pocket payments for business inputs.** (This is the \$200 you literally handed to the grocery store cashier).
Let's do the math step-by-step:
1. Find your total revenue (money you collected): \$800.
2. Find your explicit costs: \$200.
3. Subtract explicit costs from revenue: \$800 - \$200 = \$600.
Your accounting profit is \$600.

But an economist says something different. Economists look at your implicit costs. **Implicit costs represent the opportunity cost of using resources already owned by a business firm.** (This is the \$500 from the summer job you missed out on by running your stand instead!).
**Economic profit is calculated as total revenue minus the sum of explicit costs and implicit costs.**
Let's do this math step-by-step:
1. Find your total revenue: \$800.
2. Add your explicit costs (\$200) and implicit costs (\$500) together: \$200 + \$500 = \$700 in total costs.
3. Subtract these total costs from your revenue: \$800 - \$700 = \$100.
Your economic profit is only \$100! 

### The Anatomy of Production Costs
As your lemonade business grows, you run into different types of costs:
* **Fixed costs do not change with the level of business output.** (Like the \$20 wooden table you bought for your stand. It costs \$20 whether you sell 1 cup or 1,000 cups).
* **Variable costs change proportionally with the level of business output.** (Like lemons. The more cups you sell, the more lemons you must buy).
* **Total business cost is the sum of fixed costs and variable costs.**

What if you want to know the cost of an average cup? **Average total cost is calculated by dividing total cost by the quantity of output produced.**
Let's calculate it step-by-step:
1. Find your total business cost (Example: \$50).
2. Find the quantity of lemonade you made (Example: 25 cups).
3. Divide the cost by the quantity: \$50 / 25 = \$2.
Your average total cost is \$2 per cup!

When deciding if you should make just *one more* cup, you look at marginal cost. **Marginal cost is the additional monetary cost incurred from producing one additional unit of output.**

Be careful not to crowd your stand! **The law of diminishing marginal returns states that adding more of a variable input to a fixed input eventually yields successively smaller increases in output.** This means if your lemonade table (fixed input) is small, adding 5 friends to help you squeeze lemons (variable input) won't make you 5 times faster. They will just bump elbows and slow each other down!

## The Spectrum of Market Structures

Markets are like different kinds of games. Let's look at the different game modes!

### Perfect Competition: The Benchmark
**Perfect competition is a market structure characterized by many buyers and many sellers.** Think of a huge farmer's market where 100 farmers are selling the exact same type of apple. 
* **Firms in perfectly competitive markets sell completely identical products.** 
* Because every apple is exactly the same, **firms in perfectly competitive markets are market price takers.** 
* What does that mean? **A price taker must accept the prevailing market equilibrium price.** If the market says an apple costs \$1, you have to sell yours for \$1. If you try to charge \$2, everyone will just buy from the other 99 farmers!
* **Perfectly competitive markets feature zero barriers to market entry or exit.** Anyone can plant an apple tree and start playing the game.
* Because anyone can join, if one farmer starts getting super rich, tons of new farmers will plant trees. This floods the market with apples and brings the price back down. Because of this, **firms in perfectly competitive markets earn exactly zero economic profit in the long run.**

### Monopoly: The Sovereign Seller
At the total opposite end of the extreme is a monopoly. **A monopoly is a market structure with a single seller of a highly unique product.** Imagine there is only ONE person on Earth who knows how to build a flying skateboard.
* **A monopoly faces extremely high barriers to market entry.** (Nobody else has the tools or secret recipe to copy them).
* Because they have no rivals, **monopolies are price makers possessing the ability to independently set the market price.** They are the boss!
* But there's a catch. Since they represent the whole market, the only way they can sell *more* skateboards is to lower the price for everybody. Therefore, **marginal revenue for a monopoly firm is always strictly less than the price of the good.**
* To make the absolute most money possible, **a monopoly firm maximizes profit by producing the quantity where marginal revenue exactly equals marginal cost.**

Sometimes, monopolies happen naturally just because it makes sense. **Natural monopolies arise when a single firm can supply a good or service to an entire market at a lower total cost than two or more distinct firms.** 
* **Municipal public utilities serve as common historical examples of natural monopolies.** It is way cheaper and less messy for a city to have just one water company build one giant set of underground pipes, instead of having four different water companies digging up your street!

### Oligopoly: The Game of Chess
Most big businesses you know—like cell phone companies or video game console makers—are in an oligopoly. **An oligopoly is a market structure dominated by a small number of large firms.** 
* **Oligopolistic markets possess significant barriers to entry.** (It is incredibly hard for you to invent a brand new cell phone network from scratch).
* Because there are only a few players, **firms in an oligopoly exhibit strong mutual interdependence.** 
* **Mutual interdependence implies a firm's strategic decisions are significantly affected by the anticipated decisions of rival firms.** If Xbox decides to lower the price of their console, they have to carefully guess how PlayStation will react.
* Because this takes so much strategy, **game theory is frequently utilized by economists to mathematically analyze the strategic behavior of interacting firms in an oligopoly.**

Price wars hurt everyone's profits. So, **firms in an oligopoly frequently attempt to engage in collusion to act collectively like a single monopoly.** This is when they try to secretly agree to keep all their prices high!
* When they put this sneaky rule in writing, it becomes a cartel. **A cartel is a formal agreement among competing firms to artificially fix prices or restrict output.**
* **The Organization of the Petroleum Exporting Countries functions globally as a modern economic cartel.** They are a giant group of countries that agree on exactly how much oil to pump, keeping the global price of gas high!

### Market Structures at a Glance

| Feature | Perfect Competition | Oligopoly | Monopoly |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Number of Firms** | Tons of them | Just a few huge ones | Only one |
| **Type of Product** | Completely identical | Slightly different or the same | Highly unique |
| **Market Power** | Price taker | Mutual interdependence | Price maker |
| **Barriers to Entry** | Zero barriers | Significant barriers | Extremely high barriers |
| **Long-Run Economic Profit** | Exactly zero | Can make a lot of money | Can make a huge amount of money |
