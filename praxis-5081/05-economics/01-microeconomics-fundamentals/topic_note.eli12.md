Have you ever noticed that the weekend is just too short? You have exactly 48 hours to play video games, hang out with friends, play sports, do your homework, and sleep. But you literally can't do it all because your time is limited. This clash between all the awesome things we want to do and the limited time we have is the starting point of economics! To understand the world, you first need to understand how people deal with limits.

## The Fundamental Problem: Scarcity and Choice

The engine of all economic thinking is scarcity. Scarcity occurs because human wants exceed the limited resources available. It is like wanting ten different toppings on a pizza but only having enough dough for a tiny pie. Because we can't have everything we want, scarcity forces individuals and societies to make choices about resource allocation (how we divide up the things we have).

Every choice requires giving something up. A trade-off involves all the possible alternatives given up when a decision is made. If you choose to spend your weekly allowance on a new video game, your trade-offs are the candy, the comic books, and the movie tickets you *could* have bought instead. 

But how do we measure what that choice really cost you? 

> Opportunity cost is the value of the next best alternative given up when making a choice. 

It isn't a list of every single thing you missed out on; it is the specific value of your number-one backup plan. If your second favorite choice was buying the comic books, the opportunity cost of buying the video game is the value of those unpurchased comic books.

## Visualizing Trade-offs: The Production Possibilities Curve

To show this clearly, economists draw a graph. The Production Possibilities Curve illustrates the maximum possible output combinations of two goods. 

Imagine you are playing a video game where you can only spend your time doing two things: mining diamonds or building houses. 

*   Points on the Production Possibilities Curve represent the efficient use of resources. If you are on the line, you are playing perfectly and not wasting a single second.
*   Points inside the Production Possibilities Curve represent the inefficient use of resources. This is like leaving your game paused for an hour; you aren't doing as much as you could. 
*   Points outside the Production Possibilities Curve are unattainable with current resources and technology. You just don't have enough game-time to get there yet.

Why is this curve usually curved like a bow instead of a straight line? This is because of the law of increasing opportunity cost, which states that producing more of one good requires giving up increasing amounts of another good. Not all your resources are perfect for every job. 

Let's look at a step-by-step math example to see how the law of increasing opportunity cost works in your game:
1. Suppose you spend all your time mining. You have 10 diamonds and 0 houses.
2. You decide you want to build 1 house. You shift your best builder away from mining. You lose 1 diamond. Now you have 9 diamonds and 1 house. 
3. You decide you want a 2nd house. Now you have to use a player who is bad at building. It takes them longer, so you lose 2 diamonds! Now you have 7 diamonds and 2 houses.
4. You want a 3rd house. You have to use your absolute worst builder. You lose 3 diamonds! Now you have 4 diamonds and 3 houses.

Notice how the cost went from losing 1 diamond, to 2 diamonds, to 3 diamonds? The more houses you want, the steeper the cost gets!

## The Building Blocks of Output: Factors of Production

To make anything—whether it is a pizza, a toy, or a lemonade stand—you need materials. The factors of production are the resources used to produce goods and services. The four primary factors of production are land, labor, capital, and entrepreneurship. 

Each factor earns a specific type of money (income) for the person who owns it:

| Factor of Production | Definition | Economic Return |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Land** | In economics, land refers to all natural resources used in production (like trees, water, or dirt). | Rent is the economic return generated by the land factor of production. |
| **Labor** | In economics, labor refers to the human effort directed toward producing goods and services (like you squeezing lemons for lemonade). | Wages are the economic return generated by the labor factor of production. |
| **Capital** | In economics, capital refers to human-made goods used to produce other goods and services (like a lemon squeezer, an oven, or a hammer). | Interest is the economic return generated by the capital factor of production. |
| **Entrepreneurship** | Entrepreneurship involves the risk-taking and organization required to combine land, labor, and capital for production. | Profit is the economic return generated by the entrepreneurship factor of production. |

*A huge rule to remember:* Do not confuse physical capital (tools) with everyday money. Financial capital is money used to buy the tools and equipment used in economic production. A \$20 bill can't hammer a nail; it is just the money you use to *buy* the hammer!

## Organizing Society: Economic Systems

Because things are scarce, every group of people has to figure out the rules of the game. An economic system determines what to produce, how to produce, and for whom to produce. 

1.  A traditional economy relies on customs, rituals, and historical precedents to make economic decisions. You do what your grandparents did, which keeps things stable but doesn't allow for much change.
2.  A command economy features a central government authority making the major decisions regarding production and distribution. It is like a teacher telling the whole class exactly what art project to make and who gets to take it home. 
3.  A market economy relies on the voluntary exchange of buyers and sellers to allocate resources. To work, this system needs a strict rule: private property rights are a foundational requirement for a functioning market economy. If you can't officially own your stuff, you won't want to trade it!
4.  A mixed economy combines elements of both market and command economic systems. No country is just one or the other anymore. The United States operates primarily as a mixed economy; we mostly let people trade freely, but the government still runs things like schools and rules for safety.

## The Engine of the Market: Supply and Demand

In a market, we use the invisible forces of supply and demand to make choices. This is how millions of people buy and sell without a boss telling them what to do.

### Understanding Demand

Demand is all about you, the buyer! 
*   The law of demand states that quantity demanded has an inverse relationship with the price of a good. "Inverse" just means opposite. If a candy bar gets super expensive, you buy fewer. If it gets super cheap, you buy more! 
*   Because of this opposite relationship, a standard demand curve graphically slopes downward from left to right.

Watch out for this trick:
*   A change in the price of a good causes a movement along the demand curve. A movement along the demand curve is defined as a change in quantity demanded. If the price of pizza drops, you slide down the line to buy more slices.
*   Non-price determinants cause a shift of the entire demand curve. A shift of the entire demand curve is defined as a change in demand. This means you want more or less pizza even if the price stays exactly the same!

What causes a shift? 
*   **Income:** If your allowance goes up! An increase in consumer income increases the demand for normal goods (like brand-new video games). On the flip side, an increase in consumer income decreases the demand for inferior goods (like cheap, broken toys) because you can afford better things now.
*   **Substitute Goods:** Substitute goods are products that can be used in place of one another (like hot dogs and hamburgers). An increase in the price of a substitute good increases the demand for the related substitute alternative. If hot dogs get ridiculously expensive, people will just demand more hamburgers instead.
*   **Complementary Goods:** Complementary goods are products that are typically consumed together (like peanut butter and jelly). An increase in the price of a complementary good decreases the demand for the related partner good. If peanut butter costs \$100 a jar, no one will buy jelly either!

### Understanding Supply

Supply is all about the makers, the sellers!
*   The law of supply states that quantity supplied has a direct relationship with the price of a good. If people are willing to pay a ton of money for friendship bracelets, you will gladly make a lot more of them!
*   Because of this direct relationship, a standard supply curve graphically slopes upward from left to right.

The rules are similar here for the sellers:
*   A change in the price of a good causes a movement along the supply curve. A movement along the supply curve is defined as a change in quantity supplied.
*   Non-price determinants cause a shift of the entire supply curve. A shift of the entire supply curve is defined as a change in supply.

What shifts the entire supply curve?
*   **Technology:** Improvements in production technology increase the supply of a good. If you get a special loom that weaves bracelets twice as fast, you can supply way more!
*   **Input Costs:** An increase in the cost of production inputs decreases the supply of a good. If the string you use to make bracelets gets super expensive, you won't be able to make as many.

## Finding Balance: Market Equilibrium

If you draw both lines on a graph, they will cross in the middle. 

> Market equilibrium occurs at the exact price where quantity demanded equals quantity supplied. 

This is the sweet spot! At this price, everyone who wants to buy gets one, and everyone who wants to sell sells one. Because there are no leftovers, the equilibrium price is commonly referred to as the market-clearing price.

But what if the price is wrong?
*   A surplus occurs when the quantity supplied exceeds the quantity demanded at a given price. Let's do a step-by-step math problem to see how a surplus works:
    1. Imagine you try selling cups of lemonade for \$10 each (a very high price!).
    2. Because the price is high, you are excited to sell. Quantity supplied is 30 cups.
    3. Because the price is high, your friends don't want to buy. Quantity demanded is only 2 cups.
    4. You subtract what was bought from what you made: 30 cups - 2 cups = 28 leftover cups. You have a surplus!
*   A shortage occurs when the quantity demanded exceeds the quantity supplied at a given price. This happens if you set the price too low, like 1 penny per cup. Everyone wants it, but you didn't make enough!

## Government Intervention: Price Ceilings and Floors

Sometimes the government steps in because they think a price is unfair. But because these rules fight against natural supply and demand, they cause problems.

### Price Ceilings
A price ceiling is a government-imposed legal maximum price that can be charged for a good. It is like a roof; prices can't go higher than it. 

If the government says apartment rent can't be too high, that is a price ceiling. A binding price ceiling set below the equilibrium price creates a market shortage. Because the apartments are legally forced to be super cheap, tons of people want them (high demand), but builders refuse to build them for that cheap price (low supply). 

### Price Floors
A price floor is a government-imposed legal minimum price that can be charged for a good. It is like the floor; prices cannot drop below it. 

A binding price floor set above the equilibrium price creates a market surplus. If a school rule says bake-sale cookies *must* be sold for at least \$15 each, you will bake a ton of cookies to sell (high supply), but no one will want to buy them (low demand), leaving you with a huge pile of extra cookies (a surplus).
