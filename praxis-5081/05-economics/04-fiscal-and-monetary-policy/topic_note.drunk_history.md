Listen to me, slide that Praxis 5081 study guide over, take a sip of your drink, and look at me. You're trying to memorize macroeconomics like it's a sterile list of dead kings and historical dates. Stop. The modern global economy isn't a textbook. It is a massive, high-stakes reality TV show—or better yet, it’s exactly like managing the sheer, unbridled chaos of a 2,000-student high school, and you are about to be the teacher caught in the crossfire. 

To survive this exam and your future classroom, you have to understand the sheer pressure vibrating in the walls of this metaphorical school. That pressure is aggregate demand, which represents the total quantity of all goods and services demanded by the economy at different price levels. It’s the sum total of all the chaotic energy in the building. And it’s not abstract! The four components of aggregate demand are consumer spending (the kids buying cafeteria lunch), investment spending (the school building a terrifying new robotics lab), government spending (the district buying hundreds of smartboards), and net exports (trading your school’s excess textbooks with the rival district next door). 

But that energy isn't constant. The school year breathes. It has a rhythm, just like the business cycle consists of four distinct sequential phases. In the fall, everyone is hyped. Expansion is a phase of the business cycle characterized by increasing economic activity—everyone is joining clubs and buying school spirit wear. Eventually, you hit Prom Week. A peak is the highest point of economic output before a contraction begins in the business cycle. Maximum drama, maximum spending. But right after Prom? The crash. Contraction is a phase of the business cycle characterized by decreasing economic activity. The post-prom hangover sets in, spending plummets, and nobody does their homework. Finally, you hit the miserable slog of finals week. A trough is the lowest point of economic output before an expansion begins in the business cycle. Now, if that post-prom hangover gets deeply out of hand and lasts too long, you’ve got a disaster on your hands. A recession is typically defined as two consecutive quarters of negative gross domestic product growth. Think of it as an entire semester where everyone fails and the cafeteria goes bankrupt.

What causes that crash? Drama! Economic shocks! Imagine a rumor drops that the cafeteria meatloaf is made of recycled wrestling mats. Boom! Negative demand shocks cause sudden decreases in overall consumer spending. Everyone violently clenches their wallets, and because aggregate demand plummets, negative demand shocks can trigger economic recessions. On the flip side, what if the truck delivering the cafeteria food gets trapped in a blizzard? Suddenly, negative supply shocks cause sudden increases in aggregate production costs. The lunch ladies have to scramble, ingredients cost triple, and because the system is starved, negative supply shocks can trigger economic recessions just as fast.

To keep this cafeteria from descending into a Mad Max wasteland, you need social glue. You need money. Money solves the worst logistical nightmare in history because money serves as a medium of exchange by facilitating transactions without the need for a barter system. You do not want a world where a student has to trade three detention slips and a half-eaten apple for a hall pass. Money also serves as a unit of account by providing a standard measure of value for goods and services. You know exactly how many sodas equal the value of one prom ticket. And crucially, money serves as a store of value by retaining purchasing power over time. A kid can hoard their lunch money on Tuesday and confidently buy three slices of pizza on Friday. 

Back in the day, humanity used commodity money, which has intrinsic value derived from the physical material making up the currency—like trading actual gold coins, or in middle school terms, trading physical, rare trading cards. But modern economies are built on trust and vibes. We use fiat money. Fiat money has no intrinsic physical value; it’s literally just decorated paper. Think of your laminated hall passes! The plastic is mathematically worthless, but fiat money derives its value entirely from government decree. The Principal says the pass has power, so the pass has power!

Now here is where the heist movie starts. The government isn't the only one making money. The banks are pulling off an absolute magic trick every single day. Fractional reserve banking allows banks to keep only a specific percentage of their deposits in reserve. If Tommy deposits \$100 into the drama club treasury, they don't lock it all in a vault. They keep \$10 and lend \$90 to Sarah to buy paint. Sarah buys paint from Jimmy, who deposits that \$90, and the bank keeps \$9 and lends out \$81! This cascading mathematical wizardry means fractional reserve banking enables commercial banks to create new money through the lending process. The absolute limit of this wild creation? The money multiplier determines the maximum amount of new money a banking system can create from a single initial deposit.

If the banks just did this blindly, the whole system would implode. Enter the ultimate hall monitors: The Federal Reserve. The Federal Reserve is the central banking system of the United States. Following a string of brutal financial panics where the banks ran out of cash, the United States Congress established the Federal Reserve in 1913. They gave these hall monitors a massive responsibility. The Federal Reserve operates under a dual mandate established by Congress. You must burn this into your brain: the dual mandate of the Federal Reserve requires the pursuit of maximum sustainable employment (keeping as many people working as possible without breaking the system) AND the dual mandate of the Federal Reserve requires the pursuit of stable prices (making sure inflation doesn't eat us alive).

To do this, the Fed doesn't tax people. They pull invisible strings. Monetary policy involves the management of the money supply to influence macroeconomic conditions, and monetary policy involves the management of interest rates to influence macroeconomic conditions. 

The master dial they are trying to turn is the federal funds rate. The federal funds rate is the interest rate at which depository institutions lend reserve balances to other depository institutions overnight. Banks are constantly lending cash to each other in the dark to meet their vault requirements, and the Federal Reserve directly sets a target range for the federal funds rate. Why should you care about overnight bank loans? Because changes in the federal funds rate strongly influence other consumer and business interest rates throughout the economy! It trickles down to auto loans, credit cards, and the mortgage you'll eventually want on a teacher's salary.

How does the Fed actually move that dial? They have four brutal, mechanical levers. 

First up, the biggest lever: The Federal Open Market Committee is responsible for directing open market operations in the United States. Open market operations involve the buying and selling of government securities by a central bank. If the Fed wants to pump up the party, a central bank buying government securities directly increases the money supply. They buy the bonds from the banks and replace them with fresh, cold cash to lend out. If the party is too wild, a central bank selling government securities directly decreases the money supply. They hand the bonds over, vacuum up the banks' cash, and lock it away.

Second lever: the discount rate. This is the interest rate charged to commercial banks for loans received directly from a central bank. It's the penalty rate for going straight to the Principal's office for cash. Lowering the discount rate encourages commercial banks to borrow more reserve funds, and by making that cash cheap, lowering the discount rate increases the overall money supply. But if the Fed plays bad cop, raising the discount rate discourages commercial banks from borrowing reserve funds, which means raising the discount rate decreases the overall money supply.

Third lever: the reserve requirement. This is the percentage of total deposits a bank must hold as cash or on deposit with a central bank. If the Fed loosens the leash, lowering the reserve requirement allows commercial banks to lend a larger portion of their customer deposits, which dramatically means lowering the reserve requirement increases the money supply. Conversely, if they tighten the leash, raising the reserve requirement restricts the lending capacity of commercial banks, and raising the reserve requirement decreases the money supply.

Fourth lever, the modern trick: the interest on reserve balances is the rate paid by the Federal Reserve to commercial banks holding excess funds at the central bank. If the Fed wants banks to sit on their hands, raising the interest rate on reserve balances incentivizes commercial banks to hold more reserves instead of lending them out. But lowering the interest rate on reserve balances incentivizes commercial banks to lend more money to the public, forcing that cash out into the streets to find better returns!

Putting the Fed's playbook together is simple cause and effect. If we are in a trough, expansionary monetary policy lowers interest rates to stimulate borrowing by consumers and businesses. By making money cheap, expansionary monetary policy stimulates aggregate demand. If we are at a dangerous peak with inflation soaring, contractionary monetary policy raises interest rates to reduce borrowing by consumers and businesses. By making loans painfully expensive, contractionary monetary policy reduces aggregate demand.

But wait! The unelected Fed hall monitors aren't the only ones running this school. What about the politicians? The Student Council and the Principal? That’s the other side of the macro-engine. Fiscal policy involves the use of government spending to influence the economy, and fiscal policy involves the use of government taxation to influence the economy. In the United States, fiscal policy is determined by the legislative and executive branches of the federal government. 

When the economy crashes, the politicians panic and try to buy our love. Expansionary fiscal policy aims to stimulate economic growth during a recession. By pumping the gas, expansionary fiscal policy increases aggregate demand. How? Decreasing income taxes is a tool of expansionary fiscal policy. They let you keep more of your paycheck so you go buy a TV! Also, increasing government spending is a tool of expansionary fiscal policy. They build bridges and hire millions! But listen to me: throwing the ultimate pizza party while cutting the entry fee comes with a massive hangover. Expansionary fiscal policy often leads to a national budget deficit. A budget deficit occurs when government spending exceeds government revenue within a specific fiscal period.

Now, if the economy is dangerously overheating, the politicians have to act like the bad guys. Contractionary fiscal policy aims to slow down an overheating economy. By hitting the brakes, contractionary fiscal policy decreases aggregate demand. How do they ruin the fun? Increasing income taxes is a tool of contractionary fiscal policy. They rip the money right out of your wallet! And decreasing government spending is a tool of contractionary fiscal policy. They cancel the highway projects! But because they are heavily taxing you while refusing to spend, contractionary fiscal policy can lead to a national budget surplus. A budget surplus occurs when government revenue exceeds government spending within a specific fiscal period. 

That’s the whole game. The Fed turns the dials of money and interest; Congress swings the sledgehammers of taxes and spending. Master the levers, survive the Praxis, and you might just survive your first year of teaching. Now drink up, we have to talk about behavioral sciences next.
