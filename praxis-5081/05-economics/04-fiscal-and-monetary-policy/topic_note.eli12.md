Imagine your whole town is one giant shopping mall where everyone is constantly buying and selling things. The total energy or "pressure" of all this shopping is called aggregate demand. **Aggregate demand represents the total quantity of all goods and services demanded by the economy at different price levels.** 

Why do prices suddenly go up, or why is it suddenly hard for older teenagers to find summer jobs? It all comes down to the four pieces that make up this giant shopping mall. **The four components of aggregate demand are consumer spending, investment spending, government spending, and net exports.** This means aggregate demand is just you buying a video game (consumer spending), the local pizza shop buying a new oven (investment spending), the mayor buying new school buses (government spending), and people in other towns buying your town's famous sneakers (net exports). When the shopping mall gets too crazy or too quiet, the government steps in to fix it.

## The Rhythms of Macroeconomics: The Business Cycle

The economy doesn't just grow in a straight, boring line. It acts a lot like a roller coaster. **The business cycle consists of four distinct sequential phases.** 

1.  **Expansion is a phase of the business cycle characterized by increasing economic activity.** Think of the roller coaster climbing up the track. People are buying more things, and businesses are hiring more workers.
2.  **A peak is the highest point of economic output before a contraction begins in the business cycle.** This is the very top of the roller coaster before the drop. 
3.  **Contraction is a phase of the business cycle characterized by decreasing economic activity.** This is the scary drop on the ride. People stop spending as much money, and businesses sell fewer items. 
4.  **A trough is the lowest point of economic output before an expansion begins in the business cycle.** This is the bottom of the dip right before the coaster starts going up again. 

When that drop on the roller coaster is really deep and lasts a long time, we call it a recession. To measure this, experts look at a country's total score, called GDP. **A recession is typically defined as two consecutive quarters of negative gross domestic product growth.** This means the total score of the economy dropped for six months straight.

### What Triggers a Recession? Economic Shocks
Recessions don't usually happen for no reason. They are caused by nasty surprises called economic shocks.
*   **Negative demand shocks cause sudden decreases in overall consumer spending.** Imagine a scary rumor goes around town, and suddenly everyone is too terrified to spend their allowance. Because everyone stops buying things, **negative demand shocks can trigger economic recessions.**
*   **Negative supply shocks cause sudden increases in aggregate production costs.** Imagine a sudden tomato shortage makes pizza sauce incredibly expensive to make. Businesses have to hike up their prices and make fewer pizzas. Because of this stress, **negative supply shocks can trigger economic recessions.**

## The Anatomy of Money and Banking

To fix these shocks, we need to understand money. Money isn't just paper; it is a super helpful tool that solves huge problems for us. 

Money has three big jobs:
1.  **Money serves as a medium of exchange by facilitating transactions without the need for a barter system.** Instead of trading three of your favorite skateboards just to buy a new bicycle, you just use cash.
2.  **Money serves as a unit of account by providing a standard measure of value for goods and services.** It lets you easily compare the price of a movie ticket to the price of a hamburger.
3.  **Money serves as a store of value by retaining purchasing power over time.** You can save your birthday cash in a piggy bank and safely spend it next summer.

A long time ago, people used **commodity money, which has intrinsic value derived from the physical material making up the currency.** This meant people used real gold or silver coins. Today, we use **fiat money**. **Fiat money has no intrinsic physical value.** The paper itself is totally worthless! Instead, **fiat money derives its value entirely from government decree.** It has value simply because the government promises it does.

### The Alchemy of the Banking System
Here is a mind-blowing secret: the government isn't the only one that makes money. Banks make new money every single day! 

They do this using a rule called fractional reserve banking. **Fractional reserve banking allows banks to keep only a specific percentage of their deposits in reserve.** They keep a small fraction in their vault and lend the rest out to other people. By doing this over and over, **fractional reserve banking enables commercial banks to create new money through the lending process.** 

Just how much money can they magically create? **The money multiplier determines the maximum amount of new money a banking system can create from a single initial deposit.** 

Let's look at exactly how this math works, step by step:
1.  You deposit \$100 into your bank account.
2.  The bank has a rule (a reserve requirement) of 10 percent. The bank keeps 10 percent of your \$100 (which is \$10) perfectly safe in their vault.
3.  The bank lends the leftover \$90 to your older brother to buy a new bicycle.
4.  Your brother pays the bike shop owner the \$90.
5.  The bike shop owner takes that \$90 and deposits it into a totally different bank.
6.  That second bank keeps 10 percent of the \$90 (which is \$9) and lends out the leftover \$81 to someone else.
7.  This bouncing cycle repeats over and over! To find the absolute maximum amount of new money this creates, we use the money multiplier formula: 1 divided by the reserve fraction (1 / 0.10 = 10).
8.  Finally, multiply your original \$100 deposit by the multiplier of 10. The result is \$1,000 of total money created in the economy from your single deposit!

## The Federal Reserve: Masters of Monetary Policy

To make sure banks don't get too crazy, the United States has a boss bank. **The Federal Reserve is the central banking system of the United States.** After some really bad financial panics, **the United States Congress established the Federal Reserve in 1913.** 

The Federal Reserve (or "the Fed") has two main goals to worry about. **The Federal Reserve operates under a dual mandate established by Congress.** 
*   First, **the dual mandate of the Federal Reserve requires the pursuit of maximum sustainable employment.** They want to make sure everyone who wants a job can find one.
*   Second, **the dual mandate of the Federal Reserve requires the pursuit of stable prices.** They want to make sure the price of a slice of pizza doesn't suddenly triple.

To do these two jobs, they use monetary policy. **Monetary policy involves the management of the money supply to influence macroeconomic conditions.** Also, **monetary policy involves the management of interest rates to influence macroeconomic conditions.** 

> **Expansionary vs. Contractionary Monetary Policy**
> *   **Expansionary monetary policy lowers interest rates to stimulate borrowing by consumers and businesses.** By making loans super cheap, it makes people want to buy more, so **expansionary monetary policy stimulates aggregate demand.**
> *   **Contractionary monetary policy raises interest rates to reduce borrowing by consumers and businesses.** By making loans super expensive, people stop buying things, so **contractionary monetary policy reduces aggregate demand.**

### The Federal Funds Rate and Interest
The heartbeat of all this is the federal funds rate. **The federal funds rate is the interest rate at which depository institutions lend reserve balances to other depository institutions overnight.** Basically, banks lend cash to each other every single night to make sure their cash registers are balanced. 

**The Federal Reserve directly sets a target range for the federal funds rate.** Why should you care what banks charge each other? Because **changes in the federal funds rate strongly influence other consumer and business interest rates throughout the economy.** If it costs banks more to borrow money, they will charge your parents more to borrow money for a new car.

### The Four Tools of the Federal Reserve
How does the Fed actually change the money supply and interest rates? They have four main tools:

**1. Open Market Operations**
**The Federal Open Market Committee is responsible for directing open market operations in the United States.** **Open market operations involve the buying and selling of government securities by a central bank.** (Think of securities as official government IOUs). 
*   **A central bank buying government securities directly increases the money supply.** (The Fed takes the IOUs from the banks and hands them fresh cash, giving them more money to lend).
*   **A central bank selling government securities directly decreases the money supply.** (The Fed gives the banks IOUs and vacuums up their cash).

**2. The Discount Rate**
Instead of borrowing from each other overnight, banks can borrow from the Fed itself. **The discount rate is the interest rate charged to commercial banks for loans received directly from a central bank.** 
*   **Lowering the discount rate encourages commercial banks to borrow more reserve funds.** Because banks have more cash, **lowering the discount rate increases the overall money supply.**
*   **Raising the discount rate discourages commercial banks from borrowing reserve funds.** Because banks borrow less, **raising the discount rate decreases the overall money supply.**

**3. The Reserve Requirement**
**The reserve requirement is the percentage of total deposits a bank must hold as cash or on deposit with a central bank.**
*   **Lowering the reserve requirement allows commercial banks to lend a larger portion of their customer deposits.** This means **lowering the reserve requirement increases the money supply.**
*   **Raising the reserve requirement restricts the lending capacity of commercial banks.** They have to lock up more cash, so **raising the reserve requirement decreases the money supply.**

**4. Interest on Reserve Balances**
**The interest on reserve balances is the rate paid by the Federal Reserve to commercial banks holding excess funds at the central bank.** (The Fed literally pays banks just to keep extra cash sitting still).
*   **Raising the interest rate on reserve balances incentivizes commercial banks to hold more reserves instead of lending.** (They make more money leaving it alone).
*   **Lowering the interest rate on reserve balances incentivizes commercial banks to lend more money to the public.** (They want to earn better money by lending it to you instead of leaving it at the Fed).

## Fiscal Policy: The Government's Ledger

While the Fed handles banks and money, the elected government handles the other half of the puzzle: fiscal policy. 

**Fiscal policy involves the use of government spending to influence the economy.** Also, **fiscal policy involves the use of government taxation to influence the economy.** **In the United States, fiscal policy is determined by the legislative and executive branches of the federal government.** (This means Congress and the President).

| Policy Type | Goals and Actions | Impact on the Government Ledger |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Expansionary Fiscal Policy** | **Expansionary fiscal policy aims to stimulate economic growth during a recession.** It does this because **expansionary fiscal policy increases aggregate demand.** <br><br>Tools include: <br>1. **Decreasing income taxes is a tool of expansionary fiscal policy.** (You get to keep more of your paycheck).<br>2. **Increasing government spending is a tool of expansionary fiscal policy.** (The mayor pays workers to build a huge new park). | Because the government is slashing taxes but spending a ton of money, **expansionary fiscal policy often leads to a national budget deficit.** **A budget deficit occurs when government spending exceeds government revenue within a specific fiscal period.** (They spent way more than they collected). |
| **Contractionary Fiscal Policy** | **Contractionary fiscal policy aims to slow down an overheating economy.** It cools things off because **contractionary fiscal policy decreases aggregate demand.** <br><br>Tools include:<br>1. **Increasing income taxes is a tool of contractionary fiscal policy.** (Taking away more of your money).<br>2. **Decreasing government spending is a tool of contractionary fiscal policy.** (Stopping the construction on the new park). | Because the government is collecting high taxes but refusing to spend much, **contractionary fiscal policy can lead to a national budget surplus.** **A budget surplus occurs when government revenue exceeds government spending within a specific fiscal period.** (They collected way more than they spent). |

### Synthesis for the Exam

When you are taking your big social studies test, you need to combine everything. If a test question shows an economy stuck in a trough caused by a negative demand shock, look for a team of expansionary tools. You should expect to see the Federal Reserve buying bonds, lowering the discount rate, and lowering the reserve requirement. At the same time, Congress will be increasing government spending and decreasing income taxes. They will gladly create a budget deficit just to jump-start aggregate demand!

On the flip side, if the economy is stuck at a crazy, overheating peak, look for contractionary measures. The Federal Open Market Committee will be selling securities, the Fed will be raising the interest rate on reserve balances, and Congress will be increasing income taxes to calm the shopping mall down.
