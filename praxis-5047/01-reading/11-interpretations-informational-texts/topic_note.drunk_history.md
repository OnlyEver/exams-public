Grab a stool, my friend, and let me buy you a drink, because you are about to step into the absolute thunderdome. You’re prepping for the Praxis 5047? You’re going to be a middle school English Language Arts teacher? Bless your brave, foolish soul. Listen to me very closely: teaching eighth graders to read isn't about getting them to politely appreciate Shakespeare. It is about training them to be ruthless, cold-blooded detectives at a crime scene. In your classroom, you are dealing heavily with the nonfiction genre, because informational texts are categorized as a nonfiction genre. We aren't talking about wizards and dragons; we are talking about reality. These informational texts include formats such as essays, speeches, biographies, and news articles. To a twelve-year-old, that sounds like a snoozefest. But you have to show them that every article is actually a high-stakes heist movie, a web of lies, truths, and hidden motives waiting to be unraveled. 

The first thing you have to drill into their chaotic, hormonal little skulls is how to look at the raw physical evidence. This is step one of the investigation. Literal comprehension requires identifying explicitly stated facts in an informational text. It’s not about guessing; it’s about looking at the floor and seeing the footprint. Imagine a kid comes running to you in the hallway yelling, "Tyler launched a tater tot at Sarah!" Literal meaning refers to the direct and exact definition of words within a text. "Tyler." "Launched." "Tater tot." It is exactly what it sounds like. When an author writes an explicit statement, it leaves no room for confusion or doubt about the author's meaning. It is a brick in the wall. The text says Tyler launched a deep-fried potato, therefore Tyler launched a deep-fried potato. Boom. Explicit.

But here is where the real detective work begins, because the suspect—the author—isn't always going to confess so easily. Often, they leave an implicit statement, which suggests a meaning without directly stating that meaning. The text might say, "Tyler stood alone by the empty tater tot tray, a guilty smirk on his ketchup-stained face as Sarah wiped her eye." It doesn't *say* he launched it, but come on! To crack that, you have to use an inference, which is a logical conclusion drawn from textual evidence and a reader's prior knowledge. You know Tyler, you know the physics of cafeterias, and you read the clues. Inferential interpretation requires readers to synthesize clues hidden within an informational text. They have to put on the trench coat, pull out the magnifying glass, and connect the red strings on the corkboard! But you must teach them the absolute golden law of the interrogation room: a valid inference cannot contradict explicitly stated information within the same text. If the article explicitly states that Tyler was locked in the principal’s office during the entire lunch period, you cannot infer that he threw the tot! The space-time continuum doesn't allow it! This is huge for your exam, by the way. The Praxis 5047 exam assesses a candidate's ability to identify textual evidence supporting a specific inference. You have to be a sniper at picking out the exact sentence that proves the logical leap isn't just a wild hallucination.

Every suspect has an endgame, a motive they are trying to sell you. In our world, a claim is the main argument or assertion an author makes in an informational text. It’s the getaway car they want you to get into. To build their alibi, they lay down supporting details, which provide specific information to clarify an author's claim. Now, how do your middle schoolers tear down that alibi? With weapons-grade proof. The Common Core State Standards for English Language Arts require middle school students to cite specific textual evidence to support analysis. They can’t just point at a passage and say, "The author's full of it." They have to bring receipts! Sometimes, textual evidence consists of direct quotes from an informational text—word-for-word, verbatim, capturing the author's exact phrasing in quotation marks like a wiretap. Other times, textual evidence consists of paraphrased details from an informational text, where the student takes the author's broad alibi and summarizes it in their own words without losing the facts. Either way, textual evidence is used to substantiate an analysis of an informational text. It proves you aren't making things up. 

But you have to teach them that not all evidence will hold up in court. Strong textual evidence directly relates to the claim being made about a text. If the claim is "The cafeteria food is dangerously weaponizable," strong evidence is "The tater tots have the density of a \$100 titanium paperweight." That proves the point! On the flip side, weak textual evidence is loosely related to the claim being made about a text. Saying "Many students enjoy eating potatoes on Tuesdays" is weak! Who cares?! That doesn't prove they are weapons! Furthermore, evaluating an author's claim requires checking the factual accuracy of the supporting evidence. If the author claims the tot was traveling at Mach 2, your students need to check the math and realize the author is lying to their faces. This entire brutal, glorious teardown process is what we call text analysis. Text analysis involves breaking down an informational text into its component parts, stripping the engine out of the car to see how it was built. Ultimately, text analysis evaluates the manner in which an author constructs a logical argument. If the logic has holes, the whole thing crashes.

Now, let’s talk about the messy, flawed humans behind the keyboard. No one writes in a vacuum. An author's perspective is shaped by the author's individual background and personal experiences. If the article about the food fight was written by Tyler’s best friend, the entire narrative is going to be skewed! Identifying the author's purpose helps a reader understand the overall perspective of an informational text. Are they just trying to give us the casualty count, or are they trying to pardon Tyler? Sometimes they play it straight: an author's purpose in an informational text may be to objectively inform the reader about a topic. "Three tables were overturned; zero injuries." But often, they have an agenda. An author's purpose in an informational text may be to persuade the reader to adopt a specific viewpoint, like arguing that throwing tots is a justified rebellion against soggy lunches. When they lean too far into protecting their buddies, you get bias. Bias occurs when an author presents a one-sided perspective on a topic, conveniently ignoring that Sarah got hit in the face! You must train your kids to separate the robot from the poet. Objective informational texts present verifiable facts without the author's personal opinions. It's just the cold, hard data. Meanwhile, subjective informational texts heavily feature the author's personal opinions or feelings, dripping with emotion and drama. You teach them the difference by dissecting the sentences. A factual claim can be definitively proven true or false through objective evidence. We can literally measure the ketchup splatters on the wall! But an opinion represents a belief or judgment. The author saying "It was the most glorious lunch period in school history" is purely subjective. An opinion cannot be objectively proven true or false. You cannot measure "glory" with a ruler!

Pour me one more, because here is the final boss battle of middle school ELA: the collision of multiple suspects. You are going to hand your students two different articles about the exact same event, and chaos will ensue. Two informational texts on the same topic may present conflicting facts. Text A says fifty tots were thrown; Text B says it was five hundred! Somebody is lying! Or worse, two informational texts on the same topic may present conflicting perspectives based on the exact same facts. They both agree fifty tots were thrown, but Text A calls it a violent riot, and Text B calls it a beautiful avant-garde performance art piece! How do you survive this? First, a reader compares conflicting texts by identifying the differing main ideas of each text. Pin down what each author's ultimate agenda is. Then, comparing texts involves analyzing the different types of evidence each author uses to support a claim. Does Text A use detention records while Text B relies on emotional interviews with the rebellious youth? 

To figure out who to trust, you have to vet the messengers. Evaluating conflicting texts requires assessing the credibility of each author. Is the author the principal, or the kid who brought the tots? Similarly, evaluating conflicting texts requires assessing the reliability of the evidence presented in each text. Is it a rumor overheard in the bathroom, or is it on tape? You trace it to the root! A primary source is an original document created at the time under study. The actual timestamped security camera footage of the cafeteria! A secondary source analyzes or synthesizes information from primary sources, like the school newspaper article written three days later based on the footage. And if you really want an airtight case, you look for corroborating evidence, which is information from multiple independent sources that supports a single claim. If the janitor, the lunch lady, and the math teacher all independently say Tyler threw the tot, you’ve nailed him! 

To keep your students' heads from exploding during all this, you have to make the investigation visual. Graphic organizers like Venn diagrams are standard pedagogical tools used to compare conflicting texts. You draw two massive overlapping circles on the whiteboard. Text A’s wild lies go on the left, Text B’s emotional drivel goes on the right, and the cold, hard, shared, irrefutable facts go right in the middle! You teach them to do this, and you won't just pass the 5047. You'll be sending an army of elite, BS-detecting ninjas out into the world. Now drink up, we’ve got a lesson plan to write!
