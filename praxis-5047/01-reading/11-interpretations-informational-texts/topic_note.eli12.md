Reading an article or a book packed with facts is a lot like playing a detective video game. First, you notice the obvious clues—like a glowing key on a table or a locked door. But the real game starts when you put those obvious clues together to figure out the hidden story, like *why* the door is locked in the first place. Learning to read takes that same kind of double vision. You need to grab the exact facts written right there on the page, and then use them to figure out the hidden messages the author isn't saying out loud. Being able to understand the obvious facts and the hidden clues is the ultimate secret to being a great reader!

## The Anatomy of Comprehension: Literal vs. Inferential

Let's start with the basics. Informational texts are categorized as a nonfiction genre. This means they are based on real life, not made-up stories. You actually see these all the time! Informational texts include formats such as essays, speeches, biographies, and news articles. To really understand these texts, we first have to learn the difference between what is directly written on the page and what is hiding just beneath the surface.

### Literal Comprehension: The Anchor to the Page
Before you can figure out an author's hidden message, you have to get the basics down. Literal comprehension requires identifying explicitly stated facts in an informational text. This isn't about guessing; it's just looking at what is right there. 

Literal meaning refers to the direct and exact definition of words within a text. When a writer makes an explicit statement, they are giving you the straight facts. An explicit statement leaves no room for confusion or doubt about the author's meaning. 

For example, let's say an article says: "The school raised money to buy 15 new basketballs that cost \$10 each, plus a \$5 flat shipping fee." The literal meaning is exact. Let's do the math step-by-step to find the exact literal meaning of the total cost:
Step 1: Multiply the number of basketballs by the price of one basketball (15 * 10 = 150).
Step 2: Add the flat shipping fee to that amount (150 + 5 = 155).
Step 3: The exact total cost is \$155.

There is no guessing involved here. The facts are the facts!

### Inferential Interpretation: Seeing the Invisible
If literal reading is looking at the pieces of a puzzle, inferential reading is seeing the whole picture. Writers often use an implicit statement, which suggests a meaning without directly stating that meaning. 

To figure out that meaning, you have to make an inference. An inference is a logical conclusion drawn from textual evidence and a reader's prior knowledge. Basically, it's taking what the book says, adding what you already know from your own brain, and figuring out the secret message. Because of this, inferential interpretation requires readers to synthesize clues hidden within an informational text. 

> **The Golden Rule of Guessing:** 
> A valid inference cannot contradict explicitly stated information within the same text. 
> Think of it like this: If a text explicitly states that a video game console needs to be plugged into the wall to turn on, you can't infer that "it runs on magic batteries." That breaks the rules the text already set! 

When your teacher tests you on this—or when future teachers take their big certification tests—they are being tested on a very specific skill. For example, the Praxis 5047 exam assesses a candidate's ability to identify textual evidence supporting a specific inference. This means you have to be super good at pointing to the exact sentence that proves your smart guess.

## Deconstructing the Argument: Claims, Evidence, and Analysis

Once you can read the obvious facts and the hidden clues, it's time to take the reading apart like a mechanic taking apart an engine. Text analysis involves breaking down an informational text into its component parts. When you do this, text analysis evaluates the manner in which an author constructs a logical argument. You are basically checking to see if they built their argument well or if it’s going to fall apart!

### Identifying Claims and Details
Every informational text has a big idea at its center. A claim is the main argument or assertion an author makes in an informational text. Think of it as the author's final destination. To get you to go along for the ride, the author uses supporting details. Supporting details provide specific information to clarify an author's claim.

### The Currency of Analysis: Textual Evidence
In middle school, your teachers will constantly ask you to prove what you say. In fact, the Common Core State Standards for English Language Arts require middle school students to cite specific textual evidence to support analysis. 

So, what exactly counts as proof? 
1. Textual evidence consists of direct quotes from an informational text. (For example, copying word-for-word: "The pizza shop sold 500 slices today.")
2. Textual evidence consists of paraphrased details from an informational text. (For example, putting it in your own words: The local pizzeria had a massive amount of sales today.)

No matter how you do it, textual evidence is used to substantiate an analysis of an informational text. "Substantiate" is just a fancy word that means to prove it's true! But remember, not all proof is good proof. When you build an argument, you need to know the difference:

| Evidence Type | Definition | Classroom Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Strong Textual Evidence** | Strong textual evidence directly relates to the claim being made about a text. | *Claim:* We need more time for lunch. <br>*Strong Evidence:* "Studies show kids need 30 minutes to digest food properly, but we only get 15." |
| **Weak Textual Evidence** | Weak textual evidence is loosely related to the claim being made about a text. | *Claim:* We need more time for lunch. <br>*Weak Evidence:* "Pizza is the most popular food in the cafeteria." |

Also, don't just believe everything you read! Evaluating an author's claim requires checking the factual accuracy of the supporting evidence. If a writer claims a new skateboard is the fastest in the world, you have to check if their speed tests are actually real.

## Navigating the Human Element: Purpose, Perspective, and Bias

Every article is written by a real person. And every person looks at the world a little differently. To really understand what you are reading, you have to think about the person who wrote it.

### Purpose and Perspective
An author's perspective is shaped by the author's individual background and personal experiences. For example, a professional skateboarder and a security guard are going to write very different articles about building a skatepark in town! 

To figure out where the writer is coming from, look at their goal. Identifying the author's purpose helps a reader understand the overall perspective of an informational text. 
*   Sometimes, the writer just wants to give you the facts like a textbook. An author's purpose in an informational text may be to objectively inform the reader about a topic. 
*   Other times, the writer wants you to agree with them. An author's purpose in an informational text may be to persuade the reader to adopt a specific viewpoint.

### Objectivity vs. Subjectivity
When a writer lets their own feelings completely take over the facts, we get bias. Bias occurs when an author presents a one-sided perspective on a topic. We learn to spot bias by checking if a text is objective or subjective:

*   Objective informational texts present verifiable facts without the author's personal opinions. Think of a scoreboard at a basketball game—it just tells you the score, not who is playing better. 
*   Subjective informational texts heavily feature the author's personal opinions or feelings. Think of a screaming sports fan who thinks the referee is being unfair!

As you read, you always have to sort sentences into two buckets:

> **Factual Claim:** A factual claim can be definitively proven true or false through objective evidence. *(Like saying: "A standard basketball hoop is 10 feet tall.")*
> 
> **Opinion:** An opinion represents a belief or judgment. The most important thing to remember is that an opinion cannot be objectively proven true or false. *(Like saying: "Basketball is way more fun than baseball.")*

## The Collision of Ideas: Comparing Conflicting Texts

One of the toughest parts of reading is when two different articles talk about the exact same thing but say completely opposite things. 

### The Nature of Conflict
When you put two texts side by side, they usually clash in one of two ways:
1. Two informational texts on the same topic may present conflicting facts. Text A says the new roller coaster goes 70 miles per hour; Text B says it only goes 50 miles per hour.
2. Two informational texts on the same topic may present conflicting perspectives based on the exact same facts. Both texts agree the school spent \$500 on a new DJ for the dance. Text A uses this fact to argue the dance will be awesome; Text B uses the exact same fact to argue the school is wasting money on loud music.

### The Toolkit for Synthesis
How do we figure out who is right? First, a reader compares conflicting texts by identifying the differing main ideas of each text. Once you know the big differences, look closely at their proof. Comparing texts involves analyzing the different types of evidence each author uses to support a claim. Does one person use hard math while the other just shares a funny story?

Next, look at the writers themselves. Evaluating conflicting texts requires assessing the credibility of each author. Is the writer an expert? Do they want to make money off of you? Also, evaluating conflicting texts requires assessing the reliability of the evidence presented in each text. 

To check if evidence is reliable, we look at where it came from:
*   **Primary source:** A primary source is an original document created at the time under study. (Like a photo of a winning touchdown or a diary from the 1800s).
*   **Secondary source:** A secondary source analyzes or synthesizes information from primary sources. (Like a history textbook written years later).
*   **Corroborating evidence:** Corroborating evidence is information from multiple independent sources that supports a single claim. If three different kids from three different homerooms all say there is going to be a pop quiz in math, that claim is corroborated and you should probably study!

### Classroom Application: Making It Visual
To make comparing all these ideas easier, drawing a picture helps a ton. Graphic organizers like Venn diagrams are standard pedagogical tools used to compare conflicting texts. (Pedagogical tools is just a fancy teacher term for learning aids!). By putting Text A's ideas in the left circle, Text B's ideas in the right circle, and the things they agree on in the overlapping middle, you turn a confusing reading assignment into a clear, visual map. 

Mastering all these reading skills gives you a superpower. It gives you the ability to look at all the crazy information on the internet, on TV, and in books, and figure out what is real, what is fake, and what is just somebody's loud opinion!
