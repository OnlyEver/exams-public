Consider the difference between reading a meticulously [peer-reviewed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peer_review) [botanical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Botany) report on [deforestation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deforestation) and a fiery [political](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics) [op-ed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Op-ed) on the exact same subject. Both texts rely on the [English language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_language), but their mechanisms of operation are vastly different. When we examine informational texts, we are not merely decoding words on a page; we are [reverse-engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reverse_engineering) the [cognitive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cognition) machinery an author has built to direct a reader’s mind. For the aspiring [middle school](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_school) [English language arts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_language_arts) teacher preparing for the [Praxis 5047](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Praxis_test), this is the ultimate [pedagogical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pedagogy) challenge: taking [adolescents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adolescence) who have predominantly read for [plot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plot_%28narrative%29) and training them to read for *craft*. This guide deconstructs the architecture of [word choice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diction) and [rhetoric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhetoric) in informational texts, equipping you to master your certification exam and to teach your future students how to see the invisible structures of [persuasion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persuasion), precision, and [bias](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bias) that shape the information they consume every day.

![A scientific chart tracking annual deforestation rates exemplifies the objective, data-driven approach often found in informational texts, contrasting heavily with the emotional language of a political op-ed.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Annual-deforestation.svg)

## The Architecture of Intent: Purpose, Point of View, and Tone

Before we can analyze *how* an author builds a text, we must understand *why* they built it. Every informational text is an [artifact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artifact_%28archaeology%29) of human [intent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intention). 

> **An [author's purpose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Author%27s_purpose) is the primary reason the author wrote the text.** While human [motivations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Motivation) are wonderfully complex, **the three primary authorial purposes are to persuade, to inform, and to entertain.** 

When your [seventh graders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seventh_grade) read a [NASA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NASA) article about the [Mars Rover](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mars_rover), the purpose is to inform. When they read a [persuasive essay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persuasive_writing) arguing for the abolition of [plastic straws](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drinking_straw), the purpose is to persuade. But purpose is only the engine; the steering wheel is the author's [perspective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_of_view_%28philosophy%29). 

![NASA's Curiosity rover on Mars. Articles detailing such missions typically serve to inform, focusing on factual discoveries and technical specifications rather than attempting to persuade the reader of a controversial opinion.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Curiosity_Self-Portrait_at_'Big_Sky'_Drilling_Site.jpg)

**An author's [point of view](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_of_view_%28literature%29) is the author's personal perspective or opinion on the topic.** In [fiction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiction), "point of view" refers to [first-person](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First-person_narrative) or [third-person](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Third-person_narrative) narration. In informational texts, it refers to the author's stance. This stance generally falls onto a [spectrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectrum) between two poles:
*   **A [subjective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subjectivity) point of view includes the author's feelings and opinions.** 
*   **An [objective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Objectivity_%28philosophy%29) point of view presents information without the author's personal opinions.**

Middle schoolers often assume that if a text contains [facts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fact), it must be completely objective. Your job is to show them the [sleight of hand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sleight_of_hand). **An author's point of view in informational texts is revealed through the author's word choice.** But it goes deeper than just the words used. **An author's point of view in informational texts is revealed through the selection of specific facts**, highlighting [data](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data) that supports their [worldview](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Worldview). Conversely, and perhaps most dangerously, **an author's point of view in informational texts is revealed through the [omission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_of_omission) of specific facts.** An article praising a new factory for creating two thousand jobs (selection) while entirely ignoring the resulting [river pollution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water_pollution) (omission) is highly subjective, even if every printed word is factually true.

Finally, we have the emotional [resonance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resonance) of the piece. **[Tone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tone_%28literature%29) is the author's attitude toward the subject matter.** If the point of view is *what* the author thinks, the tone is *how* they sound when they say it—whether urgent, dismissive, reverent, or [cynical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cynicism_%28contemporary%29).

## The Mechanics of Meaning: Explicit vs. Implicit Information

To analyze tone and intent, readers must distinguish between what is on the page and what is hidden [between the lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reading_between_the_lines). 

**[Explicit information](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Explicit_knowledge) is stated directly and clearly within the text.** If a [biography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biography) states, "[Abraham Lincoln](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abraham_Lincoln) was born in [1809](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1809)," that is explicit. However, writers rarely spell everything out, especially when dealing with complex or [controversial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Controversy) ideas. **[Implicit information](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tacit_knowledge) is not directly stated within the text.** 

Because the author leaves gaps, **readers must use [context clues](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Context_clue) to [infer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inference) implicit information.** This is heavily tested on the Praxis 5047. You will be asked to read a passage and identify the underlying [assumption](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Premise) the author is making. 

### The Dual Nature of Words: Denotation and Connotation
The most precise tool an author has for implying [meaning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meaning_%28linguistics%29) is [vocabulary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vocabulary). Every word possesses a dual identity:
1.  **[Denotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Denotation) refers to the literal, [dictionary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dictionary) definition of a word.**
2.  **[Connotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connotation) refers to the emotional or [cultural](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Culture) associations connected to a word.**

Consider the words *frugal* and *stingy*. Their denotation is nearly identical: both mean "careful with [money](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Money)." But their connotations are worlds apart. *Frugal* implies [wisdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wisdom) and responsibility; *stingy* implies [selfishness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Selfishness). 

When an author intentionally stacks the deck with highly connotative vocabulary, they are using **[loaded language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Loaded_language)**. **Loaded language consists of words with strongly positive or negative connotations.** Instead of calling a [policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Policy) "a change in the [tax code](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tax_law)" (neutral), an author might call it "a devastating financial scheme" (negative). **Authors use loaded language to elicit a strong [emotional response](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emotion) from the reader**, bypassing [critical thought](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Critical_thinking) by triggering immediate outrage, [fear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fear), or [pride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pride).

## Building Credibility and Precision: Technical Language

Not all specialized vocabulary is designed to manipulate emotion. Sometimes, an author needs absolute, clinical precision. 

**Technical language consists of specialized vocabulary used within a specific field or profession.** When a [botanist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Botany) writes about *[photosynthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photosynthesis)*, or a [lawyer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lawyer) writes about *[habeas corpus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habeas_corpus)*, they are utilizing this specialized [lexicon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lexicon). **Technical language is also known as [jargon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jargon).**

![A diagram illustrating the mechanisms of photosynthesis. Authors use specialized scientific jargon to precisely delineate complex processes that everyday vocabulary lacks the specificity to describe.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Simple_photosynthesis_overview.svg)

Why do authors use jargon instead of plain English? There are two primary, strategic reasons:
1.  **Authors use technical language to provide precise descriptions of complex processes.** You cannot accurately explain [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics) or [appellate court](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Appellate_court) procedures using only everyday vocabulary; the standard words lack the necessary specificity.
2.  **Authors use technical language to establish [credibility](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Credibility) with the audience.** By using the precise [terminology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terminology) of a field, the author signals to the reader, "I am an insider. I know what I am talking about. You can trust me."

However, jargon comes with a critical [risk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Risk). **Technical language assumes a baseline level of prior knowledge from the [target audience](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Target_audience).** If an author misjudges their audience—for instance, a [textbook](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Textbook) writer using advanced [biochemical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biochemistry) jargon in a text meant for [sixth graders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sixth_grade)—the [communication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communication) breaks down entirely. 

## The Engine of Persuasion: Rhetoric

When an author wants to move an audience to action, they turn to ancient, mathematically precise tools of persuasion. **[Rhetoric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhetoric) is the art of effective or persuasive speaking and writing.** 

The [Greek philosopher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek_philosophy) [Aristotle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle) gave us the foundational triad of [rhetorical appeals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modes_of_persuasion), which serve as the bedrock for evaluating any persuasive informational text:

| Rhetorical Appeal | Definition | Example in Practice |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **[Ethos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethos)** | **Ethos is a rhetorical appeal to the credibility or ethical character of the speaker.** | "As a [pediatrician](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pediatrics) with twenty years of experience, I can assure you this [vaccine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vaccine) is safe." |
| **[Pathos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pathos)** | **Pathos is a rhetorical appeal to the audience's emotions.** | "If we do not fund this [animal shelter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Animal_shelter), innocent [puppies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Puppy) will be left to freeze in the winter [snow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snow)." |
| **[Logos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logos)** | **Logos is a rhetorical appeal to logic and [reason](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reason).** | "Installing [solar panels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_panel) will reduce the school's [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy) expenditures by 15%, paying for itself in four years." |

![The Greek philosopher Aristotle (384–322 BC), whose foundational triad of rhetorical appeals—ethos, pathos, and logos—remains the fundamental framework for analyzing the mechanics of persuasion.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Aristotle_Altemps_Inv8575.jpg)

The best informational and persuasive texts weave all three together. They use *ethos* to make you listen, *logos* to make you agree, and *pathos* to make you act.

## The Toolkit of Craft: Rhetorical Devices and Figurative Language

To actualize these appeals, authors use specific [stylistic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stylistics) tools. **[Figurative language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Literal_and_figurative_language) uses words in ways that deviate from literal meanings to create a specific effect.** 

### 1. Structural Devices
How a [sentence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sentence_%28linguistics%29) is built affects how it is received by the brain.
*   **Authors use [parallelism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelism_%28grammar%29) to create [rhythm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhythm) within a text** and **Authors use parallelism to emphasize equal importance among multiple ideas.** (e.g., "[We shall fight on the beaches](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/We_shall_fight_on_the_beaches), we shall fight on the landing grounds, we shall fight in the fields...")
*   **Authors use [repetition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Repetition_%28rhetorical_device%29) to reinforce a key concept or argument.** If an author repeats a phrase, it serves as an anchor for the reader's [memory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Memory).
*   **Authors use [rhetorical questions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhetorical_question) to prompt the audience to think about a point rather than to elicit an actual answer.** When an author asks, "Can we really afford to ignore [climate change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change)?", they don't want you to say "Yes" or "No"—they want you to internalize the urgency of the premise.

### 2. Devices of Comparison and Association
To explain the unknown, authors anchor it to the known.
*   **Authors use [metaphors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metaphor) in informational texts to draw comparisons between unrelated things without using the words like or as.** Calling the [human brain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_brain) "a [supercomputer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supercomputer)" immediately transfers the properties of processing power and complexity to the reader's understanding of [biology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology).
*   **An [analogy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analogy) compares two different things to explain a complex concept in simpler terms.** If an author explains [economic inflation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inflation) by comparing it to a [balloon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Balloon) expanding until it pops, that is an analogy.
*   **[Allusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allusion) is a brief reference to a historical, cultural, or literary person, place, or event.** **Authors use allusions to connect a new concept to a reader's existing knowledge.** Describing a [whistleblower](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whistleblower)'s task as a "[David and Goliath](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goliath) battle" instantly conveys the power disparity without needing a paragraph of explanation.

![An 1888 painting of David and Goliath. Alluding to this well-known narrative acts as a cognitive shortcut, allowing an author to instantly convey a severe power disparity to the reader without writing a lengthy explanation.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Osmar_Schindler_David_und_Goliath.jpg)

### 3. Devices of Scale and Contradiction
Sometimes, truth is best revealed by distorting reality.
*   **[Hyperbole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbole) is a rhetorical device that uses extreme [exaggeration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exaggeration) to make a point.** ("This new [zoning law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zoning) will destroy our town forever.")
*   **[Understatement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Understatement) is a rhetorical device used to intentionally make a situation seem less important than the situation actually is.** ("The [hurricane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tropical_cyclone) caused a bit of a breeze.")
*   **[Juxtaposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Juxtaposition) is the placement of two contrasting elements close together to highlight differences.** Placing a paragraph about immense [corporate wealth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wealth) immediately next to a paragraph about local [childhood poverty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Child_poverty) forces the reader to confront the disparity.
*   **An [oxymoron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxymoron) is a figure of speech that combines two contradictory terms** (e.g., "deafening silence" or "controlled chaos"). 
*   **A [paradox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paradox) is a statement that appears self-contradictory.** However, **a paradox can reveal an underlying truth upon closer inspection.** Take the famous [architectural](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Architecture) maxim: "Less is more." It makes no logical sense on its face, but upon reflection, it reveals the profound [aesthetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aesthetics) value of [minimalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minimalism).

### 4. Devices of Irony and Substitution
Sometimes, what is *not* said, or what is said backwards, is the most powerful choice of all.
*   **[Verbal irony](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irony) occurs when a speaker's intended meaning contrasts with the literal meaning of the words spoken.** Stepping out into a torrential downpour and saying, "What beautiful weather," is verbal irony.
*   **[Sarcasm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sarcasm) is a form of verbal irony used to mock or convey [contempt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contempt).** If a [politician](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politician) says, "Oh, brilliant strategy," in response to a rival's failure, the irony isweaponized to demean.
*   **[Euphemisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euphemism) are mild or indirect words substituted for words considered too harsh or blunt.** In informational texts, euphemisms can be a polite necessity ("passed away" instead of "died"), but they can also be used deceptively by authors to hide uncomfortable truths (calling [civilian casualties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civilian_casualty) "[collateral damage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collateral_damage)").

## The Weak Links: Logical Fallacies

A robust argument is a marvel of [logical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logic) engineering. But occasionally, the engine stalls. When analyzing rhetoric, you and your students must learn to spot the cracks in the foundation.

**A [logical fallacy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fallacy) is a flaw in [reasoning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reason) that weakens a rhetorical argument.** While there are dozens of fallacies, the Praxis 5047 expects you to be highly familiar with how authors attempt to manipulate readers when their actual *logos* (logic) is weak. Two of the most common are:

1.  **An [ad hominem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ad_hominem) fallacy attacks the person making the argument rather than the argument itself.** If a [scientist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientist) presents a paper on [renewable energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Renewable_energy), and a critic responds by saying, "We shouldn't listen to him because he's an arrogant jerk who dresses poorly," the critic has committed an ad hominem fallacy. They have ignored the data entirely to attack the human.
2.  **A [straw man fallacy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Straw_man) misrepresents an opponent's argument to make the opponent's argument easier to attack.** Imagine a [school board](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Board_of_education) member argues, "We should redirect some funding from the [football](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_football) program to the [debate team](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Debate)." If an opponent responds, "My colleague wants to destroy all school sports and hates our [student-athletes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Student_athlete)!", they have built a "straw man." They created a fake, extreme version of the argument because it is much easier to defeat than the actual, nuanced proposal.

![A 1900 political cartoon depicting a literal "straw man." In rhetoric, the straw man fallacy involves constructing a weak, easily defeated caricature of an opponent's actual argument in order to tear it down.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/_McKinley_Destroys_Imperialism_Straw_Man.jpg)

## Conclusion: The Classroom Translation

As you prepare for your Praxis exam, keep these principles grounded in the reality of the middle school classroom. When a student reads a historical speech and circles the repeated phrases, they aren't just doing a [worksheet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Worksheet); they are mapping **[parallelism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelism_%28grammar%29)** and **[repetition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Repetition_%28rhetorical_device%29)**. When they argue with you that their [curfew](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curfew) is a "[prison](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prison) sentence," they are instinctively using **[hyperbole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbole)**. 

Your goal on the exam is to correctly identify and analyze these elements in complex passages. Your ultimate professional goal is to pull back the curtain on these techniques, showing your students that language is not just a vehicle for sharing facts—it is a toolkit for shaping [reality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reality). [Mastery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mastery_learning) of these concepts ensures you are ready to pass the test and ready to teach the next generation how to read the world.
