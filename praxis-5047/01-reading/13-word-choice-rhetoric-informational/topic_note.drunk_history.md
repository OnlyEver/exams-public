You’re studying for the Praxis 5047, gearing up to teach middle school English, and you probably think informational texts are just dry little articles about the water cycle or the invention of the cotton gin. You think you’re just teaching kids how to find facts. You are so blissfully, dangerously wrong. Teaching informational text to a room full of feral twelve-year-olds isn’t a reading exercise; it’s bomb squad training. It’s a psychological heist movie. Every article, every op-ed, every documentary is an elaborate scheme to break into the reader’s brain, and your job is to teach those kids how to spot the laser grids, the tripwires, and the smoke grenades.

Before we can look at the tools of the heist, we have to establish the mastermind's motive. An author's purpose is the primary reason the author wrote the text. Nobody writes an article by accident; there's always an agenda. The three primary authorial purposes are to persuade, to inform, and to entertain. It’s either a sales pitch, a dossier, or a clown show. But knowing the purpose is only half the battle. You have to figure out the mastermind’s personal angle. This is the author's point of view, which is the author's personal perspective or opinion on the topic. When they execute this heist cleanly, presenting information without the author's personal opinions, we call that an objective point of view. It’s clinical. It’s a security camera footage of facts. But the second they let their own ego in on the job, it becomes a subjective point of view, which includes the author's feelings and opinions. 

How do we catch them slipping their opinions into a supposedly factual text? We look at the fingerprints. First, an author's point of view in informational texts is revealed through the author's word choice. But it’s also about what they pack in the getaway bag! It is revealed through the selection of specific facts—cherry-picking the data that makes their side look like the heroes. Even more deviously, an author's point of view in informational texts is revealed through the omission of specific facts. They just conveniently forget to mention the data that ruins their argument. It’s a masterful cover-up. And hanging over this entire operation is tone, which is the author's attitude toward the subject matter. Is the getaway driver frantic? Cynical? Righteous? 

To dismantle this text-bomb, your kids have to understand how the author smuggles ideas into the brain. Sometimes the author just walks through the front door. This is explicit information, which is stated directly and clearly within the text. "The bank has one million dollars." Boom. Easy. But the real pros use the air vents. Implicit information is not directly stated within the text. It’s a shadow. Because it’s hidden, readers must use context clues to infer implicit information. 

To pull off an implicit job, an author needs a very specific toolkit. Words are not just words; they have double identities. The denotation refers to the literal, dictionary definition of a word. That’s the physical lockpick. But the connotation refers to the emotional or cultural associations connected to a word. That’s the poison they dip the lockpick in before they use it. An author will deliberately weaponize this duality using loaded language, which consists of words with strongly positive or negative connotations. Instead of calling a new law a "change," they call it a "catastrophe" or a "salvation." Why? Because authors use loaded language to elicit a strong emotional response from the reader. They want you so angry or so inspired that you don’t even notice they just hijacked your brain.

But wait, what if the author doesn’t want to trigger an emotional meltdown? What if they want to look like the smartest hacker in the room? That’s when they deploy technical language, which consists of specialized vocabulary used within a specific field or profession. Think of a botanist writing about "chloroplasts" instead of "plant parts." Yes, technical language is also known as jargon. Authors pull out this specific toolkit for two very strategic reasons. First, authors use technical language to provide precise descriptions of complex processes. You simply cannot explain the internal combustion engine using the vocabulary of a toddler. Second, authors use technical language to establish credibility with the audience. They flash that jargon like a fake ID at the security desk to say, "I belong here. Trust me." But this is a high-risk maneuver! Technical language assumes a baseline level of prior knowledge from the target audience. If a writer throws advanced biochemical jargon at your seventh graders, the heist fails completely because the kids don't even know what building they're standing in. 

When an author really wants to manipulate an audience into compliance, they turn to the ancient Greek master-keys of rhetoric, which is the art of effective or persuasive speaking and writing. There are three primary vaults they try to crack. Ethos is a rhetorical appeal to the credibility or ethical character of the speaker. That’s the author wearing a lab coat and holding a clipboard to look authoritative. Pathos is a rhetorical appeal to the audience's emotions. That’s the author showing you a picture of a sad, three-legged puppy to make you bypass the security system out of pity. Logos is a rhetorical appeal to logic and reason. That’s the author sliding the actual blueprints across the table and saying, "Look at the math, it just makes sense." 

Now let’s look at how they wire the actual explosive device. They rely heavily on structural and figurative tools. Figurative language uses words in ways that deviate from literal meanings to create a specific effect. It’s the ultimate linguistic disguise! Authors use metaphors in informational texts to draw comparisons between unrelated things without using the words like or as. They'll tell you the human brain is a supercomputer, instantly downloading the concept of processing power into your head. If the target concept is heavily fortified and confusing, they’ll use an analogy, which compares two different things to explain a complex concept in simpler terms. And to hack your brain's existing hard drive, they use an allusion, which is a brief reference to a historical, cultural, or literary person, place, or event. Authors use allusions to connect a new concept to a reader's existing knowledge. If they call a court case a "David and Goliath battle," they don't have to explain the power dynamics; they just piggybacked on a story you already know. 

But it’s not just the words; it’s how they arrange them. Authors use parallelism to create rhythm within a text, making the sentences hum like a live wire, and authors use parallelism to emphasize equal importance among multiple ideas. They’ll also drill the same hole in the vault twice: authors use repetition to reinforce a key concept or argument. And just to mess with the guards, authors use rhetorical questions to prompt the audience to think about a point rather than to elicit an actual answer. "Do you really want the world to end?" They don't want a yes or no; they want panic. 

Speaking of panic, let's talk about distorting reality. Sometimes the author uses hyperbole, which is a rhetorical device that uses extreme exaggeration to make a point. "This school dress code is a literal medieval torture chamber." On the flip side, understatement is a rhetorical device used to intentionally make a situation seem less important than the situation actually is. "The meteor that killed the dinosaurs caused a bit of a dust problem." If they want to force you to see a stark difference, they use juxtaposition, which is the placement of two contrasting elements close together to highlight differences—like dropping a billionaire's yacht brochure right next to an article on local food banks. 

Sometimes, the author jams the wires together to create a short circuit. That’s an oxymoron, a figure of speech that combines two contradictory terms, like "organized chaos." And don’t confuse that with a paradox! A paradox is a statement that appears self-contradictory. It looks like a dead end, a trap, but a paradox can reveal an underlying truth upon closer inspection. "Less is more." It makes zero logical sense until you think about the elegance of a perfectly executed, minimalist heist. 

Then we have the sheer trickery of irony and substitution. Verbal irony occurs when a speaker's intended meaning contrasts with the literal meaning of the words spoken. Stepping into a cafeteria food fight and saying, "What a peaceful dining experience." When that irony gets nasty and personal, it becomes sarcasm, a form of verbal irony used to mock or convey contempt. And if the author is trying to hide the bloody reality of what they are talking about, they deploy euphemisms, which are mild or indirect words substituted for words considered too harsh or blunt. They didn’t "slaughter civilians," they engaged in "collateral damage."

But what happens when the author is a hack? What happens when their master plan has a massive hole in it? The heist collapses into a logical fallacy, which is a flaw in reasoning that weakens a rhetorical argument. When the author realizes they are losing the argument, they panic. An ad hominem fallacy attacks the person making the argument rather than the argument itself. Instead of debunking the scientist's climate data, they call the scientist an ugly, arrogant jerk. Or, out of sheer desperation, they build a fake security guard to beat up. A straw man fallacy misrepresents an opponent's argument to make the opponent's argument easier to attack. "Oh, you want to cut the defense budget by two percent? I guess you want to leave America completely defenseless and let our enemies destroy us tomorrow!"

You are giving twelve-year-olds the x-ray vision required to see how the world is constantly trying to manipulate, persuade, and trick them.
