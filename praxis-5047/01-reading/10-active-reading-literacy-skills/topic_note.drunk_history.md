You see, active reading is a process where the reader consciously interacts with a text to construct meaning. It’s not a lazy river ride; it’s a full-contact intellectual heist. You’re breaking into the author’s brain to steal the goods. And to do that, you have to outfit your twelve-year-old thieves with some serious cognitive hardware, because ultimately, active reading strategies improve a student's metacognition during the reading process. Metacognition! Thinking about their own thinking! It’s like installing a security camera in their own skull so they can watch themselves figure things out.

Let’s start with the interrogation room. We call it questioning. Questioning is an active reading skill where readers generate inquiries about the text before reading. They look at the cover, the title, the back blurbs, and they start firing off questions. *Who is this guy? Why is the sky green on this cover?* Then, the interrogation continues. Questioning is an active reading skill where readers generate inquiries about the text during reading. *Why did she just betray her best friend? What’s behind that door?* And they don't clock out when the book closes! Questioning is an active reading skill where readers generate inquiries about the text after reading, demanding to know if the ending truly resolved the conflict.

While they are interrogating, they should be hallucinating. Wait, strike that, visualizing. Visualizing is an active reading strategy where readers construct mental images of the characters described in the text, casting the movie in their heads so the protagonist isn't just a name, but a living, breathing weirdo. And it’s not just the people; visualizing is an active reading strategy where readers construct mental images of the settings described in the text. They have to mentally build the spooky mansion, the dystopian wasteland, or the agonizingly awkward middle school cafeteria. 

But how do you prove they are doing this? You give them a pen and demand vandalism. Annotating is an active reading skill involving highlighting text to isolate the juicy evidence, but a neon yellow page is just a page with a bad spray tan if they stop there. To make it count, annotating is an active reading skill involving writing marginal notes directly on a text. I’m talking aggressive marginalia. Arguments with the author, exclamation points, actual thoughts scribbled in the gutters of the page!

Now, a text is just a dead battery until the reader hooks up the jumper cables. We have to make connections. Text-to-self connections occur when a reader relates the events of a text to the reader's own personal experiences. When they read about a character failing epically in front of their crush, and they suddenly remember wiping out in the hallway yesterday, boom—spark! Then we have text-to-text connections, which involve finding similarities between the current reading and previously read materials. "Hey, this oppressive government in this book is just like that other oppressive government in the book we read last month!" They are building a literary web. Finally, we go global. Text-to-world connections link the themes of a text to historical events in the real world, like comparing a fictional rebellion to the American Revolution. But they also happen right now! But what happens when the text doesn't explicitly tell them what's going on? What if the author is playing coy? That’s when they use inferencing. Inferencing requires the reader to synthesize textual evidence with the reader's background knowledge. If a character walks in dripping wet with a broken umbrella, the text doesn't have to say "it's raining." The kid uses the clues plus their knowledge of weather to draw the conclusion. An inference is an unstated conclusion drawn directly from details provided within the text. 

And then there’s the big money: predictions. You need to pay attention here, because the Middle School English Language Arts 5047 exam tests a candidate's ability to evaluate the strength of a prediction based on textual evidence. Making a prediction involves anticipating future events in a narrative based on available clues. This isn't a random lottery guess! A valid reading prediction must be supported by specific textual evidence. If they predict the main character is going to turn into a werewolf, there better be a full moon and a sudden craving for raw steak in chapter two! Furthermore, a valid reading prediction aligns with established character traits within the narrative. If the protagonist is chronically terrified of spiders, predicting they will volunteer to clean out the arachnid exhibit at the zoo is garbage. It also has to make structural sense—a valid reading prediction aligns with the established plot trajectory within the narrative. But stories swerve, right? So active readers constantly evaluate initial predictions as new textual evidence emerges. They don’t hold onto a bad bet; active readers revise initial predictions as new textual evidence emerges. They adapt.

Of course, sometimes the whole mental operation crashes. The kid reads a paragraph and suddenly realizes they have no idea what just happened. That’s where you teach them to hit the brakes. Clarifying is a monitoring strategy used by readers to resolve comprehension breakdowns. They reread, they look up a word, they fix the glitch before the train derails completely.

Finally, we get to the absolute culinary gauntlet of the ELA world: the summary. The exam tests a candidate's ability to evaluate a summary of a reading passage, and let me tell you, kids are terrible at this at first. They want to give you a play-by-play of every breath the character took. No! Summarizing requires condensing a passage down to the passage's essential main ideas. You are boiling down a massive pot of stock into a single, potent drop of glaze. 

How do you grade it? Evaluating a summary requires determining if the summary includes all major plot points. If it leaves out the climax, throw it in the trash. Conversely, an effective summary completely omits minor details from the source text. Nobody cares what color socks the hero was wearing unless the socks were magic and saved the world. Furthermore, evaluating a summary requires determining if the summary misrepresents the original text. It has to be purely accurate to the source material. This means an effective summary accurately reflects the author's original intended meaning without twisting it. 

To achieve this, the summary must be an emotionless, sterilized operating room. An effective summary completely omits the reader's personal opinions. "The book was stupid" is not a summary; it's a Yelp review! Furthermore, an effective summary completely omits the reader's personal biases. You don't get to judge the text based on your own worldview; you just report the facts. Therefore, an objective summary maintains a neutral tone. It is the ultimate journalistic detachment.
