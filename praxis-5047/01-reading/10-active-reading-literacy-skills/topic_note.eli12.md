Reading isn't like sitting back and watching a movie while eating popcorn. It's more like playing a video game where you hold the controller! When you read, you aren't just staring at the words. Instead, active reading is a process where the reader consciously interacts with a text to construct meaning. For someone your age, changing from just "knowing the words" to really "understanding the story" means using special brain tricks. Without these tools, a big book is just ink on paper. With them, it becomes a whole new world that you can explore!

## The Architecture of Connection

Think of a new book like a brand-new video game console. It’s awesome, but it doesn't do anything until you plug it in! You are the electricity. We plug our brains into a story by making three kinds of connections.

*   **Text-to-self connections** occur when a reader relates the events of a text to the reader's own personal experiences. If you read about a character who is super nervous about playing in a big soccer game, and you remember how nervous you were before trying out for the school play, you just made a text-to-self connection!
*   **Text-to-text connections** involve finding similarities between the current reading and previously read materials. If you notice that the magic school in this book works exactly like the magic school in your favorite fantasy series, you're making a text-to-text connection.
*   **Text-to-world connections** are when you look at the big picture. These connections link the themes of a text to historical events in the real world (like comparing a story about a king taking over a country to the real-life American Revolution). Text-to-world connections link the themes of a text to current events in the real world, too (like connecting a sci-fi story about trash taking over the planet to the real news you see about recycling today).

> **The Professor's Note:** Why do connections matter? Because our brains love teaming things up! The more connections you make, the better you will remember what you read.

## The Inquiry Engine: Questioning and Visualizing

Great readers don't wait for a teacher to ask them questions; they act like detectives and ask their own! 

**Questioning** is an active reading skill where readers generate inquiries about the text all the time:
1.  *Before reading:* Questioning is an active reading skill where readers generate inquiries about the text before reading. You might look at the cover and ask, "Why is there a glowing sword on the front?"
2.  *During reading:* Questioning is an active reading skill where readers generate inquiries about the text during reading. You might pause and ask, "Why did the hero just trust the villain?"
3.  *After reading:* Questioning is an active reading skill where readers generate inquiries about the text after reading. You might ask, "Did I like how the story ended?"

While you ask questions, you should also be making a movie in your mind. **Visualizing** is an active reading strategy where readers construct mental images of the characters described in the text. Visualizing is an active reading strategy where readers construct mental images of the settings described in the text, too! It turns boring black-and-white words into full-color 3D action in your head.

To help remember all this, you can leave clues for yourself. **Annotating** is an active reading skill involving highlighting text (like using a neon marker on the best parts). But it’s not just coloring! Annotating is an active reading skill involving writing marginal notes directly on a text. This means you scribble your thoughts right in the blank spaces of the page!

## Decoding the Unstated: Inferencing and Predicting

Authors don't tell you everything. Sometimes they leave hints, and you have to play detective. We call this inferencing and predicting.

**Inferencing** requires the reader to synthesize textual evidence with the reader's background knowledge. "Synthesize" just means putting things together. An inference is an unstated conclusion drawn directly from details provided within the text. For example, if the author writes, "The girl stomped her feet, crossed her arms, and her face turned red," they didn't *say* she was angry. But you put the clues (stomping, red face) together with what you already know about people, and you figure it out!

**Making a prediction**, on the other hand, involves anticipating future events in a narrative based on available clues. It's like guessing who will win the basketball game based on the score at halftime. 

Fun fact about a big test that middle school teachers take: The Middle School English Language Arts 5047 exam tests a candidate's ability to evaluate the strength of a prediction based on textual evidence. That means teachers have to know exactly what makes a guess a *good* guess!

A valid reading prediction must have three things:
1.  A valid reading prediction must be supported by specific textual evidence (real clues from the book).
2.  A valid reading prediction aligns with established character traits within the narrative. (If a character is terrified of heights, predicting they will jump out of an airplane for fun makes no sense!)
3.  A valid reading prediction aligns with the established plot trajectory within the narrative. (It has to make sense with where the story is actually heading).

Because stories change, active readers constantly evaluate initial predictions as new textual evidence emerges. If a good guy suddenly turns evil, you don't keep guessing they will save the day. Instead, active readers revise initial predictions as new textual evidence emerges. 

## Distillation and Fidelity: The Art of the Summary

If predicting is about looking forward, summarizing is about shrinking things down. 

**Summarizing** requires condensing a passage down to the passage's essential main ideas. 

Think of condensing a story like calculating how much allowance you actually get to keep after buying a snack. Let's do a quick math calculation to show how you remove the extra stuff to find the main amount:
1. Start with your total weekly allowance (\$10).
2. Subtract the amount you spent on a slice of pizza (\$3).
3. Calculate your final remaining money (10 - 3 = \$7).

Just like you subtracted the \$3 to find out exactly what money truly matters at the end, summarizing subtracts the extra "fluff" of a story to find out what truly matters!

Another fun fact about that teacher test: The Middle School English Language Arts 5047 exam tests a candidate's ability to evaluate a summary of a reading passage. You have to know what makes a summary awesome or terrible. 

Evaluating a summary requires determining if the summary includes all major plot points. If a summary of *Cinderella* forgets to mention the glass slipper, it’s a bad summary! On the flip side, an effective summary completely omits minor details from the source text. You don't need to know the exact color of the curtains in the castle. 

Also, evaluating a summary requires determining if the summary misrepresents the original text. An effective summary accurately reflects the author's original intended meaning. It has to stay totally fair. 

| Feature of an Effective Summary | Why it Fails if Missing |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Omits personal opinions** | An effective summary completely omits the reader's personal opinions. Saying "I thought the ending was dumb" turns your summary into a review. |
| **Omits personal biases** | An effective summary completely omits the reader's personal biases. You have to judge the book by what it actually says, not by how you wish the world worked. |
| **Maintains neutral tone** | An objective summary maintains a neutral tone. Using super dramatic words messes up the author's real message. |

## Monitoring the Machinery: Clarifying and Metacognition

Even the best readers sometimes get confused. Maybe there's a weird word, or maybe the story suddenly jumped back in time. The trick is knowing what to do when you get stuck!

Great readers use **clarifying**, which is a monitoring strategy used by readers to resolve comprehension breakdowns. A "comprehension breakdown" is just a fancy way of saying "Wait, what just happened?" When you get stuck, you don't just keep reading and hope for the best. You pause. You go back. You fix it!

In the end, all of these tricks—connecting, visualizing, annotating, questioning, inferencing, predicting, summarizing, and clarifying—do one huge thing for your brain. Active reading strategies improve a student's metacognition during the reading process. **Metacognition** just means "thinking about your own thinking." 

When you learn to do all this, reading stops being a chore. You are in total control, turning a tricky book into an awesome adventure you completely understand!
