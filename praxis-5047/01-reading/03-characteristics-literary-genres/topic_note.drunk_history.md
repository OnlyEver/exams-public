You look stressed. Take a breath. Stop looking at those flashcards like they’re a ransom note, and look at me. Literature isn't just a blob of text. To survive teaching it, you need to understand the social ecosystem. Think of the literary world as a massive, chaotic middle school cafeteria. You’ve got your distinct cliques, your unwritten rules, your absolute turf wars. In our world, we call these cliques literary genres, which are simply categories of literature characterized by similarities in form, style, or subject matter. You wouldn't walk up to the goth kids and expect them to start a cheerleading pyramid, right? Same with texts. 

Let's look at the biggest table right in the middle: Prose Fiction. These kids follow the baseline rules of society. Prose fiction is written in standard grammatical structure using sentences and paragraphs. It’s blocky, glorious, and reliable. But here’s the kicker: they are professional liars. Prose fiction describes imagined events. The ringleader of this table is the narrator, who is the entity or voice telling a story in prose fiction. When that narrator starts weaving a tale through a sequence of connected events, you are witnessing a narrative. Now, sometimes this narrator will drag you into a massive, season-long commitment with a dozen subplots—that’s a novel, an extended fictional work in prose. But sometimes they just give you the quick, high-stakes gossip focusing on a single incident or character. That’s a short story, a brief fictional prose narrative. 

Within this prose fiction clique, you've got sub-groups, the flavors of the lie. You’ve got the grounded kids writing realistic fiction, which features events and characters that could plausibly exist in the real world—like bombing a math test or navigating a crush. Plausible, but still made up. Then there's the kids obsessed with corsets and trench warfare who write historical fiction, which is set in a specific verifiable past time period with fictional characters. The setting is fact, but the protagonist is imaginary. Over by the vending machines, you’ve got science fiction, which explores imaginative concepts like futuristic science, space travel, or extraterrestrial life. And finally, the green-screen kids writing fantasy literature, a genre that includes magical elements or supernatural worlds that do not exist in reality. If a dragon eats your homework, you’re in fantasy.

Now, look away from the prose table. Look at the art kids brooding by the window. They reject the paragraph. They are poetry, a literary genre organized into lines and stanzas. You have to teach your students that a line is just a single row of text in a poem, while a stanza is a grouped set of lines in a poem separated by a blank space. That blank space is the visual breath between thoughts. It’s deliberate! 

These poetry kids are entirely obsessed with sound. They care deeply about rhythm, the pattern of stressed and unstressed syllables in a line of poetry. It’s the natural heartbeat of the words. But when that heartbeat gets hooked up to a strict, mathematical metronome, it becomes meter, a regular, measured pattern of rhythm in a poem. And when they want to tie the auditory room together, they use a rhyme scheme, which is the ordered pattern of rhymes at the ends of the lines of a poem.

Of course, poets love rebelling against their own rules. So, you’ll find free verse, which is poetry that does not follow a strict meter or rhyme scheme. It just flows like chaotic thought. Then there’s the sneaky genius writing blank verse, which is unrhymed poetry written in iambic pentameter—it has that strict Shakespearean heartbeat, but it actively refuses to rhyme at the ends of the lines. You've got the obsessive, high-strung romantic writing a sonnet, a fourteen-line poem with a specific rhyme scheme and meter. And then the absolute minimalist dropping a haiku, a Japanese poetic form consisting of three lines with a five-seven-five syllable structure. 

What are they talking about over there? Well, a narrative poem tells a story and contains characters, a setting, and a plot. Yes, poems can steal the prose fiction table's lunch! But a lyric poem is pure, unfiltered emotion—it expresses personal emotions or thoughts and is typically written in the first person. But listen to me, if you take nothing else away tonight, remember this: do NOT let your middle schoolers think the poet is the one crying on the page. The voice speaking in a poem is called the speaker. It’s a mask! It’s an avatar! 

Across the room, standing on the cafeteria tables and screaming for attention, we have drama. Drama is a literary genre intended for theatrical performance by actors before an audience. They don't hand you a book; they hand you a blueprint. A script is the written text of a play. To keep from exhausting everyone, they slice that script into an act, which is a major division of a dramatic work. Think of it like a tectonic plate of the story. And within that act, you get a scene, a subdivision of an act in a play typically representing a single continuous action in one location. 

Here is the absolute golden rule of the drama table: Drama relies primarily on dialogue rather than narrative description to advance a plot. Nobody writes a paragraph saying "He felt a cold chill of sadness." No! They just scream at each other! Dialogue consists of the spoken words exchanged between characters in a dramatic work. If you desperately need someone to slap a table or turn off a lamp, you use stage directions, which are written instructions in a script indicating actor movements, lighting, and scenery. 

Let's talk about the big theatrical speeches, because your students will mix these up if you don't drill it into them. If a character delivers a long speech to other characters on stage—holding them hostage with words—that’s a monologue. But if the spotlight drops, the rest of the stage goes black, and it’s a speech delivered by a character alone on stage to reveal inner thoughts to the audience? That is a soliloquy. The character is spilling their guts to an empty room. And if they just lean over and whisper to the audience while the other characters are standing right next to them, completely oblivious? That’s an aside, a brief remark made by a character directly to the audience that other characters on stage do not hear. 

Where is all this screaming going? If it’s a tragedy, it’s a drama depicting the downfall of a heroic or noble character. Everything burns. But if it’s a comedy, it’s a dramatic work with a humorous tone intended to amuse the audience. We usually end up with a wedding or a massive party. 

Finally, hiding in the corner with a stack of CIA documents and a vintage typewriter, we have Literary Nonfiction. You might hear it called creative nonfiction or narrative nonfiction—it’s the exact same kid wearing different hats. Here is the absolute, iron-clad rule of this table: literary nonfiction must contain verifiable facts and actual historical events. Literary nonfiction describes real events. BUT! They refuse to be boring. Literary nonfiction uses literary elements such as narrative arcs and character development to present factual information. To make you feel like you are standing in the mud of a real battlefield, literary nonfiction often utilizes descriptive imagery to recreate factual settings and events. It's the truth, but dressed in its absolute best clothes.

If I write a factual account of my whole life, cradle to grave, that’s an autobiography. If I write a factual narrative focused on a specific period or theme in my life—like surviving my first year of teaching—that’s a memoir. If you write a detailed account of MY life, because I’m so endlessly fascinating, that’s a biography. And if I just want to sit here and give you a short work of autobiographical nonfiction characterized by a sense of intimacy and conversational manner? That’s a personal essay. 

Now, look at me. The Praxis is going to try to trip you up on the boundaries between these tables. The alliances! You need to know the crossovers to pass. For instance, notice how both prose fiction and literary nonfiction can utilize narrative arcs. Rising action, climax, resolution—both use the exact same roller coaster track! The only difference? Prose fiction describes imagined events, while literary nonfiction describes real events. And don't forget the storytellers in disguise: both drama and narrative poetry contain characters, plot, and setting. One uses stanzas, one uses scenes, but they both build a complete, inhabited universe.
