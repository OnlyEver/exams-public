Imagine starting a massive, open-world video game with absolutely no map. That is what staring at a blank internet search engine feels like! Without some cool strategies, you are just wandering around in a giant sea of random information. For us to really level up our brains, we need to know how to find the truth, mix different ideas together, and play fair when we write our papers. Learning to research is really just learning how to organize your awesome thoughts.

## The Architecture of Inquiry: From Search to Synthesis

**Effective research begins with formulating a clear and focused research question.** Think of this question as your main quest marker. Without it, you will just wander aimlessly! Once you have this question, you have to talk to the computer in a way it understands.

This means you need **keyword searching**, which involves extracting the most important terms from a research question to use in a search engine. But just typing a few words is not always enough. To get exactly what you want, you need **Boolean operators**—words used to connect or exclude keywords in a digital database search. They are like special cheat codes for Google!

*   **The Boolean operator AND narrows search results.** If you search `pizza AND pepperoni`, the database will only give you sites that have *both* words. This makes the giant pile of results much smaller and easier to look through.
*   **The Boolean operator OR broadens search results.** Searching `pizza OR calzone` tells the computer that either word is fine, giving you a bigger list of results to explore. 
*   **The Boolean operator NOT excludes specific terms from search results.** Searching `pizza NOT pineapple` kicks out any websites about pineapple pizza from your search.

Once you have a list of good links, you cannot spend three hours reading every single one word-for-word. That would drain your energy bar! Instead, use two speedy reading tricks:
1.  **Skimming** is a reading technique used to quickly identify the overarching main ideas of a text. (Like looking at the title, headings, and the last paragraph to see what the whole thing is about).
2.  **Scanning** is a reading technique used to locate specific facts or keywords within a text. (Like a scavenger hunt where you just look for a specific date or a name).

The goal isn't just to copy all this stuff down. Real learning means **synthesizing information**, which requires combining evidence from multiple sources to form a new and unified understanding. It is like taking flour, cheese, and tomatoes to bake a brand-new pizza! Once you mix everything together, you can create your final argument. **A research thesis statement proposes a specific and arguable claim based on gathered evidence.** It is the big, final destination that your original research quest was leading you to.

## The Anatomy of a Source: Evaluating Credibility

Not all information on the internet is good information. Knowing where a fact comes from is super important. 

Sources usually fall into two buckets, kind of like being at a sports game versus watching it on TV:
*   **Primary sources offer direct, firsthand evidence about an event or topic.** (Like a diary entry from a soldier, a photo taken at the event, or raw data from a science experiment).
*   **Secondary sources provide interpretation or analysis based on primary sources.** (Like a history book written today about something that happened 100 years ago).

When you are clicking around the web, look at the end of the website's address (the URL). These letters give you a big clue about what the site wants:
*   **A .edu domain extension indicates a website is affiliated with a recognized educational institution.** (Like a college or university).
*   **A .gov domain extension indicates a website is officially managed by a government entity.** (Like NASA or the White House).
*   **Websites with a .com domain extension exist primarily for commercial purposes.** (This means they are usually trying to make money, which sometimes changes how they share information).

But just looking at the web address isn't a magic shield. **Source credibility depends significantly on the author's expertise in the subject matter.** If you want to know about black holes, an astrophysicist is more credible than a random guy making YouTube videos in his basement. The absolute best, gold-star standard is found in **peer-reviewed journals**, which contain articles evaluated by independent experts in the same field prior to publication. It is like having a bunch of pro gamers test a game for glitches before it is allowed to be sold.

For regular websites, teachers use a funny-sounding trick to see if a site is good. **The CRAAP test is an acronym used by educators to evaluate source credibility.** It trains you to act like a detective:

> **The CRAAP Test**
> *   **Currency:** This aspect evaluates the timeliness of the published information. Is the information too old? To check this, you can use simple math:
>     1. Find the current year (for example, 2024).
>     2. Look at the webpage to find the year the article was published (let's say 1998).
>     3. Subtract the publication year from the current year: 2024 - 1998 = 26.
>     4. Evaluate the result: A 26-year-old article about smartphones is way too old to be useful!
> *   **Relevance:** This aspect assesses the importance of the information for the user's specific needs. (Does this actually help my paper?)
> *   **Authority:** This aspect examines the credentials of the information's author or publisher. (Is the author a real expert?)
> *   **Accuracy:** This aspect evaluates the reliability and correctness of the informational content. (Are there solid facts to back this up?)
> *   **Purpose:** This aspect identifies the reason the information exists. (Is this site trying to teach me something, or is it just trying to sell me a \$50 pair of sneakers?)

### Detecting Bias and Verifying Truth
While you use the CRAAP test, keep an eye out for unfair writing. **Bias in a text occurs when an author presents a one-sided perspective.** How can you spot it? **The presence of heavily emotional language in a source often indicates subjective bias.** If the writer sounds super angry or tries to make you feel guilty instead of just giving you facts, you need to be careful!

To make sure you aren't being tricked, you need to practice **cross-referencing**, which involves checking information across multiple independent sources to verify factual accuracy. If one random blog says aliens landed in your town, but no other news station is talking about it, cross-referencing proves it probably isn't true.

## The Ethics of Scholarship: Integration and Plagiarism

When you put research into your own paper, it is easy to make mistakes—not because you are a bad person, but because the rules can be tricky! 

First, let's talk about the biggest rule: **Plagiarism is the act of presenting another person's work or ideas as one's own.** You already know that copying a Wikipedia page is cheating. But did you know that **self-plagiarism occurs when a student submits their own previously graded work for a new assignment?** You always have to do fresh work!

A really common mistake for middle schoolers is copying almost everything but changing a couple of words. **Patchwriting is a form of plagiarism that involves replacing a few words of a source text with synonyms.** For example, taking "The large dog ran fast" and changing it to "The big dog sprinted quickly." Why is this bad? Because **patchwriting improperly retains the original sentence structure of the source text.** It is like taking your friend's drawing, coloring the shirt red instead of blue, and saying you drew the whole thing. 

To be honest and fair, you must use one of these three methods:

1.  **Direct Quoting:** **Direct quoting requires copying the exact words from a source.** Because you are borrowing their exact words, **direct quotes must be enclosed within quotation marks.** 
    *   *Modifying Quotes:* Sometimes you need to tweak a quote so it makes sense in your sentence. **Ellipses are used within a quotation to indicate the intentional omission of original words** (like this: "The coach said the team... played a great game"). On the flip side, **brackets are used within a quotation to indicate words added or modified by the researcher for clarity** (like this: "He [the goalie] blocked the shot").
2.  **Paraphrasing:** To really show you understand a topic, you should paraphrase. **Paraphrasing involves rewriting a specific passage from a source entirely into the researcher's own words.** To do this right, **a proper paraphrase must maintain the original factual meaning of the source text.**
3.  **Summarizing:** **Summarizing involves condensing the main ideas of a large source into a significantly shorter overview.** Paraphrasing is for a short passage, but summarizing is like telling your friend what a two-hour movie was about in just thirty seconds. 

### The Mandate of Attribution
Whether you use someone else's exact words or just rewrite their ideas, there is an absolute rule: **Both paraphrased ideas and direct quotes require a formal citation.** A citation is just giving credit to the original creator.

There is only one time you don't have to give credit. **Common knowledge consists of widely known, undisputed facts** (like the fact that a week has 7 days, or that water freezes at 32 degrees Fahrenheit). Because everybody already knows these things, **common knowledge does not require formal citation in a research paper.**

## The Map of Attribution: Documentation Techniques

A great research paper has a map that helps your teacher see exactly where you got your facts. This map has two parts: tiny signposts in your paragraphs, and a big list at the very end.

**In-text citations direct the reader to the full publication information located in the bibliography.** The way you write these depends on what subject you are studying. 

| Feature | MLA Format (Modern Language Association) | APA Format (American Psychological Association) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Primary Use** | **The Modern Language Association (MLA) style is commonly used for formatting humanities and literature papers.** | **The American Psychological Association (APA) style is commonly used for formatting social sciences papers.** |
| **In-Text Citation** | **Standard MLA in-text citations require the author's last name and the page number.** (e.g., Smith 42). | **Standard APA in-text citations require the author's last name and the year of publication.** (e.g., Smith, 2023). |

In your English class, you will almost always use MLA style. At the very end of your paper, you need a big list of everything you used. **Works Cited is the MLA terminology for the alphabetical list of sources at the end of a paper.** 

Making this page look right means following specific typing rules. For example, **a hanging indent is required for formatting multi-line entries in an MLA Works Cited page.** This means the very first line of the source is pushed all the way to the left margin, but the lines under it are pushed in (indented). It makes it super easy for your teacher's eye to read down the alphabetical list of authors!

### Advanced Citations and Bibliographies
Sometimes you will be reading a book by Author A, and Author A quotes a really cool sentence spoken by Author B. You can't just pretend you read Author B's book! Instead, **an indirect citation is used when referencing a source that is quoted within another secondary source.** To do this the right way, **in MLA format, the abbreviation 'qtd. in' is placed inside the parenthetical citation to indicate an indirect source** (It stands for "quoted in," like this: "The sky is falling" (qtd. in Henny Penny 14)).

Finally, if you are doing a massive project, your teacher might ask you to make a special list before you even start writing your paper. **An annotated bibliography includes a brief summary and evaluative commentary for each listed source.** It is like making a giant set of trading cards for your sources, where you prove you understand what the article says and why it is a trustworthy place to get your facts!
