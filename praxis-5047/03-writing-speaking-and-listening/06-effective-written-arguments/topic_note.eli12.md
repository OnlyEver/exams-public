Imagine marching up to your parents to ask for a \$10 per week allowance. You do not just ask and hope for the best. You probably list off your reasons, make big predictions about how helpful you will be, and tell stories about friends who already get an allowance. 

When you organize these thoughts on paper, you are building an argument. An argument is a structured set of reasons provided to support a specific conclusion. To be really good at this, you need to understand how to build your case with solid facts and clear thinking. By doing this, you can figure out exactly why someone's ideas make sense, or why they fall apart.

## The Architecture of an Argument

Every piece of writing has a different job to do. Expository texts primarily seek to explain or inform readers about a specific topic. You can think of them like a video game instruction manual. Persuasive texts primarily seek to convince readers to adopt a specific viewpoint. In a lot of cases, persuasive texts often urge readers to take a specific action, like buying a certain brand of sneakers or joining a club.

At the very center of a persuasive essay is the claim. A claim is an arguable statement presented as a fact. It is the engine that drives your whole essay. An effective written argument requires a clear and debatable central claim. If everyone in the world already agrees with it (like "pizza is a food"), there is no argument to make!

A thesis statement establishes the central claim of a written argument. It is the anchor of the text. When you read different arguments, you will notice that these claims usually fall into three main categories:

1. A claim of fact asserts that something is demonstrably true or false. *(Example: "Playing video games before bed makes it harder to fall asleep.")*
2. A claim of value asserts that something is fundamentally good or bad. *(Example: "The new superhero movie is the worst movie of the year.")*
3. A claim of policy asserts that a specific course of action should be taken. *(Example: "Parents should let kids choose their own bedtimes.")*

## The Logic Engine: Reasoning and Soundness

Once you have your claim, you have to prove it is true. Moving from your starting claim to your final point takes reasoning. We do not just ask if an argument "sounds okay." We check to see if the logic is sturdy. 

In logic, you start with premises, which are your starting facts. 
> Valid reasoning dictates that a conclusion must logically follow from the provided premises. 
> Sound reasoning requires both logically valid structures and demonstrably true premises. 

An argument might follow a logical path but still be totally wrong if the starting fact is made up. *(For example: All middle schoolers are aliens. You are a middle schooler. Therefore, you are an alien. The steps follow each other, but it is not sound because the first fact is totally false!)*

People usually think things through using two different kinds of logic:

*   Deductive reasoning moves from general rules to a specific conclusion. If your general rule is true, your final answer will definitely be true. Let's look at a math example to show how deductive reasoning works. If your general rule is that every video game costs exactly \$50, and you buy 3 games, you deduce the total cost by following these steps:
    1. Identify the rule: Each video game costs \$50.
    2. Identify the specific observation: You are buying 3 video games.
    3. Multiply the cost by the number of games: 50 x 3 = 150.
    4. Conclusion: Your total cost is \$150.
*   Inductive reasoning moves from specific observations to a broader generalization. For example, if you see three friends eating pepperoni pizza, you might guess that everyone in your whole class loves pepperoni. Because you are guessing about a big group based on a small group, inductive reasoning yields probabilistic conclusions (meaning it's just a probability). Because of this, inductive reasoning does not guarantee absolute certainty. 

## The Pitfalls: Logical Fallacies

Sometimes, the logic in an argument breaks down. Logical fallacies are structural flaws in reasoning. Think of them like glitches in a video game that make the game crash. Logical fallacies undermine the validity of a written argument. 

You should be able to spot these glitches easily so you don't get tricked!

| Fallacy | What It Means | Everyday Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Ad Hominem** | An ad hominem fallacy attacks the character of an opponent. An ad hominem fallacy ignores the substance of an opponent's actual argument. | "Don't listen to his idea for a new school club because he is bad at math." |
| **Straw Man** | A straw man fallacy intentionally misrepresents an opponent's argument. A straw man fallacy aims to make an opponent's argument easier to attack. | *Mom:* "Please clean your room." <br>*You:* "Mom wants me to spend 24 hours a day scrubbing floors!" |
| **Slippery Slope** | A slippery slope fallacy asserts that one minor event will inevitably trigger a chain of extreme consequences. | "If I don't get this new video game, my friends will ignore me, and I'll end up lonely forever." |
| **Red Herring** | A red herring fallacy introduces irrelevant information into a debate. A red herring fallacy aims to distract an audience from the primary argument. | "I know I forgot to take out the trash, but aren't you glad I fed the dog?" |
| **Hasty Generalization** | A hasty generalization fallacy draws a broad conclusion from a very small sample size. A hasty generalization fallacy can stem from an unrepresentative data sample. | "I tried one bite of broccoli and hated it, so all vegetables are disgusting." |
| **Circular Reasoning** | Circular reasoning occurs when an argument's conclusion is used as one of the argument's supporting premises. | "This is the best sports team because they are better than everyone else." |
| **False Dilemma** | A false dilemma fallacy presents exactly two competing options. A false dilemma fallacy deliberately ignores other viable possibilities. | "You either buy me a skateboard, or you want me to be bored forever." |
| **Post Hoc Ergo Propter Hoc** | The post hoc ergo propter hoc fallacy assumes that a preceding event directly caused a subsequent event. | "I wore my lucky socks and scored a goal, so my socks made me score." |

## The Building Blocks: Types of Evidence

An argument is only as strong as the pieces used to build it. Evidence consists of verifiable data or information utilized to support a claim. 

When you look at evidence, you have to ask two questions. First, does it stay on topic? Relevant evidence directly connects to the specific claim being made. Beyond just connecting, relevant evidence actively supports the specific claim being made. Second, is there enough of it? Sufficient evidence means a writer has provided enough distinct data points to fully justify a claim. One single fact can't prove a huge claim about the whole world.

Writers use different types of materials to build their arguments:

*   Factual evidence is based on objective realities. Factual evidence can be independently proven true or false. (Like knowing water freezes at 32 degrees).
*   Opinion evidence relies entirely on personal judgments or beliefs. (Like saying "Vanilla is boring.")
*   Statistical evidence employs numerical data to validate a claim. Let's look at another math example for statistics. If you want to find the average score of your last three math tests (80, 90, 100), you use statistical evidence by following these steps:
    1. Add the test scores together: 80 + 90 + 100 = 270.
    2. Count the total number of tests taken: 3.
    3. Divide the sum by the number of tests: 270 / 3 = 90.
    4. Conclusion: The statistical average is 90.
*   Empirical evidence is gathered through direct scientific observation. Also, empirical evidence is gathered through controlled scientific experimentation. (Think of science fair projects).
*   Expert testimony consists of formal statements made by recognized authorities in a specific field. (Like a dentist telling you to brush your teeth).
*   Anecdotal evidence relies on isolated personal stories. (Like telling a funny story about your cousin). While stories are fun to read, anecdotal evidence is objectively insufficient to prove a broad, generalized claim.

## Rhetoric, Bias, and Source Evaluation

A smart reader doesn't just look at the facts; they look at *how* the writer is sharing those facts. 

The tone of a text tells you a lot. An objective tone avoids personal pronouns to present information neutrally. An objective tone avoids emotional language to present information neutrally. It sounds like a textbook. On the other hand, a subjective tone deliberately includes personal feelings and opinions. 

We also have to watch out for unfairness. Authorial bias occurs when personal prejudices improperly influence a writer's argument. As readers, we also have to check our own brains! Confirmation bias is the human tendency to favor information confirming preexisting beliefs. If you already love a certain video game, you might only read reviews that agree with you.

### The Aristotelian Appeals
Thousands of years ago, a philosopher named Aristotle figured out how writers persuade people. A great argument uses three tools:
1. Logos is a rhetorical appeal based entirely on logic and objective evidence. 
2. Ethos is a rhetorical appeal based on the credibility of the author. Ethos is a rhetorical appeal based on the ethical character of the author. (If a famous, honest athlete tells you to buy sports gear, you trust them).
3. Pathos is a rhetorical appeal designed to evoke a strong emotional response from the audience. (Like a sad commercial with puppy dogs).

### Source Evaluation
When you read an argument, you need to check where the writer got their facts. 
*   Primary sources provide firsthand evidence concerning a specific historical event or scientific topic. (Like a photograph from the event or original lab data).
*   Secondary sources offer analysis or interpretation of primary sources. (Like a history textbook written years later).

To know if a source is trustworthy, you have to do some investigating. Evaluating source credibility requires examining the author's professional credentials. (Are they a real doctor, or just a guy on the internet?) Evaluating source credibility requires examining the reputation of the publishing organization. (An article from a major science magazine is better than a random blog post).

## The Art of the Counter-Strike

Finally, the best arguments don't run away from the other side; they invite the other side in! A counterclaim is an opposing perspective to the writer's central claim. 

Why would you ever bring up a reason *against* your own idea? Because acknowledging counterclaims demonstrates a writer's comprehensive understanding of a topic. It shows the reader you did your homework! 

Right after bringing it up, you get to strike it down. A rebuttal is a direct response intended to disprove a counterclaim. By bringing up the other side's point and showing why it is wrong, you create an unbeatable argument.
