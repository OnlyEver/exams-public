Think of writing like building a custom level in your favorite video game. Why are you making it? Well, **the purpose of a piece of writing is the author's primary reason for creating the text**. Generally, you have three main choices. **Persuading the reader is one of the primary purposes for writing** (like trying to convince your parents to buy you a new game). **Informing the reader is one of the primary purposes for writing** (like making a game walkthrough guide for your friends). And **entertaining the reader is one of the primary purposes for writing** (like writing a fun story to read). In school, we group these types of writing into four main buckets: **the four traditional modes of discourse are narration, description, exposition, and argumentation**.

Before you write a single word, you have to think about who is reading it. The **audience is the specific group of readers for whom a text is intended**. You wouldn't talk to your school principal the exact same way you talk to your best friend! Because of this, **an author must adapt the vocabulary of a piece of writing to suit the intended audience**. They also have to think about their attitude. **An author must adapt the tone of a piece of writing to suit the intended audience**.

What exactly is tone? **Tone is the author's attitude toward the subject matter of a text**. At the same time, **tone is the author's attitude toward the audience of a text**. Think of tone as the sound of your voice—are you serious, joking, or annoyed? Now, contrast that with mood. While tone is how the *writer* feels, **mood is the emotional atmosphere a piece of writing creates for the reader** (like the spooky vibe of a haunted house story).

## The Architecture of Reason: Argumentative vs. Persuasive Text

In middle school, things get a little more advanced. **The Common Core State Standards for English Language Arts differentiate between argumentative writing and persuasive writing**. They might sound similar, but they are totally different games!

Let's talk about arguments first. **Argumentative writing aims to convince the reader to accept a specific claim**, or sometimes, **argumentative writing aims to convince the reader to take a specific action**. It is not about begging. Instead, **argumentative writing relies on logical reasoning to support claims** and **argumentative writing relies on factual evidence to support claims**.

A **claim in argumentative writing is a statement asserting a specific viewpoint** (like "I should get a higher allowance"). But you can't just say that; you need backup. **Evidence in argumentative writing includes verifiable facts** (things you can prove are 100% true), **evidence in argumentative writing includes relevant statistics** (numbers and math), and **evidence in argumentative writing includes expert opinions** (quotes from people who know their stuff).

For example, if you use math to build your argument for a higher allowance using relevant statistics, you might show your work like this:
Step 1: Count your weekly chores (let's say you have 5 chores).
Step 2: Find the average price a professional cleaner charges for one chore online (for example, \$2).
Step 3: Multiply your chores by the value to show what your labor is worth (5 chores * \$2 = \$10).
Step 4: Conclude that your fair allowance should be \$10 instead of your current \$5!

But just throwing numbers at people isn't quite enough. You need reasoning. **Reasoning in argumentative writing explains how evidence proves a claim**. Underneath that explanation is something called a warrant. **A warrant is the underlying logical assumption connecting evidence to a claim** (the invisible rule that says "doing more work equals more money").

Because the real world is tricky, **argumentative writing must acknowledge alternate or opposing claims**. You have to admit what the other side thinks! You do this by introducing a counterclaim. **A counterclaim in argumentative writing presents an opposing viewpoint to the main claim** (like "My parents might say I don't do my chores fast enough"). Then, you crush it with a rebuttal. **A rebuttal in argumentative writing is a response refuting a presented counterclaim**.

Because it is so focused on facts, **the tone of argumentative writing is generally objective** (fair and factual) and **the tone of argumentative writing is generally formal** (professional, not slang). It is like playing a puzzle strategy game: **argumentative writing prioritizes logical appeals over emotional appeals**. It **uses the rhetorical appeal of logos to engage the audience's sense of reason** (logos = logic!). It also **uses the rhetorical appeal of ethos to establish authorial credibility** (showing you are trustworthy and fair). But watch out for cheating: **logical fallacies undermine the validity of a claim in argumentative writing** (these are bad arguments that don't actually make sense). Finally, **a well-structured argumentative essay concludes with a statement naturally following from the presented argument**.

Where do you see this in real life? **An editorial is a common example of argumentative writing**, and **an op-ed piece is a common example of argumentative writing** (both of these are opinion pieces found in newspapers). Inside your classroom, **a literary analysis essay is an argumentative text presenting a claim about a piece of literature** (a book). When you write one, **a literary analysis essay uses direct quotations from the primary text as evidence**.

Now, compare this to persuasion. **Persuasive writing relies heavily on emotional appeals to convince an audience**. It is all about feelings! It **prioritizes the author's personal credibility to convince an audience**. And unlike arguments where you must show the other side's view, **persuasive writing often ignores opposing viewpoints entirely**.

## The Mechanics of Clarity: Informative and Explanatory Writing

Let's switch gears. **Exposition is a traditional mode of discourse intended to inform or define a subject**. Basically, it's just explaining stuff. In school, **the Common Core State Standards group informative and explanatory writing into a single category**.

Here is the difference between the two: **informative writing aims to convey complex ideas clearly** and **informative writing aims to convey factual information accurately**. On the other hand, **explanatory writing aims to clarify the mechanics of a concept** (how something works) or **explanatory writing aims to clarify the steps of a process** (how to do something). The single most important rule is that **informative writing does not attempt to persuade the reader to adopt a specific viewpoint**. It's just the facts!

Every informative paper starts with a core idea. **A thesis statement in informative writing introduces the central idea of the text**. Then, you break it down into paragraphs. In each paragraph, **a topic sentence introduces the main idea of a specific paragraph within a larger text**. Next, **supporting details provide specific evidence to back up a topic sentence**. Finally, **a concluding sentence summarizes the main point of a paragraph**.

**The tone of informative writing is typically objective** and **the tone of informative writing is typically unbiased** (meaning you don't pick sides or share your own feelings). Because you are teaching the reader, **informative writing requires the selection of concrete details to support the topic**. To make it flow smoothly, **transition words are used in informative writing to clarify relationships among concepts** (words like "therefore," "next," or "however").

You can organize this information in a few cool ways. **Informative writing frequently utilizes cause-and-effect organizational structures** (this happened, so that happened). It also **frequently utilizes compare-and-contrast organizational structures** (how two things are alike and different). Sometimes, **informative writing frequently utilizes problem-and-solution organizational structures**, or it **frequently utilizes chronological organizational structures** (putting events in time order).

To make reading easier, **informative texts frequently incorporate formatting elements like headings to organize information**. They also **frequently incorporate text features like bold print to emphasize key terms**, **frequently incorporate glossaries to define domain-specific vocabulary** (like a mini-dictionary at the back of a book), and **frequently incorporate indexes to help readers locate specific information**.

Need some real-world examples? **A scientific report is a common example of informative writing**. So is the news! **A news article is an informative text reporting on current events**. And if you look something up online, **an encyclopedia entry is an informative text providing a factual summary of a topic**.

### Subcategories of Informative Text
Sometimes you need to tell people exactly how to do something step-by-step. **Procedural writing is a subcategory of informative writing explaining how to complete a task**. Because you have to do things in a specific order, **procedural writing typically uses sequential organization**. It also **often uses the imperative mood to give direct commands** (like "Chop the onions!"). **Instruction manuals are common examples of procedural writing**, and **recipes are common examples of procedural writing**.

Another type is for reporters. **Journalistic writing aims to report news objectively**. When writing an article, **journalistic writing explicitly answers the questions who, what, when, where, why, and how**. To grab your attention fast, **journalistic writing typically uses the inverted pyramid structure**. What's that? **The inverted pyramid structure places the most important information at the very beginning of an article**, with the least important details at the very end.

## The Geometry of Experience: Narrative Writing

Narrative writing is all about telling stories. **Narrative writing aims to develop real experiences** or **narrative writing aims to develop imagined experiences**. To make a story work, **narrative writing relies on well-structured sequences of events**. This skeleton structure is called the plot. **A plot is the sequence of events making up a narrative**.

What makes a story interesting? A problem! **A conflict is a struggle between opposing forces driving the plot of a narrative**. This struggle grows over time. First, **an inciting incident is a narrative plot element introducing the central conflict** (the moment the trouble begins). Things get crazier until **the climax is the narrative plot element representing the point of highest tension** (the big boss battle!). Finally, the story cools down, and **the resolution is the narrative plot element where the main conflict is resolved**.

Let's look at the characters. **A protagonist is the primary character driving the action in a narrative** (the hero). On the flip side, **an antagonist is the character or force opposing the protagonist in a narrative** (the villain). How do we learn about them? **Characterization is the process by which an author reveals the personality of characters in a narrative**. One of the best ways to do this is when characters talk. **Narrative writing frequently utilizes dialogue to advance the plot** and **narrative writing frequently utilizes dialogue to reveal character traits**. We also get to see inside their heads: **narrative writing uses reflection to provide insight into a character's internal thoughts**.

Every story happens somewhere. **The setting of a narrative establishes the time period of the story's events** (like the year 3000) and **the setting of a narrative establishes the physical location of the story's events** (like a spaceship). But what is the story *really* about? **The theme of a narrative is the underlying message of the story** (like "friendship beats evil").

Stories are told through different lenses. **Point of view is the perspective from which a narrative is told.**
*   **First-person point of view uses the pronoun 'I' to narrate a story from a specific character's perspective** (like "I grabbed my sword").
*   **Second-person point of view addresses the reader directly using the pronoun 'you'** (like "You look around the room"). Note: outside of stories, **second-person point of view is frequently used in instructional explanatory writing**!
*   **Third-person limited point of view reveals the internal thoughts of only one character** (the narrator watches from above but only knows what the hero thinks).
*   **Third-person omniscient point of view reveals the internal thoughts of multiple characters** (the narrator is like a mind-reading ghost who knows everyone's secrets).

Writers can control how fast the story feels. **Narrative pacing controls the speed at which a story unfolds for the reader**. If you are reading an action scene, **authors use short sentences to increase pacing during high-action narrative scenes** (He ran. He jumped. Bam!). On the other hand, **authors use long sentences to slow down pacing in narrative writing** to make you relax or think. Writers can also play with time! **Foreshadowing is a narrative technique providing hints about future events in the story**, while **flashbacks are narrative techniques interrupting chronological order to describe past events**. To make you feel like you are really there in the scene, **narrative writing relies heavily on sensory details to create vivid imagery** (describing smells, sounds, and sights).

### Classifications of Narrative Writing
Stories about real life:
*   **A personal narrative is a true story about a real-life experience written from the author's perspective**.
*   **A biography is a narrative account of a person's life written by someone else**.
*   **An autobiography is a narrative account of a person's life written by the subject of the account**.
*   **A memoir is a narrative account focusing on a specific thematic period in the author's life** (like a whole book just about your middle school years).

Made-up stories:
*   **A short story is a common example of fictional narrative writing**.
*   **A novel is a long-form example of fictional narrative writing**.
*   **Realistic fiction is a narrative genre featuring events that could happen in real life** (like a story about trying out for the basketball team).
*   **Historical fiction is a narrative genre placing fictional characters in factual historical settings** (like a made-up kid riding on the real Titanic).
*   **Science fiction is a narrative genre exploring imagined futuristic concepts** (aliens and space travel!).
*   **Fantasy is a narrative genre featuring magical or supernatural elements** (dragons and wizards!).
*   **A fable is a brief narrative conveying a moral lesson**. In these, **fables frequently feature anthropomorphized animal characters** (animals acting and talking like humans, like the tortoise and the hare).
*   **A myth is a traditional narrative explaining natural phenomena** (like a story about why the sun sets). **Myths frequently feature gods or superhuman characters**.

## The Art of Observation: Descriptive Writing

Have you ever tried to describe a giant, cheesy pepperoni pizza so perfectly that it makes your friend's mouth water? That's what this is! **Descriptive writing aims to paint a vivid mental picture of a specific subject**. Because it is so good at building worlds, **descriptive writing is often incorporated into narrative writing to establish a vivid setting**.

How do you do it? **Descriptive writing relies heavily on figurative language to create imagery**. It **frequently incorporates similes to compare dissimilar subjects** using "like" or "as" (for example, "the pizza was like a sun of cheese"). It also **frequently incorporates metaphors to draw direct comparisons between subjects** without using "like" or "as" (for example, "the pepperoni slices were fiery little lakes"). It also **relies on precise nouns to create clear images** (saying "a rusty pickup truck" instead of just "a car") and **relies on vivid verbs to create clear images** (saying "sprinted" instead of just "ran").

If you are describing a whole room, you need a plan. **Spatial organization is a common structure used in descriptive writing**. What does that mean? **Spatial organization arranges descriptive information according to physical location** (like describing your bedroom starting from the floor up to the ceiling). A cool example of this in the real world is a travel diary: **a travelogue frequently blends descriptive and narrative writing elements** to tell a story while describing beautiful places.

## Beyond Prose: Poetry, Drama, and Higher-Order Texts

There are types of writing that don't look like normal paragraphs.

First, there are poems! **Poetry is a type of writing utilizing rhythmic qualities of language to evoke meaning**. Instead of paragraphs, **poetry organizes text into stanzas** (those little blocks of text). Instead of sentences stretching across the whole page, **poetry utilizes line breaks to control rhythm**.

Next, there are plays! **Drama is a type of writing intended for performance by actors**. Because there isn't a narrator explaining everything out loud, **drama relies heavily on dialogue to communicate the plot to an audience**. But how do the actors know what to do on stage? **Drama utilizes stage directions to provide instructions on character movement** and to **provide instructions on stage lighting**.

As you get older, you'll tackle some heavy-duty assignments:
*   **Analytical writing breaks down a complex topic into smaller parts for detailed examination**. For English class, **analytical writing frequently evaluates the effectiveness of an author's rhetorical choices** (figuring out exactly *why* a writer used a specific word).
*   **Synthesis in writing involves combining information from multiple distinct sources to form a new conclusion** (like mixing three different ingredients to make a brand-new flavor). In college, **a literature review is a common example of writing that synthesizes multiple sources**.
*   **Reflective writing explores the author's personal insights regarding a specific experience**. If you ever have to keep a journal for class, **a learning journal is a common example of reflective writing**.

## Equipping the ELA Classroom: Pedagogy in Practice

Teachers have special tools to help you build great writing! Before you start mashing the keyboard, **graphic organizers help students visually structure ideas before drafting an essay**. They are like blueprints.
*   **A Venn diagram is a graphic organizer specifically suited for planning compare-and-contrast writing** (those two overlapping circles!).
*   **A T-chart is a graphic organizer frequently used to outline claims and counterclaims for argumentative writing** (a literal "T" shape with pros on one side and cons on the other).
*   **Story maps are graphic organizers used to plan the setting, characters, and plot sequence of a narrative**.

Teachers also show you what winning looks like. **Mentor texts are published texts used to illustrate specific genre characteristics for students**. It is like watching a pro-gamer stream a level before you try to beat it yourself.

Finally, how do you know exactly how to get an A? You read the rules! **A writing rubric is an evaluation tool explicitly defining the criteria for grading a specific type of writing assignment**, telling you exactly what you need to do to succeed.
