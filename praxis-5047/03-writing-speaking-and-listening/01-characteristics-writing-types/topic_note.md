A piece of writing is fundamentally an architectural [blueprint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blueprint). If an engineer’s primary reason for creating a [schematic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schematic)—the **purpose of a piece of writing**—is to build a [suspension bridge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suspension_bridge), they do not use the design language of a [cathedral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cathedral). [Form follows function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Form_follows_function). In the linguistic universe, **[persuading](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persuasion) the reader**, **informing the reader**, and **entertaining the reader** are the primary purposes for writing. We categorize these efforts into the **four [traditional modes of discourse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modes_of_discourse): [narration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Narrative), [description](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Description), [exposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expository_writing), and [argumentation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Argumentation_theory)**. As an educator preparing students for complex [literacy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Literacy), your mastery of how these texts operate structurally allows you to diagnose student writing, parse complex reading passages, and demystify the mechanics of the [English language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_language).

![A 1944 architectural blueprint illustrating how complex designs rely on precise schematics, much like how different modes of writing rely on deliberate structural frameworks.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/John_C._Butler-class_destroyer_escort_outboard_profile%2C_29_September_1944_(20737474).JPG)

Before a single word is drafted, writers must calibrate their instruments. A blueprint is useless if the builder cannot read it; thus, the **[audience](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Audience) is the specific group of readers for whom a text is intended**. To communicate effectively, **an [author](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Author) must adapt the [vocabulary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vocabulary) of a piece of writing to suit the intended audience** and **adapt the [tone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tone_%28literature%29) of a piece of writing to suit the intended audience**. 

What exactly is tone? Simply put, **tone is the author's attitude toward the subject matter of a text**, and concurrently, **tone is the author's attitude toward the audience of a text**. Contrast this with [mood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mood_%28literature%29): if tone is the author's voice, **mood is the emotional [atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_%28literature%29) a piece of writing creates for the reader**. 

Let us examine the specific structural characteristics of the text types you will teach and analyze in your classroom.

## The Architecture of Reason: Argumentative vs. Persuasive Text

In [middle school](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_school) ELA, we must untangle the frequent confusion between argument and persuasion. **The [Common Core State Standards](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Common_Core_State_Standards_Initiative) for English Language Arts differentiate between [argumentative writing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Argumentative_essay) and persuasive writing**, treating them as distinct intellectual exercises. 

Argumentative writing is the rigorous domain of [logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logic). By definition, **argumentative writing aims to convince the reader to accept a specific [claim](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proposition)** or **to take a specific action**. It does not beg or plead; instead, **argumentative writing relies on [logical reasoning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logical_reasoning) to support claims** and relies on **factual evidence to support claims**.

![A diagram mapping argument terminology in logic, visualizing how premises, inferences, and evidence connect to establish a valid conclusion or claim.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Argument_terminology.svg)

A **claim in argumentative writing is a statement asserting a specific viewpoint**. To uphold this claim, the writer must build pillars of evidence. **Evidence in argumentative writing includes verifiable [facts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fact)**, **relevant [statistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistics)**, and **expert opinions**. Yet, [data](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data) cannot stand alone. **Reasoning in argumentative writing explains how evidence proves a claim**. The hidden bedrock beneath this connection is the warrant; a **[warrant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toulmin_method) is the underlying logical assumption connecting evidence to a claim**.

Because the real world is multifaceted, **argumentative writing must acknowledge alternate or opposing claims**. A rigorous writer introduces a **[counterclaim](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Counterargument) in argumentative writing**, which **presents an opposing viewpoint to the main claim**, only to dismantle it with a **[rebuttal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rebuttal)**, a **response refuting a presented counterclaim**. 

As a result of this structure, **the tone of argumentative writing is generally [objective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Objectivity_%28philosophy%29)** and **generally [formal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formality)**. It deliberately **prioritizes logical appeals over [emotional appeals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Appeal_to_emotion)**. It **uses the rhetorical appeal of [logos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logos) to engage the audience's sense of reason**, while simultaneously relying on the **rhetorical appeal of [ethos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethos) to establish authorial [credibility](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Credibility)**. Writers must be vigilant, as **[logical fallacies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logical_fallacy) undermine the [validity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Validity_%28logic%29) of a claim in argumentative writing**. Finally, **a well-structured argumentative essay concludes with a statement naturally following from the presented argument**.

![An early 20th-century poster demonstrating a false dilemma, a type of logical fallacy that forces an artificial choice between two restricted options, thereby undermining an argument's validity.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Young_America's_dilemma_-_Dalrymple._LCCN2010651418.jpg)

> **Real-World Application:** **An [editorial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Editorial) is a common example of argumentative writing**, as is **an [op-ed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Op-ed) piece**. Inside your classroom, **a [literary analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Literary_criticism) essay is an argumentative text presenting a claim about a piece of literature**. To prove its [thesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thesis_statement), **a literary analysis essay uses [direct quotations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quotation) from the [primary text](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primary_source) as evidence**.

Compare this to its less objective counterpart. **[Persuasive writing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persuasion) relies heavily on emotional appeals to convince an audience**. It **prioritizes the author's personal credibility to convince an audience** and, unlike argumentation, **persuasive writing often ignores opposing viewpoints entirely**.

## The Mechanics of Clarity: Informative and Explanatory Writing

Let’s turn our attention to exposition. **[Exposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expository_writing) is a traditional mode of discourse intended to inform or define a subject**. At a macro level, **the Common Core State Standards group informative and explanatory writing into a single category**, yet they possess distinct micro-functions. 

**Informative writing aims to convey complex ideas clearly** and to **convey factual information accurately**. Meanwhile, **explanatory writing aims to clarify the mechanics of a concept** or **clarify the steps of a process**. Critically, **informative writing does not attempt to persuade the reader to adopt a specific viewpoint**. 

The anchor of this text is the thesis. **A thesis statement in informative writing introduces the central idea of the text**. Beneath this umbrella, [paragraphs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paragraph) follow a strict anatomy: **a [topic sentence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topic_sentence) introduces the main idea of a specific paragraph within a larger text**, **supporting details provide specific evidence to back up a topic sentence**, and **a [concluding sentence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conclusion) summarizes the main point of a paragraph**.

**The tone of informative writing is typically objective** and **[unbiased](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutral_point_of_view)**. It **requires the selection of concrete details to support the topic**. Furthermore, **[transition words](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transition_%28linguistics%29) are used in informative writing to clarify relationships among concepts**.

Writers select organizational structures based on the relationship between variables. **Informative writing frequently utilizes [cause-and-effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Causality) organizational structures**, **compare-and-contrast organizational structures**, **problem-and-solution organizational structures**, and **[chronological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chronology) organizational structures**.

Visual architecture is also paramount. **Informative texts frequently incorporate formatting elements like headings to organize information**, utilize **text features like [bold print](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emphasis_%28typography%29) to emphasize key terms**, provide **[glossaries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary) to define domain-specific vocabulary**, and include **[indexes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Index_%28publishing%29) to help readers locate specific information**.

**A [scientific report](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Academic_writing) is a common example of informative writing**. **A [news article](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/News_article) is an informative text reporting on [current events](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/News)**, and **an [encyclopedia entry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Encyclopedia) is an informative text providing a factual summary of a topic**.

### Subcategories of Informative Text
A distinct subcategory exists for actionable instruction: **[procedural writing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Technical_writing) is a subcategory of informative writing explaining how to complete a task**. Because of its nature, **procedural writing typically uses sequential organization** and **often uses the [imperative mood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imperative_mood) to give direct commands**. Think of your daily life: **[instruction manuals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/User_guide) are common examples of procedural writing**, and **[recipes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recipe) are common examples of procedural writing**.

Likewise, **[journalistic writing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Journalism) aims to report news objectively**, explicitly answering **the questions [who, what, when, where, why, and how](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Five_Ws)**. To do this efficiently, **journalistic writing typically uses the [inverted pyramid structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverted_pyramid_%28journalism%29)**, which **places the most important information at the very beginning of an article**.

![The inverted pyramid structure, widely used in journalistic writing, prioritizes the fundamental "Five Ws" at the very beginning to quickly deliver the most critical information to the reader.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Inverted_pyramid_2.svg)

## The Geometry of Experience: Narrative Writing

Narrative writing transports us. Whether exploring [history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History) or [fantasy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fantasy), **narrative writing aims to develop real experiences** or **develop imagined experiences**. To function, **narrative writing relies on well-structured sequences of events**. This skeleton is the plot. **A [plot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plot_%28narrative%29) is the sequence of events making up a narrative**.

Within the plot, **a [conflict](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conflict_%28narrative%29) is a struggle between opposing forces driving the plot of a narrative**. This struggle escalates in predictable stages: **an [inciting incident](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inciting_incident) is a narrative plot element introducing the central conflict**. The [rising action](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dramatic_structure%23Rising_action) pushes toward the pinnacle: **the [climax](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climax_%28narrative%29) is the narrative plot element representing the point of highest tension**, finally yielding to the end, where **the [resolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resolution_%28narrative%29) is the narrative plot element where the main conflict is resolved**.

![Freytag's Pyramid models the structural sequence of events in a narrative plot, tracking the escalation of conflict from the inciting incident up to the climax, and down through the resolution.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Freytag's_Pyramid_with_English_text.svg)

Who populates these stories? **A [protagonist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protagonist) is the primary character driving the action in a narrative**, while **an [antagonist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antagonist) is the character or force opposing the protagonist in a narrative**. Through action and words, we see them change. **[Characterization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Characterization) is the process by which an author reveals the personality of characters in a narrative**. To this end, **narrative writing frequently utilizes [dialogue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dialogue) to advance the plot** and **utilizes dialogue to reveal character traits**. Additionally, **narrative writing uses reflection to provide insight into a character's internal thoughts**.

Where and why does it happen? **The [setting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Setting_%28narrative%29) of a narrative establishes the time period of the story's events** and **the physical location of the story's events**. Woven through all of this is the meaning: **the [theme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theme_%28narrative%29) of a narrative is the underlying message of the story**.

We must also consider the lens through which the story is told. **[Point of view](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_of_view_%28literature%29) is the perspective from which a narrative is told.**
*   **[First-person point of view](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First-person_narrative) uses the [pronoun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pronoun) 'I' to narrate a story from a specific character's perspective.**
*   **[Second-person point of view](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second-person_narrative) addresses the reader directly using the pronoun 'you'**. (Note that outside of narrative fiction, **second-person point of view is frequently used in instructional explanatory writing**).
*   **[Third-person limited point of view](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Third-person_narrative) reveals the internal thoughts of only one character.**
*   **[Third-person omniscient point of view](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Third-person_narrative) reveals the internal thoughts of multiple characters.**

Authors manipulate time to control the reader's heartbeat. **Narrative [pacing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pacing_%28narrative%29) controls the speed at which a story unfolds for the reader**. Like shifting gears, **authors use short sentences to increase pacing during high-action narrative scenes** and **use long sentences to slow down pacing in narrative writing**. They bend time further: **[foreshadowing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foreshadowing) is a narrative technique providing hints about future events in the story**, and **[flashbacks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flashback_%28narrative%29) are narrative techniques interrupting chronological order to describe past events**. To fully immerse the reader, **narrative writing relies heavily on sensory details to create vivid [imagery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imagery)**.

### Classifications of Narrative Writing
In the realm of reality: **A [personal narrative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Personal_narrative) is a true story about a real-life experience written from the author's perspective.** If exploring a whole life, **a [biography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biography) is a narrative account of a person's life written by someone else**, while **an [autobiography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autobiography) is a narrative account of a person's life written by the subject of the account**. A narrower lens yields a memoir: **a [memoir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Memoir) is a narrative account focusing on a specific thematic period in the author's life**.

In the realm of imagination: **A [short story](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Short_story) is a common example of fictional narrative writing**, and **a [novel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Novel) is a long-form example of fictional narrative writing**. These branch into specific [genres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genre):
*   **[Realistic fiction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Literary_realism) is a narrative genre featuring events that could happen in real life**.
*   **[Historical fiction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Historical_fiction) is a narrative genre placing fictional characters in factual historical settings**.
*   **[Science fiction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Science_fiction) is a narrative genre exploring imagined futuristic concepts**.
*   **[Fantasy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fantasy) is a narrative genre featuring magical or supernatural elements**.
*   **A [fable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fable) is a brief narrative conveying a moral lesson**, and **fables frequently feature [anthropomorphized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anthropomorphism) animal characters**.
*   **A [myth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myth) is a traditional narrative explaining natural phenomena**, and **myths frequently feature [gods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deity) or superhuman characters**.

## The Art of Observation: Descriptive Writing

To make narratives and essays breathe, writers rely on description. **[Descriptive writing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Description) aims to paint a vivid mental picture of a specific subject**. Because of its immersive power, **descriptive writing is often incorporated into narrative writing to establish a vivid setting**.

How does it work? **Descriptive writing relies heavily on [figurative language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Figurative_language) to create imagery**. It **frequently incorporates [similes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simile) to compare dissimilar subjects** and **frequently incorporates [metaphors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metaphor) to draw direct comparisons between subjects**. At the mechanical level of speech, **descriptive writing relies on precise [nouns](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noun) to create clear images** and **relies on vivid [verbs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Verb) to create clear images**.

To organize this imagery seamlessly, **spatial organization is a common structure used in descriptive writing**. **Spatial organization arranges descriptive information according to physical location** (e.g., describing a room from ceiling to floor). A perfect real-world application is the travel journal: **a [travelogue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Travel_literature) frequently blends descriptive and narrative writing elements**.

## Beyond Prose: Poetry, Drama, and Higher-Order Texts

Let’s step outside standard paragraph constraints. 

**[Poetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poetry) is a type of writing utilizing rhythmic qualities of language to evoke meaning**. Instead of paragraphs, **poetry organizes text into [stanzas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stanza)**. Instead of standard [punctuation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Punctuation) governing the breath, **poetry utilizes [line breaks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_break_%28poetry%29) to control [rhythm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metre_%28poetry%29)**.

**[Drama](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drama) is a type of writing intended for performance by [actors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Actor)**. Bereft of internal reflection paragraphs, **drama relies heavily on dialogue to communicate the plot to an audience**. To direct the action, **drama utilizes [stage directions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stage_direction) to provide instructions on character movement** and **provide instructions on [stage lighting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stage_lighting)**.

As students advance, they interact with complex academic modes:
*   **[Analytical writing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analysis) breaks down a complex topic into smaller parts for detailed examination**. For ELA students, **analytical writing frequently evaluates the effectiveness of an author's rhetorical choices**.
*   **Synthesis in writing involves combining information from multiple distinct sources to form a new conclusion**. In [higher education](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Higher_education), **a [literature review](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Literature_review) is a common example of writing that synthesizes multiple sources**.
*   **[Reflective writing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflective_writing) explores the author's personal insights regarding a specific experience**. **A learning journal is a common example of reflective writing**.

![Graham Gibbs's model of reflection visualizes the cyclical process of reflective writing, breaking down an experience from concrete description through active evaluation and future action planning.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Steph_Gib_Model.jpg)

## Equipping the ELA Classroom: Pedagogy in Practice

To teach these varied structures effectively, we must make the invisible visible. Before drafting begins, students need [scaffolding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Instructional_scaffolding). **[Graphic organizers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graphic_organizer) help students visually structure ideas before drafting an essay**. The organizer must match the text's purpose:
*   **A [Venn diagram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venn_diagram) is a graphic organizer specifically suited for planning compare-and-contrast writing**.
*   **A T-chart is a graphic organizer frequently used to outline claims and counterclaims for argumentative writing**.
*   **Story maps are graphic organizers used to plan the setting, characters, and plot sequence of a narrative**.

![A basic Venn diagram functions as a visual graphic organizer, allowing students to systematically map out similarities and differences prior to drafting a compare-and-contrast essay.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Venn_diagram_of_legs_and_flying.svg)

Students also need to see success in action. **Mentor texts are published texts used to illustrate specific genre characteristics for students**. By [reverse-engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reverse_engineering) published works, students uncover the architecture of strong writing. 

Finally, expectations must be transparent. **A writing [rubric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rubric_%28academic%29) is an evaluation tool explicitly defining the criteria for [grading](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grading_in_education) a specific type of writing assignment**, ensuring your students understand exactly what standard of architecture they are striving to build.
