Slide that coaster over here and listen to me, because you are about to walk into a middle school ELA classroom, which is essentially a maximum-security prison run by hormonal inmates fueled entirely by Takis and social media drama. To survive, you can't just know how to read; you need to understand the architectural blueprints of human communication. Writing isn't just words on a page! The purpose of a piece of writing is the author's primary reason for creating the text, the literal engine driving the car. And in this universe, we have three main engines: persuading the reader is one of the primary purposes for writing, informing the reader is one of the primary purposes for writing, and entertaining the reader is one of the primary purposes for writing. To accomplish these missions, we use the four traditional modes of discourse, which are the fundamental operating systems of language: narration, description, exposition, and argumentation. 

But before your middle schoolers write a single word, they have to scan the room. They have to know who they are talking to. The audience is the specific group of readers for whom a text is intended. You don't speak to the principal the same way you speak to your best friend when they steal your crush! An author must adapt the vocabulary of a piece of writing to suit the intended audience, swapping out slang for SAT words, and similarly, an author must adapt the tone of a piece of writing to suit the intended audience. Tone is critical. Tone is the author's attitude toward the subject matter of a text—whether they think the topic is a tragedy or a joke—and at the exact same time, tone is the author's attitude toward the audience of a text. Do they respect the reader, or are they talking down to them? Now, don't mix that up with mood. If tone is the attitude being broadcast, mood is the emotional atmosphere a piece of writing creates for the reader. Tone is the DJ's attitude; mood is the vibe on the dance floor. 

Let's dive into the absolute bloodbath that is argumentation versus persuasion. Middle schoolers argue constantly, but they are terrible at it. As an ELA teacher, you have to know that the Common Core State Standards for English Language Arts differentiate between argumentative writing and persuasive writing. They are not the same beast! Argumentative writing is a cold, calculated courtroom assassination. Argumentative writing aims to convince the reader to accept a specific claim, or sometimes, argumentative writing aims to convince the reader to take a specific action. It doesn't cry. It doesn't beg. Argumentative writing relies on logical reasoning to support claims and relies on factual evidence to support claims. 

In this courtroom, a claim in argumentative writing is a statement asserting a specific viewpoint. It's your thesis on trial. To win, you need hard proof. Evidence in argumentative writing includes verifiable facts, relevant statistics, and expert opinions. But dumping numbers on a jury isn't enough. Reasoning in argumentative writing explains how evidence proves a claim. And beneath all of this is the trapdoor: a warrant is the underlying logical assumption connecting evidence to a claim. If the jury doesn't believe the warrant, your case falls into the basement! Because you are dealing with a smart jury, argumentative writing must acknowledge alternate or opposing claims. You have to anticipate the enemy's move! A counterclaim in argumentative writing presents an opposing viewpoint to the main claim, pulling the rug out from the opposition before they even speak, which you then destroy with a rebuttal, because a rebuttal in argumentative writing is a response refuting a presented counterclaim. 

Because of this brutal, calculated structure, the tone of argumentative writing is generally objective and the tone of argumentative writing is generally formal. It leaves the drama at the door; argumentative writing prioritizes logical appeals over emotional appeals. It uses the rhetorical appeal of logos to engage the audience's sense of reason, making the math add up perfectly in the reader's head, while concurrently, argumentative writing uses the rhetorical appeal of ethos to establish authorial credibility, proving you are the expert in the room. You must warn your students to avoid mental booby traps, because logical fallacies undermine the validity of a claim in argumentative writing. When the trial is over, a well-structured argumentative essay concludes with a statement naturally following from the presented argument, dropping the mic logically. In the wild, an editorial is a common example of argumentative writing, and an op-ed piece is a common example of argumentative writing. In your classroom, though, a literary analysis essay is an argumentative text presenting a claim about a piece of literature. And just like a lawyer presenting a bloody glove, a literary analysis essay uses direct quotations from the primary text as evidence. 

Now look at persuasion. Persuasive writing is a reality TV contestant in the diary room crying to the camera. Persuasive writing relies heavily on emotional appeals to convince an audience. It prioritizes the author's personal credibility to convince an audience, basically saying "Trust me, I'm a good person!" And unlike the rigorous courtroom, persuasive writing often ignores opposing viewpoints entirely. It lives in its own echo chamber. 

Let's cool down and look at the bomb squad technicians of the writing world: informative and explanatory texts. Exposition is a traditional mode of discourse intended to inform or define a subject. Now, the Common Core State Standards group informative and explanatory writing into a single category, but under the hood, they have slightly different jobs. Informative writing aims to convey complex ideas clearly and aims to convey factual information accurately. Explanatory writing, on the other hand, aims to clarify the mechanics of a concept or aims to clarify the steps of a process. The golden rule here? Informative writing does not attempt to persuade the reader to adopt a specific viewpoint. It just cuts the blue wire to defuse the bomb. 

The structure here is rigid. A thesis statement in informative writing introduces the central idea of the text. Then, we zoom in on the paragraph level, which operates like a Russian nesting doll. A topic sentence introduces the main idea of a specific paragraph within a larger text. Beneath that, supporting details provide specific evidence to back up a topic sentence. Finally, a concluding sentence summarizes the main point of a paragraph, sealing the lid tight. Because it's just delivering the news, the tone of informative writing is typically objective and the tone of informative writing is typically unbiased. Informative writing requires the selection of concrete details to support the topic. To keep the reader from getting lost in the data, transition words are used in informative writing to clarify relationships among concepts. 

How you arrange this data depends on the mission. Informative writing frequently utilizes cause-and-effect organizational structures when explaining why things happen, compare-and-contrast organizational structures when looking at two different variables, problem-and-solution organizational structures to fix something broken, and chronological organizational structures when time is the main factor. To make it scannable, informative texts frequently incorporate formatting elements like headings to organize information, text features like bold print to emphasize key terms, glossaries to define domain-specific vocabulary at the back of the book, and indexes to help readers locate specific information instantly. A scientific report is a common example of informative writing. A news article is an informative text reporting on current events, and an encyclopedia entry is an informative text providing a factual summary of a topic. 

Within this world, there is a hyper-specific squad. Procedural writing is a subcategory of informative writing explaining how to complete a task. Because you can't build an IKEA dresser backward, procedural writing typically uses sequential organization. And because it needs you to take action right now, procedural writing often uses the imperative mood to give direct commands. Instruction manuals are common examples of procedural writing, and recipes are common examples of procedural writing. Then there are the news hounds. Journalistic writing aims to report news objectively. They don't have time to waste, so journalistic writing explicitly answers the questions who, what, when, where, why, and how. To hit the reader with the heavy stuff first, journalistic writing typically uses the inverted pyramid structure, which means the inverted pyramid structure places the most important information at the very beginning of an article.

Now, buy me another drink, because we are moving on to the rollercoaster. Narrative writing! Narrative writing aims to develop real experiences or aims to develop imagined experiences. It is pure escapism, but to work, narrative writing relies on well-structured sequences of events. This track the rollercoaster rides on? That's the plot. A plot is the sequence of events making up a narrative. But a rollercoaster without a steep drop is just a train. A conflict is a struggle between opposing forces driving the plot of a narrative. This struggle kicks off when an inciting incident is a narrative plot element introducing the central conflict. The tension ratchets up the hill until you scream—the climax is the narrative plot element representing the point of highest tension. Finally, you coast back to the station where the resolution is the narrative plot element where the main conflict is resolved. 

Who is screaming on this ride? A protagonist is the primary character driving the action in a narrative, while an antagonist is the character or force opposing the protagonist in a narrative. We only care about them because characterization is the process by which an author reveals the personality of characters in a narrative. Narrative writing frequently utilizes dialogue to advance the plot—having characters literally argue their way into the next scene—and narrative writing frequently utilizes dialogue to reveal character traits. When they aren't talking, narrative writing uses reflection to provide insight into a character's internal thoughts. All of this happens somewhere; the setting of a narrative establishes the time period of the story's events and the setting of a narrative establishes the physical location of the story's events. Woven invisibly through all of this is the soul of the story: the theme of a narrative is the underlying message of the story.

You also have to decide whose eyes we are looking through. Point of view is the perspective from which a narrative is told. First-person point of view uses the pronoun 'I' to narrate a story from a specific character's perspective—you are inside their head. Third-person limited point of view reveals the internal thoughts of only one character, hovering right over their shoulder. Third-person omniscient point of view reveals the internal thoughts of multiple characters, playing God. Then there is the weird one: second-person point of view addresses the reader directly using the pronoun 'you'. While rare in fiction, second-person point of view is frequently used in instructional explanatory writing.

To keep the reader hooked, authors manipulate time. Narrative pacing controls the speed at which a story unfolds for the reader. In a car chase, authors use short sentences to increase pacing during high-action narrative scenes. Boom. Crash. Run. Conversely, authors use long sentences to slow down pacing in narrative writing, letting the tension breathe and stretch out lazily over the page. They also cheat time entirely! Foreshadowing is a narrative technique providing hints about future events in the story, while flashbacks are narrative techniques interrupting chronological order to describe past events. To make the world feel real, narrative writing relies heavily on sensory details to create vivid imagery. 

Narratives come in all flavors. In the real world, a personal narrative is a true story about a real-life experience written from the author's perspective. If you write about someone else entirely, a biography is a narrative account of a person's life written by someone else, but if you write your entire own life story, an autobiography is a narrative account of a person's life written by the subject of the account. If you just focus on your angsty middle school years, a memoir is a narrative account focusing on a specific thematic period in the author's life. In the fictional universe, a short story is a common example of fictional narrative writing, and a novel is a long-form example of fictional narrative writing. We break these into genres! Realistic fiction is a narrative genre featuring events that could happen in real life. Historical fiction is a narrative genre placing fictional characters in factual historical settings. If you want lasers, science fiction is a narrative genre exploring imagined futuristic concepts. If you want dragons, fantasy is a narrative genre featuring magical or supernatural elements. If you want to teach a kid not to lie using a talking fox, a fable is a brief narrative conveying a moral lesson, and fables frequently feature anthropomorphized animal characters. And to explain why the sun rises, a myth is a traditional narrative explaining natural phenomena, and myths frequently feature gods or superhuman characters.

But a story without scenery is just a blank stage. That’s where the interior designers come in. Descriptive writing aims to paint a vivid mental picture of a specific subject. Because it sets the stage so well, descriptive writing is often incorporated into narrative writing to establish a vivid setting. How do they do it? Descriptive writing relies heavily on figurative language to create imagery. Descriptive writing frequently incorporates similes to compare dissimilar subjects using 'like' or 'as', and frequently incorporates metaphors to draw direct comparisons between subjects, saying something literally IS something else. At the foundational level, descriptive writing relies on precise nouns to create clear images, and descriptive writing relies on vivid verbs to create clear images. You don't just "walk" to a "building"; you "trudge" to a "fortress." To keep from making the reader dizzy, spatial organization is a common structure used in descriptive writing. Think of a movie camera panning down a hallway—spatial organization arranges descriptive information according to physical location. Out in the real world, a travelogue frequently blends descriptive and narrative writing elements to make you feel like you are hiking the Alps.

Now, let's step outside the normal paragraph constraints. Meet the rule breakers. Poetry is a type of writing utilizing rhythmic qualities of language to evoke meaning. It doesn't care about your margins. Poetry organizes text into stanzas, and instead of letting grammar tell you when to breathe, poetry utilizes line breaks to control rhythm. Drama is a type of writing intended for performance by actors. You can't read their minds on stage, so drama relies heavily on dialogue to communicate the plot to an audience. And since the actors need to know not to bump into the set, drama utilizes stage directions to provide instructions on character movement, as well as utilizes stage directions to provide instructions on stage lighting. 

As your students get older, you'll drag them into the academic big leagues. Analytical writing breaks down a complex topic into smaller parts for detailed examination. In ELA, analytical writing frequently evaluates the effectiveness of an author's rhetorical choices. Did their metaphor work or flop? Synthesis in writing involves combining information from multiple distinct sources to form a new conclusion. You take three different books and build a brand new house out of their bricks, which is why a literature review is a common example of writing that synthesizes multiple sources. And when they need to look inward, reflective writing explores the author's personal insights regarding a specific experience. A learning journal is a common example of reflective writing. 

So how do you actually teach this to a room full of distracted pre-teens? You give them tools. Graphic organizers help students visually structure ideas before drafting an essay. If they are comparing two things, a Venn diagram is a graphic organizer specifically suited for planning compare-and-contrast writing. If they are prepping for that courtroom argument, a T-chart is a graphic organizer frequently used to outline claims and counterclaims for argumentative writing. If they are writing a wild fantasy tale, story maps are graphic organizers used to plan the setting, characters, and plot sequence of a narrative. 

You also have to show them what winning looks like. Mentor texts are published texts used to illustrate specific genre characteristics for students. It's like showing a chef a Michelin-star meal before they start cooking. Finally, you have to show them the exact target they need to hit. A writing rubric is an evaluation tool explicitly defining the criteria for grading a specific type of writing assignment. You hand them the rubric, you hand them the mentor text, you hand them the graphic organizer, and you pray to the ELA gods that they actually remember to use a period. Welcome to middle school! Drink your water, you're going to need it.
