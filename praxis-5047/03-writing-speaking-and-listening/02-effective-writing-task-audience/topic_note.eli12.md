Imagine trying to buy a new video game that costs \$60, and you earn \$15 a week for your allowance. To find out how long it will take to save up, you use math:
1. Take the total cost of the game (\$60).
2. Divide that amount by your weekly allowance (\$15).
3. See that 60 / 15 = 4.
4. The answer is 4 weeks.

Just like solving that math problem requires knowing the right numbers and following the correct steps, writing works the exact same way. When you sit down to write, you aren't just putting random words on a page. You have to figure out exactly what the rules are, why you are writing, and who will be reading it. 

## The Architecture of Writing: Task, Purpose, and Audience

To figure out if a piece of writing is actually good, we have to look closely at three main things: the task, the purpose, and the audience. In fact, for teachers, **the Praxis 5047 exam tests a candidate's ability to evaluate middle school student writing based on task, purpose, and audience.** Once you master these three ideas, you'll have the cheat codes for great writing.

### Decoding the Task
Think of the "task" like the game mode you select before a match. **The writing task defines the specific format required by a writing prompt**, which means it tells you if you need to write an essay, a poem, or a letter. Also, **the writing task defines the specific parameters required by an assignment**, like whether it needs to be 500 words long or turned in by Friday.

Before you start writing, you have to read the instructions carefully. **Analyzing a writing prompt helps an author correctly identify the required mode of writing.** If your teacher says "write a story," you know right away you shouldn't be writing a science report! **The effectiveness of a piece of writing depends heavily on its strict adherence to the specific constraints of the assigned task.** If the rules say to write about space, and you write a beautiful essay about pizza, you still lose points because you didn't follow the task.

### Defining the Purpose
If the task is *what* you are doing, the purpose is *why* you are doing it. **The writing purpose represents the author's primary goal in creating a text.** 

Every piece of writing has a job to do. **The Common Core State Standards for English Language Arts categorize writing into three main text types: argument, informative or explanatory, and narrative.** We'll look closer at those in a minute, but here are the three main goals you might have:
*   **One primary purpose for writing is to persuade the audience.** (Like convincing your parents to let you stay up late!)
*   **One primary purpose for writing is to inform the audience.** (Like sharing cool facts about sharks.)
*   **One primary purpose for writing is to entertain the audience.** (Like telling a hilarious joke.)

Sometimes, you can do more than one at the same time. Take a book review, for example. **A book review utilizes an informative purpose to summarize the plot of a literary text**, but at the exact same time, **a book review utilizes an evaluative purpose to judge the quality of a literary text** (like giving it 5 stars!).

### Understanding the Audience
The audience is simply who you are writing for or talking to. **The intended audience consists of the specific readers the author wants to reach.** Or, if you are giving a presentation out loud, **the intended audience consists of the specific listeners the author wants to reach.**

If you are explaining a brand new video game to your grandpa, you have to remember he probably doesn't know the controls. **An author must consider the prior background knowledge of the intended audience when selecting vocabulary.** You also wouldn't use giant, complicated dictionary words with a kindergartener. **An author must adjust vocabulary complexity based on the reading level of the intended audience**, and similarly, **an author must adjust sentence structure based on the reading level of the intended audience.** You change how you write depending on who is reading!

## The Three Pillars of Purpose

Let's look at the three main types of writing and see how your goal changes the way you write.

### 1. Argumentative and Persuasive Writing
**Argumentative writing aims to convince an audience to accept a specific viewpoint** (like "dogs are the best pets") or **argumentative writing aims to convince an audience to take a specific action** (like "adopt a shelter dog today!").

People don't just believe things automatically. Because of that, **argumentative writing relies heavily on logical reasoning to achieve its purpose**, and **argumentative writing relies heavily on relevant evidence to achieve its purpose.** To see if an argument is actually strong, you have to check the proof. **Evaluating writing effectiveness requires analyzing the alignment between the chosen evidence and the author's core claim.** If you say dogs are the best pets, but your evidence is about how cute cats are, your whole argument falls apart!

*   **Handling Doubts:** People might disagree with you. **Persuasive writing often requires the inclusion of counterarguments to effectively address skeptical audiences.** A counterargument is when you say, "I know you think dogs are messy, BUT..." and then explain why they are still great.
*   **The Final Push:** At the very end of your argument, you want people to do something. **A call to action is a concluding component of a persuasive text aimed at motivating an audience to change behavior.**
*   *Real-World Example:* **A persuasive letter is the most appropriate type of writing for convincing a school board to change a policy** (like making recess longer!).

### 2. Informative and Explanatory Writing
**Informational writing aims to convey verifiable facts to an audience**, while **explanatory writing aims to explain complex processes to an audience** (like how a volcano erupts).

Because you are just sharing the truth, **an objective tone is required in informational writing to present facts without personal bias.** That means you leave your own feelings and opinions completely out of it.

*   **Organizing the Facts:** To keep your facts neat and easy to read, **a hierarchical structure using headings is highly effective for organizing complex informational texts.** (Just like the bold headings you see in this very study guide!) 
*   **Connecting the Dots:** **Clear transitional phrases improve the effectiveness of informational writing by smoothly guiding the reader through complex ideas.** Words like "first," "however," and "as a result" help the reader follow along.
*   *Real-World Example:* **An expository essay is the most appropriate type of writing for explaining the causes of a historical event.**

### 3. Narrative Writing
**Narrative writing aims to share a real experience with an audience** (like a true story about your summer vacation) or **narrative writing aims to share an imagined experience with an audience** (like a made-up story about dragons).

To make the story fun to read, **an author writing to entertain relies heavily on sensory details** (describing what things look, sound, smell, and feel like) and **an author writing to entertain relies heavily on figurative language** (like saying "he ran as fast as a cheetah").

*   **Feelings and Time:** Instead of being neutral, **a subjective tone is often used in narrative writing to convey personal emotions.** You also get to play with time! **A writer adjusts pacing in a narrative to suit the purpose of building suspense for the audience** (like describing a creaky door opening *very, very slowly*).

## Calibrating the Instrument: Tone, Register, and Diction

Once you know your task, purpose, and audience, you have to pick the right words. 

> **Diction** refers to your specific word choices. 
> **Tone** is the attitude in your writing.
> **Register** is how formal or casual you are acting.

### The Mechanics of Diction
Words are tools. **Diction refers to the specific word choices an author makes to convey a precise meaning**, as well as **the specific word choices an author makes to establish a specific tone.**

Think about special gamer words like "HP," "spawn point," or "lag." **Technical jargon is highly effective when writing for an audience of experts in a specific field** because they know exactly what those words mean. But if you use those words with someone who has never played a video game, they'll be totally lost. **Technical jargon severely hinders comprehension when writing for a novice audience.** 

### Controlling Tone
**Tone represents the author's underlying attitude toward the subject matter**, and just as importantly, **tone represents the author's underlying attitude toward the audience.** 

If you write a super happy, laughing essay about a really sad historical event, you'll fail the assignment. **Evaluating writing effectiveness requires assessing the alignment between the tone and the expectations of the intended audience.** Your attitude has to match what the reader expects.

### Mastering Register
**Register refers to the level of linguistic formality used in a particular social context.** You talk differently to your best friend on the weekend than you do to the school principal during an assembly!

| Formality Level | What it Looks Like | When to Use It |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Formal Tone / Register** | **A formal tone utilizes standard grammar.** To stay professional, **a formal tone completely avoids the use of slang** (like "sus" or "cap") and **a formal tone completely avoids the use of conversational colloquialisms** (like "what's up man"). | **A student must employ a formal register when writing a formal request to a school administrator.** Similarly, **a writer must choose a professional tone when drafting a cover letter for a job application.** |
| **Informal Tone / Register** | **An informal tone frequently includes colloquialisms** and **an informal tone frequently includes grammatical contractions** (like using "can't" instead of "cannot"). | Perfect for texting friends, writing in a personal diary, or making characters talk in a story. |

### The Power of Point of View
The point of view you choose changes how close the reader feels to the writing.

*   **First-Person ("I", "We"):** **First-person point of view is highly effective in personal narratives to build intimacy with the audience.** It makes the reader feel like they are right there with you! However, because it's all about your personal feelings, **first-person point of view is generally avoided in formal academic research papers to maintain an objective tone.**
*   **Second-Person ("You"):** **Second-person point of view is highly effective in instructional writing to directly address the reader.** (Like a recipe telling *you* to put the cheese on the pizza.)
*   **Third-Person ("He", "She", "They", "It"):** This is the best choice when you just want to explain facts and keep a professional distance.

Writing isn't just magic. By analyzing your Task, defining your Purpose, and picking the right words for your Audience, you can build an amazing piece of writing every single time!
