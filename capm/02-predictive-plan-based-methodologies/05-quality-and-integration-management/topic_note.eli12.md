Imagine your school is putting on a massive play. You have a team building sets, a team sewing costumes, a team working the lights, and actors memorizing lines. If the lighting team uses dark blue lights without telling the costume team, the actors wearing dark blue costumes will completely disappear on stage! The skill that stops this kind of disaster is called integration. Once everything is integrated and working together, the play still has to be good. If a costume rips in half during a dance, the audience won't be happy. Making sure things work perfectly is the world of quality. Together, integration and quality act like the brain and the strong bones of any successful project. In this guide, we will explore exactly how both work.

## The Central Nervous System: Project Integration Management

When working on a big goal, it is easy to look at a project as just a bunch of random chores. This is a huge mistake. **Project integration management includes the processes required to combine and coordinate all project management activities.** It is the glue that makes all the different pieces stick together to make one awesome final result.

Because integration means looking at the big picture, one person has to be the boss of it. You can ask a friend to help you make a schedule, or ask a teammate to buy supplies, but **the project manager is solely responsible for project integration management.** This is your job and yours alone; **project integration management cannot be delegated to other project team members.** You are like the director of a movie. You don't hold the camera or act in the scenes, but you alone are responsible for making sure the whole movie makes sense.

### The Lifecycle of Integration

Integration happens through the whole project. You have to do specific steps at each stage of your project's life.

#### 1. Initiation: The Spark
A project isn't real until your school or company officially says it is. **The Develop Project Charter process formally authorizes the existence of a project.** A project charter is like a permission slip; it is a fundamental mandate. By officially naming you as the boss, **the project charter provides the project manager with the authority to apply organizational resources to project activities.** This means you are now allowed to spend money and ask people to do work! 

#### 2. Planning: The Blueprint
With your permission slip signed, you must put all your plans together. **The Develop Project Management Plan process defines and integrates all subsidiary project plans**—like your plans for time, money, and quality. This master document is your ultimate rulebook. Not only does it show the team what to do, because **the project management plan defines how the project is executed,** but it also sets the rules for how you will check on things, because **the project management plan defines how the project is monitored and controlled.**

#### 3. Execution: Leading and Learning
Execution is when you actually start spending money and doing the physical work. **Direct and Manage Project Work is the process of leading the work defined in the project management plan.** 

As your team does the work, they learn a lot of smart things. Integration means you have to capture this brainpower! **Manage Project Knowledge is the process of using existing knowledge to achieve project objectives**, so you don't repeat mistakes you made in the past. But your brain doesn't stop there; as you figure out new puzzles, **Manage Project Knowledge involves creating new knowledge to achieve project objectives.** 

We split this knowledge into two different types:
*   **Explicit Knowledge:** This is easy stuff to write down. **Explicit knowledge can be readily codified using words, pictures, or numbers.** A list of high scores in a video game, a recipe for pizza, or a math textbook are all explicit.
*   **Tacit Knowledge:** This is the "gut feeling" stuff that is super hard to share. **Tacit knowledge is personal knowledge that is difficult to express.** You can't easily write down exactly how it feels to balance perfectly on a skateboard. Because of this, **tacit knowledge includes personal beliefs, experience, and insights.** To share tacit knowledge, you usually have to let people practice together and talk things out.

#### 4. Monitoring and Controlling: Guarding the Baseline
As work happens, things will definitely not go exactly to plan. **Monitor and Control Project Work is the process of tracking project progress against the performance objectives.** 

When surprises happen, people will ask you to change the plan. Change isn't bad, but wild, out-of-control change ruins projects. So, **Perform Integrated Change Control is the process of reviewing all project change requests.** This means if someone wants to add a new scene to the school play, you stop and check how it affects your budget and your schedule first. Because of this, **Perform Integrated Change Control includes managing approved changes to project deliverables.** 

To be totally fair about what changes get approved, groups often use a special team of judges. **The Change Control Board is a formally chartered group responsible for reviewing and evaluating project changes.** This board is like a project's supreme court; **the Change Control Board holds the authority to approve, defer, or reject change requests.** Once they make a ruling, you have to update your paperwork, because **approved change requests frequently require updates to the project management plan.**

#### 5. Closing: The Formal Conclusion
Projects are temporary; they have to finish eventually. **Close Project or Phase is the process of finalizing all activities to formally complete a project.** 

Closing is more than just going home. It means securely handing the prize over. **The Close Project or Phase process finalizes the transfer of the final product to the customer.** Also, the smart things you learned need to be saved so next year's team can read them. That is why **lessons learned documentation must be formally archived during the Close Project or Phase process.**

## The Project's DNA: Quality Management

Sometimes people confuse the word "fancy" with the word "quality." In project management, this is a huge trap! **Project quality management ensures the project requirements are met and validated.** It just means you kept the promise you made about what the thing would do.

### The Classic Trap: Quality vs. Grade

To be great at quality, we have to know the difference between quality and grade. 

> **Quality is the degree to which a set of inherent characteristics fulfills requirements.** (Does it do exactly what it promised?)
> **Grade is a category assigned to deliverables having the same functional use but different technical characteristics.** (Is it a basic version or a fancy version?)

Think about a basic \$15 backpack and a \$500 designer leather backpack. They have the same use (carrying things), but they are in different categories. The basic backpack is a low-grade item. The designer backpack is a high-grade item.

Now, imagine the \$15 basic backpack holds your books perfectly all year without breaking. It fulfills its requirements. Therefore, it is a high-quality product! 
But imagine the \$500 designer backpack has a cheap zipper that gets stuck on the first day. It fails its requirements. It is a low-quality product!

This tells us a golden rule of project management:
*   **Low grade is not inherently a problem if the deliverable meets the assigned quality requirements.** (It's okay to build something simple and cheap, as long as it works!)
*   **Low quality is inherently a problem in project management.** (It is never okay to build something broken!)

### Planning Quality and the Cost of Quality

When planning, we have to figure out the rules for what "working right" looks like. **The Plan Quality Management process identifies specific quality requirements for the project.** Once you set those targets, **the quality management plan specifies how the project team will implement organizational quality policies.**

A big rule in quality is that stopping a mistake early is way cheaper than fixing it later. 
*   **Prevention keeps errors out of a project process.** (Like training your team or buying good glue).
*   **Inspection keeps errors out of the hands of the customer.** (Like testing a toy before putting it in a shiny box).

We talk about money using the Cost of Quality (CoQ). **Cost of Quality includes all costs incurred over the life of a product to prevent nonconformance to requirements.** 

We split this money into two buckets: money spent to get things right, and money lost when things go wrong.

Let's look at a step-by-step math example of how a toy company might calculate their "Cost of Conformance" (the good bucket):
1.  **Step 1:** The company spends \$500 on training workers how to use the machines safely.
2.  **Step 2:** The company spends \$300 on testing the toys to make sure they don't break.
3.  **Step 3:** We add these together (\$500 + \$300 = \$800). The total Cost of Conformance is \$800!

Here is how all the costs break down:

| Cost Category | Sub-Category | What it Includes |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Cost of Conformance** | Prevention Costs | Training, buying good materials. **Cost of Conformance includes prevention costs.** |
| | Appraisal Costs | Testing the product, checking the work. **Cost of Conformance includes appraisal costs.** |
| **Cost of Nonconformance** | Internal Failure Costs | Fixing mistakes or throwing away broken parts *before* the customer sees it. **Cost of Nonconformance includes internal failure costs.** |
| | External Failure Costs | Refunds, angry customers, paying for broken things *after* the customer bought it. **Cost of Nonconformance includes external failure costs.** |

By spending money early on Conformance, you save a ton of money on Nonconformance later!

### Execution vs. Monitoring: Manage Quality vs. Control Quality

You need to know the difference between "managing" quality and "controlling" quality. They sound like the same thing, but they are totally different.

**Manage Quality is the process of translating the quality management plan into executable quality activities.** This is about looking at your *process*. When you manage quality, you aren't staring at a baked pizza; you are fixing the oven so every pizza bakes perfectly. **Manage Quality focuses on improving the project processes.** To check your processes, you do an audit. **Quality audits determine if project activities comply with organizational policies.** 

On the other hand, **Control Quality is the process of monitoring and recording results of executing the quality management activities.** This is about looking at the *final product*. When you control quality, you hold the actual pizza in your hands and check if it is burned. **Control Quality focuses on verifying that project deliverables meet the required standards.** 

### The Quality Toolkit

When mistakes happen, project managers use special tools to play detective and figure out why.

#### 1. The Cause-and-Effect Diagram
When there is a huge problem, you have to find the real reason it happened. **A cause-and-effect diagram breaks down the potential causes of a problem into discrete categories** (like checking if the problem was the tools, the materials, or the people). 
Because this drawing looks exactly like a fish skeleton—with a long spine pointing to the problem at the head, and ribs sticking out for the categories—**a cause-and-effect diagram is also known as a Fishbone diagram.** Also, to respect the smart person who invented it, **a cause-and-effect diagram is also known as an Ishikawa diagram.**

#### 2. The Pareto Chart
If your video game has tons of bugs, how do you know which ones to fix first? You use a Pareto chart. **A Pareto chart is a type of histogram used to identify the most frequent sources of problems.** It uses a cool math trick called the Pareto principle. **The 80/20 rule in a Pareto chart suggests that 80 percent of problems are due to 20 percent of causes.** 

Here is a step-by-step math example of how the 80/20 rule works in real life:
1.  **Step 1:** Imagine your video game crashes 100 times in one day (100 total crashes).
2.  **Step 2:** You look at the game code and find 10 different bugs causing these crashes.
3.  **Step 3:** Calculate 20 percent of the bugs (20/100 * 10 bugs = 2 main bugs). 
4.  **Step 4:** Calculate 80 percent of the crashes (80/100 * 100 crashes = 80 crashes). 
5.  **Step 5:** This means those 2 main bugs are causing 80 out of the 100 crashes! If you fix just those 2 things, almost all your problems go away!

#### 3. Control Charts
A project manager needs to know if a machine is working normally or going crazy. **Control charts determine whether a process is stable or has predictable performance.** 

A control chart is a graph with a line down the middle (the average). If a data point jumps way too high or way too low off the graph, your machine is out of control. 

But sometimes, the graph can warn you *before* the machine breaks! Normally, dots on the graph should bounce up and down randomly across the middle line. If they stop bouncing randomly, something is secretly wrong. **The Rule of Seven states that seven consecutive data points on one side of the mean in a control chart indicate an out-of-control process.** Even if those seven dots haven't jumped off the graph yet, the fact that they are all stuck on one side proves your machine isn't acting normal anymore.

Integration makes sure all the scattered pieces of your project are glued together perfectly. Quality makes sure the final result works exactly like you promised. Master both of these, and you will be an amazing project manager!
