Imagine you are building the biggest, most complicated Lego castle ever. The picture on the box looks perfect, but it doesn't tell you if you are missing pieces or if it's going to take you way longer to build than you thought. Real life is messy. As someone in charge of a project, your job isn't just to draw the instructions. You have to steer the project through all the surprises that pop up along the way. To do this, you measure the exact difference between what you planned to happen and what is actually happening right now. This checking and fixing is what we call predictive project controls. 

In a plan-driven (or predictive) project, we map out everything before we start building. But how do we make sure our project actually follows that map? We use special documents to lock in our goals, tracking tools to watch out for hiccups, and some simple math called Earned Value Management to see exactly how healthy our project is in real-time. 

Let's look at how this works, starting with the rules of the game and ending with the fun math that tracks our progress!

## The Anatomy of Intention: Predictive Artifacts

Before you can manage a project, you need to write down your plan. In the project world, a **predictive project artifact** is any document, template, or output created to manage a plan-driven project. Think of artifacts as the official rulebooks and scorecards for your game.

The very first step is the **Project Charter**. This is a predictive artifact that formally authorizes the existence of a project. It gives the project manager official permission to use time, money, and helpers to get the work done. Without a charter, you don't have a real project; you just have a fun idea.

Once you get the official green light, your team makes the **Project Management Plan**. This isn't just a simple calendar. The Project Management Plan is a comprehensive predictive artifact that defines how the project is executed, monitored, and controlled. It holds all your mini-plans for how to handle helpers, communication, buying supplies, and handling problems. 

### The Triad of Baselines

Inside your Project Management Plan are three super important targets called baselines. A baseline is your officially approved, locked-in plan. It is the "save game" file you compare your actual progress against. 

1. **The Scope Baseline**: What exactly are we building? The Scope Baseline is a predictive artifact comprising three parts: the approved project scope statement, the **Work Breakdown Structure**, and the **Work Breakdown Structure dictionary**. 
    * The **Work Breakdown Structure (WBS)** is a hierarchical decomposition of the total scope of work to be carried out by the project team. That is a fancy way of saying it breaks a massive project down into tiny, bite-sized tasks. 
    * Just drawing a box that says "build the roof" isn't enough instruction for your helpers. So, it comes with the **Work Breakdown Structure dictionary**, which is a predictive artifact providing detailed deliverable, activity, and scheduling information about each component in the Work Breakdown Structure.
2. **The Cost Baseline**: How much allowance money can we spend? The Cost Baseline is the approved version of the time-phased project budget. It maps out exactly how much money you are expected to spend over the project's timeline. Here is a super important rule to remember: **The Cost Baseline excludes project management reserves.** Management reserves are secret emergency funds hidden away by the big bosses for completely unexpected disasters (like an alien invasion ruining your bake sale). They are not part of your normal, everyday budget.
3. **The Schedule Baseline**: When will this be finished? The **Schedule Baseline** is the approved version of a schedule model used as a basis for comparison to actual results. Because time only moves forward, **the Schedule Baseline can only be changed through formal change control procedures.** You cannot simply secretly change the due date because your friend was late delivering supplies; you have to ask for official permission to change the baseline.

## Navigational Instruments: Registers and Logs

If baselines are the map of where you want to go, registers and logs are your car's dashboard telling you how the drive is actually going. You will check these documents every single day.

* **The Risk Register**: This is a predictive artifact containing the details of identified individual project risks. It lists things that *might* happen (like a rainstorm during your outdoor pizza party), the chance of it happening, and your backup plan.
* **The Issue Log**: A risk is a "maybe." An issue is an "uh-oh, it actually happened." The Issue Log is a predictive project document used to record and monitor items under discussion or in dispute. If the pizza delivery driver gets a flat tire and your food is late, it moves from being a guessed risk to a real entry in the Issue Log.
* **The Change Log**: Remember how baselines need official permission to change? The Change Log is a predictive artifact used to document all formal changes submitted during a project. It keeps track of whether those changes were approved, delayed, or completely rejected.
* **The Requirements Traceability Matrix**: How do you prove your final video game actually has all the levels you promised months ago? The Requirements Traceability Matrix is a predictive grid linking product requirements from their origin to the deliverables that satisfy them. It makes sure you didn't accidentally drop any cool features you promised.
* **The Lessons Learned Register**: You learn a lot by doing. The Lessons Learned Register is a predictive artifact used to record knowledge gained during a project to improve future performance. It helps you make sure you don't repeat the exact same mistakes next time!

## The Physics of Measurement: Project Controls and EVM

Now that we have our plans and logs, it's time to actually steer the ship! This is called **project controls**. Project controls encompass the data gathering, management, and analytical processes used to predict, understand, and influence the time and cost outcomes of a project. That means collecting clues to understand if you are going to finish on time and stay within budget.

The main tool we use for this is **variance analysis**, which is the technique used to determine the cause and degree of difference between the baseline (your awesome plan) and actual project performance (what is really happening). 

To do this tracking easily, professionals use **Earned Value Management (EVM)**. EVM is a methodology that combines scope, schedule, and resource measurements to assess project performance. 

EVM sounds complicated, but it just asks three simple questions:

1. How much work did we *plan* to finish by today?
2. How much work did we *actually* finish by today?
3. How much did we *actually spend* to do that work?

Let's use a fun example. Imagine you are making 10 custom friendship bracelets to sell. You plan to make 1 bracelet per day. The string for each bracelet costs \$2. So, your plan is that it will take 10 days and cost \$20 in total. 

Now, imagine it is the end of Day 4. Let's look at the three big EVM clues:

### 1. Planned Value (PV)
**Planned Value** is the authorized budget assigned to the scheduled work to be accomplished for a given activity. 
* *How to think about it:* By Day 4, your plan said you should have 4 bracelets finished. Since each bracelet has a budget of \$2, your Planned Value is \$8. You *planned* to have \$8 worth of bracelets done by now.

### 2. Earned Value (EV)
**Earned Value** is the measure of work performed expressed in terms of the budget authorized for that specific work.
* *How to think about it:* You check your desk on Day 4. Oh no! You got distracted playing video games and only made 3 bracelets. We measure your progress by looking at the budget value of what you finished. You finished 3 bracelets, and they are valued at \$2 each. So, your Earned Value is \$6. You have officially "earned" \$6 worth of your planned work.

### 3. Actual Cost (AC)
**Actual Cost** is the total realized cost incurred in accomplishing work on an activity during a specific time period.
* *How to think about it:* You look at your receipts. You had to buy some extra fancy beads to finish those 3 bracelets, so it actually cost you \$9 total. Your Actual Cost is \$9.

> **The Day 4 Check-In:**
> * **PV:** \$8 (You should have 4 bracelets done)
> * **EV:** \$6 (You actually got 3 bracelets done)
> * **AC:** \$9 (You spent \$9 making them)

Even without math, you can tell: *We are behind schedule (only 3 bracelets instead of 4) and we spent too much money (we spent \$9 to get \$6 worth of work).*

EVM just uses simple math formulas to show exactly how far behind or over budget we are.

## Calculating Reality: Variances and Indices

There are two ways to measure how far reality is from your plan: finding the exact difference in numbers (called a Variance) or finding a score of how well you are doing (called an Index).

### Analyzing Cost

**Cost Variance (CV)** calculates the amount of budget deficit or surplus at a given point in time during a project. To find it, you just subtract what you actually spent from the value of the work you finished.

> **Cost Variance Formula:**
> Cost Variance = Earned Value - Actual Cost 
> *(CV = EV - AC)*

Here is the step-by-step math for our bracelet project:
1. Find your Earned Value (EV), which is \$6.
2. Find your Actual Cost (AC), which is \$9.
3. Subtract AC from EV: 6 - 9 = -3.
4. Your Cost Variance is -\$3.

Since the number is negative, it tells us exactly how much extra money we accidentally spent!

| Cost Variance Result | Meaning | Real-World Translation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Positive (+)** | A positive Cost Variance indicates the project is operating **under budget**. | You spent less than you planned! You are saving your allowance! |
| **Zero (0)** | A Cost Variance of zero indicates the project is **exactly on budget**. | You spent exactly what you planned. |
| **Negative (-)** | A negative Cost Variance indicates the project is operating **over budget**. | You spent more than you planned. You are losing money. |

Sometimes it helps to look at a "score" rather than dollar amounts. We call this a **Cost Performance Index (CPI)**. Cost Performance Index is a measure of the cost efficiency of budgeted resources expressed as the ratio of earned value to actual cost. Instead of subtracting, we divide.

> **Cost Performance Index Formula:**
> Cost Performance Index = Earned Value / Actual Cost 
> *(CPI = EV / AC)*

Here is the step-by-step math:
1. Find your Earned Value (EV), which is 6.
2. Find your Actual Cost (AC), which is 9.
3. Divide EV by AC: 6 / 9 = 0.66.
4. Your CPI is 0.66.

Think of this as a grade on a test. A CPI of 0.66 means for every \$1.00 you spend, you are only getting 66 cents worth of actual work done. That's a failing grade for your budget!

| Cost Performance Index | Meaning |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Greater than 1.0** | Indicates a **cost underrun** for project performance to date. You are doing amazing! |
| **Exactly 1.0** | You are earning exactly \$1 of value for every \$1 you spend. |
| **Less than 1.0** | Indicates a **cost overrun** for project work completed. You are being inefficient. |

### Analyzing Schedule

We can measure our time just like we measured our money! Instead of comparing to what we spent, we compare to what we *planned* to do. 

**Schedule Variance (SV)** measures schedule performance expressed as the difference between the earned value and the planned value. 

> **Schedule Variance Formula:**
> Schedule Variance = Earned Value - Planned Value 
> *(SV = EV - PV)*

Here is the step-by-step math for our bracelets:
1. Find your Earned Value (EV), which is \$6.
2. Find your Planned Value (PV), which is \$8.
3. Subtract PV from EV: 6 - 8 = -2.
4. Your Schedule Variance is -\$2.

Why is our time measured in dollars? Because we turn time into the budget value of the work! Being down \$2 in Schedule Variance means you are exactly one bracelet behind schedule.

| Schedule Variance Result | Meaning |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Positive (+)** | A positive Schedule Variance indicates the project is progressing **ahead of schedule**. |
| **Zero (0)** | A Schedule Variance of zero indicates the project is progressing **exactly on schedule**. |
| **Negative (-)** | A negative Schedule Variance indicates the project is progressing **behind schedule**. |

Just like with money, we can find our schedule "score." **Schedule Performance Index (SPI)** is a measure of schedule efficiency expressed as the ratio of earned value to planned value.

> **Schedule Performance Index Formula:**
> Schedule Performance Index = Earned Value / Planned Value 
> *(SPI = EV / PV)*

Here is the step-by-step math:
1. Find your Earned Value (EV), which is 6.
2. Find your Planned Value (PV), which is 8.
3. Divide EV by PV: 6 / 8 = 0.75.
4. Your SPI is 0.75. 

An SPI of 0.75 means you are moving at only 75% of your planned speed. You need to hurry up!

| Schedule Performance Index | Meaning |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Greater than 1.0** | Indicates **more work was completed** than was originally planned. |
| **Exactly 1.0** | You are progressing at exactly the planned speed. |
| **Less than 1.0** | Indicates **less work was completed** than was originally planned. |

*Quick cheat code to remember:* 
*If a variance is negative (-), it's bad.* 
*If an index is under 1.0, it's bad.* 
*Earned Value (EV) always comes FIRST in every formula!* 
* *CV = EV - AC*
* *SV = EV - PV*
* *CPI = EV / AC*
* *SPI = EV / PV*

## The Discipline of Correction: Control Processes

Knowing you are behind schedule or out of money doesn't help if you don't do anything to fix it! Measuring without acting is just watching a disaster happen. That is why we have formal control processes. 

**Control Schedule** is the predictive project management process of monitoring the status of the project to update the project schedule and managing changes to the schedule baseline. If your SPI drops to a sad 0.75, you jump into the Control Schedule process to figure out how to catch up. Maybe you ask a friend to help you make the bracelets so you can go faster!

At the same time, **Control Costs** is the predictive project management process of monitoring the status of the project to update the project costs and managing changes to the cost baseline. If your CPI is a failing 0.66, you use the Control Costs process to stop losing money. Maybe you find cheaper beads or ask permission to change the plan so you don't completely empty your piggy bank.

### Summary

Predictive project controls turn guessing into a science. By writing down your plan using artifacts and baselines (Scope, Cost, and Schedule), tracking hiccups with registers and logs, and using simple math formulas to check reality (CV, SV, CPI, SPI), you become a project superstar. You get a superpower: the ability to see if your project is in trouble while there is still plenty of time to save the day!
