Listen to me, put down the pretzel and look me in the eye. You’re studying for your CAPM, and you’re looking at predictive project management like it’s a stack of boring corporate flowcharts. Erase that from your mind right now. I need you to understand that running a predictive project is exactly like orchestrating a multi-million-dollar, high-stakes casino heist. You do not just “wing” a heist. You predict, you plan, and you execute in a rigidly controlled chain of events.

But before we even look at the blueprints, we need to establish the most fundamental rule of the criminal—and project management—underworld. Project management process groups are not identical to project phases! Do not confuse the management *processes* with the chronological timeline of the job. A single project phase can involve activities from all five project management process groups. For instance, if Phase One of our heist is just "Casing the Joint," you initiate the casing, you plan the casing, you execute the casing, you monitor your casing, and you close the casing phase. Process groups are states of cognitive and administrative effort, not days on a calendar.

Now, let’s start at the very beginning. We are in a smoky back room. The Initiating Process Group consists of activities performed to define a new project or a new phase of an existing project. Its entire purpose is that it establishes the formal authorization to start a new project or phase. You can't just rent a getaway van because you feel inspired; you need the boss’s blessing. Developing the project charter is a primary activity within the Initiating Process Group. Think of this charter as a golden briefcase from the syndicate—the development of the project charter provides the project manager with the authority to apply organizational resources to project activities. Once I have that charter, I can legally spend the syndicate's money. While I have that briefcase, identifying stakeholders is a core activity within the Initiating Process Group. Identifying stakeholders involves documenting relevant information regarding stakeholder interests, and it includes assessing stakeholder involvement and potential impact on project success. Who is the casino owner? Who is the beat cop on the corner? Who is the getaway driver's paranoid mother? If you don't map out who holds the power to champion or ruin your heist, you are dead before you start.

With the charter in hand, we lock ourselves in a basement plastered with schematics. The Planning Process Group establishes the total scope of the predictive project effort. Because predictive projects leave absolutely nothing to chance, the Planning Process Group contains the largest number of individual project management processes. Our magnum opus here is the blueprint itself: developing the project management plan is a primary activity within the Planning Process Group. This master document dictates everything. The project management plan defines how the predictive project is executed, it defines how the predictive project is monitored and controlled, and it defines how the predictive project is formally closed.

To build this master plan, we systematically define every dimension of the heist. First, we need to know what we are stealing. Collecting requirements is a Planning activity used to determine stakeholder needs to meet project objectives. Does the boss want the cash, the diamonds, or the bearer bonds? Once we know, defining scope is a Planning activity that develops a detailed description of the project and the product. We draw a hard line: we are hitting the main vault, not the teller cages. To actually estimate the job, creating the Work Breakdown Structure is a Planning activity that subdivides project deliverables into smaller components. We don't just say "rob the casino." We break it down to "disable cameras," "cut the alarm," and "drill the lock." With the pieces laid out, defining activities is a Planning process that identifies the specific actions required to produce the project deliverables. But you can't drill the lock before the cameras go dark, right? Sequencing activities is a Planning process that identifies and documents relationships among the project activities. We need to know how long each step takes, so estimating activity durations is a Planning process that provides quantitative assessments of time periods required to complete specific tasks. Two minutes for the laser, forty seconds for the bags. All of this feeds into developing the schedule, a Planning activity that creates the project schedule model. Developing the schedule analyzes activity sequences, durations, and resource requirements to give us a realistic, minute-by-minute timeline. 

Then comes the money and the gear. Estimating costs is a Planning process that develops an approximation of the monetary resources needed to complete project work. Determining the budget is a Planning activity that aggregates the estimated costs of individual activities to establish an authorized cost baseline. That baseline is the hard financial line we cannot cross without getting our kneecaps broken. We also need quality gear. Planning quality management is a Planning process that identifies quality requirements and standards for the project deliverables. Our thermal drills must melt steel at exactly 3,000 degrees—that is our standard! For the crew and equipment, planning resource management is a Planning process that defines how to estimate, acquire, manage, and use physical resources and human resources. Estimating activity resources is a Planning process that identifies the type and quantities of materials, equipment, and supplies necessary to perform project work. We need exactly four duffel bags and two grappling hooks. We also need a way to talk securely, so planning communications management is a Planning process that develops an appropriate approach for project communication activities based on the information needs of each stakeholder. The sniper on the roof needs two radio clicks; the boss gets a single text message. 

Then, the paranoia sets in. Planning risk management is a Planning process that defines how to conduct risk management activities for a project. Identifying risks is a Planning process that determines which risks may affect the project, and identifying risks includes documenting the characteristics of potential project risks. What if the vault timer is upgraded? Performing qualitative risk analysis is a Planning process that prioritizes individual project risks for further analysis by assessing risk probability of occurrence and potential impact. Is a guard walking by highly probable or just highly impactful? Performing quantitative risk analysis is a Planning process that numerically analyzes the combined effect of identified individual project risks on overall project objectives. We then engage in planning risk responses, a Planning activity that develops options and actions to address overall project risk exposure—like having a sewer escape route just in case. If we don’t have the explosives we need, planning procurement management is a Planning process that documents project procurement decisions and specifies the procurement approach. Crucially, planning procurement management identifies potential sellers for project resources, like our underground explosives guy. Finally, planning stakeholder engagement is a Planning process that develops approaches to involve project stakeholders based on stakeholder needs, expectations, and interests, keeping the syndicate calm while we work.

Fast forward. It’s Friday night. We drop through the casino skylight. The Executing Process Group consists of activities performed to complete the work defined in the project management plan. Because we are physically blowing the vault and moving the crew, the Executing Process Group typically consumes the largest portion of the project budget and typically requires the largest amount of physical and human project resources. On the floor, directing and managing project work is an Executing activity that leads and performs the work defined in the project management plan. Crucially, as the team works, gathering work performance data is a typical Executing activity happening right there in the vault! We are also managing project knowledge, an Executing activity that uses existing knowledge and creates new knowledge to achieve the project objectives. Managing quality is an Executing activity that translates the quality management plan into executable quality activities—our safe-cracker actively auditing his drill's temperature. We are acquiring resources, an Executing activity that obtains team members, facilities, equipment, and materials necessary to complete project work. Developing the team is an Executing activity that improves competencies, team member interaction, and the overall team environment so nobody panics. Managing the team is an Executing activity that tracks team member performance and provides feedback. Managing the team includes resolving issues and managing team changes—if someone freezes up, I swap them out immediately! Managing communications is an Executing activity that ensures timely collection, creation, distribution, and storage of project information. If the alarm trips, implementing risk responses is an Executing activity that enacts agreed-upon risk response plans. We are conducting procurements, an Executing activity that obtains seller responses, selects a seller, and awards a contract—maybe paying the getaway driver his bonus early. And we are managing stakeholder engagement, an Executing activity that communicates and works with stakeholders to meet stakeholder needs and expectations.

But where am I during this chaos? I’m the guy in the unmarked van across the street with five computer monitors. The Monitoring and Controlling Process Group consists of activities required to track, review, and regulate project progress. The golden rule of the van is this: the Monitoring and Controlling Process Group interacts continuously with all other process groups throughout the project life cycle! I don't wait until the heist is over to check the cameras; I watch from day one! 

Listen closely to how information flows into my van, because this will save your life on the exam. You guys physically gathering work performance data inside the vault is an Executing activity. But me sitting in the van analyzing work performance data to produce work performance information is a typical Monitoring and Controlling activity! I crunch the raw numbers. Then, monitoring and controlling project work—a Monitoring and Controlling activity that tracks overall progress against the project management plan—takes that analyzed information and spits out reports. Work performance reports are generated as an output of the Monitoring and Controlling project work process. Finally, those work performance reports are distributed to stakeholders during the Executing process of managing communications.

If my monitors show you guys drilling too slowly, I intervene. Corrective actions are generated as a result of Monitoring and Controlling activities to realign project performance with the project management plan. Preventive actions are recommended during Monitoring and Controlling activities to ensure future project performance aligns with the project management plan. If a diamond gets scratched during extraction, defect repairs are identified during Monitoring and Controlling activities to modify nonconforming product components. But before we just alter the master blueprint, performing integrated change control is a Monitoring and Controlling activity that involves reviewing all change requests and approving changes. Performing integrated change control includes managing changes to project deliverables and organizational process assets in a rigidly documented way. 

Now, look at the loot. Controlling quality is a Monitoring and Controlling activity that records the results of executing quality management activities to assess performance. Controlling quality ensures the project outputs are complete and meet customer expectations, and it focuses on the correctness of the deliverables and meeting the specified quality requirements. Are the diamonds flawless? That’s quality control. But validating scope is a Monitoring and Controlling activity that formalizes acceptance of the completed project deliverables. Validating scope focuses on the formal acceptance of deliverables by the customer or sponsor. Getting the Godfather to nod and accept the bag of diamonds is validating scope! 

Meanwhile, my eyes are still on the monitors. Controlling scope is a Monitoring and Controlling activity that monitors project scope status and manages changes to the scope baseline, ensuring we don’t accidentally steal the casino’s cheap silverware on the way out. Controlling the schedule is a Monitoring and Controlling activity that monitors project status to manage changes to the schedule baseline. Controlling costs is a Monitoring and Controlling activity that monitors project status to manage changes to the cost baseline. Controlling resources is a Monitoring and Controlling activity that ensures the physical resources assigned to the project are available as planned—is the helicopter actually on the roof? Monitoring communications is a Monitoring and Controlling activity that ensures the information needs of the project and stakeholders are met. Monitoring risks is a Monitoring and Controlling activity that involves tracking identified risks and identifying new risks. Monitoring risks includes evaluating risk process effectiveness and monitoring residual risks. Controlling procurements is a Monitoring and Controlling activity that manages procurement relationships and monitors contract performance with our mercenary pilot. And monitoring stakeholder engagement is a Monitoring and Controlling activity that monitors project stakeholder relationships, and it involves tailoring strategies for engaging stakeholders based on project progress.

Finally, we make it out. We’re in a safehouse dividing the \$10 million haul. But we aren't done until the paperwork is clean. The Closing Process Group consists of activities performed to formally complete a project, phase, or contract. Closing the project or phase is an activity within the Closing Process Group that finalizes all activities across all Project Management Process Groups. We don't leave fingerprints! Archiving project information is an activity performed during the Closing process to retain records for future reference, in case we rob a sister casino in ten years. Updating organizational process assets is an activity performed during the Closing process to capture final lessons learned—maybe next time we use quieter drills. And lastly, releasing organizational resources is an activity performed during the Closing process to free up personnel and physical assets for other projects. The safe-cracker goes home, the van is returned to the lot, and the greatest predictive project of our lives is officially closed. Finish your pretzel. You're ready for the exam.
