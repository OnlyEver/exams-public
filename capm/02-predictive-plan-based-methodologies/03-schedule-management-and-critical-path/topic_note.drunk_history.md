Look me in the eye. Put down the pretzel and look at me. If you want to survive as a project coordinator, if you want to stop putting out fires and start lighting them on your own terms, you have to understand time. You can requisition titanium, you can borrow a developer from another department, you can even manufacture a million widgets, but you cannot manufacture a single extra second. That is why the Project Schedule Management knowledge area includes the processes required to manage the timely completion of the project. It is the art of controlling the fourth dimension, and my friend, we are going to learn how to manipulate it like we’re planning an underground, high-stakes Vegas casino heist. 

To rob a casino—or, you know, launch a global software update—you can’t just scribble a to-do list on a napkin. You need a blueprint. A crazy, red-string-on-a-corkboard map. In our world, the Precedence Diagramming Method is a technique used for constructing a schedule model. Imagine a massive wall in our secret hideout. The Precedence Diagramming Method represents project activities as nodes—think of them as those menacing polaroid pictures pinned to the wall. Then, the Precedence Diagramming Method graphically links activities by logical relationships to show the sequence of performance. The red string connects the photos, creating the inescapable laws of our timeline. 

But the string isn't just string. We have four absolute rules for how one task hands off to another, and you cannot violate them. First is the most common: a Finish-to-Start logical relationship means a successor activity cannot start until a predecessor activity has finished. The getaway driver cannot start tearing down the highway until the vault team has finished loading the cash. Period. Next, we have the Start-to-Start logical relationship, which means a successor activity cannot start until a predecessor activity has started. The hacker’s video feed download cannot start until the infiltration team has at least started breaching the mainframe. Then there’s the Finish-to-Finish logical relationship, meaning a successor activity cannot finish until a predecessor activity has finished. The diversionary explosion outside the casino cannot finish until the silent safe-cracking inside has finished, or else the cops will show up too early! Finally, the rarest and wildest one: a Start-to-Finish logical relationship means a successor activity cannot finish until a predecessor activity has started. Think of the security guard we bribed; his shift (the successor) cannot finish and let him go home until the relief guard (the predecessor) has started their shift to take over the post.

Why are we tied to these rules? Because dependencies dictate our reality. Mandatory dependencies are logical relationships inherent in the nature of the project work, or they are logical relationships that are contractually required. It’s physics and law! You physically cannot hack the vault door before you slice the power cables. It's non-negotiable. Meanwhile, discretionary dependencies are logical relationships established based on specific project team preferences, or established based on best practices. Our crew likes to synchronize watches before putting on our tactical ski masks. We could do it in reverse, but this is how we prefer to operate. Then we have external dependencies, which involve a logical relationship between project activities and non-project activities outside the project team's control. We have to wait for the city’s scheduled power grid maintenance to drop the casino’s lights—totally out of our hands. On the flip side, internal dependencies involve a logical relationship between project activities strictly within the control of the project team, like our tech guy configuring the radios before handing them to the demolition expert. 

Sometimes, we need to bend time. We use a lead, which is the amount of time whereby a successor activity can be advanced with respect to a predecessor activity. If the vault takes an hour to crack, but the getaway van needs a five-minute warm-up, we use a lead to start the engine five minutes before the cracking is totally finished. We overlap them! Conversely, a lag is the amount of time whereby a successor activity is delayed with respect to a predecessor activity. If we use chemical acid to melt the lock, we must wait ten agonizing minutes for the acid to eat the metal before we pull the handle. That dead air—that excruciating wait where no active work happens—is a lag. 

Are you tracking with me? Drink some water, because now we get to the math. We are going to use the Critical Path Method. The Critical Path Method was originally developed in the late 1950s by Morgan R. Walker and James E. Kelley Jr.—two absolute legends who basically figured out how to mathematically guarantee when a massive operation would finish. The Critical Path Method is a schedule network analysis technique used to estimate the minimum project duration. 

Imagine our heist has three teams doing different sequences of jobs. One sequence takes four hours, one takes six hours, and the vault-cracking sequence takes a brutal ten hours. The ten-hour sequence is the dictator of our fate. The critical path is the sequence of activities representing the longest continuous path through a project network diagram. Paradoxically, because it is the longest path of mandatory work, the critical path determines the shortest possible time required to complete the entire project. We cannot finish the heist in under ten hours, no matter how fast the other teams work. Because of this, activities on the critical path typically have zero total float. There is no wiggle room. If the vault drill jams for one minute, the entire heist is delayed by one minute. 

To figure out this path, we run an algorithm with two sweeps. First, we do a forward pass. A forward pass calculates the Early Start dates for all project activities, and a forward pass calculates the Early Finish dates for all project activities. The Early Start date is the earliest possible point in time an activity can begin based on schedule network logic, and the Early Finish date is the earliest possible point in time an activity can finish based on schedule network logic. We sweep from the beginning of the night to the end. Then, we look at our sunrise deadline and sweep backwards! A backward pass calculates the Late Start dates for all project activities, and a backward pass calculates the Late Finish dates for all project activities. The Late Start date is the latest possible point in time an activity can start without delaying the project finish date, and the Late Finish date is the latest possible point in time an activity can finish without delaying the project finish date. 

When you compare the early dates and the late dates, you find your breathing room. Total float is the amount of time an activity can be delayed without delaying the overall project finish date. The formula for total float is Late Start minus Early Start ($LS - ES$). An alternative formula for total float is Late Finish minus Early Finish ($LF - EF$). If the hacker can start bypassing the cameras at 1:00 AM (Early Start) but doesn't strictly have to start until 1:30 AM (Late Start) without ruining the 5:00 AM escape, he has 30 minutes of total float. He can literally eat a sandwich. Do not confuse this with free float! Free float is the amount of time an activity can be delayed without delaying the Early Start date of any immediate successor activity. Total float is about the whole project; free float is just about not pissing off the very next guy in line.

Now, God forbid a project schedule network diagram can have multiple critical paths running concurrently. If you have two ten-hour sequences of tasks, the existence of multiple critical paths increases the overall schedule risk of a project. You now have two separate arteries that can bleed out the whole operation! And keep your eyes peeled for a near-critical path, which is a sequence of activities with a very small amount of total float. A near-critical path will become the new critical path if its activities experience delays exceeding their total float. If the hacker's 9-hour sequence hits a 2-hour delay, congratulations, the hacker is your new biggest problem.

But the plan is just a plan until the bullets start flying. Once execution begins, we measure our survival using Earned Value Management metrics. We need to know if we are bleeding time in dollars. We look at Planned Value, which represents the authorized budget assigned to the scheduled work expected to be accomplished. This is what the blueprint *said* we’d have done by 2:00 AM. Then we look at Earned Value, which represents the authorized budget assigned to the scheduled work that has actually been completed. This is the cold, hard reality of the vault door right in front of us.

Schedule Variance is an Earned Value Management metric used to measure overall schedule performance. It asks: "Are we rich in time, or bankrupt?" The formula for Schedule Variance is Earned Value minus Planned Value ($EV - PV$). If the math spits out a positive Schedule Variance, it indicates that the project is currently ahead of schedule! We cracked the safe faster than planned! A negative Schedule Variance indicates that the project is currently behind schedule. Panic time. A Schedule Variance of exactly zero indicates that the project is perfectly on schedule. 

For a gauge of pure speed, we use the Schedule Performance Index, an Earned Value Management metric used to measure schedule efficiency. The formula for the Schedule Performance Index is Earned Value divided by Planned Value ($EV / PV$). A Schedule Performance Index greater than 1.0 indicates more work was completed than originally planned—we are working at 120% efficiency! A Schedule Performance Index exactly equal to 1.0 indicates the project work is proceeding exactly as planned. But a Schedule Performance Index less than 1.0 indicates less work was completed than originally planned. We are sluggish. We are failing.

If we check our metrics and we are behind schedule but the sunrise is coming, we have to act. We need schedule compression techniques! Schedule compression techniques shorten the project schedule duration without reducing the project scope. We still steal all the money; we don't leave gold bars behind, we just do it faster! 

How? First option: Crashing. Crashing is a schedule compression technique used to shorten the schedule duration by adding extra resources. The vault isn't opening fast enough? Hand a second drill to another mercenary! Throw bodies at the problem! But beware, the crashing schedule compression technique typically results in increased overall project costs. You have to pay that second mercenary a cut of the loot. 

Second option: Fast tracking. Fast tracking is a schedule compression technique where activities normally done in sequence are instead performed in parallel. Instead of waiting for the lasers to be fully disabled before walking across the room, we start walking *while* the hacker is disabling them! We overlap the danger! Because you are destroying the natural order, the fast tracking schedule compression technique often results in increased project risk. If the hacker slips up, the lasers turn back on while we are in the middle of the room. Thus, the fast tracking schedule compression technique frequently results in increased project rework—which, in our case, means starting the heist over from a jail cell.
