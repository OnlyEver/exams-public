Think of a company like a busy school club or a big video game team. To keep things going every day, you have to do the same normal stuff—like doing your daily homework or cleaning up the club room. But to get better and learn new things, you have to try something totally new, like building a custom gaming computer or throwing a huge tournament. The normal, everyday stuff is called operations. The big, new leaps are projects. Let's look at how groups separate the everyday chores from the exciting new ideas.

## The Nature of a Project: The Vehicle for Change

At its core, **a project is a temporary endeavor undertaken to create a unique product, service, or result.** 

Think about deciding to build a giant, custom Lego castle. That is a project. You do not build that exact same castle every single day. **Projects are unique endeavors whereas operations are repetitive endeavors.** Making your bed every morning is repetitive, so it is an operation, not a project. 

Because a project is temporary, it has a strict timer. **Projects have a definite beginning and a definite end.** They do not go on forever. As the person in charge, **project management focuses on delivering specific scope within given constraints of time, cost, and quality.** That just means you have to build exactly what you promised (scope), by the deadline (time), using only your allowance (cost), and make sure it does not fall apart (quality). 

Here is how you might manage your cost constraint with simple math:
1.  Check your total project budget: You have \$20.
2.  Buy the special Lego pieces you need for \$15.
3.  Calculate your remaining allowance: \$20 - \$15 = \$5. You stayed under budget!

### Moving from Current to Future State

Why do we put in all this hard work to do projects? Because **projects drive organizational change by moving an enterprise from a current state to a desired future state.**

*   **The current state represents the condition of the organization before the project begins.** For example, your current state might be, "I share a really messy room with my brother."
*   **The future state represents the condition of the organization after the project is successfully completed.** Now, your future state is, "We built awesome bunk beds and have a clean gaming corner."

The project was the actual work of cleaning the room and building the beds to get to that better future.

### The Creation of Business Value

By getting to that future state, **projects enable the creation of business value for an organization.** "Business value" just means making the group better, richer, or more popular. There are two kinds of value:

1.  **Tangible business value:** These are real things you can touch or count. **Tangible business value includes monetary assets, stockholder equity, and fixtures.** For example, cash in your piggy bank (monetary assets) or new wooden shelves you built for your room (fixtures). 
2.  **Intangible business value:** These are awesome things you cannot actually touch. **Intangible business value includes brand recognition, strategic alignment, and reputation.** If you start a project to clean up the local park, you might not earn any money, but your reputation goes way up because everyone in town thinks you are great!

### When Does a Project Actually End?

Since a project does not go on forever, we have to know exactly when to stop. There are three main reasons a project stops:
1.  **A project reaches completion when the project objectives have been achieved.** You finished the Lego castle! The project is a success and it is over.
2.  **A project reaches completion when the project objectives cannot be met.** Maybe your dog chewed up the most important pieces, and you cannot finish the castle. This is a fail, but the project still ends.
3.  **A project reaches completion when the need for the project no longer exists.** Imagine you were building a custom snow sled, but then your family moved to a hot beach in Florida. You do not need a sled anymore, so you just stop building it. 

## Operations: Sustaining the Core

If projects are how we change and do cool new things, operations are how we survive the everyday. 

> **Operations consist of ongoing execution of activities.** Remember the big rule: **projects are temporary endeavors whereas operations are ongoing endeavors.** 

**Operations produce the same repetitive product or provide a repetitive service.** Think of a local pizza shop. Making the exact same pepperoni pizza hundreds of times a day is an operation. Delivering those pizzas every night is an operation. 

Because of this, **operations management focuses on sustaining the core business processes.** The goal here is not to invent something wild and new; it is to keep the pizza shop running smoothly, making sure the ovens are hot and the cheese is fresh every single day.

### The Intersection of Projects and Operations

In real life, projects and operations mix together all the time. Here are the three times they meet:

1.  **Projects intersect with operations when a new product or service is handed over to the operational team.** Think of inventing a brand-new type of spicy stuffed-crust pizza. Inventing and testing it is the project. Once the recipe is perfect, you hand it over to the kitchen staff (the operational team) so they can bake it every single day as an operation.
2.  **Projects intersect with operations when an existing operational process requires improvement or upgrading.** Imagine the pizza shop's delivery drivers keep getting lost. You start a temporary project to install GPS trackers in all the delivery cars. Once the GPS project is done, the daily deliveries (operations) become much faster.
3.  **Projects intersect with operations at the end of a product lifecycle during decommissioning.** "Decommissioning" is a big word for safely taking something apart when it is too old to use. If a giant pizza oven breaks after 20 years of daily operations, you need a project to carefully unhook the gas, tear the oven down, and throw it away safely.

## The Architecture of Scale: Programs and Portfolios

Sometimes, a project is just a solo mission. **A project can exist as a standalone endeavor outside of a program or portfolio.** If you decide to paint your skateboard in the garage, that is a standalone project. But in big groups, projects are usually teamed up into larger categories.

### Programs: Mastering the Interdependencies

If you have a bunch of projects that are all connected to each other, you put them in a program. **A program is a group of related projects managed in a coordinated manner.** That means **a project can exist as a component within a larger program.** 

Why group them together? Because **coordinated management of a program provides benefits unobtainable from managing the component projects individually.** 

Imagine you are putting on a big school play. Project 1 is building the stage. Project 2 is making the costumes. Project 3 is practicing the acting. These are heavily connected! If the stage builders are running late, the actors cannot practice on it. **Program management focuses on resolving project interdependencies within the program.** "Interdependencies" just means how the projects rely on each other. By managing them as a group, the program manager makes sure all the teams share their stuff and talk to each other so the play is a huge success.

### Portfolios: Aligning with Strategic Intent

The biggest group of all is the portfolio. **A portfolio is a collection of projects, programs, subsidiary portfolios, and operations.** Think of the portfolio like the principal of your school looking at everything happening in the whole building. Sometimes, **a project can exist as a direct component within a larger portfolio**, skipping the program step completely if it does not rely on other projects.

While a program manager just wants the school play to work out, **portfolio management ensures the organization is executing the correct programs and projects to meet strategic goals.** A "strategic goal" is the big, ultimate dream of the group. 

Because money and helpers are limited, **portfolio management prioritizes resource allocation across different programs and projects.** "Resource allocation" means deciding who gets the money or the tools. 

Here is how a portfolio manager uses math to make decisions:
1.  The school has \$100 total to spend for the year.
2.  They want to fund the drama program (\$60) and the basketball program (\$60).
3.  Add them up to see the total cost: \$60 + \$60 = \$120. They do not have enough money for both!
4.  The portfolio manager decides that winning sports games is the big strategic goal, so they allocate \$60 to basketball first.
5.  Calculate the remaining budget for drama: \$100 - \$60 = \$40. The drama program will have to make do with \$40.

In the end, **portfolio components are managed as a group to achieve strategic business objectives.**

## Summary of the Management Hierarchy

To remember how all these pieces fit together, use this simple table!

| Concept | Definition & Nature | Primary Focus | Success Measure |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Project** | A unique, temporary endeavor with a definite start and end. | Delivering exactly what was promised on time and on budget. | Reaching the finish line and moving to a better future state. |
| **Operations** | Ongoing, repetitive execution of activities. | Keeping everyday things running smoothly. | Doing the same thing every day without any big mistakes. |
| **Program** | A group of related projects managed in a coordinated way. | Making sure connected projects play nicely together. | Getting special benefits you couldn't get if the projects worked all by themselves. |
| **Portfolio** | A strategic collection of projects, programs, and operations. | Deciding who gets the money and time to meet the big goals. | Winning the big overall goals of the whole group. |
