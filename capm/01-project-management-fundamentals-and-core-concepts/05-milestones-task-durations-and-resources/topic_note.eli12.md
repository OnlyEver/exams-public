Imagine building an epic, massive blanket fort. The gathering of pillows, the draping of heavy blankets, and the moving of couch cushions are all intense actions that take plenty of energy and time. But then, when the roof is finally stable, you and your friends pause to stick a flag on top. The moving of cushions is the effort; the flag-planting is just a marker showing you succeeded. In the world of project management, separating the hard work that uses up our stuff from the markers that simply show our progress is super important. Knowing the difference helps us figure out exactly what it takes to build the fort and how to easily show our awesome progress to anyone waiting to play!

## The Anatomy of Time: Tasks vs. Milestones

To make a good schedule that actually works, a project manager has to split the project into tasks and milestones.

### Project Tasks and Durations
**A project task represents a distinct piece of work requiring effort to complete.** Like doing your math homework or baking a pizza, it's actual work! Because work happens in the real world, **project tasks consume time during project execution**, and equally importantly, **project projects consume project resources during project execution**. You need time, ingredients, and energy to bake that pizza!

When we measure this time, we look at the duration. **A task duration represents the total elapsed time from the start of an activity to the completion of the same activity.** If you start baking a pizza at 5:00 PM and take it out of the oven at 5:30 PM, the task duration is 30 minutes. 

### The Nature of Milestones
If tasks are the long car ride, milestones are the "Welcome to Florida" sign. **A project milestone represents a significant point in a project timeline**, or you can also say that **a project milestone represents a significant event in a project timeline**.

> **The Zero-Duration Rule:** Because milestones are just a quick moment in time (like crossing a finish line) rather than an ongoing chore, **project management scheduling tools universally assign a duration of zero to project milestones.** 

Because of this rule, **a project milestone does not consume time**, and similarly, **a project milestone does not consume project resources.** They are just the marker showing you made it!

Project managers rely on these markers for two big reasons:
1. **Internal Tracking:** **Project managers use milestones to track major progress points in a project schedule.** It helps them easily check if the team is moving fast enough.
2. **External Communication:** Nobody wants to read a boring 50-page list of chores. Instead, **project managers use milestones to report high-level schedule status to stakeholders** (like your parents or teachers), giving them a quick, easy-to-read snapshot of how things are going.

Examples of these critical markers are everywhere:
* **Completing a major project phase serves as a common project milestone.** (Like finishing the 6th Grade!)
* **Obtaining a key stakeholder signature serves as a common project milestone.** (Like getting your mom to sign your permission slip for a field trip).
* **Delivering a final project product serves as a common project milestone.** (Like handing in your big science fair volcano).

## The Fuel of the Project: Understanding Resources

If the schedule is your map, resources are the fuel that makes the car go. In project management, resources are sorted into groups, and we keep track of them using a big rulebook.

**The resource management plan** is your main playbook. This plan **dictates how project resources are categorized**, **dictates how project resources are allocated** (handed out to do the work), and finally, **dictates how project resources are released** (given back when the work is done).

### The Categories of Project Resources
A good project plan looks at a few different kinds of resources:
* **Human resources represent the team members providing the labor required to complete project activities.** (This is you and your friends building the fort!)
* **Physical resources include the tangible assets required to complete project activities.** "Tangible" just means things you can touch. We break these down into two distinct types:
  * **Material resources are consumable items used during the execution of a project.** These get used up! (Like pizza ingredients, glue, or printer paper).
  * **Equipment resources are non-consumable tools or machinery used to complete project activities.** These do not get used up. (Like an oven, a video game console, or a hammer). 
* Finally, **Financial resources represent the funding required to execute project activities.** This is the allowance or money needed to buy everything else!

To keep everything neatly organized, **a Resource Breakdown Structure categorizes project resources hierarchically**. It's basically a big family tree for your stuff! **Project managers use a Resource Breakdown Structure to organize project resource utilization** when they are planning. Later on, **project managers use a Resource Breakdown Structure to report project resource utilization** to their bosses, showing exactly who and what did the work.

## The Calculus of Work: Estimating Activity Resources

Before we can schedule the work, we have to figure out exactly what we need. **The Estimate Activity Resources process** is how we do this. This process **identifies the type of resources required for project activities**, **identifies the quantity of resources required for project activities**, and **identifies the characteristics of resources required for project activities** (like knowing you need an expert gamer versus a total beginner).

Figuring this out takes a few steps:
* **Determining resource types involves identifying the specific skills required to complete a project task**, and also **determining resource types involves identifying the specific equipment required to complete a project task**. 
* **Determining resource quantities involves calculating the exact amount of materials needed for a project task**, and **determining resource quantities involves calculating the number of personnel needed for a project task**.

### Tools and Constraints of Estimation
We don't just guess out of nowhere. We look at clues and rules around us.

First, we look at history. **Organizational Process Assets provide historical data to help determine project resource needs.** If your team built a LEGO castle last year, that old instruction manual (your asset) tells you exactly how many bricks it actually took.

But the real world can get in the way. **Enterprise Environmental Factors can constrain the availability of specific project resources.** If the toy store is totally sold out of LEGOs, that outside factor is a strict limit on your plans.

We also have to look at the calendar! **A resource calendar documents the working days and shifts available for specific project resources.** Since friends have soccer practice and computers need to be turned off for updates, **project managers use resource calendars to determine when a specific resource is available to perform project tasks.**

### The Three Pillars of Estimation
When it is time to do the math, project managers use three main techniques:

| Technique | How it Works | Accuracy vs. Effort |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Analogous Estimating** | **Analogous estimating uses historical data from a similar past project to estimate current project resource needs.** | Fast to do, but it is a bit of a guess. It only works if the two projects are very similar! |
| **Parametric Estimating** | **Parametric estimating uses an algorithm based on historical data and project parameters to calculate resource quantities.** | Very accurate! Here is a step-by-step math example for baking cookies:<br>1. Find the rate: 1 kid can bake 12 cookies in 1 hour.<br>2. Identify the target: You need 60 cookies total.<br>3. Divide the target by the rate: 60 / 12 = 5.<br>4. Answer: It takes 5 hours for 1 kid to bake 60 cookies. |
| **Bottom-Up Estimating** | **Bottom-up estimating calculates resource needs for individual project tasks**, and then **bottom-up estimating aggregates individual task resource requirements to determine overall project resource needs.** | Highly precise, but it takes lots of time. "Aggregates" just means adding them all together. Here is a step-by-step math example for a pizza party:<br>1. Estimate task one: Buying snacks costs \$10.<br>2. Estimate task two: Buying pizzas costs \$30.<br>3. Estimate task three: Buying drinks costs $5.<br>4. Aggregate (add) them together: $10 + $30 + $5 = \$45 total. |

## The Physics of Productivity: Resources, Time, and Complexity

Time and resources are a crazy mix. **Task durations are directly influenced by the skill level of the resources assigned to the task.** A pro skateboarder will learn a new trick way faster than a kid who has never been on a board! Also, **task durations are directly influenced by the quantity of resources assigned to the task.** 

It seems obvious that **adding more resources to a task may decrease the overall task duration.** If one kid can clean a bedroom in 2 hours, maybe two kids can do it in 1 hour. 

But wait! **Adding more resources to a task introduces coordination overhead.** If you have five kids cleaning one small bedroom, they have to stop and talk, figure out who is doing what, and avoid bumping heads. That takes time!

Because of this bumping-heads problem, **The Law of Diminishing Returns dictates that adding resources to a task will eventually yield lower marginal productivity increases.** That is a fancy way of saying that eventually, adding more helpers doesn't help much at all; they just get in each other's way and slow everything down.

## Resource Optimization: Leveling vs. Smoothing

Sometimes your math shows an impossible schedule—like your best friend is scheduled to work 16 hours in a single day! To fix this, we use resource optimization. 

### Resource Leveling
**Resource leveling is a resource optimization technique** used when you absolutely cannot get more of a resource. If you only have one video game controller, you just have to take turns. 

**Resource leveling resolves resource overallocation by adjusting activity start and finish dates.** You simply move the task to tomorrow! Because you are waiting your turn, **resource leveling can increase the original project duration.** The project takes longer, but it becomes actually possible to finish.

### Resource Smoothing
If your science project is due on Friday, you cannot just push it to next week! In this case, we use another trick.

**Resource smoothing is a resource optimization technique** that fixes the schedule without breaking the big deadline. **Resource smoothing adjusts project activities within the limits of available free float** and **resource smoothing adjusts project activities within the limits of available total float.** "Float" is just a project manager's word for extra wiggle room or free time in your schedule. 

Because it only shifts tasks into the free time you already have, **resource smoothing does not delay the overall project completion date.** However, because we cannot stretch the final deadline, **resource smoothing may not fully resolve all project resource overallocations.** You might still have to stay up a little late to get your homework done on time!

## The Agile Perspective on Resources

The ideas we just talked about require planning everything perfectly at the very start. In "Agile" project management, we care more about being fast, flexible, and ready to adapt.

**Agile project management methodologies emphasize the use of cross-functional teams.** Instead of having one team that only draws, one team that only writes, and one team that only builds, agile puts all those people together! **A cross-functional team contains all the skills necessary to complete a working product increment** (a small, finished piece of the project) from start to finish.

To be super fast, **agile teams often utilize generalizing specialists to reduce bottlenecks in resource availability.** A "bottleneck" is like a traffic jam where everyone is waiting on one person. 

What is a generalizing specialist? **Generalizing specialists possess core expertise in one area alongside broad knowledge in other areas.** Think of a soccer player who is an amazing goalie (their core expertise) but is also pretty good at playing forward if the team needs help scoring. By sharing the work like this, an agile team avoids the traffic jams that normal project managers spend hours trying to fix!
