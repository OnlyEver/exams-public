Every video game console you play, the smartphone in your pocket, or the giant bridge in your town has a lifespan. A **product life cycle** represents the series of phases representing the evolution of a product from concept through delivery, growth, maturity, and retirement. 

Think of a popular video game: it starts as a cool idea (concept), gets built and released to the public (delivery), becomes super popular (growth), stays steady with a loyal fan base (maturity), and eventually the servers are shut down when the new console comes out (retirement). But making that game or building that console does not happen by magic. It happens in specific, separate steps. Because a product can exist for years or decades, a single product life cycle can encompass multiple project life cycles. Making the original game is one project. Releasing a huge expansion pack three years later is a totally different project! 

If you are the person in charge of getting these projects done, your main tool for organizing your work is the **project life cycle**. A project life cycle is the series of phases that a project passes through from its start to its completion. How you set up these steps—and how you handle surprises along the way—decides if you win or lose!

## The Anatomy of Project Phases and Boundaries

When doing big things, you usually do not do them all at once in one giant sprint. We break work into chunks called phases. The phases of a project life cycle can be sequential, iterative, or overlapping. 
*   **Sequential** means doing one phase completely before starting the next.
*   **Iterative** means repeating steps over and over to get them right.
*   **Overlapping** means starting the next phase before the first one is totally finished to save time.

Because projects cost time, energy, and money, you cannot just blindly keep working without stopping to check your progress. This is why we have a **phase gate**. 

> A **phase gate** is a formal review at the end of a project phase to determine whether to continue, modify, or end the project or phase. 

You will also hear phase gates commonly referred to as **phase reviews**, **stage gates**, or **kill points**. The phrase "kill point" sounds serious because it is! At these stopping points, if your idea is not working out, you might have to "kill" or stop the project. A phase gate decision is made by comparing current project performance and progress against the initial business documents. (Initial business documents are the original papers that explain why you started the project and what you hoped to get out of it). If your project is costing way too much compared to what you originally planned, the gate stops you from wasting more money.

## Choosing Your Approach: The Predictive to Adaptive Spectrum

One of the biggest choices you make is how you plan your project. This choice depends on how much you already know and how much you think things might change.

### Predictive Project Life Cycles
**Predictive project life cycles** are also known as waterfall or fully plan-driven life cycles. Imagine you are following step-by-step instructions to build a massive, 5,000-piece Lego castle. In a predictive project life cycle, the project scope, time, and cost are determined in the early phases of the project. You know exactly what you are building, how long it will take, and how much the pieces cost before you even snap the first brick into place.

Because everything is perfectly planned out early on, predictive life cycles manage changes strictly through a formal change control process. If your friend suddenly says, "Let's make it a spaceship instead!", you cannot just start throwing different bricks on. You have to stop, ask for official permission, and figure out how it changes your current plan. Furthermore, predictive life cycles aim to deliver the final product, service, or result at the very end of the project. You do not get to play with the castle until the last brick is placed. Therefore, a predictive approach is best suited for projects with well-understood requirements and low levels of uncertainty.

### Iterative and Incremental Project Life Cycles
Sometimes, you do not have a perfect instruction booklet. In these cases, we use iterative or incremental approaches.

**Iterative project life cycles** are all about learning as you go. In this approach, iterative project life cycles determine the project scope early in the project life cycle. You know what the final goal is (like drawing a portrait of a dog). However, iterative project life cycles modify time and cost estimates as the project team's understanding of the product increases. Iterative approaches develop the product through repeated cycles of activities to refine the solution. You start with a quick, messy pencil sketch. Then you go over it in marker. Then you add paint. Each "cycle" makes it better. An iterative approach is optimal when learning and discovering the correct solution are the primary goals of the project.

**Incremental project life cycles**, on the other hand, are about giving people pieces of the project to use as fast as possible. Incremental project life cycles deliver the product through a series of functional additions. The most important rule here is that an incremental project life cycle produces a complete, usable deliverable at the end of each increment. 
Think of launching a new video game. First, you release the multiplayer racing mode so people can play it immediately (a complete, usable deliverable). The next month, you add a story mode (a functional addition). An incremental approach is optimal when the customer requires rapid delivery of partial functional capabilities.

### Adaptive Project Life Cycles
When things are super unpredictable, you need to be agile. **Adaptive project life cycles** are also known as agile or change-driven life cycles. They are a smart mash-up of the last two ideas! Adaptive life cycles combine the characteristics of both iterative and incremental life cycles.

In an adaptive life cycle, the detailed project scope is defined and approved just before the start of each specific iteration. (An iteration is just a short burst of work, often called a "sprint"). Instead of getting upset when plans change, adaptive life cycles expect and accommodate a high degree of change throughout the project duration. 

Because you are constantly changing directions based on what works, adaptive life cycles require frequent stakeholder involvement to provide feedback on delivered increments. (Stakeholders are the people who care about or pay for the project, like your customers or parents). You show them what you built, they tell you what they like, and you use their advice to plan your next sprint. Because of this flexibility, an adaptive approach is best suited for projects with high levels of uncertainty, complex requirements, or rapidly changing environments.

### Hybrid Project Life Cycles
Sometimes, a project is a mix of things you know for sure and things you are guessing on. A **hybrid project life cycle** combines elements of both predictive and adaptive project life cycles. 

Imagine building a fancy new remote-controlled robot. Building the metal arms and wheels is pretty standard stuff. So, in a hybrid life cycle, well-known project elements follow a predictive approach. But the artificial intelligence software that helps the robot see obstacles is totally new and needs lots of testing. Therefore, in a hybrid life cycle, evolving or uncertain project elements follow an adaptive approach. You use the best tool for each specific part of the job!

### Comparing Development Approaches

| Feature | Predictive (Waterfall) | Iterative | Incremental | Adaptive (Agile) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Requirements** | Locked in early | Improved over repeated cycles | Dynamic, decided per new piece | Dynamic, decided right before a sprint |
| **Delivery** | Given to you once at the very end | Given to you once at the end (after getting better) | Frequent, smaller usable pieces | Frequent, small usable pieces |
| **Change Management** | Very strict, requires official permission | Welcomes making the design better | Welcomes adding new working features | Highly expected, built right into the rules |
| **Best Used For** | Clear plans with very few surprises | High need to learn and figure things out | High need to get working pieces quickly | High surprises and very complex rules |

## The Engine of Progress: Planning in the Face of the Unknown

Even if you plan very carefully, you cannot see the future perfectly on day one. That is why project managers use a trick called **progressive elaboration**. Progressive elaboration is the iterative process of increasing the level of detail in a project management plan as more information becomes available. You sketch out the big idea first, and fill in the tiny details later when you know more.

A really helpful form of progressive elaboration is **rolling wave planning**. In rolling wave planning, near-term work is planned in detail and future work is planned at a higher level. 
Think about riding your bike down a dark sidewalk with a flashlight. Your light clearly shows every single rock and puddle for the next 20 feet (near-term work planned in detail). You know you are heading toward your friend's house a mile away, but you do not know exactly what that far-away street looks like yet (future work planned at a higher level). As your bike rolls forward, your light reveals more of the path.

## The Five Gears of Project Mechanics: The Process Groups

Now let us talk about the actual actions a project manager takes to get work done. 

Project management processes are logically grouped into five categories called **Process Groups**. The five Project Management Process Groups are:
1.  Initiating
2.  Planning
3.  Executing
4.  Monitoring and Controlling
5.  Closing

> **CRITICAL EXAM DISTINCTION:** Process Groups are not project phases. 

This is a super important rule to remember! A "phase" is a block of time (like the "Summer Phase" or the "Testing Phase"). Process Groups are the *types of actions* you take. In real life, Process Groups interact and overlap continuously throughout the duration of a project or project phase. You might be executing work, checking on that work, and fixing your plan all on the very same day!

Let us look at exactly what each group does:

### 1. Initiating Process Group
Before you spend a single dollar, the project has to be officially born. The **Initiating Process Group** consists of processes performed to define a new project or a new phase of an existing project by obtaining authorization to start. This is like getting formal permission from your parents before you start building a treehouse in the backyard. 

### 2. Planning Process Group
Once you are allowed to start, you need a plan. The **Planning Process Group** establishes the total scope of the effort, defines objectives, and develops the course of action required to attain those objectives. This means deciding exactly how big the treehouse will be, what it will look like, and writing a step-by-step instruction guide for how to build it.

### 3. Executing Process Group
With the plan made, it is time to do the actual work. The **Executing Process Group** consists of processes performed to complete the work defined in the project management plan to satisfy the project requirements. This is where you actually saw the wood, hammer the nails, and paint the walls.

### 4. Monitoring and Controlling Process Group
While you are building, you need to make sure you are not messing up or spending too much allowance money. The **Monitoring and Controlling Process Group** tracks, reviews, and regulates the progress and performance of the project. 

For example, let us say your parents gave you an allowance budget to buy supplies. Here is how you use math to monitor your budget:
1.  Write down your total starting budget: \$50.
2.  Add up what you have spent so far on wood (\$20) and nails (\$10): 20 + 10 = \$30.
3.  Subtract what you spent from your starting budget to see how much money you have left: 50 - 30 = \$20.

If you realize you are running out of money, or the treehouse walls look crooked, this group takes action. The Monitoring and Controlling Process Group identifies any areas in which changes to the plan are required and initiates the corresponding changes. It gets you back on track!

### 5. Closing Process Group
When you are done, you do not just walk away and leave your tools in the grass. The **Closing Process Group** consists of processes performed to formally complete or close a project, phase, or contract. This means cleaning up your tools, showing the final treehouse to your friends, and officially saying, "The project is complete!"
