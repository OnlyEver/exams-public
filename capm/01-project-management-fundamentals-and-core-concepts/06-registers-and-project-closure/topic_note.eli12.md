Imagine organizing a massive school dance or a big multiplayer video game tournament. You have limited allowance money—maybe a budget of \$50—lots of borrowed equipment, and a bunch of friends, teachers, and parents waiting to see how it goes. Managing something this big can’t be done just by guessing. You need organized lists to track two huge things: the people involved, and the surprise events that might pop up. Also, when the tournament is finally over, you can’t just turn off the TV and walk away leaving a mess. Cleaning up, giving back controllers, and officially ending the event is just as important as playing the game. 

Learning how to keep track of your people, guess your problems, and finish strong separates a beginner from a real project manager. Let's look at how these parts of project management work!

## The Stakeholder Register: Mapping the Human Landscape

Before you can build a project, you need to know exactly who cares about it, who might try to stop it, and who gets to enjoy it. We keep track of this in the **stakeholder register**, which is a project document that includes information about identified project stakeholders. 

> **Stakeholder Register** 
> This list is an output of the **Identify Stakeholders** process. Think of it like your ultimate guest list and cheat sheet for keeping everyone happy.

Because you can’t plan a project without knowing who it is for, the project team creates the stakeholder register during the **project initiation phase** (the very beginning of the project). But things change! A new teacher might decide to help, or a new friend might join your group. Because of this, a project manager updates the stakeholder register whenever a new stakeholder is identified during the project lifecycle.

### What Goes into the Register?

To be useful, your list needs to be super detailed. It captures two main types of information:
1. **Identification Information:** This tells you who they are. It contains identification information such as the stakeholder name (like "Mr. Davis") and identification information such as the stakeholder organizational position (like "School Principal").
2. **Assessment Information:** This tells you what they want. It includes assessment information like the major requirements of each stakeholder (what they *must* have, like "the dance must end by 9 PM"). It also includes assessment information like the expectations of each stakeholder (what they *hope* will happen, like "expecting the music to not be too loud").

### Analyzing and Categorizing Stakeholders

You will probably have way too many people to focus on all at once. A project manager uses the stakeholder register to classify stakeholders based on their power or interest. This helps you figure out who needs the most attention. We use two main tools to group them:

*   **The Power/Interest Grid:** This is a classification tool used in the stakeholder register to group stakeholders based on their level of authority and concern. The Principal has high authority (power) and high concern (interest), so you listen to them closely! A student from another school has low authority and low concern, so you just keep an eye out for them.
*   **The Salience Model:** This is another classification tool used in the stakeholder register to group stakeholders by power, urgency, and legitimacy. "Urgency" means they need answers right now, and "legitimacy" means their request is fair and proper. If the fire marshal shows up at your dance, they have high power, urgency, and legitimacy, so you stop everything to help them.

By mapping people out, the stakeholder register helps the project manager develop an effective stakeholder engagement strategy (your game plan for keeping people happy). It also helps determine the appropriate communication methods for different project stakeholders. For example, you might text your friends, but you would send a formal email to the Principal.

## The Risk Register: Navigating Uncertainty

If stakeholders are the *people* in a project, risks are the *what-ifs*. To handle surprises, we use a **risk register**, which is a document that records the details of all identified individual project risks. 

> **Risk Register**
> This is an output of the **Identify Risks** process. It is your master plan for predicting the future.

Just like your stakeholder list, a project manager updates the risk register continuously throughout the project lifecycle. Surprises can happen at any time!

### Expanding the Definition of Risk

Usually, we think "risk" means something bad. But in project management, risk just means *uncertainty*. 
*   **Threats:** A risk register tracks uncertainty that could negatively affect project objectives. (Like the risk of it raining on your outdoor game tournament).
*   **Opportunities:** A risk register tracks uncertainty that could positively affect project objectives. (Like the risk that a local pizza place might donate \$20 worth of free food to your event!). 

### The Anatomy of a Risk Register

A good risk register doesn't just list fears and hopes; it scores them and assigns someone to watch them. For every risk, the register includes:
*   **Probability and Impact:** The risk register includes the probability of occurrence for each identified project risk (how likely it is to happen). It also includes the potential impact on project objectives for each identified risk (how big of a deal it is if it happens). 
*   **Risk Rating:** To know which risk to worry about first, a risk rating is calculated and stored in the risk register to prioritize project risks. Let's look at exactly how a project manager calculates this with a simple math formula:
    1.  **Step 1:** Give the probability (likelihood) a number from 1 to 5. Let's say rain is very likely, so we score it a 4.
    2.  **Step 2:** Give the impact (how bad it would be) a number from 1 to 5. Rain would completely ruin the TV screens outside, so we score it a 5.
    3.  **Step 3:** Multiply the two numbers together. 4 * 5 = 20.
    4.  **Step 4:** Record the final score. Your risk rating is 20! You will prioritize this highly compared to a risk that only scored a 5.
*   **Risk Owner:** The risk register includes the name of the risk owner assigned to manage each specific risk. If you assign "getting ice" to "everybody," nobody will do it. You have to assign it to one specific person!
*   **Risk Responses:** The risk register documents potential responses for each identified project risk. Will you bring umbrellas? Move indoors? 

While the project is happening, project teams use the risk register to monitor the status of previously identified risks to see if things are changing.

### Secondary, Residual, and Realized Risks

When you try to fix a risk, sometimes your fix creates new problems!
1.  **Secondary Risks:** A risk register records secondary risks that arise as a direct result of implementing a risk response. (For example: You fix the rain risk by moving the party into a tiny basement. But that creates a *secondary risk* that people might trip over TV cords because it's crowded).
2.  **Residual Risks:** A risk register documents residual risks that remain after planned risk responses have been implemented. (For example: You tape down the cords to stop tripping, but there is still a tiny 1% *residual risk* that someone might stumble on the tape).

What happens if someone actually trips? When a risk actually happens, it isn't a guess anymore—it's a real problem. The project team moves an identified risk from the risk register to the issue log when the risk actually occurs. 

## Project Closure and Transitions: The Art of Finishing

You have managed your people and your risks. The video game tournament was a blast. But your job is not over. 

**Project closure** is the final phase of the project life cycle where all project activities are formally concluded. This uses a rule called **Close Project or Phase**, which is a process within the Project Integration Management knowledge area. 

Think of closure as properly packing up the game console. Even if the power goes out and you have to cancel the party early, the rules still apply. Early project termination requires the project manager to execute formal project closure procedures for the completed work. 

### 1. Formal Acceptance and Hand-off

During project closure, the project manager formally transfers the final deliverables to the customer or operations team. If you built a custom Minecraft server for your school, you have to hand the controls over. But you can't hand it over until they say they like it!

*   **Written Acceptance:** The project manager must obtain formal written acceptance of the project deliverables from the customer during project closure (like having the school sign a paper saying it looks great). Also, the project manager must obtain formal written acceptance of the project deliverables from the sponsor during project closure (the person who gave you the money to build it).
*   **Requirements Verification:** Project closure procedures include confirming that all project requirements have been fully met. Did you include the mini-games you promised? 
*   **The Transition:** To make sure they know how to run the server, a project transition plan outlines the steps required to move the project deliverables into active operational use. During this time, an operational hand-off occurs during project transitions to ensure the receiving team can support the new project deliverables without your help. 

### 2. Contracts and Finances

A project manager is responsible for the budget. During project closure, the project manager ensures that all project contracts are formally closed. If you rented chairs, give them back! The project manager also ensures that all procurement agreements are formally closed (meaning you make sure the pizza guy was fully paid). Project closure includes finalizing all financial records related to the project to show exactly how you spent that \$50 allowance.

### 3. Releasing Resources

Your friends and your gear need to rest. Project closure requires the formal release of all project team members, so they can go back to doing their regular homework. Also, project closure requires the formal release of all physical project resources, which means returning borrowed controllers or tables to their rightful owners.

### 4. Administrative Closure and Knowledge Transfer

A project teaches you a lot of lessons. **Administrative closure** means saving those lessons so you can be even better next time.
*   Administrative closure involves gathering all project records (putting all your lists in one folder).
*   Administrative closure involves archiving project information for future use. 
*   To learn from the event, the project team conducts a final lessons learned session during the project closure phase. You basically sit around and ask, "What went well? What was a disaster?" 
*   The project manager updates the lessons learned repository during project closure to benefit future organizational projects. 
*   Finally, organizational process assets are updated during project closure to save project files, templates, and historical data into your main system.

### 5. Final Evaluation

At the very end, we look back to see if we won. A project manager evaluates the final project performance against the original project business case during project closure. Did the party bring in the fun or the charity money you promised on day one? The answers are put into the **final report**, which is an output of the project closure process that provides a summary of the overall project performance (like a final report card!).

### A Note on Agile Closure

If you are doing a step-by-step predictive project, you close when the exact checklist is done. But Agile projects (like building a cool LEGO city piece-by-piece until it looks awesome) are a little different. In agile methodologies, final project closure occurs when the product vision is achieved (it looks perfect!). Also, because Agile cares a lot about money and time, in agile methodologies, final project closure occurs when the project is no longer economically viable. This means if adding one more LEGO building costs \$30 but doesn't make the city look any better, you just stop and close the project there!
