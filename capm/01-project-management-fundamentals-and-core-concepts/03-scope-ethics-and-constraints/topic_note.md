Imagine trying to build a [bridge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bridge) across a [turbulent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turbulence) [river](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/River) without knowing the [strength of your materials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strength_of_materials), the [physical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics) boundaries of your [construction site](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Construction_site), or the [ethical obligations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethics) you owe to the [town](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Town) relying on your [structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Structure). [Project management](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Project_management) is exactly this exercise in [bounded reality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bounded_rationality). It is the [art](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Art) of achieving highly specific [goals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goal) under strict [limitations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limitation), where every [decision](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decision-making) cascades through a [system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/System) of interconnected [variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variable_%28mathematics%29). To succeed, you must understand the [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics) of your [project environment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Business_environment), accurately map the [terrain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terrain) of your [scope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scope_%28project_management%29), and strictly adhere to the [professional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Professional) rules of engagement.

## The Physics of Project Management: Categorizing [Uncertainty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uncertainty)

Before a [project team](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Project_team) lays a single [brick](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brick) or writes a line of [code](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Source_code), they must understand the [forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force) acting upon their environment. Project professionals must flawlessly distinguish between four critical forces: constraints, [assumptions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Assumption), [risks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Risk), and issues. 

### Constraints: The [Laws of Physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laws_of_science)
A **project constraint** is a known limiting factor that restricts the project team's options during execution. Think of constraints as the [walls](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wall) of a [room](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Room)—they dictate the absolute boundaries within which a project team must operate. 

> **The [Iron Triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Project_management_triangle)**
> The traditional [Iron Triangle of project management](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Project_management_triangle) constraints includes **[project scope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scope_%28project_management%29), [time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time), and [cost](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cost)**. The defining characteristic of the Iron Triangle is its interconnectedness: modifying one project constraint typically forces an impact on at least one other project constraint. 

For instance, increasing the [scope of a project](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scope_%28project_management%29) usually requires an increase in project time or project cost. You cannot simply build a larger bridge without either spending more [money](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Money) on [materials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Building_material) or extending the [deadline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_limit). Modern project management recognizes that this [triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle) is an oversimplification. Today, the [Project Management Institute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Project_Management_Institute) (PMI) identifies six primary project constraints. The six primary project constraints are **scope, [schedule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schedule_%28project_management%29), [budget](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Budget), [quality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quality_%28business%29), [resources](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resource), and risks**. 

![The Project Management Star model expands the traditional Iron Triangle to visually include quality, risk, and resources as primary interconnected constraints.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/TripleConstraint.jpg)

### Assumptions: The [Hypotheses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypothesis)
A **[project assumption](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Assumption)** is a factor in the [planning process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planning) that is considered to be true without requiring immediate [proof](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proof_%28truth%29). If you assume your [software developers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Software_developer) will be available 40 [hours](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hour) a [week](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Week), you plan accordingly. However, project assumptions carry an inherent degree of [risk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Risk) because the assumptions might prove to be false. Therefore, project assumptions must be regularly [validated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Verification_and_validation) throughout the [project lifecycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Project_management). If left unchecked, unvalidated project assumptions can become project risks.

### Risks vs. Issues: Potential vs. [Reality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reality)
A **[project risk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Risk_management)** is an uncertain [event](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Event_%28philosophy%29) or condition that can impact project objectives if the event occurs. It is vital to understand that risk is not inherently bad. While a project risk can have a negative effect on project objectives (e.g., a critical [supplier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vendor) going [bankrupt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bankruptcy)), a project risk can also have a positive effect on project objectives (e.g., a new [technology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Technology) releasing early, accelerating your schedule). All project risks must be logged and tracked in a **[risk register](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Risk_register)** document.

![A risk register matrix plots the potential impact of identified risks against their probability of occurrence to prioritize management efforts.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Hou710_RiskLog.svg)

An issue, on the other hand, is a risk that has crossed the [threshold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Threshold) from [probability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability) into reality. A **project issue** is a current condition or situation that has already occurred and is impacting project objectives. When a project risk materializes into a reality, the risk becomes a project issue. Because they require immediate management, project issues must be logged and tracked in an **[issue log](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Issue_log)** document.

| Concept | Definition | Status | Tracking Document |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Constraint** | A known limiting factor restricting team options. | Certain / Absolute | [Project Management Plan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Project_plan) |
| **[Assumption](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Assumption)** | A factor considered true without immediate proof. | Unproven | [Assumption Log](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Assumption) |
| **[Risk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Risk)** | An uncertain event impacting objectives. | Potential (Future) | [Risk Register](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Risk_register) |
| **Issue** | A current condition impacting objectives. | Materialized (Current) | [Issue Log](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Issue_log) |

## The [Geography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography) of the Project: Navigating Scope

If constraints are the laws of physics, scope is the [map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Map) of your [territory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Territory). Defining scope precisely is the only way to know when you have actually finished a project.

### Product Scope vs. Project Scope
We must draw a sharp line between the [product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Product_%28business%29) and the project.
*   **[Product scope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scope_%28project_management%29)** refers to the specific [features](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Software_feature) and [functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_%28engineering%29) that characterize a final product, [service](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Service_%28economics%29), or result. (e.g., The [software](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Software) will have a [login screen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logging_in), a [dashboard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dashboard_%28business%29), and export functionality).
*   **[Project scope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scope_%28project_management%29)** encompasses all the work performed to deliver a product, service, or result with the specified features. (e.g., The [coding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_programming), the [testing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Software_testing), the [team meetings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meeting), and the [deployment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Software_deployment) processes required to build that software).

### The Scope Baseline and the WBS
To manage the work, you must document it. The **[project scope statement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scope_%28project_management%29)** documents the entire scope, including [deliverables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deliverable), [requirements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Requirement), and boundaries. When you are critiquing a project scope statement, the process involves verifying that all project deliverables have measurable [acceptance criteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acceptance_testing). If a deliverable cannot be objectively measured, it cannot be formally accepted.

A crucial part of defining boundaries is knowing where the map ends. A **project exclusion** is an explicit statement of what is deliberately *not* included in the project scope. Documenting project exclusions helps manage [stakeholder expectations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stakeholder_%28corporate%29) by preventing assumptions about unstated features.

The definitive, locked-in version of this map is the **[project scope baseline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scope_%28project_management%29)**. The project scope baseline consists of the approved project scope statement, the **[Work Breakdown Structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Work_breakdown_structure) (WBS)**, and the [Work Breakdown Structure dictionary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Work_breakdown_structure). The Work Breakdown Structure is a [hierarchical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hierarchy) [decomposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decomposition_%28computer_science%29) of the total scope of work to be carried out by the project team. It breaks massive deliverables down into manageable, estimable components.

![A Work Breakdown Structure (WBS) hierarchically decomposes the total project scope into manageable, deliverable-oriented components.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/WbsConstruction.png)

### Monsters on the Map: Scope Creep and Gold Plating
Two [behavioral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_behavior) phenomena continually threaten the scope baseline:
1.  **[Scope creep](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scope_creep)** is the uncontrolled expansion of product or project scope without corresponding adjustments to time, cost, and resources. Scope creep typically occurs when project changes bypass the formal [integrated change control](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Change_control) process. It is the slow, deadly accumulation of "just one more tiny feature."
2.  **[Gold plating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gold_plating_%28project_management%29)** occurs when project team members intentionally deliver more features than the [customer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Customer) explicitly requested. A [developer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Software_developer) might think, *“I know they didn’t ask for [dark mode](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light-on-dark_color_scheme), but I’m going to build it because it looks great!”* This is highly dangerous. Gold plating wastes project resources on unapproved features, introducing unnecessary risk and [maintenance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Software_maintenance) burdens.

### Reaching the Destination: Control and Validation
Throughout execution, we perform **[scope control](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scope_%28project_management%29)**, which is the process of monitoring the status of the project scope and managing changes to the scope baseline. 

When the work is complete, we enter **[scope validation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Verification_and_validation)**. Scope validation is the formalized [acceptance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acceptance_testing) of the completed project deliverables by the proper [authority](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Authority). Crucially, the project team does not validate their own work—the project customer or the [project sponsor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Project_sponsor) typically performs the formal scope validation process.

## The Rules of Engagement: PMI [Code of Ethics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethical_code)

Just as a project is governed by constraints and scope, a [project manager](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Project_manager) is governed by [professional ethics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Professional_ethics). The Project Management Institute Code of Ethics and Professional Conduct is built on four core [values](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Value_%28ethics%29): **[responsibility](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moral_responsibility), [respect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Respect), [fairness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fairness), and [honesty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Honesty)**. 

[Ignorance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ignorance) of the Project Management Institute Code of Ethics and Professional Conduct is not a valid excuse for [unethical behavior](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Business_ethics). 

### Who Must Comply?
The Code’s reach is broad. It applies to:
*   All [Project Management Institute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Project_Management_Institute) members.
*   Non-members who hold a Project Management Institute [certification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Professional_certification).
*   Non-members who apply to commence a Project Management Institute certification process.
*   Non-members who serve the Project Management Institute in a [volunteer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volunteering) capacity.

However, professional ethics exist alongside [civil society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civil_society). Project managers must obey the [laws](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law) of the [country](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nation_state) in which the project managers are operating. The Project Management Institute Code of Ethics and Professional Conduct does not supersede [local](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_government) or [national laws](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_law).

### Aspirational vs. Mandatory Standards
The Code divides its rules into two categories:
*   **[Aspirational](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aspiration) standards** in the [Code of Ethics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethical_code) represent the optimal ethical behaviors that project managers strive to exhibit. 
*   **Mandatory standards** in the Code of Ethics represent the minimum acceptable ethical behaviors for project managers. Violating a mandatory standard subjects a practitioner to [disciplinary procedures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disciplinary_action) before the Project Management Institute Ethics Review Committee.

Let's examine how the four core values dictate behavior in the [real world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reality).

### 1. Responsibility
**[Responsibility](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moral_responsibility)** is the duty to take ownership of [decisions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decision-making) made, [actions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_%28philosophy%29) taken, and the resulting [consequences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consequence). 

Ethical responsibility starts before the project even begins: a project manager must accept assignments only if the project manager has the appropriate background, [experience](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Experience), [skills](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skill), and qualifications. Faking your way into a highly technical [engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering) project is an ethical breach.

During the project, a project manager must protect [proprietary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proprietary_information) and [confidential information](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confidentiality) entrusted to the project manager by [clients](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Client_%28business%29) or [employers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Employment). Furthermore, if you witness wrongdoing, silence is [complicity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complicity). Reporting unethical or [illegal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law) conduct to the appropriate authorities is a mandatory standard under the value of responsibility.

### 2. Respect
**[Respect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Respect)** is the duty to show a high regard for [oneself](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self), others, and the resources entrusted to the practitioner. 

In a [business environment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Business_environment), this means a project manager must explicitly respect the [intellectual property rights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intellectual_property) of others. You cannot steal a [competitor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Competition_%28economics%29)'s code or [pirate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Software_piracy) software for your team. It also means a project manager must not use the project manager's position or [expertise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expert) to influence decisions for personal [benefit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Profit_%28economics%29). 

When dealing with [vendors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vendor) and [stakeholders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stakeholder_%28corporate%29), [negotiating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Negotiation) in [good faith](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Good_faith) is an aspirational standard under the ethical value of respect. You strive to reach [agreements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contract) where all parties understand the terms honestly.

### 3. Fairness
**[Fairness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fairness)** is the duty to make decisions and act [impartially](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Impartiality) and [objectively](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Objectivity_%28philosophy%29). The ethical value of fairness requires practitioners to remain free from competing [self-interest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-interest), [prejudice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prejudice), and [favoritism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Favoritism). 

This value is frequently tested by [conflicts of interest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conflict_of_interest). A conflict of interest occurs when an individual is in a position to [exploit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exploitation) a professional capacity for personal benefit—for example, if you are selecting a software vendor for your [company](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Company), and one of the bidding vendors is owned by your [spouse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spouse). In these situations, [proactively](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proactivity) disclosing any real or potential conflicts of interest to stakeholders is a mandatory standard under the value of fairness. [Transparency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transparency_%28behavior%29) neutralizes the conflict.

### 4. Honesty
**[Honesty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Honesty)** is the duty to understand the [truth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Truth) and act in a truthful manner in all [communications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communication) and conduct. 

In project management, truth is usually found in [data](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data). Providing [accurate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accuracy_and_precision) and truthful project status reports is a mandatory standard under the value of honesty. If your project is [three months](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Month) behind schedule and heavily over budget, you cannot [manipulate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manipulation_%28psychology%29) the reporting [dashboard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dashboard_%28business%29) to show "green" status. Withholding negative project information from a project sponsor violates the mandatory ethical standard of honesty.

![Honest project reporting relies on data integrity, which requires both accuracy (reflecting the true status) and precision (consistent measurement).](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Accuracy_and_precision.svg)

Honesty also applies to how you treat your [peers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peer_group). Filing a [frivolous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frivolous_litigation) ethics complaint against a [colleague](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colleague) violates the Project Management Institute Code of Ethics. To protect the [integrity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integrity) of the [profession](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Profession), all formal ethics complaints submitted by a project manager must be fully [substantiated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evidence) by [facts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fact).
