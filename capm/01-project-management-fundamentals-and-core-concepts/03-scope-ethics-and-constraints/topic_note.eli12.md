Imagine trying to build the ultimate, custom treehouse without knowing how much wood you have, how big the treehouse is supposed to be, or the neighborhood rules you have to follow. Project management is a lot like this! It is all about achieving highly specific goals while dealing with strict limits. Every choice you make changes something else. To build your treehouse successfully, you have to understand the limits of your project, map out exactly what you are building, and play by the professional rules.

## The Physics of Project Management: Categorizing Uncertainty

Before you and your friends hammer a single nail, you need to understand the forces around you. A great project manager must know the difference between four very important things: constraints, assumptions, risks, and issues.

### Constraints: The Laws of Physics
Think of constraints like the absolute rules of a video game. A **project constraint** is a known limiting factor that restricts the project team's options during execution. Just like walls in a maze, project constraints dictate the absolute boundaries within which a project team must operate.

> **The Iron Triangle**
> The traditional Iron Triangle of project management constraints includes **project scope, time, and cost**. The most important thing to know about the Iron Triangle is that modifying one project constraint typically forces an impact on at least one other project constraint. 

Let's look at some step-by-step math to see how increasing the scope of a project usually requires an increase in project time or project cost. Imagine you are planning a pizza party for your gaming club:
1. Your starting budget (cost) is \$20.
2. A large pizza costs \$10.
3. With your budget, you can buy exactly 2 pizzas (\$20 / \$10 = 2).
4. You decide to invite 4 more friends, which means you are increasing the "scope" of your party.
5. To feed the extra kids, you need 2 extra pizzas. Your new total is 4 pizzas.
6. Multiply 4 pizzas by \$10 to find your new cost (4 x \$10 = \$40).
7. Your budget just jumped from \$20 to \$40!

While the Iron Triangle is famous, modern project management is a bit bigger. The Project Management Institute identifies six primary project constraints. The six primary project constraints are **scope, schedule, budget, quality, resources, and risks**.

### Assumptions: The Hypotheses
Let's say you assume your best friend is bringing all the hammers to the treehouse build. A **project assumption** is a factor in the planning process that is considered to be true without requiring immediate proof. However, project assumptions carry an inherent degree of risk because the assumptions might prove to be false (what if your friend forgets the hammers?). Because of this, project assumptions must be regularly validated throughout the project lifecycle. If you don't check on them, unvalidated project assumptions can become project risks.

### Risks vs. Issues: Potential vs. Reality
A **project risk** is an uncertain event or condition that can impact project objectives if the event occurs. Risk isn't always a bad thing! Sometimes, a project risk can have a positive effect on project objectives (like finding the paint you wanted on a 50% off sale). Other times, a project risk can have a negative effect on project objectives (like a sudden rainstorm ruining your freshly painted walls). Because we need to watch out for these, project risks must be logged and tracked in a **risk register** document.

An issue is a risk that has actually happened in real life. A **project issue** is a current condition or situation that has already occurred and is impacting project objectives. Simply put, when a project risk materializes into a reality, the risk becomes a project issue. Since you have to deal with them right away, project issues must be logged and tracked in an **issue log** document.

| Concept | What it means in simple terms | Status | Where we track it |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Constraint** | A known limiting factor restricting team options. | Certain / Absolute | Project Management Plan |
| **Assumption** | A factor considered true without immediate proof. | Unproven | Assumption Log |
| **Risk** | An uncertain event impacting objectives. | Potential (Future) | Risk Register |
| **Issue** | A current condition impacting objectives. | Materialized (Current) | Issue Log |

## The Geography of the Project: Navigating Scope

If constraints are the rules of the game, "scope" is your map. Defining your scope is the only way to know when you have finally won the game and finished the project.

### Product Scope vs. Project Scope
We have to separate the "thing" we are building from the "work" we do to build it:
*   **Product scope** refers to the specific features and functions that characterize a final product, service, or result. (For example, the treehouse will have a rope ladder, three windows, and a trapdoor).
*   **Project scope** encompasses all the work performed to deliver a product, service, or result with the specified features. (For example, buying the wood, measuring the boards, sawing, and painting).

### The Scope Baseline and the WBS
To get the work done, you have to write it down clearly. The **project scope statement** documents the entire scope, including deliverables, requirements, and boundaries. When you are critiquing a project scope statement, it involves verifying that all project deliverables have measurable acceptance criteria. (If you can't measure it, how do you know it's done?).

It's also super important to write down what you are *not* doing. A **project exclusion** is an explicit statement of what is deliberately not included in the project scope. For instance, stating "We are NOT building a zip-line." Documenting project exclusions helps manage stakeholder expectations by preventing assumptions about unstated features.

The locked-in, final version of all this planning is called the baseline. The **project scope baseline** consists of the approved project scope statement, the Work Breakdown Structure, and the Work Breakdown Structure dictionary. The **Work Breakdown Structure (WBS)** is a hierarchical decomposition of the total scope of work to be carried out by the project team. That is just a fancy way of saying you take a massive job and break it down into tiny, bite-sized tasks!

### Monsters on the Map: Scope Creep and Gold Plating
Watch out for two bad habits that can wreck your project:
1.  **Scope creep** is the uncontrolled expansion of product or project scope without corresponding adjustments to time, cost, and resources. This usually happens when project changes bypass the formal integrated change control process. It's like adding a slide, then a balcony, then a solar panel to your treehouse without ever asking for more allowance money!
2.  **Gold plating** occurs when project team members intentionally deliver more features than the customer explicitly requested. Maybe you thought it would be cool to paint the treehouse neon green, even though your sibling just wanted plain wood. This is bad because gold plating wastes project resources on unapproved features.

### Reaching the Destination: Control and Validation
While you are doing the work, you use **scope control**, which is the process of monitoring the status of the project scope and managing changes to the scope baseline. 

When you finally finish the treehouse, it's time for **scope validation**. Scope validation is the formalized acceptance of the completed project deliverables by the proper authority. The team doesn't get to grade their own homework! Instead, the project customer or the project sponsor typically performs the formal scope validation process to say, "Yes, this is exactly what I asked for."

## The Rules of Engagement: PMI Code of Ethics

Just like a project has boundaries, a project manager has rules for being a good, honest professional. The Project Management Institute Code of Ethics and Professional Conduct is built on four core values. The four core values of the Project Management Institute Code of Ethics are **responsibility, respect, fairness, and honesty**. 

Saying "I didn't know the rules!" won't get you out of trouble. Ignorance of the Project Management Institute Code of Ethics and Professional Conduct is not a valid excuse for unethical behavior.

### Who Must Comply?
The Code applies to almost everyone in the project world. It applies to:
*   All Project Management Institute members.
*   Non-members who hold a Project Management Institute certification.
*   Non-members who apply to commence a Project Management Institute certification process.
*   Non-members who serve the Project Management Institute in a volunteer capacity.

However, real-world laws always come first. Project managers must obey the laws of the country in which the project managers are operating. The Project Management Institute Code of Ethics and Professional Conduct does not supersede local or national laws.

### Aspirational vs. Mandatory Standards
The rules in the Code are split into two types:
*   **Aspirational standards** in the Code of Ethics represent the optimal ethical behaviors that project managers strive to exhibit. (This is like trying to get an A+ and be the nicest kid in school).
*   **Mandatory standards** in the Code of Ethics represent the minimum acceptable ethical behaviors for project managers. (This means "Do not cheat, or else"). Violating a mandatory standard subjects a practitioner to disciplinary procedures before the Project Management Institute Ethics Review Committee.

Here is how those four core values actually work:

### 1. Responsibility
**Responsibility** is the duty to take ownership of decisions made, actions taken, and the resulting consequences. 

For example, you shouldn't agree to build a rocket ship if you only know how to build a skateboard! A project manager must accept assignments only if the project manager has the appropriate background, experience, skills, and qualifications. 

If someone shares a secret formula with you, a project manager must protect proprietary and confidential information entrusted to the project manager by clients or employers. And if you see someone breaking the rules, you have to speak up. Reporting unethical or illegal conduct to the appropriate authorities is a mandatory standard under the value of responsibility.

### 2. Respect
**Respect** is the duty to show a high regard for oneself, others, and the resources entrusted to the practitioner. 

This means you don't steal other people's ideas. A project manager must explicitly respect the intellectual property rights of others. Also, a project manager must not use the project manager's position or expertise to influence decisions for personal benefit (like using club money to buy yourself a new video game). 

When you are making a deal with someone, negotiating in good faith is an aspirational standard under the ethical value of respect. It means being fair and clear with everyone.

### 3. Fairness
**Fairness** is the duty to make decisions and act impartially and objectively. The ethical value of fairness requires practitioners to remain free from competing self-interest, prejudice, and favoritism. 

Sometimes, you might face a tricky situation. A conflict of interest occurs when an individual is in a position to exploit a professional capacity for personal benefit. For example, if your job is to hire a DJ for the school dance, and your older brother is a DJ who wants the job, that's a conflict! In this case, proactively disclosing any real or potential conflicts of interest to stakeholders is a mandatory standard under the value of fairness. You have to tell the school it's your brother so everything is out in the open.

### 4. Honesty
**Honesty** is the duty to understand the truth and act in a truthful manner in all communications and conduct. 

In project management, this means sharing the real numbers. Providing accurate and truthful project status reports is a mandatory standard under the value of honesty. If your project is out of money, you can't lie and say everything is fine. Withholding negative project information from a project sponsor violates the mandatory ethical standard of honesty.

Honesty also means being truthful about your coworkers. Filing a frivolous ethics complaint against a colleague violates the Project Management Institute Code of Ethics. You can't just try to get someone in trouble because you are mad at them! All formal ethics complaints submitted by a project manager must be fully substantiated by facts.
