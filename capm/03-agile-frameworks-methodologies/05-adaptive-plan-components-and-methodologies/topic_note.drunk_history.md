Now, the most famous crew in this underworld runs a system called Scrum. Scrum is an agile framework that uses timeboxed iterations called Sprints. Think of a Sprint as your absolute window of execution—a Sprint is a timeboxed iteration in Scrum lasting exactly one month or less. Not a day longer, or the feds catch you. You see, Scrum methodology is based on the theory of empiricism. We don’t guess what’s behind the vault door; we crack a window, look inside, and adjust. Before the Sprint even starts, the mastermind pulls out the master hit-list. The Product Backlog in Scrum is an ordered list of what is needed to improve the product. But what if you aren't pulling discrete heists? What if you are running the underworld’s emergency money laundering operation? A chaotic laundry front where dirty cash flows in 24/7? You can't tell a mob boss to wait for a two-week Sprint to wash his money! For this, you need Kanban. Kanban is an agile methodology focused on managing the continuous flow of work. Because the cash never stops, Kanban methodology does not require fixed timeboxes or fixed length iterations. Instead, the crew uses a massive wall monitor: a Kanban board visually depicts workflow stages and the work items currently in the system—bags coming in, bags being scrubbed, bags going to offshore accounts. But if you pile fifty bags of cash onto one accountant's desk, they will have a nervous breakdown. To stop this, you enforce strict rules. Work-in-Progress limits in Kanban restrict the maximum number of work items allowed in a specific workflow stage. By doing this, Work-in-Progress limits are used in Kanban to prevent bottlenecks. The accountant only gets three bags at a time! This means Kanban operates as a pull system. Instead of the mob boss shoving money down their throats, a pull system draws work into the next stage only when capacity is available. The accountant finishes a bag, holds up their hand, and *pulls* the next one. Smooth, unbreakable flow.
