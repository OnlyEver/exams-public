Imagine you are building a giant Lego spaceship without a manual. If you plan every single brick before you start, you might realize halfway through that the wings are too heavy and the whole thing is falling apart. If you stubbornly stick to your first drawing, you end up with a broken toy. 

Making video games, planning school events, or starting a new club are like building that spaceship. Things change! Because of this, **adaptive planning focuses on iterative refinement rather than strict adherence to an initial baseline**. This means you make a plan, try a little bit of it, and keep tweaking it step-by-step, rather than forcing yourself to stick to your very first guess. Because teams are always learning, **adaptive methodologies welcome changing requirements even late in the development cycle**. If you decide to give your video game hero a jetpack right before the game is finished, that is totally fine! You just adjust your plan and keep moving forward.

## The Currency and Calculus of Adaptive Work

Before we talk about specific team rules, we need to know how these teams measure their chores and tasks. 

Really huge tasks are called **Epics**. Imagine you want to "Create a completely new racing video game." That is an Epic. **Epics in agile planning are large bodies of work that must be broken down into smaller user stories.** You cannot build a whole game in one day, so you chop it up. 

When you chop it up, you get a **user story**, which is **a brief description of a product feature written from the perspective of the end user**. Instead of writing complicated computer instructions, you write a simple sentence like a player would say it: "As a player, I want to be able to drift my car around corners so I can win races faster." 

How do teams know how hard a user story is? They don't use hours or days. Instead, they use **story points**. **Story points are a relative unit of measure used in agile estimating to gauge the effort required to implement a user story.** If making the car honk its horn is 2 points, making the car drift might be 4 points because it is twice as hard! 

Over time, a team figures out its **velocity**, which is **an agile metric that measures the amount of work a team successfully completes during a single iteration** (an iteration is just a round of work). 

Here is how you calculate a team's velocity using step-by-step math:
1. Look at the point values for the user stories the team finished this round. Let's say Story A is worth 3 points, Story B is worth 2 points, and Story C is worth 5 points.
2. Add up all the completed story points. 3 + 2 + 5 = 10.
3. The total is 10. Therefore, the team's velocity for this round is 10 story points!

To track if they are winning or falling behind, teams draw two kinds of charts:
*   **Burndown Chart:** This is a graph that **visually tracks the amount of work remaining against time in an agile iteration**. Think of it like a countdown timer. The line goes down, down, down until there is zero work left.
*   **Burnup Chart:** This is a graph that **visually tracks the amount of work completed against time in an agile project**. Think of this like your high score. The line goes up, up, up as you finish more tasks.

## Scrum: The Engine of Empiricism

If you meet an agile team, they are probably using Scrum. **Scrum is an agile framework that uses timeboxed iterations called Sprints.** "Timeboxed" just means you set a strict timer for the work. 

At its heart, **Scrum methodology is based on the theory of empiricism**. Empiricism is a big word that simply means learning by doing and seeing what happens, rather than just guessing. **Empiricism in Scrum relies on the three pillars of transparency, inspection, and adaptation.** That means everyone is honest about their work (transparency), everyone checks the work carefully (inspection), and everyone is willing to change the plan if something isn't working (adaptation).

### The Scrum Team
In Scrum, there are no bossy managers. The team is broken into three simple roles:
1.  **The Product Owner:** This is the person who decides what is most important. **The Product Owner in Scrum maximizes the value of the product resulting from the work of the Scrum Team**. They make the final choice on what features the team builds next.
2.  **The Scrum Master:** This is like the team's coach or referee. **The Scrum Master in Scrum establishes the Scrum framework as defined in the Scrum Guide**. They make sure everyone follows the rules and helps remove any roadblocks.
3.  **Developers:** These are the creators. **Developers in Scrum are the individuals who create the usable Increment each Sprint.** They are the artists, coders, or builders doing the actual work.

### The Artifacts
Scrum teams have three main tools, called artifacts, to keep their work organized:
*   **The Product Backlog:** This is an **ordered list of what is needed to improve the product**. It is basically a giant wish list. It is an **emergent artifact that constantly evolves over time**, meaning new ideas are added to it every single day as the team learns more.
*   **The Sprint Backlog:** This is the to-do list for just the current round of work. **The Sprint Backlog in Scrum contains the Sprint Goal** (the main objective). It also **contains the selected Product Backlog items for the Sprint**, and it **contains an actionable delivery plan** (how they will get it done).
*   **The Increment:** This is what you actually made! **An Increment in Scrum is a concrete stepping stone toward the Product Goal.** It must be completely finished and ready to use, like a fully playable level in a game.

### The Events
Scrum has a steady heartbeat of five events. The biggest one is the **Sprint**, which is **a timeboxed iteration in Scrum lasting exactly one month or less**. All the other events happen *inside* the Sprint.

1.  **Sprint Planning:** This is the kickoff meeting. **Sprint Planning in Scrum lays out the work to be performed for the upcoming iteration.**
2.  **Daily Scrum:** This is a super quick morning huddle. **The Daily Scrum is a fifteen minute daily event for the Developers of the Scrum Team to synchronize activities** and make sure they are on track for the day.
3.  **Sprint Review:** At the very end of the timer, it's show-and-tell. **The Sprint Review in Scrum inspects the Sprint outcome to determine future adaptations.** You show off the new game level to your friends to see if it's fun.
4.  **Sprint Retrospective:** After the show-and-tell, the team talks about themselves. **The Sprint Retrospective in Scrum concludes the Sprint.** Instead of talking about the product, **the Sprint Retrospective in Scrum focuses on ways to increase team quality and effectiveness**. It's when you say, "Next time, let's communicate better so we finish faster."

## Kanban: The Physics of Flow

Scrum works on a strict timer. But what if you work at a fast-food drive-thru? You can't tell a customer, "Wait for our next two-week Sprint to get your burger!" For work that never stops, teams use Kanban. 

**Kanban is an agile methodology focused on managing the continuous flow of work.** Because it deals with a steady stream of tasks, **Kanban methodology does not require fixed timeboxes or fixed length iterations**. 

To keep track of everything, teams use a **Kanban board**, which **visually depicts workflow stages and the work items currently in the system**. Imagine a whiteboard divided into columns: "To Do," "Doing," and "Done." Sticky notes move across the board as work is finished.

The secret to Kanban is that it acts like a magnet. **Kanban operates as a pull system**, which means **a pull system draws work into the next stage only when capacity is available**. You only pull a new chore from the list when you have totally finished your last chore. 

To make sure nobody gets overwhelmed, Kanban uses strict rules. **Work-in-Progress limits in Kanban restrict the maximum number of work items allowed in a specific workflow stage.** For example, you might make a rule that only 3 sticky notes are allowed in the "Doing" column at once. Because of this rule, **Work-in-Progress limits are used in Kanban to prevent bottlenecks** (traffic jams where too much work piles up and nothing gets finished).

## Extreme Programming (XP): Engineering for Quality

Scrum tells you how to organize your team, but it doesn't tell you how to write good computer code. That is where Extreme Programming (XP) comes in. **Extreme Programming is an agile software development framework focused on improving software quality and responsiveness to changing requirements.** It takes good habits and turns them up to the maximum level! Here are the XP rules you need to know:

*   **Test-Driven Development:** This is like writing the grading rubric before you write your essay. It **requires developers to write automated tests before writing the functional code**, ensuring the code is perfect from the very start.
*   **Pair Programming:** Like two players playing a co-op game on one screen, this **involves two developers working together collaboratively at a single workstation**. One types while the other watches for mistakes.
*   **Continuous Integration:** This **requires developers to frequently merge code changes into a central repository**. It's like saving your video game progress online multiple times a day so everyone is playing the same updated version.
*   **Refactoring:** This is cleaning up! It **involves restructuring existing code to improve design without changing external behavior**. It’s like organizing your messy closet so things are easier to find, without throwing away any of your clothes.
*   **Collective Code Ownership:** Everyone shares everything. This **allows any team member to modify any part of the project code at any time**. 
*   **Small Releases:** This practice means you ensure **frequent delivery of working software to gather quick feedback from users**. You let people play a tiny piece of the game early so they can tell you if it's fun.
*   **Sustainable Pace:** You can't run a marathon at a sprinting speed. This rule **dictates that team members should not work excessive overtime to maintain high quality outputs**. Sleepy programmers make messy code, so resting is part of the job!

## Scaled Agile Framework (SAFe): Orchestrating the Enterprise

Scrum, Kanban, and XP are awesome for a small group of 5 to 10 friends. But what if you are a massive company trying to build an actual rocket ship with hundreds of people? A Daily Scrum with 200 people would take hours!

To fix this, massive companies use SAFe. **The Scaled Agile Framework is a set of workflow patterns intended to guide enterprises in scaling agile practices beyond a single team.** 

Instead of one team, SAFe creates a giant super-team. **An Agile Release Train in the Scaled Agile Framework is a long-lived team of agile teams that incrementally develops solutions.** Because it combines lots of small groups, **an Agile Release Train typically consists of fifty to one hundred twenty five individuals**. 

To keep this massive super-team moving in the right direction, you need a head coach. **The Release Train Engineer in the Scaled Agile Framework serves as the chief Scrum Master for the Agile Release Train.** 

Just like a small team has a Sprint, the giant super-team has a massive timer called a Program Increment. **A Program Increment in the Scaled Agile Framework is a timebox during which an Agile Release Train delivers incremental value.** Because it takes a long time for 125 people to finish big parts of a rocket ship, **a Program Increment in the Scaled Agile Framework typically lasts eight to twelve weeks**.

To make sure everyone agrees on what to build over those weeks, they hold a huge kickoff party. **Program Increment Planning is a cadence-based event that aligns all the teams on an Agile Release Train to a shared mission.**
