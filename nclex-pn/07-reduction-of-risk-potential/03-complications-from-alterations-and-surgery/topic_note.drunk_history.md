And what if the tenant didn't even make it to the main apartment? What if the embryo unpacked its bags in the narrow hallway of the fallopian tube? That's an ectopic pregnancy. Because that tiny hallway isn't built for a couch and a TV, an ectopic pregnancy typically presents with unilateral sharp lower abdominal pain—just on one side, where the hallway is stretching—and it can present with early vaginal spotting because the walls are already taking damage. 

By the middle of the lease, we have to do an audit on the sugar metabolism, which is why gestational diabetes screening typically occurs between 24 and 28 weeks of gestation. But the real nightmare is when the maternal plumbing suddenly turns into a high-pressure geyser. We’re talking about preeclampsia. The pipes spasm, the pressure skyrockets, and the filters break. That’s why new-onset hypertension after 20 weeks of gestation is a primary characteristic of preeclampsia, and because those broken kidney filters are leaking the good stuff, proteinuria after 20 weeks of gestation is a characteristic finding in this exact same condition. 

Because the pipes are leaking protein, the water follows it straight out into the tissues. Suddenly, your patient looks like they lost a boxing match with a bee colony. Swelling of the face and hands during pregnancy is a massive clinical indicator of potential preeclampsia. And Brenda, if that fluid backs up into the literal penthouse—the brain—it swells! If that hostile takeover moves to the liver, swelling its capsule until it screams, the patient will have epigastric pain during pregnancy, which is a potential sign of HELLP syndrome. 

Now, let's look at the baby's personal swimming pool. The amniotic fluid. If the pool is draining dry, oligohydramnios is a condition characterized by a lower than normal volume of amniotic fluid. Okay, take a sip of your drink, because we are moving from the uterus up to the penthouse safe. The Skull. The brain is the mob boss of the body, and it lives in a rigid bone box with absolutely no room for expansion. 

When the boss gets squeezed by bleeding or swelling, the pressure in the box goes up. We call this increased intracranial pressure, and before the boss's vital signs crash, he just gets a little loopy. An altered level of consciousness is often the earliest sign of increased intracranial pressure! To check on the boss, we use a performance review called the Glasgow Coma Scale. It's incredibly strict. The Glasgow Coma Scale evaluates eye opening to assess neurologic status. The Glasgow Coma Scale evaluates verbal response to assess neurologic status. And the Glasgow Coma Scale evaluates motor response to assess neurologic status. If the boss is failing his review, a decrease in the Glasgow Coma Scale score indicates a deterioration in neurologic status. 

You have to do a focused neurologic assessment constantly. Assessing orientation to person and place is a standard component of evaluating neurologic status, but you also have to look at the windows to the brain: the eyes! A focused neurologic assessment includes checking the pupillary response to light. If the nerve wrapping around the brainstem gets crushed by the pressure, the security cameras malfunction. Sluggish pupillary changes indicate potential neurologic compromise, and asymmetrical pupillary changes—one pupil blown wide open, the other tiny—indicate potential neurologic compromise. 

We also test the boss's soldiers! The nurse assesses the strength of hand grasps during a focused neurologic exam, and the nurse assesses the strength of foot pushes. If someone ordered a hit on one side of the brain, like a clot blocking blood flow, you'll see half the body go rogue. New-onset unilateral weakness is a symptom of a possible stroke! If the communication hub takes a bullet, aphasia is a neurological deficit involving the inability to understand or express speech. They can't take orders, they can't give orders. Total blackout. 

If the brain damage is so severe that upper management is completely offline, primitive reflexes from infancy come back. A positive Babinski reflex in an adult is an abnormal finding indicating upper motor neuron damage—if you stroke the bottom of their foot and their toes fan backward, the big boss is no longer in charge. The bodyguards then take over with extreme posturing. Decorticate posturing involves abnormal flexion of the upper extremities towards the core—they pull their arms to their chest to protect the heart. Worse is when the brainstem itself is dying: Decerebrate posturing involves abnormal extension of the upper extremities, pushing away out of the body. 

If the pressure isn't fixed, the boss makes one final, desperate stand to force blood up into the crushed safe. This is Cushing's triad, and Cushing's triad is a late sign of increased intracranial pressure! It has three terrifying parts: a widening pulse pressure (the systolic number shoots to the moon while the diastolic stays put), bradycardia (the crushing weight on the brainstem drops the heart rate to a crawl), and irregular respirations (the breathing control center is completely glitching out). 

Oh, and if your patient just took a baseball bat to the skull? Watch their nose! Clear drainage from the nose after head trauma may indicate a cerebrospinal fluid leak. The boss's private stash is leaking out! How do we know it's not just a runny nose? We also have to train them for the aftermath *before* we cut them. Preoperative client education includes demonstrating the use of an incentive spirometer, because when they wake up in agony, they won't want to learn how to blow into a plastic tube. We also wrap their legs up; sequential compression devices are applied preoperatively or postoperatively to prevent venous stasis, squeezing the legs so the blood doesn't curdle into a clot. 

Once they get to the recovery room, the engine is sputtering. Postoperative monitoring includes assessing vital signs every 15 minutes during the initial recovery phase. We need to warm them up, because hypothermia in the immediate postoperative period can prolong recovery from anesthesia—a cold engine won't turn over. But if the engine suddenly catches on fire? That’s a nightmare genetic glitch. Malignant hyperthermia is a severe reaction to certain anesthetic agents characterized by a rapid increase in body temperature. They are literally cooking from the inside! You have to immediately know that Dantrolene is the primary medication used to treat malignant hyperthermia. It shuts off the hypermetabolic furnace. 

Before you give that groggy patient a sip of ginger ale, hold up! Their throat might still be paralyzed. The nurse must assess the client's gag reflex before allowing oral intake after procedures involving sedation. 

Now, let's watch for the leaks. We just sliced open a bunch of pipes. If they are bleeding internally, the heart panics and pumps faster to compensate for the missing volume, meaning tachycardia in the postoperative period is an early sign of potential hemorrhage.
