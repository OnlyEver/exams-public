## Part 1: The Incubation Chamber (Prenatal Complications)

Pregnancy is basically a nine-month project where the body builds a new player from scratch using an amazing fluid and life-support system. Mom's body has to change really fast. When the parts of this system start glitching, the body gives us specific, obvious clues. 

### Early System Errors
In the beginning, mom's body is just trying to get used to new chemicals and set up the connection. Feeling a little sick to your stomach is normal early on. But, if she keeps throwing up all the time, **persistent severe vomiting past the first trimester indicates hyperemesis gravidarum**. The engine is basically throwing up its own fuel, which puts mom at risk of drying out (dehydration). 

Also, where is the baby growing? Sometimes the fertilized egg gets stuck in the pipes (the fallopian tubes) instead of making it to the main womb. When this happens, it is an **ectopic pregnancy, which typically presents with unilateral sharp lower abdominal pain** (a really sharp pain on just one side of her lower belly). Because those tubes aren't stretchy enough to hold a growing baby, an **ectopic pregnancy can present with early vaginal spotting** (a little bit of bleeding). 

### Pressure and Protein: The Preeclampsia Spectrum
Around the middle of the pregnancy, mom's blood pipes are working extra hard. We have to test how her body handles sugar fuel, so **gestational diabetes screening typically occurs between 24 and 28 weeks of gestation.** 

Let's do a quick math check! If pregnancy is 40 weeks total, when does the 24-week screening happen?
Step 1: Divide 24 by 4 to find the number of months.
Step 2: 24 / 4 = 6.
Step 3: So the screening happens right around the 6-month mark!

The biggest danger to the blood pipes is a glitch called preeclampsia. 

> **The Preeclampsia Rule of Thumb:** 
> When mom's blood pipes squeeze too tight, the pressure skyrockets, and her filters (the kidneys) start leaking. This means **new-onset hypertension after 20 weeks of gestation is a primary characteristic of preeclampsia** (hypertension just means high blood pressure). Because the kidney filters are leaking, **proteinuria after 20 weeks of gestation is a characteristic finding in preeclampsia** (proteinuria means we find protein in her pee).

Because the pipes are leaky, water escapes into her skin. Therefore, **swelling of the face and hands during pregnancy is a clinical indicator of potential preeclampsia**. But what about her brain? The high blood pressure causes her brain to swell up a little bit. If she complains of a **severe headache unresponsive to medication during pregnancy, that is a warning sign of preeclampsia**! This swelling also squeezes the eye wires, meaning **visual disturbances such as blurred vision or seeing spots are symptoms of preeclampsia**. 

If this high-pressure damage spreads to her liver, it makes the liver stretch like a balloon. She might feel **epigastric pain during pregnancy, which is a potential sign of HELLP syndrome** (a super severe form of the glitch, and epigastric means pain right under the ribs).

### The Placenta and The Fluid
The baby lives in a protective swimming pool of water, fed by a power cord called the placenta. If the pool doesn't have enough water, we call it **oligohydramnios, which is a condition characterized by a lower than normal volume of amniotic fluid**. If the pipes back up and there is an **excessive accumulation of amniotic fluid, it is called polyhydramnios** (way too much water). The pool can only hold so much; a **sudden gush of fluid from the vagina before 37 weeks gestation indicates premature rupture of membranes** (the water balloon popped too early). 

If mom tells you the baby has stopped bouncing and kicking, take it super seriously. **Decreased fetal movement reported by the pregnant client requires immediate fetal heart rate monitoring.** We have to listen to the baby's heartbeat right away!

And what if the baby is just built super big? **Fetal macrosomia increases the risk of birth trauma during vaginal delivery.** The physics simply don't allow a safe exit without a higher risk of bumps or bruises.

Let's look at the power cord (the placenta).
*   **Placenta Previa:** The placenta has grown over the exit door. As the door stretches to open, it tears the cord. Thus, **vaginal bleeding during pregnancy is a potential clinical sign of placenta previa**. 
*   **Placental Abruption:** The placenta rips away from the inner wall of the womb too early. This hurts a lot. **Severe continuous abdominal pain during pregnancy can indicate a placental abruption**, and just like previa, **vaginal bleeding during pregnancy is a potential clinical sign of placental abruption**.

## Part 2: The Control Board (Neurologic Status)

The human brain is the main computer chip of the body, locked inside a hard bone helmet (the skull). It has absolutely no room to expand. If it bleeds or swells, the pressure inside that helmet goes way up. We call this Increased Intracranial Pressure.

How do you, the mechanic, know the pressure is rising? You do a focused check-up on their nerves and brain. 

**Assessing orientation to person and place is a standard component of evaluating neurologic status.** You just ask them who they are and where they are! The absolute biggest rule of brain checks is this: **an altered level of consciousness is often the earliest sign of increased intracranial pressure.** Before their heart rate gets weird, before their breathing changes, the patient just gets a little confused, sleepy, or zoned out. Always pay attention to that!

### The Glasgow Coma Scale
To measure brain power like a video game score, we use the Glasgow Coma Scale. It's super simple. 
*   **The Glasgow Coma Scale evaluates eye opening to assess neurologic status.** 
*   **The Glasgow Coma Scale evaluates verbal response to assess neurologic status** (talking). 
*   **The Glasgow Coma Scale evaluates motor response to assess neurologic status** (moving). 
*   **A decrease in the Glasgow Coma Scale score indicates a deterioration in neurologic status** (meaning if the score drops, they are getting worse). 

Let's do a step-by-step math check on a score drop!
Step 1: The highest (best) score you can get is 15.
Step 2: Let's say a patient scores a 14 in the morning, but by the afternoon their score drops by 5 points.
Step 3: 14 - 5 = 9.
Step 4: Since a score of 9 is way lower, this tells the nurse the patient's brain is in trouble!

### Windows to the Brain: Pupils and Movement
When you shine a flashlight into someone's eyes, you are testing a wire that wraps right around the brain. **A focused neurologic assessment includes checking the pupillary response to light.** If the brain is swollen, it squishes that wire, and the dark circles in the eyes (pupils) won't shrink properly. **Sluggish pupillary changes indicate potential neurologic compromise** (they shrink super slowly), and **asymmetrical pupillary changes indicate potential neurologic compromise** (one stays big while the other gets small). 

What about checking the body's wires? In a focused check, **the nurse assesses the strength of hand grasps** (squeezing your fingers) and **the nurse assesses the strength of foot pushes** (pushing their feet like gas pedals). If a blood pipe in the brain gets blocked, parts of the body lose power. **New-onset unilateral weakness is a symptom of a possible stroke** (sudden weakness on just one side of the body). If the language center of the brain loses power, you'll see **aphasia, which is a neurological deficit involving the inability to understand or express speech** (they suddenly can't talk or understand words).

If the brain takes major damage, old baby reflexes come back. **A positive Babinski reflex in an adult is an abnormal finding indicating upper motor neuron damage.** (If you tickle the bottom of a grown-up's foot, their toes should curl down. If they fan wide open, that's a positive Babinski, and it means the main brain wires are hurt!)

As the brain gets crushed by pressure, the body curls up weirdly:
1.  **Decorticate posturing involves abnormal flexion of the upper extremities towards the core.** (They tightly curl their arms straight in toward their chest).
2.  **Decerebrate posturing involves abnormal extension of the upper extremities.** (This is worse; they push their arms stiffly straight down by their sides).

### Cushing's Triad: The Final Alarm
If the pressure isn't fixed, the bottom of the brain gets crushed. The body tries to save itself by blasting blood up into the head with crazy force. This sets off a terrifying alarm system. 

> **Cushing's triad is a late sign of increased intracranial pressure.** It consists of three classic clues:
> 1.  **A widening pulse pressure is a classic component of Cushing's triad.** (The top blood pressure number shoots super high, and the bottom one stays low).
> 2.  **Bradycardia is one of the three classic components of Cushing's triad.** (The heart rate slows way, way down).
> 3.  **Irregular respirations constitute one of the three classic components of Cushing's triad.** (Their breathing gets choppy and weird).

*Note on bumps to the head:* If your patient smashed their head and their nose is running, be careful! **Clear drainage from the nose after head trauma may indicate a cerebrospinal fluid leak** (brain juice is leaking!). How do we check? We look for a special ring. **The halo test detects the presence of cerebrospinal fluid in bloody drainage from the ear or nose** (if you catch the drip on a tissue, a yellow ring will form around the red blood).

## Part 3: Controlled Trauma (Perioperative Care)

Surgery is like taking the car into the shop to fix a broken part. We have to carefully cut, fix, and use sleep medicine, and then it's our job to help the body put itself back together. 

### Before the Cut: Preoperative Care
Before we even think about rolling the bed to the operating room, we run down our **preoperative checklist, which ensures all necessary preparations are completed before surgery**. 

1.  **Permission Slip:** We don't touch anyone until **preoperative care includes verifying the presence of signed informed consent in the medical record.** 
2.  **Safety First:** Never guess who the patient is. **The nurse must verify the preoperative client's identity using two identifiers before transferring to the operating room** (like asking for their name and birthday).
3.  **Fighting Bugs:** **Administering prophylactic antibiotics within one hour prior to surgical incision reduces surgical site infections.** (Giving germ-fighting medicine right before we cut keeps infections away).
4.  **Airway Protection:** When someone is fully asleep, their stomach valve relaxes. If they have a belly full of pizza, it could come up and go into their lungs! Therefore, **the nurse must ensure the preoperative client has been NPO for the prescribed time prior to surgery** (NPO means nothing to eat or drink). This is because **NPO status before surgery reduces the risk of aspiration during the administration of anesthesia** (aspiration means breathing food or liquid into the lungs).
5.  **Practice for Later:** Don't teach a patient how to use breathing toys when they are waking up sore! **Preoperative client education includes demonstrating the use of an incentive spirometer** (a cool plastic tube you blow into). We also set up leg-squeezing sleeves because **sequential compression devices are applied preoperatively or postoperatively to prevent venous stasis** (preventing blood from sitting still in the legs).

### The Immediate Aftermath: The PACU
When the patient rolls out of surgery, their engine is sputtering. They are cold, sleepy, and healing. 
*  **Postoperative monitoring includes assessing vital signs every 15 minutes during the initial recovery phase** (checking their pulse and breathing constantly). 
*   Keep them warm! **Hypothermia in the immediate postoperative period can prolong recovery from anesthesia** (being too cold means it takes them way longer to wake up). 
*   Watch out for a super rare body glitch: **Malignant hyperthermia is a severe reaction to certain anesthetic agents characterized by a rapid increase in body temperature.** Their engine gets way too hot, way too fast! How do we stop it? **Dantrolene is the primary medication used to treat malignant hyperthermia.**

What about letting them drink water? Before you give them a sip, remember their throat might still be asleep. **The nurse must assess the client's gag reflex before allowing oral intake after procedures involving sedation** (if they can't swallow properly, they could choke!).

### Watching for the Leaks: Hemorrhage and Fluid Dynamics
We just fixed parts inside them. Are the patches holding? If the patient starts bleeding on the inside, the heart will panic because the pipes are emptying. It will pump super fast to try and keep the engine running. Therefore, **tachycardia in the postoperative period is an early sign of potential hemorrhage** (tachycardia means a really fast heart rate). As they lose more fluid, the pressure drops—meaning **hypotension in the postoperative period is an early sign of potential hemorrhage** (hypotension means low blood pressure).

On the outside, **the nurse monitors the surgical dressing for excessive bloody drainage during the postoperative period** (we check their bandages!). Often, the doctor leaves a little squishy bulb behind to help drain extra fluid: **A Jackson-Pratt drain uses negative pressure to remove excess fluid from a surgical site** (it acts like a tiny vacuum). 

Don't forget the kidney filters! They tell you if the body has enough water. **A urine output of less than 30 milliliters per hour postoperatively indicates potential fluid volume deficit** (they are running dry!). 

Let's check a patient's pee math!
Step 1: The patient pees 100 milliliters into their bag over a 4-hour period.
Step 2: Divide 100 by 4 to find out how many milliliters they pee in one hour.
Step 3: 100 / 4 = 25 milliliters per hour.
Step 4: Since 25 is less than 30, this patient is running dry and might need more fluids!

Also, make sure blood is reaching their toes and fingers: **checking peripheral pulses distal to a surgical site assesses vascular compromise.** (If you had knee surgery, you better have a strong heartbeat down in your foot!).

### The Lungs and The Legs (Atelectasis and DVT)
When a patient has a sore belly from surgery, they take tiny, shallow breaths. The little air balloons at the bottom of their lungs flatten out. **Postoperative atelectasis presents with diminished breath sounds** (their breathing sounds super quiet because the lung balloons are flat). 

How do we puff them back up? **Deep breathing and coughing exercises help clear respiratory secretions after surgery** (it blows out all the gunk!). But coughing hurts! So, **instructing the client to splint the abdominal incision with a pillow reduces pain during coughing exercises** (hugging a pillow tight to the belly feels much better). Furthermore, **postoperative pain management promotes client participation in breathing exercises** (if you give them good medicine so they aren't hurting, they will happily do their breathing exercises for you).

| Post-Op Complication | The Physiology | The Nursing Intervention/Sign |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Atelectasis** | Air balloons flatten out due to tiny, shallow breathing. | **Early ambulation after surgery decreases the incidence of postoperative atelectasis.** (Walking around forces them to take deep breaths!). |
| **Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT)** | Blood gets stuck in the legs from lying still in bed. | **Unilateral leg swelling is a potential clinical sign of a deep vein thrombosis.** (One leg gets big and puffy). Treat with **early ambulation after surgery reduces the risk of deep vein thrombosis** (walking keeps blood moving!). |
| **Pulmonary Embolism** | A clump of blood from the leg breaks off and shoots into the lungs. | **A sudden onset of shortness of breath postoperatively may indicate a pulmonary embolism.** (They suddenly can't breathe!). |
| **Paralytic Ileus** | The tummy and intestines "go to sleep" and stop squeezing food through after belly surgery. | **Absent bowel sounds 24 hours after abdominal surgery can indicate a paralytic ileus.** (You don't hear any gurgles in their tummy!). |

### The Worst Case Scenario: The Incision Fails
Sometimes, the stitches don't hold tight enough. If the patient coughs too hard and the cut pops open like a broken zipper, that is **postoperative wound dehiscence, which is the separation of surgical incision edges**. 

But what if it's way worse? What if it pops wide open and their insides spill out? That is a **postoperative wound evisceration, which involves the protrusion of internal organs through a surgical incision.** This is a really scary moment, but you are a brilliant mechanic and you know exactly what to do: **A client with a surgical evisceration requires immediate coverage of the protruding organs with sterile saline-soaked dressings.** You just cover them up with clean, wet bandages to keep them safe until the doctor gets there!
