# The Engine of Life: A Masterclass on Vital Signs and Laboratory Values

Hello! If you are learning how to take care of patients, you have probably seen huge lists of numbers to memorize. It might look like a boring math test, but it is actually really cool! 

Think of the human body like a super-advanced video game console. **Vital signs** are like the health bar and stamina meters right on your TV screen. **Laboratory values** are like looking at the secret code behind the game to see exactly how much battery power or memory is left. 

When you understand why the body gives us these numbers, you don't have to just memorize them. You will know exactly what is going on inside your patient. Let's look at how the body stays alive and what to do when the numbers change!

---

## Part 1: The Dashboard (Vital Signs)

When you play a game, you need to know your starting score so you can tell if you are winning or losing. In the hospital, a baseline vital sign measurement serves as the reference point for all subsequent vital sign assessments for a specific patient. It tells you what is "normal" for that person.

Why do we care about the starting score? Because a significant deviation from a patient's baseline vital signs indicates a potential physiological change requiring intervention. That means if the numbers suddenly jump way up or drop way down, you need to step in and help! 

Also, do not forget to ask how the patient feels. Pain assessment is considered the fifth vital sign in routine patient monitoring. If a person is in pain, their body's engine will start acting differently.

### 1. Temperature: The Engine Heat

Just like a computer can get hot when it runs heavy graphics, our bodies make heat. 
* Normal adult oral temperature ranges from 36.5 to 37.5 degrees Celsius (97.7 to 99.5 degrees Fahrenheit).

Sometimes we check temperature inside the ear using a special thermometer. For this to work like a laser pointer hitting a target, the tympanic thermometer must be aimed toward the patient's tympanic membrane (the eardrum) to obtain an accurate temperature reading. 

Why do we care if the body gets too hot? Because an elevated body temperature increases the body's metabolic demand for oxygen. Think of it like a car driving faster—it burns up its fuel and needs more air right away!

### 2. The Pump: Heart Rate & Pulses

Your heart is a pump that pushes blood around the body. 
* A normal adult resting heart rate is between 60 and 100 beats per minute.
* Babies have tiny, fast little hearts! A normal resting heart rate for a newborn ranges from 110 to 160 beats per minute.

Sometimes the heart beats way faster than normal, which is called tachycardia. This is actually the body's smart backup plan! 
* Tachycardia acts as an early compensatory mechanism for hypoxia. This means if the body does not have enough oxygen, the heart beats faster to deliver whatever oxygen it *does* have.
* Tachycardia acts as an early compensatory mechanism for hypovolemia. This means if there isn't enough blood or fluid in the body (like a leaky water balloon), the heart spins faster to keep the pressure up.

**Checking the Pulse:**
Usually, we feel the pulse at the wrist (the radial pulse). But if the heartbeat feels bumpy or uneven, an irregular radial pulse requires the nurse to assess the apical pulse for one full minute. 

Where is that? You listen right over the heart! The apical pulse is assessed by auscultating (listening to) the heart at the fifth intercostal space at the midclavicular line. That just means you count down five spaces between the ribs, right below the middle of the collarbone.

Sometimes, the heart beats, but the wave of blood isn't strong enough to reach the wrist. We check for this by finding a **pulse deficit**. The presence of a pulse deficit indicates cardiac arrhythmias (the heart is beating out of rhythm). 

A pulse deficit is calculated by subtracting the radial pulse rate from the apical pulse rate obtained simultaneously. Here is the step-by-step math:
1. Have one nurse listen to the apical pulse rate on the chest (let's say they count 90 beats per minute).
2. Have another nurse feel the radial pulse rate on the wrist at the exact same time (let's say they count 75 beats per minute).
3. Subtract the radial pulse rate from the apical pulse rate (90 - 75 = 15).
4. The answer, 15, is your pulse deficit!

### 3. The Bellows: Respirations & Oxygenation

Your lungs are like the air intake vents on a car.
* A normal adult respiratory rate is between 12 and 20 breaths per minute.
* Just like with their heart rate, babies breathe very fast! A normal respiratory rate for a newborn ranges from 30 to 60 breaths per minute.

We also measure how much oxygen is actually making it into the blood using a cool little finger clip called a pulse oximeter. Normal oxygen saturation measured by pulse oximetry is 95 percent to 100 percent for healthy adults.

### 4. The Pipe Pressure: Blood Pressure

Blood pressure is just how hard the blood is pushing against the walls of your blood vessels, kind of like water pushing inside a garden hose.
* A normal adult systolic blood pressure is less than 120 millimeters of mercury. (This is the top number, when the heart squeezes hard).
* A normal adult diastolic blood pressure is less than 80 millimeters of mercury. (This is the bottom number, when the heart relaxes).

We also look at the gap between those two numbers. Pulse pressure is calculated by subtracting the diastolic blood pressure from the systolic blood pressure. 
Here is how you do the math, step-by-step:
1. Write down the patient's systolic blood pressure (let's say it is 115).
2. Write down the patient's diastolic blood pressure (let's say it is 75).
3. Subtract the diastolic number from the systolic number (115 - 75 = 40).
4. The answer, 40, is the pulse pressure!

A normal pulse pressure ranges from 30 to 50 millimeters of mercury.

**Using the Right Equipment:**
If you wear shoes that are too tight, your feet hurt. Blood pressure cuffs are the same way!
* A falsely elevated blood pressure reading occurs when a blood pressure cuff is too small for the patient's arm. (It squeezes way too tight!).
* A falsely decreased blood pressure reading occurs when a blood pressure cuff is too large for the patient's arm. (It doesn't squeeze quite enough).

**Standing Up Too Fast:**
Have you ever stood up really fast and felt dizzy? Sometimes the blood pressure drops too much when a person stands. This is called orthostatic hypotension. 
* Orthostatic hypotension involves a decrease in systolic blood pressure of 20 millimeters of mercury or more upon standing.
* Orthostatic hypotension involves a decrease in diastolic blood pressure of 10 millimeters of mercury or more upon standing.

## Part 2: The Diagnostic Readout (Laboratory Values)

Lab tests are like checking the oil and battery levels in a car. Sometimes, we get a test back that is super dangerous. A critical laboratory value represents a test result significantly outside the normal range requiring immediate provider notification. This means you don't wait—you call the doctor right away!

### 1. The Complete Blood Count (CBC)

This test looks at the different cells swimming in the blood.

* **White Blood Cells:** These are your body's army that fights off germs. The normal adult white blood cell count ranges from 5000 to 10000 cells per microliter. If the army is big, they are fighting a battle! An elevated white blood cell count typically indicates an ongoing inflammatory or infectious process.
* **Platelets:** These are the repair crew that make scabs so you stop bleeding. The normal adult platelet count ranges from 150000 to 400000 platelets per microliter. If you don't have enough builders, you can bleed too easily. A decreased platelet count increases a patient's risk for spontaneous bleeding.
* **Hemoglobin:** Think of these as tiny backpacks that carry air. Hemoglobin carries oxygen within red blood cells to tissues throughout the body. 
  * The normal adult hemoglobin range for males is 14 to 18 grams per deciliter.
  * The normal adult hemoglobin range for females is 12 to 16 grams per deciliter.
* **Hematocrit:** This measures how crowded the blood is with red cells. Hematocrit represents the percentage of total blood volume composed of red blood cells. 
  * The normal adult hematocrit range for males is 42 percent to 52 percent.
  * The normal adult hematocrit range for females is 37 percent to 47 percent.

### 2. The Basic Metabolic Panel (Electrolytes & Waste)

Electrolytes are like the electrical juice in a battery that helps muscles move.
* **Sodium:** The normal adult range for serum sodium is 135 to 145 milliequivalents per liter.
* **Potassium:** The normal adult range for serum potassium is 3.5 to 5.0 milliequivalents per liter. This one is super important! Elevated serum potassium levels place a patient at high risk for life-threatening cardiac dysrhythmias. (Too much potassium makes the heart's electricity glitch out).
* **Calcium:** The normal adult range for serum calcium is 8.5 to 10.5 milligrams per deciliter.
* **Magnesium:** The normal adult range for serum magnesium is 1.5 to 2.5 milliequivalents per liter.
* **Chloride:** The normal adult range for serum chloride is 98 to 106 milliequivalents per liter.

**Kidney Trash Filters:**
Your kidneys act like trash filters. If they break, the trash builds up.
* The normal adult range for Blood Urea Nitrogen is 10 to 20 milligrams per deciliter.
* The normal adult range for serum creatinine is 0.6 to 1.2 milligrams per deciliter.
* Elevated Blood Urea Nitrogen and serum creatinine levels indicate impaired renal function. (This means the kidney filters are broken).

**Blood Sugar (The Fuel):**
* The normal adult range for fasting blood glucose is 70 to 99 milligrams per deciliter.
* We also have a test that shows an average score over three months. A normal glycosylated hemoglobin level for an adult without diabetes is less than 5.7 percent.

### 3. Coagulation Profiles (The Clotting Timers)

These tests act like stopwatches to see how fast blood turns into a clot. 
* The normal adult range for Prothrombin Time is 11 to 12.5 seconds.
* The normal adult range for activated Partial Thromboplastin Time is 30 to 40 seconds.
* The normal adult range for International Normalized Ratio for a patient not on anticoagulant therapy is 0.8 to 1.1. 
* Sometimes patients take a medicine called warfarin to slow down clotting on purpose. Because of this medicine, the therapeutic International Normalized Ratio for a patient on warfarin therapy typically ranges from 2.0 to 3.0.

### 4. Arterial Blood Gases (The Exhaust Mix)

This shows if the blood is too acidic (like lemon juice) or too basic (like soap).
* The normal arterial blood pH range is 7.35 to 7.45.
* The normal arterial partial pressure of carbon dioxide ranges from 35 to 45 millimeters of mercury.
* The normal arterial bicarbonate level ranges from 22 to 26 milliequivalents per liter.

### 5. Lipid Panel (The Pipe Sludge)

Lipids are fats. Too much fat acts like grease clogging up a sink drain.
* The normal adult range for total serum cholesterol is less than 200 milligrams per deciliter.
* The normal adult range for low-density lipoprotein cholesterol is less than 100 milligrams per deciliter. (This is the bad grease).
* The good cholesterol acts like pipe-cleaners! The normal adult range for high-density lipoprotein cholesterol is greater than 40 milligrams per deciliter for males.
* The normal adult range for high-density lipoprotein cholesterol is greater than 50 milligrams per deciliter for females.
* The normal adult range for serum triglycerides is less than 150 milligrams per deciliter.

### 6. Specialty Biomarkers and Drug Levels

* **Brain Natriuretic Peptide:** If the heart stretches too much, it sends out a signal. The normal adult range for Brain Natriuretic Peptide is less than 100 picograms per milliliter.
* **Digoxin:** This is a heart medicine. The normal adult therapeutic level for digoxin is 0.8 to 2.0 nanograms per milliliter.
* **Lithium:** This is a medicine for the brain. The normal adult therapeutic level for lithium is 0.6 to 1.2 milliequivalents per liter.

## Part 3: The Art and Science of Venipuncture

Getting blood out of a vein is a very careful procedure. You have to do it exactly right!

### 1. Preparation and Patient Safety

Before you start, you have to be totally sure you have the right person. 
* The practical nurse must verify the patient's identity using two unique identifiers prior to performing a venipuncture. (Like asking for their name and birth date).
* You also have to ask permission! A patient must provide informed consent before a blood draw procedure.

### 2. Location and Mechanics

* **Where to Look:** Vein selection for venipuncture should prioritize distal sites on the upper extremities. This just means you start looking lower down on the arms, like near the hands, before moving up. 
* **The Best Spot:** However, the preferred venipuncture site for adults is the median cubital vein located in the antecubital fossa. That is the big, bouncy vein right in the inner crease of your elbow!
* **How to Poke:** You don't want to poke straight down. The needle insertion angle for a standard venipuncture ranges from 15 degrees to 30 degrees. This is like an airplane making a very gentle landing.
* **No Cheating:** If a patient has an IV dripping watery medicine into their arm, you can't take blood from above that spot. Drawing blood from an arm containing a running intravenous infusion dilutes the laboratory sample. (You would just be testing the watery medicine!).

### 3. The Danger of the Tourniquet

A tourniquet is the tight, stretchy rubber band we tie around the arm to make the veins pop up. But you have to be quick!
* A tourniquet should not remain on a patient's arm for more than one minute during a venipuncture procedure.

If you leave it on too long, it traps the blood and squeezes the water out of the vein, leaving all the big stuff behind (like draining the water out of a bowl of soup so only the noodles are left). 
* Prolonged tourniquet application during venipuncture causes hemoconcentration.
* Hemoconcentration artificially elevates the concentration of cells and large molecules in a blood sample. 
* Even worse, the pressure squishes the cells until they leak! Leaving a tourniquet in place for over a minute increases the risk of false hyperkalemia in a blood sample. (This means it looks like potassium is way too high, but it's only because you squished the cells).

### 4. Handling the Tubes

We collect blood in colorful little tubes, and there are strict rules for them!
* **Order Matters:** You can't just pick any color tube first. The correct order of draw prevents cross-contamination of tube additives during venipuncture. For example, blood culture tubes must be drawn first in the order of draw for multiple blood samples to make sure no germs from other tubes get inside.
* **Fill them Up:** Some tubes have medicine in them. Sodium citrate tubes must be completely filled to maintain a specific blood-to-anticoagulant ratio. 
* **Be Gentle!** Red blood cells are like delicate microscopic water balloons. Vigorous shaking of blood collection tubes causes hemolysis of red blood cells. (Hemolysis means the balloons pop!). If they pop, their insides spill out. Hemolysis of red blood cells in a blood sample leads to falsely elevated serum potassium readings. 

### 5. Post-Draw Complications

When you pull the needle out, you have to stop the bleeding. 
* Applying direct pressure to a venipuncture site immediately after needle removal prevents hematoma formation. (A hematoma is a giant blood blister under the skin).
* If you see a bump growing *while* you are drawing blood, stop! A hematoma at a venipuncture site requires immediate removal of the needle and application of firm pressure.
* Later on, you also want to check for angry, irritated veins. Phlebitis presents as localized redness and swelling along the course of a punctured vein.
