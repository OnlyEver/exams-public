## Part 1: The Mathematics of Healing

To safely give medicines, you have to do a little bit of math. It might seem scary, but it's really just like figuring out how many arcade tickets you need to buy a prize! 

To figure out how much medicine to give, you use a super reliable math rule. The basic dosage calculation formula divides the desired dose by the available dose and multiplies the result by the available quantity. 

Let's look at a step-by-step example! Let's say the doctor wants you to give 500 mg of medicine (your desired dose), but your pharmacy only sent you pills that are 250 mg each (your available dose). Let's say 1 pill is your available quantity.

*   **Step 1:** Take your desired dose (500) and divide it by your available dose (250). So, 500 / 250 = 2.
*   **Step 2:** Multiply that result (2) by your available quantity (1 pill). So, 2 x 1 = 2 pills!

But wait! This formula only works if your measurements match. If the doctor asks for grams but you only have milligrams, you have to translate it, just like trading dollar bills for quarters. Here are the measurement rules you have to memorize:

### The Essential Conversions

| Measurement Type | Conversion Fact |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Micro (mcg)** | **One milligram** is equal to **one thousand micrograms**. |
| **Milli (mg)** | **One gram** is equal to **one thousand milligrams**. |
| **Weight** | **One kilogram** is equal to **2.2 pounds**. |
| **Spoons** | **One teaspoon** is equal to **5 milliliters**. |
| **Spoons** | **One tablespoon** is equal to **15 milliliters**. |
| **Fluid Ounce** | **One fluid ounce** is equal to **30 milliliters**. |

If you know these, you can calculate anything!

## Part 2: The Sacred Ritual of Safety

Even with great math, people can still make mistakes. In nursing, mistakes can be really dangerous. To stop errors before they happen, we follow strict rules called the "Rights" of giving medicine.

### Identifying the Target
First, we need the **Right Patient**. You can't just walk into a room and guess who is who! The Right Patient requires verification using two distinct patient identifiers. Acceptable patient identifiers include the patient's **full legal name**. Acceptable patient identifiers include the patient's **date of birth**. Acceptable patient identifiers include the patient's **medical record number**. 

Notice what isn't on that list? Patient room numbers are invalid identifiers for medication administration. People change rooms all the time, just like you might swap seats in a classroom!

### Verification and Timing
Next is the **Right Medication**. The Right Medication involves verifying the medication label against the Medication Administration Record three separate times. You have to be totally sure!

We also have the **Right Time**. The Right Time requires administering time-critical medications within 30 minutes of the scheduled administration time. If you do it too early or too late, the medicine won't work correctly.

Finally, we have the **Right Documentation**. The Right Documentation requires charting medication administration immediately after the patient receives the drug. Don't write it down before they actually take it!

### The Danger Zone: High-Alert Medications and Zeros
Some medicines are so strong that a tiny mistake is a huge deal. High-alert medications require independent double-verification by a second licensed nurse prior to administration. This means two nurses check it together to be completely safe. 
* Insulin is classified as a high-alert medication because it changes blood sugar very fast. 
* Heparin is classified as a high-alert medication because it changes how blood clots. 

How we write numbers is also super important! 
* Trailing zeros in medication orders increase the risk of tenfold dosing errors. If you write "5.0", someone rushing might read it as "50" and give way too much! Because of this, a trailing zero must never be used in medication dosage documentation. 
* On the flip side, leading zeros before a decimal point reduce the risk of missed decimal points in dosing. A leading zero must always be used before a decimal dose of less than one (like writing "0.5").

## Part 3: The Journey of the Pill (Oral & Enteral Routes)

When you swallow a pill, it drops into your stomach, which is full of strong acid that breaks down food.

### The Architecture of a Pill
Some pills have a special shield called an enteric coating. Enteric-coated medications are designed to bypass the acidic stomach environment. Instead, enteric-coated medications are designed to dissolve in the alkaline environment of the small intestine. What happens if you smash it up? Crushing an enteric-coated tablet destroys the protective coating of the medication, ruining its special shield!

We also have extended-release pills. Extended-release capsules deliver medication slowly over a prolonged period. It's like getting your allowance a little bit each week instead of all at once. Crushing an extended-release medication causes immediate absorption of a potentially toxic dose. It gives the body all the medicine at exactly the same time, which is really dangerous!

### The Enteral Tube Challenge
What if a patient eats through a plastic feeding tube? Solid enteral medications must be crushed to a fine powder before feeding tube administration. Once it is powder, crushed enteral medications must be dissolved in 15 to 30 milliliters of sterile water prior to administration. 

To keep the patient from accidentally breathing in their food or medicine, elevating the head of the bed to at least 30 degrees during enteral medication administration reduces aspiration risk. 

Think of the feeding tube like a water slide; you have to keep it clean! Enteral tubes must be flushed with 15 to 30 milliliters of water before medication administration. Also, enteral tubes must be flushed with 15 to 30 milliliters of water after medication administration. Most importantly, flushing enteral tubes with water between different medications prevents chemical incompatibilities. You don't want two different medicines mixing and getting stuck in the plastic tube!

## Part 4: Breaking the Barrier (Injections)

When we need to get medicine past the stomach completely, we use shots (injections).

### Subcutaneous (SQ) Injections
Subcutaneous injections are deposited into the adipose tissue layer beneath the skin. (Adipose is just a fancy science word for fat!). Because this space is small, the maximum medication volume for a single subcutaneous injection is one milliliter. 

Common subcutaneous injection sites include the outer aspect of the upper arms. Common subcutaneous injection sites include the anterior thighs (the front of your legs). Common subcutaneous injection sites include the abdomen (your belly). If you use the belly, the abdomen subcutaneous injection site must be located at least two inches away from the umbilicus (your belly button).

The angle of your needle depends on the person! Subcutaneous injections are administered at a 90-degree angle for patients with adequate adipose tissue. But for super skinny patients, subcutaneous injections are administered at a 45-degree angle for patients with minimal adipose tissue so you don't accidentally poke their muscle!

> **The Heparin Rules**
> When giving Heparin (that strong blood medicine we talked about) in the fat layer, you have two extra rules:
> 1. Aspirating the syringe prior to subcutaneous heparin administration increases the risk of tissue trauma. (Aspirating means pulling back on the plunger—so don't do it!).
> 2. Massaging the injection site after administering subcutaneous heparin increases the risk of hematoma formation (which means a big, painful bruise).

### Intramuscular (IM) Injections
Muscles hold more blood, so medicines go to work faster here. Intramuscular injections are administered at a 90-degree angle, straight in! 

Adult intramuscular needles are typically one to one and a half inches in length. Adult intramuscular needles are typically 20 to 25 gauge in diameter. 

Where do they go? 
* The deltoid muscle can accommodate a maximum injection volume of one milliliter. (That's your shoulder muscle). 
* The ventrogluteal site is the preferred location for large volume intramuscular injections in adults. (That's the hip area). 
* Babies don't walk yet, so their hip muscles are tiny! Because of this, the vastus lateralis is the preferred intramuscular injection site for infants. (That's the outside of their little thigh).

Sometimes medicine can leak out of the muscle. To stop this, we use a cool trick. The Z-track method involves pulling the skin laterally before inserting an intramuscular needle. The Z-track method prevents intramuscular medication from leaking back into the subcutaneous tissue by basically closing a sliding door behind the medicine!

## Part 5: The Surface and Senses (Topical, Eyes, and Ears)

Medicines can also go *on* your body! 

### Topical & Transdermal 
A nurse must wear clean gloves when applying topical medications (like creams or patches). Wearing clean gloves during topical medication application prevents the nurse from absorbing the active drug. You don't want the patient's medicine getting into your own skin and changing how your body feels!

For medicine patches, a nurse must remove an old transdermal patch before applying a new transdermal patch. Also, rotating transdermal patch application sites prevents local skin irritation.

### The Eyes (Ophthalmic)
Your eyes are super delicate! Ophthalmic drops are instilled directly into the lower conjunctival sac (the little pink pocket under your eye). Instilling ophthalmic drops into the lower conjunctival sac prevents direct injury to the cornea (the clear dome over your eye). 

Once the drop is in, you don't want it draining down into your nose. Applying gentle pressure to the nasolacrimal duct (the tear duct area) after eye drop instillation prevents systemic absorption of the drug. 

### The Ears (Otic)
To get ear drops all the way inside, you have to straighten out the ear's tunnel. 
* For a child under three years old, the ear pinna is pulled down and back during otic medication administration. 
* For individuals three years and older, the ear pinna is pulled up and back during otic medication administration. 

Fun fact: the inside of your ear helps you balance. Administering cold otic drops can cause severe dizziness in the patient. To stop them from feeling like they are spinning on a crazy carnival ride, otic drops should be warmed to room temperature prior to administration.
