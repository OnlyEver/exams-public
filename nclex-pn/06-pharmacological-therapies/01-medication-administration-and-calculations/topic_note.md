Welcome to the fascinating, high-stakes world of [medication administration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Route_of_administration). Look, anybody can just follow orders and hand a [pill](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tablet_%28pharmacy%29) to a [patient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patient). But to be an elite [nurse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nursing)—to truly master the craft—you have to understand *why* we do what we do. You have to understand the [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics) of a [syringe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syringe), the [chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemistry) of the [digestive tract](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gastrointestinal_tract), and the simple, profound logic of keeping your patients safe. 

When you administer a [medication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medication), you are introducing a powerful [chemical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_substance) variable into the magnificent, complex system of the [human body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_body). There is no room for guessing. 

So, let's roll up our sleeves, figure out how this works, and get you ready to crush the [NCLEX-PN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NCLEX).

## Part 1: The [Mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics) of [Healing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Healing)

To safely administer [drugs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drug), you have to speak the language of [dosages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dose_%28biochemistry%29). [Math](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics) scares some people, but it shouldn't! It’s really just translating what the [doctor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physician) wants into what you hold in your hands. 

To figure out how much of a [drug](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drug) to give, you just need the universal engine of nursing math. The basic dosage calculation formula [divides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_%28mathematics%29) the desired [dose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dose_%28biochemistry%29) by the available dose and [multiplies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication) the result by the available quantity. It looks like this:

> **The Universal [Dosage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dose_%28biochemistry%29) Formula**
> **(Desired [Dose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dose_%28biochemistry%29) ÷ Available Dose) × Available Quantity = Amount to Administer**

But wait, the formula only works if the *Desired* and *Available* [units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_of_measurement) match! If the doctor orders [grams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gram) but you only have [milligrams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milligram), you have to translate. Here are the fundamental conversion facts of the universe you must memorize:

### The Essential Conversions

| Measurement Type | Conversion Fact |
| :--- | :--- |
| **[Micro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Micro-) (mcg)** | **One [milligram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milligram)** is equal to **one thousand [micrograms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microgram)**. |
| **[Milli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milli-) (mg)** | **One [gram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gram)** is equal to **one thousand [milligrams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milligram)**. |
| **[Weight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weight)** | **One [kilogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kilogram)** is equal to **2.2 [pounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pound_%28mass%29)**. |
| **[Spoons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spoon)** | **One [teaspoon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teaspoon)** is equal to **5 [milliliters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Litre)**. |
| **Spoons** | **One [tablespoon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tablespoon)** is equal to **15 [milliliters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Litre)**. |
| **[Fluid Ounce](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluid_ounce)** | **One [fluid ounce](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluid_ounce)** is equal to **30 [milliliters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Litre)**. |

If you know these, you can [calculate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculation) anything. You just [convert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conversion_of_units) the units to match, plug them into the formula, and boom—you have the exact [volume](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume) or number of [tablets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tablet_%28pharmacy%29) to hand to your patient.

## Part 2: The Sacred Ritual of Safety

Now, even if your math is flawless, humans make mistakes. Our [brains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brain) take shortcuts. In nursing, shortcuts can be fatal. To combat [human error](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_error), we follow stringent rules known as the "Rights" of medication administration.

### Identifying the Target
First, we need the **Right Patient**. You can't just walk into a room and say, "Hey, Bob?" because the guy in the next bed might also be named Bob and simply nod. The Right Patient requires verification using two distinct patient identifiers. Acceptable patient identifiers include the patient's **full legal name**, their **[date of birth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Birthday)**, and their **[medical record number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medical_record)**. 

Notice what isn't on that list? Patient room numbers are invalid identifiers for medication administration. People move rooms all the time! Never rely on [geography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography) to identify [biology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology).

### Verification and Timing
Next is the **Right Medication**. This involves verifying the [medication label](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Label) against the [Medication Administration Record](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medication_administration_record) (MAR) three separate times. Once when you pull it, once when you prepare it, and once right at the bedside before you give it. 

We also have the **Right Time**. The rule here is that the Right Time requires administering time-critical medications within 30 [minutes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minute) of the scheduled administration time. Give it too early or too late, and the chemical levels in the [blood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood) get completely out of whack.

Finally, the **Right Documentation**. The strict rule is that the Right Documentation requires [charting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medical_record) medication administration immediately *after* the patient receives the drug. Not before! If you chart it before, and the patient drops the pill on the floor and refuses a new one, your [medical record](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medical_record) is now a lie.

### The Danger Zone: High-Alert Medications and Zeros
Some drugs are so [potent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potency_%28pharmacology%29) that a tiny mistake causes a catastrophe. We call these **high-alert medications**. [Insulin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Insulin) is classified as a high-alert medication because it radically alters [blood sugar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_sugar_level). [Heparin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heparin) is classified as a high-alert medication because it alters [blood clotting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coagulation). Because the stakes are so high, high-alert medications require independent double-verification by a second [licensed nurse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nurse) prior to administration. Two brains, two sets of eyes, zero assumptions.

![High-alert medications like Heparin require independent double-verification by a second nurse to prevent potentially fatal dosage errors.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Heparin_Sodium_sample.jpg)

Speaking of zero—how we write numbers matters immensely. 
* **[Trailing zeros](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trailing_zero)** in [medication orders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medical_prescription) increase the risk of tenfold dosing errors. If you write "5.0 mg", someone rushing might read it as "50 mg". Therefore, a trailing zero must **never** be used in medication dosage documentation. 
* Conversely, **[leading zeros](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leading_zero)** before a [decimal point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_separator) reduce the risk of missed decimal points in dosing. If you write ".5 mg", someone might read it as "5 mg". Therefore, a leading zero must **always** be used before a decimal dose of less than one (write "0.5 mg").

## Part 3: The Journey of the Pill ([Oral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oral_administration) & [Enteral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enteral_administration) Routes)

When a patient [swallows](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swallowing) a pill, it goes into a vat of [hydrofluoric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrofluoric_acid) and [hydrochloric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid)—the [stomach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stomach). [Pharmaceutical companies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pharmaceutical_industry) are smart; they design medications to navigate this environment.

![Anatomy of the stomach. The highly acidic environment dictates whether oral medications require special enteric coatings to survive until they reach the small intestine.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/2414_Stomach.jpg)

### The Architecture of a Pill
Some medications are destroyed by [stomach acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gastric_acid), or they severely irritate the [stomach lining](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gastric_mucosa). To fix this, we use an **[enteric coating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enteric_coating)**. Enteric-coated medications are designed to bypass the acidic stomach environment. Instead, enteric-coated medications are designed to dissolve in the [alkaline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkali) environment of the [small intestine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Small_intestine). What happens if you crush it? Crushing an enteric-coated tablet destroys the protective coating of the medication, rendering it either useless or dangerous.

Similarly, we have **[extended-release](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modified-release_dosage)** [capsules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capsule_%28pharmacy%29). Extended-release capsules deliver medication slowly over a prolonged period. It’s like a slow-burning log on a fire. Crushing an extended-release medication is like turning that log into explosive kindling—it causes immediate [absorption](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absorption_%28pharmacokinetics%29) of a potentially [toxic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toxicity) dose. 

### The Enteral Tube Challenge
But what if the patient can't swallow and has an enteral [feeding tube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feeding_tube)? You can't just shove a pill down a [plastic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plastic) tube. Solid enteral medications must be crushed to a fine [powder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Powder) before feeding tube administration (provided they aren't enteric-coated or extended-release, of course!). 

Once crushed, crushed enteral medications must be [dissolved](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dissolution_%28chemistry%29) in 15 to 30 milliliters of [sterile water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Purified_water) prior to administration. 

To keep the patient safe from breathing in their own [stomach contents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chyme), elevating the head of the bed to at least 30 degrees during enteral medication administration reduces [aspiration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulmonary_aspiration) [risk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Risk). 

Think of the tube like a [plumbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plumbing) system. It needs to be primed and cleaned. Enteral tubes must be flushed with 15 to 30 milliliters of [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water) *before* medication administration to check patency, and flushed with 15 to 30 milliliters of water *after* medication administration to ensure the whole dose reaches the stomach. Crucially, flushing enteral tubes with water *between* different medications prevents chemical incompatibilities. You don't want two different drugs reacting inside the plastic tube and turning into [concrete](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concrete)!

![Liquid or crushed medications being administered via a gastric tube. The tube must be thoroughly flushed with water before and after administration to maintain patency.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Administering_medication_into_a_gastric_tube.jpg)

## Part 4: Breaking the Barrier ([Injections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Injection_%28medicine%29))

When we bypass the [digestive system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_digestive_system) entirely, we use [needles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypodermic_needle). The depth of the [tissue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tissue_%28biology%29) dictates the technique. 

![Anatomy of a standard disposable syringe and needle. Understanding these components is critical for maintaining sterility and accurately measuring medication volumes.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Labeled_syringe.png)

### [Subcutaneous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subcutaneous_injection) (SQ) Injections
Subcutaneous injections are deposited into the [adipose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adipose_tissue) ([fat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fat)) tissue layer beneath the [skin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_skin). Fat doesn't have a massive [blood supply](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circulatory_system), so absorption here is steady and slow. Because space in this layer is limited, the maximum medication volume for a single subcutaneous injection is **one [milliliter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Litre)**. 

Common subcutaneous injection sites include the **outer aspect of the [upper arms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arm)**, the **anterior [thighs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thigh)**, and the **[abdomen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abdomen)**. If you use the abdomen, remember: the abdomen subcutaneous injection site must be located at least two [inches](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inch) away from the [umbilicus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Navel) to avoid major [blood vessels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_vessel).

The [angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle) of your needle depends on the patient's [anatomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anatomy). Subcutaneous injections are administered at a **90-degree angle** for patients with adequate adipose tissue. But for very thin patients, subcutaneous injections are administered at a **45-degree angle** for patients with minimal adipose tissue so you don't accidentally plunge into the [muscle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muscle)!

> **The Heparin Rules**
> When giving subcutaneous heparin (our high-alert [blood thinner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anticoagulant)), you have two special rules:
> 1. Aspirating the syringe prior to subcutaneous heparin administration increases the risk of tissue [trauma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Injury). **Do not aspirate.**
> 2. [Massaging](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Massage) the injection site after administering subcutaneous heparin increases the risk of [hematoma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hematoma) formation. **Do not rub it.**

### [Intramuscular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intramuscular_injection) (IM) Injections
Muscles have a rich blood supply, so [IM injections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intramuscular_injection) act faster. Intramuscular injections are administered at a **90-degree angle**, straight in! 

![Comparison of needle insertion angles. Intramuscular injections are given straight in at 90 degrees, while subcutaneous injections are administered at 45 or 90 degrees depending on tissue depth.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Needle-insertion-angles-1_(edited).png)

Adult intramuscular needles are typically **one to one and a half inches in length**, and adult intramuscular needles are typically **20 to 25 [gauge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Birmingham_gauge)** in diameter. 

Where do we stick them? 
* For standard, small doses, we use the [shoulder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shoulder). The [deltoid muscle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deltoid_muscle) can accommodate a maximum injection volume of **one milliliter**. 

![The deltoid muscle in the shoulder is a common site for small-volume intramuscular injections, safely accommodating up to one milliliter of fluid.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Deltoid_Muscle.png)

* For larger volumes, we need a bigger muscle. The [ventrogluteal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gluteal_muscles) site is the preferred location for large volume intramuscular injections in adults. 
* What about [babies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infant)? [Infants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infant) don't have developed [gluteal muscles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gluteal_muscles) because they don't walk yet! Therefore, the [vastus lateralis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vastus_lateralis_muscle) (the outer thigh) is the preferred intramuscular injection site for infants.

Sometimes, IM medications can be highly irritating if they leak back up the needle track into the subcutaneous fat. To stop this, we use a neat physical trick. The **Z-track method** involves pulling the skin [laterally](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anatomical_terms_of_location) before inserting an intramuscular needle. Once you inject and pull the needle out, you let the skin slide back into place. The Z-track method prevents intramuscular medication from leaking back into the subcutaneous tissue by basically closing the [sliding door](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sliding_door) behind it. Brilliant!

## Part 5: The Surface and Senses ([Topical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topical_medication), [Eyes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_eye), and [Ears](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ear))

Drugs don't just go into the body; sometimes they go *on* the body. 

### Topical & [Transdermal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transdermal) 
When applying an [ointment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ointment) or a [patch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transdermal_patch), remember that you are a [biological organism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism) too! A nurse must wear clean [gloves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medical_glove) when applying [topical medications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topical_medication). Wearing clean gloves during topical medication application prevents the nurse from absorbing the active drug. You don't want your patient's [blood pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_pressure) medication dropping *your* blood pressure halfway through your shift.

For patches, a nurse must remove an old [transdermal patch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transdermal_patch) before applying a new transdermal patch. Leaving multiple patches on stacks the doses. Furthermore, rotating transdermal patch application sites prevents local [skin irritation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irritation).

![When applying a transdermal patch, the nurse should wear gloves to prevent self-dosing. The patch must also be clearly dated and initialed.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Applying_transdermal_patch.jpg)

### The Eyes ([Ophthalmic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ophthalmology))
Your eyes are incredibly sensitive, particularly the clear dome called the [cornea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cornea). Because of this, [ophthalmic drops](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eye_drop) are instilled directly into the lower [conjunctival sac](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conjunctiva) (that little pink pocket you create when you gently pull down the lower [eyelid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eyelid)). Instilling ophthalmic drops into the lower conjunctival sac prevents direct injury to the cornea. 

![Proper instillation of ophthalmic drops into the lower conjunctival sac. Notice how the lower eyelid is pulled down to avoid dropping medication directly onto the sensitive cornea.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Instilling_eye_medication.jpg)

Once the drop is in, the [tear ducts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nasolacrimal_duct) want to drain that liquid down into the [nose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_nose) and [throat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Throat), where it can enter the [bloodstream](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circulatory_system). To stop this, applying gentle pressure to the [nasolacrimal duct](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nasolacrimal_duct) after eye drop instillation prevents [systemic absorption](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Systemic_circulation) of the drug. We want the drug in the eye, not the [heart](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heart)!

### The Ears ([Otic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ear))
The [ear canal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ear_canal) is a tunnel, but it changes shape as we grow. To get [ear drops](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ear_drop) to flow all the way to the [eardrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eardrum), we have to straighten the tunnel. 
* For a [child](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Child) under three years old, the [ear pinna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Auricle_%28anatomy%29) is pulled **down and back** during otic medication administration. 
* For [individuals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Person) three years and older, the ear pinna is pulled **up and back** during otic medication administration. 

And here is a beautiful, if uncomfortable, fact about [human physiology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_physiology): the ear's [balance center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vestibular_system) is incredibly sensitive to [temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperature) changes. Administering cold otic drops can cause severe [dizziness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dizziness) in the patient, triggering [vertigo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertigo) and [nausea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nausea). To prevent sending your patient on a miserable biological roller-coaster, otic drops should be warmed to [room temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Room_temperature) prior to administration. 

![The vestibular system of the inner ear. Because these semicircular canals are highly sensitive to temperature changes, cold ear drops can cause severe dizziness and vertigo.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Vertigo.png)

### Final Thoughts
Look at what we've covered. It’s not just rules to memorize; it’s an entire system of physical logic designed to deliver healing chemicals perfectly to a [biological machine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biological_machine). 

Know your [conversions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conversion_of_units). Respect the [zeros](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/0). Check your identifiers. Protect the stomach, respect the tissues, and always, *always* think about the *why* before you push the [plunger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syringe). Master this, and you won't just pass the [NCLEX](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NCLEX)—you'll be the kind of nurse everyone trusts. Now get out there and study!
