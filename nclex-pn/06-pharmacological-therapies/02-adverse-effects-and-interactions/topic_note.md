# The Beautiful, Messy [Chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemistry) of [Nursing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nursing): [Adverse Effects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adverse_effect), [Contraindications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contraindication), and [Interactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drug_interaction)

Welcome, students! Let me ask you a question. When you administer a [pill](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tablet_%28pharmacy%29), an [injection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Injection_%28medicine%29), or a freshly hung [IV bag](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intravenous_therapy) to a client, do you think that [chemical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_substance) knows where it’s supposed to go? Of course not! A [medication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medication) is just a tiny, blind [molecule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecule) floating through a vast, dark, salty ocean of [blood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood), bumping into things. We drop these [molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecule) into the most complex, brilliant machine in the universe—the [human body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_body)—and we expect perfect harmony. 

But reality? Reality is exquisitely messy.

Sometimes [medications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medication) do exactly what we want. But often, they interact with the wrong [receptors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Receptor_%28biochemistry%29), fight with the client's lunch, or trigger alarms in the [immune system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system). To be a phenomenal [nurse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nursing), you can't just know what a [drug](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drug) is *supposed* to do. You have to anticipate exactly how it will act out. 

Let's explore the fascinating rules of the road when [chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemistry) goes rogue.

---

## 1. The Cost of Doing Business: [Side Effects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Side_effect) vs. [Adverse Effects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adverse_effect)

Nature doesn't care about our intentions. When a [drug](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drug) enters the body, it acts on whatever [cellular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_%28biology%29) locks it has the keys for. 

We need to make a critical distinction right away between two concepts that people often confuse: [side effects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Side_effect) and [adverse effects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adverse_effect). 

> **A [side effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Side_effect)** is a predictable and often unavoidable secondary effect produced at a usual [therapeutic dose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Therapeutic_window). 

It’s the biological cost of doing business. If you give a client a [medication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medication) that dries up [mucus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mucus) in their [lungs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lung), it’s probably going to dry up the [saliva](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saliva) in their mouth, too. We anticipate it, we warn the client, and we manage it.

> **An [adverse drug effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adverse_drug_reaction)**, on the other hand, is an unintended and unexpected response to a [medication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medication) occurring at standard [therapeutic doses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Therapeutic_window). 

This is the ambush. It’s not the standard "cost of doing business"—it is a [physiological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physiology) detour that can range from mildly bothersome to utterly catastrophic. As a [nurse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nursing), you are the early warning system for these detours.

## 2. The Red Lights: Understanding [Contraindications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contraindication)

There are times in [nursing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nursing) when you look at a [medication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medication) order, look at the client’s chart, and realize, "If I give this, disaster will strike." This is the realm of [contraindications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contraindication). 

We classify these into two distinct categories:

*   **A relative [contraindication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contraindication)** is a condition where extreme caution should be used when two [drugs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drug) or procedures are used together. It means "Proceed, but keep your eyes wide open and monitor heavily."
*   **An absolute [contraindication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contraindication)** is a situation where a specific [medication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medication) must *never* be administered due to severe life-threatening risks. It means "Stop immediately."

### The Ultimate Absolute: [Pregnancy Category](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pregnancy_category) X
When a client is pregnant, we are treating two clients. **[Pregnancy Category](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pregnancy_category) X medications have proven [fetal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fetus) risks that outweigh any possible benefits to the pregnant client.** There is no debate; the risk of [birth defects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Birth_defect) is too profound. For example, **[isotretinoin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotretinoin) is absolutely [contraindicated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contraindication) in pregnant clients due to severe [teratogenic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teratogenesis) effects** (effects that cause severe developmental abnormalities in the [fetus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fetus)). 

### Other Hard "No"s in [Pharmacology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pharmacology)
Let's look at how a client's pre-existing conditions create absolute [contraindications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contraindication):

| Client Condition | Problematic Medication | The Physiological "Why" |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **[Asthma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asthma)** | Nonselective [beta-blockers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beta_blocker) | By blocking [beta-2 receptors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beta-2_adrenergic_receptor) in the [lungs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lung), **nonselective [beta-blockers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beta_blocker) are [contraindicated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contraindication) in clients with [asthma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asthma) due to the risk of inducing severe [bronchoconstriction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bronchoconstriction).** |
| **Active [GI Bleeding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gastrointestinal_bleeding)** | [NSAIDs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonsteroidal_anti-inflammatory_drug) | **[Nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonsteroidal_anti-inflammatory_drug) are [contraindicated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contraindication) in clients with active [gastrointestinal bleeding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gastrointestinal_bleeding)** because they block the [prostaglandins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prostaglandin) that protect the [stomach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stomach) lining and inhibit [platelet aggregation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platelet). |
| **Severe [Hypertension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypertension)** | [OTC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Over-the-counter_drug) Cold Meds ([Pseudoephedrine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudoephedrine)) | [Pseudoephedrine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudoephedrine) works by causing massive [vasoconstriction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vasoconstriction) to clear [nasal congestion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nasal_congestion). Therefore, **[over-the-counter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Over-the-counter_drug) cold [medications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medication) containing [pseudoephedrine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudoephedrine) are [contraindicated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contraindication) in clients with severe [hypertension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypertension).** |
| **[Viral Illness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viral_disease) (Children)** | [Aspirin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aspirin) | **[Aspirin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aspirin) is [contraindicated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contraindication) in children with [viral illnesses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viral_disease) due to the high risk of developing [Reye's syndrome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reye_syndrome)**, a rare but deadly condition causing [brain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brain) and [liver](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liver) swelling. |

Nonselective beta-blockers exacerbate this by blocking the beta-2 receptors responsible for bronchodilation, potentially triggering life-threatening bronchoconstriction.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Blausen_0620_Lungs_NormalvsInflamedAirway.png)

## 3. The Body Fights Back: [Allergic Reactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allergy) & [Anaphylaxis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anaphylaxis)

Sometimes, the body looks at a perfectly good [medication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medication) and decides it's an invading army. 

**A true [medication allergy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drug_allergy) involves an [immunological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immunology) response resulting in the production of [antibodies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antibody) against the [drug](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drug).** The first time the client takes the drug, nothing much happens—the [immune system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system) is just studying it, quietly building specific [antibodies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antibody). But the *next* time the drug enters the system? The [immune system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system) launches a massive, [systemic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Systemic_disease) counter-attack.

### [Anaphylaxis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anaphylaxis): The Systemic Meltdown
The most terrifying form of this is [anaphylaxis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anaphylaxis). **[Anaphylaxis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anaphylaxis) is a rapid, severe, and potentially life-threatening [systemic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Systemic_disease) [allergic reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allergy).** The [mast cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mast_cell) dump massive amounts of [histamine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Histamine) into the [bloodstream](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circulatory_system). 

What does this look like? [Blood vessels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_vessel) [dilate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vasodilation) wildly, fluid leaks into [tissues](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tissue_%28biology%29), and [smooth muscles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smooth_muscle) spasm. **Symptoms of [anaphylaxis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anaphylaxis) include [bronchospasm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bronchospasm), [wheezing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wheeze), severe [hypotension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypotension), and [tachycardia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tachycardia).** 

If your client is receiving an [IV medication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intravenous_therapy) and suddenly starts [wheezing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wheeze) and dropping their [blood pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_pressure), you are in a race against time. Here is your exact sequence of survival:
1.  **The first [nursing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nursing) action during an [intravenous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intravenous_therapy) [medication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medication) [allergic reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allergy) is to stop the [medication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medication) infusion immediately.** Stop the invading [chemical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_substance)!
2.  **After stopping an [intravenous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intravenous_therapy) [medication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medication) during an [allergic reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allergy), the [nurse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nursing) must establish and maintain a patent [airway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Airway).** If they can't breathe, nothing else matters.
3.  Administer the [antidote](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antidote). **[Epinephrine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adrenaline) is the primary [pharmacological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pharmacology) treatment for [anaphylactic shock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anaphylaxis).** It slams the [blood vessels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_vessel) shut (raising BP) and forces the [airways](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Airway) open.

![During anaphylaxis, exposure to an allergen triggers mast cells to degranulate, releasing massive amounts of inflammatory mediators like histamine. This results in systemic vasodilation, dangerous hypotension, and severe bronchoconstriction.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Blausen_0018_Anaphylaxis.png)

### The Slow Burn: [Stevens-Johnson Syndrome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stevens%E2%80%93Johnson_syndrome)
Not all severe [immune](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system) responses happen in minutes. **[Stevens-Johnson syndrome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stevens%E2%80%93Johnson_syndrome) is a rare and severe [hypersensitivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypersensitivity) reaction involving blistering and peeling of the [skin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skin) and [mucous membranes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mucous_membrane).** It is agonizing and dangerous, essentially acting like severe, full-body [burns](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burn). You must know that **[Stevens-Johnson syndrome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stevens%E2%80%93Johnson_syndrome) can be a life-threatening [adverse effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adverse_effect) of certain [anticonvulsants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anticonvulsant) and [sulfonamide antibiotics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfonamide_%28medicine%29).**

## 4. The Biochemical Cocktail Party: [Interactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drug_interaction)

When you mix two [chemicals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_substance) together in a beaker, you get a reaction. When you mix them inside the human [GI tract](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gastrointestinal_tract) or [bloodstream](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circulatory_system), things get really interesting. 

[Drugs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drug) and foods can interact in two primary ways:
*   **[Synergistic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synergy) [drug interactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drug_interaction) occur when the combined effect of two [medications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medication) is greater than the sum of their individual effects.** (1 + 1 = 3). 
*   **[Antagonistic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Receptor_antagonist) [drug interactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drug_interaction) occur when one [medication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medication) decreases or blocks the [physiological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physiology) effect of another [medication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medication).** (They fight for the same parking spots on the [cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_%28biology%29)).

### The Food Imposters
You'd be amazed at how common foods completely sabotage our [pharmacological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pharmacology) plans.

*   **The Grapefruit Blockade:** **[Grapefruit juice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grapefruit_juice) inhibits the [cytochrome P450](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cytochrome_P450) [enzyme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enzyme) system in the [liver](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liver).** Think of [Cytochrome P450](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cytochrome_P450) as the bouncer at the door of the [bloodstream](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circulatory_system), breaking down [drugs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drug) so they don't overwhelm the body. [Grapefruit juice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grapefruit_juice) takes the bouncer out of commission. Because the [drugs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drug) aren't being broken down, **[grapefruit juice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grapefruit_juice) can cause [toxic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toxicity) levels of [calcium channel blockers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calcium_channel_blocker) and [statins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statin) to accumulate in the [bloodstream](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circulatory_system).**
*   **The [Vitamin K](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vitamin_K) Tug-of-War:** [Warfarin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Warfarin) is an [anticoagulant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anticoagulant) that works by blocking [Vitamin K](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vitamin_K). Therefore, **foods high in [Vitamin K](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vitamin_K) decrease the [anticoagulant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anticoagulant) effects of [warfarin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Warfarin).** What should you teach your client to monitor? **Leafy green vegetables such as [spinach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spinach) and [kale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kale) are high in [Vitamin K](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vitamin_K).** 
*   **The Dairy Trap:** [Calcium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calcium) binds to certain [chemicals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_substance) like a magnet, creating heavy [molecular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecule) complexes that the [gut](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gastrointestinal_tract) cannot absorb. Because of this, **[dairy products](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dairy_product) decrease the intestinal absorption of [tetracycline antibiotics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetracycline_antibiotics).**
*   **The Antacid Shield:** Similar to dairy, **[over-the-counter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Over-the-counter_drug) [antacids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antacid) containing [aluminum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aluminium) or [magnesium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnesium) can decrease the [gastrointestinal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gastrointestinal_tract) absorption of concurrently administered oral [medications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medication).** Never give [antacids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antacid) at the same time as other crucial [pills](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tablet_%28pharmacy%29)!

![Warfarin operates within a very narrow therapeutic window. High intake of Vitamin K-rich foods antagonizes the drug, dropping the client out of the therapeutic range and increasing the risk of blood clots.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Vitamin_K1_warfarin_therapeutic_window.png)

### The Herbal Wildcards
[Herbal supplements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herbal_medicine) are not "just teas." They are [pharmacologically](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pharmacology) active compounds that clients often forget to report. As a [nurse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nursing), you must actively hunt for these [interactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drug_interaction).

**The Bleeders:** 
Herbs love to thin the [blood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood). 
*   **[Ginkgo biloba](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ginkgo_biloba) increases the risk of bleeding when taken concurrently with [anticoagulant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anticoagulant) or [antiplatelet medications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiplatelet_drug).** 
*   Similarly, **[garlic supplements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Garlic) increase the bleeding risk in clients taking [warfarin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Warfarin).** 

**The Brain & Nerve Changers:**
*   **[St. John's Wort](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/St._John%27s_wort) combined with [selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Selective_serotonin_reuptake_inhibitor) increases the risk of developing [serotonin syndrome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Serotonin_syndrome)**—a potentially fatal overload of [serotonin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Serotonin) causing [tremors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tremor), [fever](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fever), and rigidity.
*   **[Valerian root](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Valerian_%28herb%29) causes additive [central nervous system depression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_nervous_system_depression) when taken concurrently with [sedative-hypnotic medications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypnotic).** 

**The System Disruptors:**
*   **[Black cohosh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Actaea_racemosa) increases the [hypotensive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypotension) effects of prescribed [antihypertensive medications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antihypertensive_drug).** (The [blood pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_pressure) drops too low).
*   **[Ginseng](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ginseng) can significantly alter the effectiveness of prescribed [antidiabetic medications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anti-diabetic_medication)**, throwing [blood sugars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_sugar_level) into chaos.
*   **[Kava Kava](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kava) can cause severe [hepatotoxicity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hepatotoxicity) when combined with [alcohol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alcohol_%28drug%29) or other [liver](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liver)-metabolized [medications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medication).**

## 5. Collateral Damage: Specific [Toxicities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toxicity) and Quirks

Every major [drug class](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drug_class) has its own unique fingerprint of chaos. Memorize these specific [toxicities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toxicity)—they are classic [NCLEX-PN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NCLEX) targets because identifying them early saves lives.

### [Digoxin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digoxin) and the [Potassium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium) See-Saw
[Digoxin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digoxin) is a [cardiac glycoside](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardiac_glycoside) that makes the [heart](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heart) pump harder. But here is the trick: [Digoxin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digoxin) and [Potassium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium) compete for the exact same binding sites on the [heart muscle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardiac_muscle). If the client's [potassium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium) drops too low, all the parking spots are empty, and [Digoxin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digoxin) takes over *everywhere*. Therefore, **[hypokalemia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypokalemia) increases [myocardial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardiac_muscle) sensitivity to [digoxin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digoxin).** 
When [digoxin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digoxin) levels get too high, it messes with the [optical nerves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optic_nerve). **[Digoxin toxicity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digoxin_toxicity) symptoms include [visual disturbances](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visual_impairment) such as yellow-green halos around lights.**

![An electrocardiogram demonstrating the classic "scooped out" ST segment depression associated with digoxin use. Monitoring for digoxin toxicity is critical, particularly when the client is at risk for hypokalemia.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/DigToxCrop.jpg)

### [ACE Inhibitors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ACE_inhibitor): The [Bradykinin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bradykinin) Buildup
[Angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) inhibitors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ACE_inhibitor) (like [lisinopril](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lisinopril)) lower [blood pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_pressure). But they also stop the breakdown of an [inflammatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inflammation) substance called [bradykinin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bradykinin). This buildup irritates the [lungs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lung), meaning **[angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ACE_inhibitor) can cause a persistent dry [cough](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cough) as an [adverse effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adverse_effect).** 
If that [bradykinin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bradykinin) builds up in the soft tissues of the face? You get an emergency. **[Angioedema](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angioedema) is a severe, life-threatening [adverse effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adverse_effect) of [angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ACE_inhibitor) involving swelling of the face and [airway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Airway).**

![Angioedema presenting as severe swelling of the upper lip and face. This is a medical emergency often triggered by ACE inhibitors, requiring immediate airway management and discontinuation of the drug.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Angioedema_of_the_face.jpg)

### [Antipsychotics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antipsychotic) and the Loss of Control
[First-generation antipsychotics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Typical_antipsychotic) (like [haloperidol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haloperidol)) work by blocking [dopamine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dopamine) in the [brain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brain). But [dopamine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dopamine) is what allows us to have smooth, coordinated muscle movements! By blocking it, **[extrapyramidal symptoms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extrapyramidal_symptoms) are potential [neurological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neurology) [adverse effects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adverse_effect) of [first-generation antipsychotic medications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Typical_antipsychotic).** 
If left unmanaged, this can become permanent. **[Tardive dyskinesia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tardive_dyskinesia) is an [extrapyramidal symptom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extrapyramidal_symptoms) involving involuntary and repetitive movements of the face and jaw** (like lip-smacking or tongue rolling).

### [Lithium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lithium_%28medication%29) and the [Sodium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium) Confusion
[Lithium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lithium_%28medication%29) is a salt used for [bipolar disorder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bipolar_disorder). [Sodium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium) is a salt we eat every day. The [kidneys](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kidney) are remarkably bad at telling them apart. If a client gets dehydrated and their [sodium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium) drops, the [kidneys](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kidney) panic and hoard whatever salt they can find—which happens to be the [Lithium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lithium_%28medication%29). Thus, **[lithium toxicity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lithium_toxicity) can be precipitated by [hyponatremia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyponatremia).**

### [Antibiotics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antibiotic): The Nuclear Option
[Antibiotics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antibiotic) don't just kill the bad [bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacteria); they kill the good [bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacteria), too. **The administration of [broad-spectrum antibiotics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Broad-spectrum_antibiotic) can destroy [normal intestinal flora](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gut_microbiota).** 
Think of the [gut](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gastrointestinal_tract) like a lush garden. The [antibiotics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antibiotic) act like napalm, wiping out the garden. What grows back first in burnt soil? Weeds. **The destruction of [normal intestinal flora](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gut_microbiota) by [antibiotics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antibiotic) can lead to a [superinfection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superinfection) such as [Clostridium difficile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clostridioides_difficile)** (C. diff), causing severe, infectious [diarrhea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diarrhea).

![The human gastrointestinal tract hosts a massive, diverse microbiome. Broad-spectrum antibiotics can indiscriminately wipe out these protective flora, creating an opportunity for severe superinfections like C. diff to take over.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Microbiome.jpg)

**A Special Note on [Vancomycin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vancomycin):**
[Vancomycin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vancomycin) is a powerful [IV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intravenous_therapy) [antibiotic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antibiotic). If you infuse it too fast, it triggers a massive [histamine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Histamine) release. **[Red man syndrome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_man_syndrome) is an [adverse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adverse_effect) infusion reaction to [vancomycin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vancomycin) characterized by flushing and [pruritus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Itch) of the upper body.** How do we fix this? Simple [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics): slow down the delivery! **[Red man syndrome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_man_syndrome) can be prevented or minimized by slowing the [intravenous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intravenous_therapy) infusion rate of [vancomycin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vancomycin).**

## 6. The Erasers: Pharmacological [Antidotes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antidote)

Finally, what happens when we make a mistake, or a client takes an [overdose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drug_overdose)? We reach for the chemical erasers—the [antidotes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antidote). An [antidote](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antidote) is an [antagonistic drug](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Receptor_antagonist) that competes with the [toxic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toxicity) [medication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medication) and turns it off. 

![A publicly available Naloxone kit. Naloxone acts as an emergency competitive antagonist, ripping opioids off the brain's receptors to rapidly reverse life-threatening respiratory depression.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Naloxone_kit%2C_Mount_Royal_University%2C_Calgary%2C_Alberta%2C_2025-07-11.jpg)

Knowing your [antidotes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antidote) is non-negotiable for a [practical nurse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Licensed_practical_nurse):

*   **The specific [pharmacological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pharmacology) [antidote](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antidote) for an [opioid overdose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Opioid_overdose) is [naloxone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naloxone).** It rips the [opioid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Opioid) right off the [brain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brain)'s [receptors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Receptor_%28biochemistry%29).
*   **The specific [pharmacological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pharmacology) [antidote](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antidote) for a [benzodiazepine overdose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzodiazepine_overdose) is [flumazenil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flumazenil).**
*   **The specific [pharmacological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pharmacology) [antidote](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antidote) for [warfarin toxicity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Warfarin) is [vitamin K](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vitamin_K).** (Since [warfarin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Warfarin) blocks [Vitamin K](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vitamin_K), giving massive amounts of [Vitamin K](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vitamin_K) overwhelms the [drug](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drug)).
*   **The specific [pharmacological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pharmacology) [antidote](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antidote) for [heparin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heparin) toxicity is [protamine sulfate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protamine_sulfate).**
*   **The specific [pharmacological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pharmacology) [antidote](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antidote) for [acetaminophen toxicity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paracetamol_toxicity) is [acetylcysteine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylcysteine).** It protects the [liver](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liver) from the [toxic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toxicity) [metabolites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metabolite) of [acetaminophen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paracetamol).

### Final Thoughts from the Professor

[Chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemistry) isn't magic; it is a highly predictable set of microscopic collisions. By understanding *why* [drugs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drug) act the way they do—why [Grapefruit juice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grapefruit_juice) blocks [liver enzymes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liver_function_tests), why low [potassium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium) invites [Digoxin toxicity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digoxin_toxicity), why [antibiotics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antibiotic) create [superinfections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superinfection)—you transition from someone who just hands out [pills](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tablet_%28pharmacy%29) to someone who deeply understands the magnificent machinery of the [human body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_body).

Study the "whys," keep your eyes wide open for the [adverse effects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adverse_effect), and you will be a phenomenal [nurse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nursing). Now, get back to studying!
