You need to know the rules of the chaos!

Imagine the hospital is an exclusive, high-end restaurant. Sometimes the disaster is a total kitchen meltdown—an internal disaster refers to an event occurring within a healthcare facility that threatens structural integrity or patient safety. The threat is in the house! Examples of internal disasters include facility fires, power outages, and active shooter situations. The roof is literally caving in on your dinner service. But then, there's the other side of the coin. Your restaurant is perfectly fine, but the rival mob down the street just blew up a city block. Suddenly, fifty bleeding guys are banging on your front doors. That’s an external disaster—it refers to an event occurring outside the healthcare facility that creates a sudden influx of patients. Examples of external disasters include natural disasters, mass transit accidents, and terrorist attacks. You aren’t running from a collapsing roof; you’re being invaded by sheer numbers!

When this happens, you don't get to improvise your lines. The Emergency Operations Plan dictates specific roles for all facility staff during a disaster. It is the holy script of survival. As a Practical Nurse, you don't get to play rogue cowboy. During a disaster, the Practical Nurse functions under the direction of the Registered Nurse or the designated incident commander. You are the enforcer, executing the director's vision. And what are you enforcing when the mob hits your doors? Believe it or not, a primary role of nursing staff during an external disaster is identifying stable clients for safe discharge. You become the ruthless bouncer! Why are we throwing people out? Because discharging stable clients during a mass casualty event frees up facility beds for incoming critical patients. You gotta kick out the guys sipping tap water so you can treat the folks who are literally falling apart. And what about your crew, the roadies? Unlicensed Assistive Personnel focus on vital signs, patient transport, and basic hygiene during a facility disaster. They keep the gears turning, but you do not let them play bouncer! Unlicensed Assistive Personnel do not perform triage or independent patient assessments during mass casualty events. That takes a license and a ruthless clinical eye!

Speaking of ruthless, let’s talk about the velvet rope. When the external disaster hits, we use the Simple Triage and Rapid Treatment—the START method—which categorizes disaster victims based on the severity of their injuries. We slap colored tags on them like VIP wristbands. A red tag? That indicates a victim with life-threatening injuries requiring immediate intervention. They are hovering over the abyss, and you drag them straight to the front of the line. A yellow tag indicates a victim with serious injuries that can tolerate a delay in medical treatment. They’re broken, but their vitals are stable, so they wait in the lounge. A green tag indicates a victim with minor injuries requiring minimal care. They are the walking wounded, crying about a sprained ankle while Rome burns. Ignore their whining. And then... the black tag. A black tag indicates a victim who is deceased or has nonsurvivable injuries. In peacetime, we’d fight for them, but in a war zone, we cannot waste the whole kitchen’s resources on a meal that’s already burnt.

Now, what if the kitchen actually *is* on fire? Facility fire response protocols typically follow the RACE acronym. It’s your survival choreography! R stands for Rescue patients from immediate danger—grab the grandma and run! A stands for Alarm activation—scream for the cavalry! C stands for Contain the fire by closing doors and windows. Fire breathes oxygen; you have to choke it out by slamming the doors shut! E stands for Extinguish the fire or Evacuate the area. If you’re going to be a hero with a fire extinguisher, fire extinguisher operation follows the PASS acronym: Pull the pin, Aim at the base, Squeeze the handle, Sweep side to side. Why the base? Because aiming at the flames is just giving the fire a dramatic blowout! You have to aim at the source of the drama!

Speaking of toxic drama, hospitals are full of industrial chemicals that will melt your shoes. If someone knocks over a jug of high-grade solvent, you don't just grab a mop! Facility protocol requires immediate consultation of the Safety Data Sheet following any staff exposure to a hazardous chemical. Think of the SDS as the little black book of toxic relationships—Safety Data Sheets provide critical information on the chemical properties, health hazards, and safe handling of specific substances. It tells you exactly how this chemical is going to ruin your life and how to flush it out of your eyes. Once you've consulted the book, specialized spill kits must be used to clean up hazardous chemical spills according to specific facility guidelines. 

And don't get me started on the biological trash! Blood-soaked dressings must be disposed of in designated red biohazard bags. They are red because they are dangerous, like a stop sign for someone else’s DNA! Used needles and scalpel blades are the sharp-tongued gossips of the ward—they will backstab you if you look away. Used needles and scalpel blades must be disposed of immediately in rigid, puncture-resistant sharps containers. And for the love of all that is holy, do not overstuff them! Sharps containers must be replaced when they reach the designated fill line to prevent accidental needle-stick injuries. You try to force one last syringe in there, and it’s going to bounce back and pierce your thumb!

Then we have the invisible, toxic ex: radiation. Radiation exposure risk is managed by optimizing time, distance, and shielding. Keep the visits short, back the heck up, and put a lead wall between you and the bad vibes! If you are caring for a patient who is literally radioactive from the inside out, healthcare workers assigned to care for clients receiving internal radiation must wear a dosimeter badge to measure cumulative exposure. This badge is your accountant; it tallies up every ounce of toxic energy you absorb so you don't glow in the dark. And their family members? Visitors must maintain a distance of at least six feet from a client undergoing internal radiation therapy. No goodbye hugs for the radioactive!

Now, lean in, because the real enemies are microscopic. They are the ultimate biological mobsters. Standard precautions apply to the care of all patients regardless of their presumed infection status. I don't care if they look like a saint; assume they are carrying a wire! Standard precautions require the use of gloves when touching blood, body fluids, secretions, or non-intact skin. If it is wet and it is not yours, wrap your hands! But gloves aren't magic forcefields. Hand hygiene is the single most effective method for preventing the spread of infectious agents. 

But there is one mob boss who laughs at your hand sanitizer: Clostridioides difficile. C. diff wears a microscopic Kevlar vest made of spores. Alcohol-based hand rubs are ineffective against the spores produced by Clostridioides difficile. You just give them a warm, boozy bath! Therefore, healthcare workers must wash their hands with soap and water when caring for clients infected with Clostridioides difficile. You have to use brute physical friction to tear them off your skin and send them down the drain!

When we know exactly how these microscopic mobsters operate, we escalate our defenses. Contact precautions are required for clients infected with multidrug-resistant organisms such as Methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus. MRSA is a clinger; it wants to grab your clothes and catch a ride to the next room. So, contact precautions require healthcare workers to wear a gown and gloves upon entering the client's room. You wrap yourself in plastic armor!

What about the spitters? Droplet precautions are required for infectious agents transmitted through large respiratory droplets. Influenza, pertussis, and mumps require the implementation of droplet precautions. These droplets are heavy; they shoot out and crash to the floor like bowling balls. Because the splash zone is tight, healthcare workers must wear a surgical mask when working within three feet of a client on droplet precautions. 

But the scariest ones are the invisible ninjas. Airborne precautions are required for infectious agents transmitted through small particle aerosols that remain suspended in the air. They don't fall; they float on the breeze like a terrible rumor. Tuberculosis, measles, and varicella require the implementation of airborne precautions. You can't just close the door. Clients on airborne precautions must be placed in a negative-pressure isolation room. This room actively sucks the air inward so the ninjas can't escape into the hallway! And to protect your own lungs, healthcare workers must wear a fit-tested N95 respirator or a higher-level respirator when entering an airborne precaution room. It seals to your face like a vault!

Putting all this armor on and taking it off is a sacred ritual. If you mess up the order, you just smeared the disaster all over your face! The proper sequence for donning personal protective equipment is gown, mask or respirator, goggles or face shield, and then gloves. You cover your massive body first, protect your airway, shield your eyes, and put your gloves on last so they overlap the gown’s cuffs! A perfect seal! But when the battle is over and you are covered in microscopic filth? The proper sequence for doffing personal protective equipment is gloves, goggles or face shield, gown, and then mask or respirator. Listen to the logic! Your gloves are the absolute dirtiest things in the room—tear them off first! Then safely remove your eye protection, peel off the contaminated gown, and you leave your breathing mask on until the absolute bitter end. You take the mask off last so you don't inhale the battlefield while you are stripping down!
