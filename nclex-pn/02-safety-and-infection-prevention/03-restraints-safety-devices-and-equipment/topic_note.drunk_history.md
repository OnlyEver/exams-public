Think of a patient’s bodily autonomy as an exclusive VIP nightclub. You, the nurse, are head of security. Sometimes, a patron gets totally out of control, and you have to restrict their freedom. In our world, restraints are classified into two specific, legally terrifying categories: physical and chemical. A physical restraint is any manual method or mechanical device attached to a patient's body to hold them down. It’s the bouncer physically grabbing you. A chemical restraint? That’s when the bartender slips a sedative into your mocktail—it is a medication used specifically to control behavior or restrict movement rather than treating an actual medical condition. And don't think you can just play a game of hide-and-seek to avoid the paperwork. Seclusion, which involves confining a client alone in a room and preventing the client from leaving, is legally and clinically classified as a form of restraint. Locking someone in the VIP room alone is revoking their liberty, period.

But here is the absolute golden rule of the club: physical restraints must only be utilized as a last resort to protect the client or others from harm. You do not drop the nuclear bomb because someone is being annoying! Nurses must exhaust all less restrictive alternative measures before applying physical restraints. Before you break out the straps, ask yourself: is this person just lost, or do they have to pee? Seriously! Reorientation and frequent toileting are considered less restrictive alternative measures to restraints. Try talking to them and giving them a bathroom break before you turn the hospital into a prison.

If those alternatives fail, you still don't have the authority to just tie people up on a whim. Healthcare providers must prescribe physical and chemical restraints prior to application. The Provider is the Mob Boss, and you need their official sign-off before you put a hit out on someone's freedom. And that prescription cannot be a blank check! As-needed or PRN prescriptions for any type of restraint are strictly prohibited. The boss can't just say, "Eh, tie 'em up whenever they get feisty." No! A valid restraint prescription must explicitly document three exact details: the specific client behavior warranting the restraint, the specific type of restraint to be used, and the exact duration of the restraint use. 

Now, what if a patron suddenly goes completely feral and starts swinging? Are you supposed to just stand there and get punched while waiting for the paperwork? No! A licensed nurse can apply a physical restraint without a prior prescription in a strict emergency. But the second you do, the countdown timer starts ticking like a bomb in an action movie. Emergency application of a physical restraint requires obtaining a provider prescription within one hour of application. One hour! 

When you do apply that physical restraint, you better understand architectural physics. Physical restraints must be securely tied to an immovable part of the bed frame. The frame is the foundation; it doesn't shift. Physical restraints must never be tied to the movable side rails of a hospital bed! Imagine tying someone's wrist to a side rail, and then someone else drops the rail down. You will brutally wrench that patient's limb like a wishbone at Thanksgiving. And if the room catches fire, you need an instant escape route. Nurses must secure physical restraints using a quick-release knot. A slip knot is an acceptable quick-release knot for securing physical restraints—pull the tail, and boom, they are free. Also, you aren't trying to sever their hand. A properly applied physical restraint allows the nurse to fit exactly two fingers between the restraint and the client's skin.

You also have to match the wardrobe to the crime. Mitt restraints are utilized to prevent clients from grasping or pulling on essential medical devices—they look like big boxing gloves so the patient can't pick at their surgical wounds. Wrist restraints are utilized to prevent clients from dislodging intravenous lines or enteral feeding tubes. Belt restraints are utilized to maintain a client's center of gravity; they prevent clients from accidentally falling out of a hospital bed or wheelchair while still letting them move their arms. And if you have to use a vest restraint, it must be applied so the crossover section is positioned on the client's front chest. If you put that vest on backward, the V-neck rides up and you’ve just engineered a tragic strangulation device. 

Once they are restrained, you turn into a totally obsessed, helicopter-parenting stalker. For your standard, confused patient, nurses must assess clients in non-violent physical restraints at least every two hours. During this two-hour check, you are practically doing a forensic sweep. Routine two-hour restraint assessments must include a thorough skin integrity check of the restrained area to ensure you aren't causing friction burns. It must also include a comprehensive circulatory check of the restrained extremity. You are checking the plumbing and the wiring! A circulatory check involves assessing the peripheral pulse of the restrained extremity, assessing capillary refill and skin color of the restrained extremity, assessing the skin temperature of the restrained extremity, and assessing sensory and motor function of the restrained extremity. Is it pink? Is it warm? Can they wiggle their fingers? 

You also can't just leave them trussed up forever. Nurses must temporarily remove physical restraints at least every two hours. This temporary removal facilitates the performance of active or passive range of motion exercises so their joints don't freeze up like rusty hinges. Plus, they are still human beings! Basic needs including food and fluids must be offered every two hours to a restrained client, and toileting opportunities must be offered every two hours. 

How closely are you watching them? It depends on the drama level. Clients placed in violent restraints require continuous one-to-one observation by healthcare staff. You stare at them unblinking, like you’re watching a reality TV villain who is about to throw a glass of wine. On the flip side, clients placed in non-violent restraints require documented observation at least every fifteen to thirty minutes. 

And you better keep your receipts, because the legal system loves a paper trail. Restraint documentation must detail the specific less restrictive alternative interventions attempted prior to restraint application. It must capture the client's physiological and psychological response to the restraint—are they calming down or hulking out? Furthermore, restraint documentation must record the exact time the physical restraint was applied, AND it must record the exact time the physical restraint was permanently removed. 

Now, let's pivot away from restraints and talk about the hospital environment itself, which is basically a sci-fi kitchen packed with lethal, high-voltage booby traps. First rule of the machine uprising: all electrical medical equipment must display a current safety inspection sticker from the facility. Facility protocols dictate the specific preventative maintenance schedule for all client care equipment. No sticker? It doesn't touch your patient. If you see a machine acting up, defective medical equipment must be clearly labeled as faulty and it must be routed directly to the biomedical engineering department for evaluation. Nurses are strictly prohibited from attempting to repair broken or malfunctioning medical equipment! You are a medical professional, not a mechanic; do not take a screwdriver to an IV pump! And if the hardware is literally crumbling? Healthcare staff must immediately remove any electrical equipment with frayed cords from the client care area. A frayed cord in a hospital is an arsonist waiting to strike.

Look at the plugs. Three-pronged electrical plugs are mandatory for all hospital-grade medical equipment. That third, weird little round peg isn't just for decoration; the third prong on a medical equipment plug provides electrical grounding to prevent fatal shocks. It gives rogue lightning a safe path into the earth instead of into your patient's heart. And look at the walls! Some outlets are white, and some are blazing red. Life-sustaining medical equipment plugs must be inserted exclusively into red wall outlets. Why? Because red wall outlets in a hospital are directly connected to the facility's emergency backup generator. If the city grid completely collapses, the red outlets keep breathing.

When those machines talk to you, you listen. Intravenous infusion pumps must be programmed strictly according to the manufacturer's operational instructions—you are dripping potent chemicals directly into someone's veins. And when the machines start screaming, nurses must never silence clinical equipment alarms without first investigating and resolving the underlying cause. Muting a heart monitor without checking the patient is like turning off the fire alarm because the noise is annoying while the kitchen burns down.

Speaking of bombs in the kitchen, respect the invisible gases. Portable oxygen cylinders must be stored in a secured upright position at all times. If a heavy metal cylinder tips over and the pressurized valve snaps off, you have just initiated a disaster. Unsecured oxygen cylinders carry a high risk of exploding or acting as high-velocity projectiles. It will literally shoot through a brick wall like a torpedo. Secure the tank!

Finally, don't let the getaway car ruin the heist. Falls are the ultimate preventable tragedy. A client's hospital bed must be maintained in the lowest possible elevation to prevent fall injuries—if they roll out, you want them dropping four inches, not three feet. Hospital bed wheels must remain in the locked position at all times unless actively transporting the client. When it's time to move, wheelchair wheels must be securely locked prior to transferring a client into the wheelchair, and the wheelchair wheels must be securely locked prior to transferring a client out of the wheelchair. Lock the brakes! And before you ever walk out of that room, give them their lifeline: a functioning call bell must be placed within the client's immediate physical reach at all times.
