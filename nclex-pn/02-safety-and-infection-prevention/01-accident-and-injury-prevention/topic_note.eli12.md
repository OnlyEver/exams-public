## 1. The Epistemology of Nursing: How Do We Know Who Is Who?

Imagine giving a super important video game controller to the wrong player. In a hospital, giving the wrong medicine to the wrong person is a huge deal. You can never just guess who someone is.

Because of this, the Joint Commission (the big rule-makers for hospitals) requires the use of at least two client identifiers before providing care, treatment, or services. 

Let's do a quick safety math check to see how this works:
Step 1: Find one good identifier (like asking for the patient's name). Total = 1 identifier.
Step 2: Find a second good identifier (like checking their birthdate). Total = 1 + 1 = 2 identifiers.
Step 3: You have reached the magic number of 2, so you are ready to give care safely!

### Acceptable vs. Unacceptable Identifiers

You have to use facts that belong only to that specific person. 

Acceptable client identifiers include the client's first and last name. Acceptable client identifiers include the client's date of birth. Also, an assigned medical record number is an acceptable client identifier. Think of the medical record number like a unique gamer tag made just for them!

Now, what should we never use? 
A client's room number is never an acceptable client identifier. Also, a client's bed assignment is never an acceptable client identifier. Why? Because people move around all the time! If your best friend moves from the couch to a beanbag chair, they are still the exact same person. You wouldn't call them "Beanbag Chair Person." Rooms change, but names and birthdays do not!

### The Verification Ritual

Before you give any medicine, you have to do a quick safety check. Nurses must check the client identification band against the medical record before administering medications. 

Hospitals also have super cool technology to help. Barcode scanning systems provide an additional safety measure during medication administration. It is just like scanning a barcode at the grocery store when you buy a snack! But remember, computers can sometimes glitch. Because of that, barcode scanning systems do not eliminate the requirement to verbally verify client identifiers. Always talk to your patient and ask them their name!

## 2. Biochemistry in Action: Identifying and Managing Allergies

Sometimes, a person's body gets really confused and attacks totally normal things, like a food or a medicine. This is called an allergy.

To keep patients safe, nurses must verify client allergies upon admission to a healthcare facility. That means you check right when they walk in the door! After that, nurses must document all client allergies in the permanent medical record so the whole doctor and nurse team knows about it. 

To make sure everyone can easily see the danger, healthcare facilities typically use red wristbands to alert staff to a client allergy. Red is the universal color for "STOP and pay attention!" Before you give them anything new, nurses must verify client allergies in the medication administration record before giving any medication.

### The Intravenous Crisis

Sometimes medicine goes straight into the body through a tiny tube in the arm called an IV. If you are doing this and the patient suddenly has a bad allergic reaction, what do you do? 

The immediate nursing action for a suspected allergic reaction during an intravenous infusion is to stop the infusion. Think of it like a leaky hose flooding your yard—the very first thing you do is turn off the water! 

### The Bizarre Biology of Latex and Fruit

Latex is a very stretchy rubber used to make things like balloons and hospital gloves. Clients with spina bifida have a high risk of developing a latex allergy. This is because they have to go through lots of surgeries starting from when they are babies, so their bodies are exposed to a lot of latex rubber.

Here is a crazy science fact: your body can get confused because the stuff inside latex rubber looks exactly like the stuff inside certain fruits! Because of this trick of nature:
* Latex allergies share cross-reactivity with bananas.
* Latex allergies share cross-reactivity with avocados.
* Latex allergies share cross-reactivity with kiwis.

If someone says their mouth feels itchy when they eat a banana or kiwi, you need to be super careful with rubber gloves around them!

## 3. Designing a Safe Environment: Fighting Gravity and Chaos

A hospital room can be tricky to navigate. We have to be like environmental engineers to stop people from falling or getting hurt by electricity.

### The Lifeline: The Call Light

The call light is a special button the patient presses when they need a nurse. It is just like an SOS button! Assessing the client's understanding of the call light system promotes a safe care environment. You have to make sure they know exactly how to push it. 

Nurses must place the call light within the client's continuous reach. It should always be right next to their hand! Also, nurses must educate clients to request assistance before getting out of bed. 

### Outsmarting Gravity: Bed and Floor Physics

When someone gets out of bed, we want to make sure they don't fall hard. So, the client bed must be kept in the lowest possible position to minimize fall injury risk. Falling off a low step hurts way less than falling off a high wall!

We also don't want the bed rolling away like a skateboard. Bed wheels must remain locked at all times when a client bed is stationary. Nurses must unlock bed wheels only during active transport of the client bed (like when you are rolling them down the hallway to get an X-ray).

To help patients grip the floor, clients assessed as a high fall risk must wear non-skid footwear during ambulation. Ambulation just means walking around. It’s exactly like wearing those grippy socks when you go to an indoor trampoline park!

We also need to make sure they can see where they are going. Adequate lighting in the client's room reduces the risk of tripping and falling. And just like your parents ask you to clean your room, nurses must clear the client's floor of clutter to eliminate tripping hazards. 

If a patient forgets they need help standing up, we use cool gadgets. Bed alarms alert healthcare staff when a client attempts to exit the bed unassisted. It beeps to let us know they are moving!

### Managing the Electrical Threat

Hospitals use tons of electricity to run machines. Nurses must immediately remove frayed electrical cords from the client care environment. A frayed cord is like a broken phone charger with wires poking out—it can shock you! If a machine starts smoking or acting weird, faulty medical equipment must be tagged and removed from the client care area immediately.

## 4. The Biomechanics of Transferring Clients

Moving a person from a bed to a wheelchair takes good teamwork and science.

Before you try to help someone stand, nurses must determine a client's weight-bearing capacity before initiating any physical transfer. You have to figure out if their legs are strong enough to hold their own body weight!

### Transfer Tools

We use awesome tools to help move people safely.

* **Gait Belts:** Gait belts provide a secure grip point for the nurse during client ambulation. It acts like a strong, safe handle you can hold while they walk. Nurses must apply a gait belt over the client's clothing to prevent skin injury. You don't want it pinching their bare skin!
* **Slide Boards:** Slide boards facilitate seated transfers between a bed and a wheelchair. It creates a smooth little bridge so the patient can slide across instead of standing up!
* **Mechanical Lifts:** Mechanical lifts are required to transfer clients who are completely immobile. It is like a super safe indoor crane! Mechanical lifts require the assistance of at least two staff members for safe operation. 
Let's look at the lifting math:
Step 1: Count yourself to push the machine buttons (1 person).
Step 2: Add one helper to safely guide the patient (1 person).
Step 3: Add them together: 1 + 1 = 2 staff members minimum!

### Wheelchair Physics

When you push forward, the thing under you wants to roll backward. To stop this, nurses must engage wheelchair brakes prior to transferring a client into the wheelchair. Also, nurses must engage wheelchair brakes prior to transferring a client out of the wheelchair. Always lock the wheels!

## 5. Thermodynamics and Combustion: Fire and Oxygen Safety

Fires need heat, fuel (like paper), and oxygen to burn. When an emergency happens, we don't panic. We use easy memory tricks to know exactly what to do!

### The RACE Protocol

The RACE acronym guides staff actions during a facility fire emergency. 

* **R - Rescue:** The letter R in the RACE fire acronym stands for Rescue clients in immediate danger. Get everyone to safety first!
* **A - Alarm:** The letter A in the RACE fire acronym stands for Alarm or activate the fire alarm system. Pull the alarm so the fire trucks come!
* **C - Confine:** The letter C in the RACE fire acronym stands for Confine the fire by closing doors. Lock the fire in a room so it can't breathe!
* **E - Extinguish or Evacuate:** The letter E in the RACE fire acronym stands for Extinguish the fire or Evacuate the area. Put the fire out, or get out of the building!

### The PASS Protocol for Fire Extinguishers

If you need to use a fire extinguisher, you follow the word PASS. The PASS acronym provides instructions for the correct use of a fire extinguisher.

* **P - Pull:** The letter P in the PASS acronym stands for Pull the pin on the fire extinguisher. 
* **A - Aim:** The letter A in the PASS acronym stands for Aim the extinguisher nozzle at the base of the fire. You aim at the bottom where the fuel is!
* **S - Squeeze:** The first letter S in the PASS acronym stands for Squeeze the handle of the fire extinguisher. 
* **S - Sweep:** The second letter S in the PASS acronym stands for Sweep the extinguisher nozzle from side to side. Spray it like you are washing a car!

### The Dangers of an Oxygen-Enriched Environment

Sometimes patients need extra oxygen to breathe. Oxygen makes fires burn super fast and bright. Oxygen comes in heavy metal tanks. Oxygen cylinders must be stored in upright carts or stands to prevent tipping. You don't want a heavy tank falling over like a bowling pin!

Because oxygen makes fire so wild, smoking is strictly prohibited in any area where oxygen is in use. 

There is one more sneaky fire starter: static electricity! You know how rubbing a balloon on your hair makes a tiny spark? Synthetic fabrics pose a risk in oxygen-enriched environments due to static electricity sparks. Materials like nylon or polyester make too many sparks. Because of this, cotton blankets are recommended for clients receiving oxygen therapy to minimize static electricity. Cotton is safe and spark-free!
