# The Physics of the Human Body: Mobility, Immobility, and Assistive Devices

Welcome, future nurses! Think of the human body like a high-tech video game console or a super-fast sports car. When you play the game or drive the car, everything runs great. The gears turn, the fluids pump, and the electrical wires fire perfectly. But what happens when you leave a sports car parked in a garage for weeks? The battery dies, the oil gets thick, and the tires go flat. 

The human body is the same way! Gravity usually works *with* us to keep us strong, but if we stay in bed too long, gravity becomes the enemy. Fluids pool up, muscles shrink, and bones get weak. 

In this guide, we are going to learn how to help our patients move around. We will learn how to check their strength, protect them when they are stuck in bed, and use tools to get them back on their feet safely. Let's level up!

---

## 1. The Pre-Flight Check: Assessing Mobility and Strength

Before you start a tough video game boss fight, what do you do? You check your character's stats! As a nurse, you have to check your patient's stats before you move them out of bed.

First, **nurses must assess a client's baseline mobility level before attempting to transfer the client.** We need to know what they were able to do yesterday so we know what is safe to try today. Also, you can't run a race with zero energy. That is why **nurses must assess a client's muscle strength before initiating ambulation** (which is a fancy word for walking).

To score their strength, we use a number system, just like leveling up. **The muscle strength grading scale ranges from zero to five.** Let's look at the math for these scores:

Step 1: Start at the top. **A muscle strength grade of five indicates normal muscle strength against full resistance.** This means they can push back hard, like a totally healthy person!
Step 2: Imagine going all the way down to the bottom level. **A muscle strength grade of zero indicates no visible muscle contraction.** You can't see the muscle move at all.
Step 3: But what if you try to feel it with your hand? **A muscle strength grade of zero indicates no palpable muscle contraction.** Palpable means you can feel it, so a zero means you feel absolutely nothing happening.

Finally, when the patient is standing up, we watch how they move. The **nurse evaluates a client's gait to determine the risk for falls.** "Gait" just means the way someone walks. If they are wobbling, we know they might fall!

## 2. The Danger of Stagnation: Complications of Immobility

If you leave a slice of pizza sitting on the counter for three days, it gets pretty gross. When a body sits totally still, things inside start to break down, too. Let's look at what happens.

### Cardiovascular and Respiratory Mayhem
Blood is supposed to zoom around your body. But if you sit still, **immobility increases the risk of deep vein thrombosis due to venous stasis.** "Venous stasis" just means the blood stops flowing and gets stuck in the legs. Stagnant blood turns into bad clumps called deep vein thrombosis (blood clots).

Also, your heart forgets how to fight gravity. Have you ever stood up way too fast and felt super dizzy? **Immobility increases the risk of orthostatic hypotension during position changes.** This is when your blood pressure drops because you stood up.

In your lungs, things get messy. **Immobility causes pooling of respiratory secretions in the lower lung fields.** This means spit and mucus drip down and settle at the bottom of your lungs. When this happens, two bad things occur:
1. The tiny air balloons inside your lungs get flat. **Pooling of respiratory secretions from immobility increases the risk of atelectasis** (which means collapsed lungs).
2. Germs love that warm, gross fluid! **Pooling of respiratory secretions from immobility increases the risk of pneumonia** (a really bad chest infection).

### The Musculoskeletal and Gastrointestinal Standstill
Muscles and bones have a rule: "Use it or lose it." 
* If you don't use them, **prolonged immobility leads to muscle atrophy** (meaning the muscles shrink away).
* If you don't bend your arms and legs, **prolonged immobility leads to joint contractures** (meaning the joints get stuck and frozen).
* Because you aren't putting any walking weight on your bones, your body thinks you don't need strong bones. **Immobility causes increased calcium excretion from bones.** The bones literally leak their calcium out!
* Where does all that spilled calcium go? Right into your kidneys! **Increased calcium excretion from immobility increases the risk of renal calculi.** Renal calculi is the science term for painful kidney stones.

Meanwhile, inside your belly, your stomach and intestines slow down like a bad internet connection. **Immobility decreases gastrointestinal motility.** Since the food is moving way too slowly, **decreased gastrointestinal motility from immobility leads to constipation.** 

### The Integumentary Breakdown
"Integumentary" is just a big word for your skin. **Sustained pressure on tissue from immobility leads to pressure injuries.** If you lay on the exact same spot all day, the heavy weight pinches your skin, blocking off the oxygen until the skin actually gets a bad sore.

## 3. The Antidote to Gravity: Nursing Interventions

To fight the bad effects of staying still, we use motion, clever tools, and strict timers!

### Defeating Pressure and Sheer
To stop those terrible skin sores, nurses have to do some math to figure out their schedules:
* **Nurses must reposition bedridden clients at least every two hours.** 
  Let's calculate a nurse's turning schedule for a 12-hour shift:
  Step 1: Take the total shift time (12 hours).
  Step 2: Divide by the maximum time a patient can stay in bed without turning (2 hours). 12 / 2 = 6.
  Step 3: This means you will flip the patient like a pancake at least 6 times during your shift!
* What if they are sitting in a chair? All their body weight is on their bottom! So, **nurses must reposition chair-bound clients at least every one hour.**

When we move patients, we never drag them. Dragging gives them bad carpet-burns! Instead, **a friction-reducing device decreases sheer forces on the client's skin during bed repositioning.** This is like putting a slippery slip-n-slide under them to move them safely.

> **Special Case: The Logroll**
> If a patient hurt their spine, we can't twist their back at all. **Clients with spinal cord injuries require the logrolling technique for repositioning.** Why? Because **the logrolling technique keeps the client's spinal column in strict alignment during movement.** It keeps them perfectly straight like a wooden log. Since it is hard to keep someone totally straight, **logrolling a client requires a minimum of two healthcare workers.**

### Defeating Blood Clots and Lung Collapse
To fight off those bad leg clots, we put special tools on the patient's legs to push the blood back to the heart.
1. **Sequential compression devices promote venous return.** These are cool leg sleeves that squeeze like a blood-pressure cuff to help the blood return to the heart. **Enhanced venous return from sequential compression devices prevents deep vein thrombosis.** 
2. We also use tight socks! **Anti-embolism stockings apply graduated pressure to promote venous return.** But because they squeeze so tight, **nurses must remove anti-embolism stockings at least once per shift to assess the underlying skin.**

To keep the lung balloons filled up with air, **clients must perform deep breathing exercises to prevent atelectasis during bed rest.** But honestly, the best medicine is getting out of bed. **Early ambulation prevents respiratory complications of immobility.** 

But wait! Before you stand them up, you have to remember that dizzy feeling we talked about. **Clients must dangle their legs on the side of the bed before standing to prevent orthostatic hypotension.** This gives their body a few minutes to adjust to the pressure change.

## 4. Keeping the Gears Oiled: Range of Motion (ROM)

If a patient is stuck in bed, we still have to stretch their arms and legs to keep their joints moving smoothly, just like stretching before a big sports game. We call this Range of Motion (ROM).
* **Active range of motion exercises are performed independently by the client.** They do it all by themselves, like a solo workout.
* **Passive range of motion exercises are performed by the caregiver for the client.** This means *we* move their arms and legs for them.

When we move their joints for them, we have to protect the joint like a fragile toy. **Caregivers must support the joint proximally during passive range of motion exercises** (which means holding the arm *above* the elbow). And **caregivers must support the joint distally during passive range of motion exercises** (which means holding *below* the elbow, too).

> **The Golden Rules of Range of Motion:**
> 1. **Range of motion exercises must be stopped immediately if the client experiences pain.**
> 2. **Range of motion exercises must be stopped immediately if joint resistance is met.** (If the arm feels stuck, don't keep pushing!).
> 3. **Joints must never be forced beyond their normal physiological range of motion limits.** We are trying to help them, not twist them into a pretzel!

## 5. Safely Launching the Ship: Client Transfers

When it is finally time to help someone out of bed, we use science to do it safely!

First, we put a super strong belt on them. **A gait belt is placed securely around the client's waist prior to ambulation.** This gives the nurse a safe handle to hold onto. When lifting, **the nurse must grasp the gait belt using an underhand grip during client transfers.** An underhand grip uses your big bicep muscles instead of your weak finger muscles!

When moving a patient from the bed to their wheelchair:
* **During a bed-to-wheelchair transfer, the wheelchair is positioned on the client's stronger side.** This lets them pivot on their good leg!
* **The nurse must lock the wheels of the bed before transferring a client.**
* And **the nurse must lock the wheels of the wheelchair before transferring a client.** If you forget to lock the wheels, the bed and chair will roll away like skateboards, and the patient will fall on the floor!

## 6. Tools for the Journey: Assistive Devices

Sometimes, patients need extra tools to walk around. Let’s learn the cool mechanics of canes, walkers, and crutches!

### Canes: The Third Leg
A cane gives you an extra leg for balance. **Quad canes provide a wider base of support than standard straight canes** because they have four little feet at the bottom instead of just one!

**Getting the Right Fit:**
* **The top of the cane must align with the client's greater trochanter.** (The greater trochanter is just the bony part of your hip!)
* Let's figure out how bent the arm should be:
  Step 1: Start with a straight arm, which is a 180-degree angle.
  Step 2: Bend the elbow slightly inward. 
  Step 3: **The client's elbow must be flexed at a 15- to 30-degree angle while holding a cane.**

**How to Use It:**
* This might sound backwards, but **a client must hold a cane on the unaffected side of the body.** This means holding it on the *good* side!
* **The Walking Sequence:**
  1. **When walking with a cane, the client first moves the cane forward.**
  2. **After moving the cane forward, the client steps forward with the affected leg.** (The hurt leg).
  3. **After stepping with the affected leg, the client steps forward past the cane with the unaffected leg.** (The strong leg powers forward!).

### Walkers: The Mobile Cage
Walkers are like carrying a little safety cage around with you! 

**Getting the Right Fit:**
* **A client must stand in the center of the walker during use.**
* **The top of the walker must align with the crease of the client's wrist.**

**How to Use It:**
* **To ambulate with a walker, the client first advances the walker forward.**
* **After advancing a walker, the client steps forward with the affected leg.**
* **After moving the affected leg into a walker, the client steps forward with the unaffected leg.**
* *Safety rule:* If the walker does not have wheels, **standard non-wheeled walkers must be completely lifted off the ground to move forward.** If you drag it, you will trip! 
* But what if it *does* have wheels? **Wheeled walkers are contraindicated for clients who have unpredictable balance issues.** "Contraindicated" means it's a bad idea—because if they lose their balance, the walker will just roll away from them!
* Getting up: **Clients must never pull on the walker to stand up from a seated position.** Walkers are super light and will tip over onto you! Instead, **clients must use the armrests of a chair for leverage when standing up to use a walker.**

### Crutches: The Pendulum Walk
Crutches let you swing around, but they take a lot of arm strength.

**Getting the Right Fit:**
* **Crutch pads must rest one to two inches below the client's axilla.** (Axilla means your armpit!).
* Why do we leave a gap under the armpit? Because **placing crutch pads directly into the axilla causes brachial plexus nerve damage.** It squishes the delicate nerves in your armpit and makes your arm go numb!
* Instead, **clients must bear their weight on the hand grips of the crutches.**
* **The hand grips of the crutches must allow for a 20- to 30-degree elbow flexion.**

**The Crutch Gaits (Walking Styles):**

| Gait Type | How It Works |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Two-Point Gait** | **The two-point crutch gait requires the client to move one crutch simultaneously with the opposite leg.** This looks like normal walking! **The two-point crutch gait requires partial weight-bearing status on both legs.** |
| **Three-Point Gait** | **The three-point crutch gait requires the client to bear all weight on the unaffected leg.** The hurt leg stays up in the air! **In the three-point crutch gait, the client moves both crutches forward simultaneously with the affected leg.** |
| **Four-Point Gait** | **The four-point crutch gait provides the maximum base of support.** It is super slow and steady. Let's look at the math for the 4 simple steps:<br>Step 1: **The four-point crutch gait begins by advancing one crutch forward.**<br>Step 2: **After advancing the first crutch in a four-point gait, the client moves the opposite foot forward.**<br>Step 3: **After moving the opposite foot forward in a four-point gait, the client advances the second crutch.**<br>Step 4: **After advancing the second crutch in a four-point gait, the client moves the remaining foot forward.** |
| **Swing-Through Gait** | **The swing-through crutch gait requires the client to advance both crutches simultaneously.** Then, **in the swing-through crutch gait, the client swings both legs forward past the crutches simultaneously.** |

**Mastering Stairs with Crutches:**
When using stairs, always remember: "Up with the good leg, down with the bad leg." Let's break down the steps!

* **Ascending (Going UP):**
  1. **When ascending stairs with crutches, the client places the unaffected leg onto the upper step first.** (The strong leg does the heavy lifting!).
  2. **After the unaffected leg is on the upper step, the client moves the crutches up to the upper step.**
  3. **After the unaffected leg is on the upper step, the client moves the affected leg up to the upper step.**

* **Descending (Going DOWN):**
  1. **When descending stairs with crutches, the client places the crutches onto the lower step first.** (The crutches catch you!).
  2. **After the crutches are on the lower step, the client moves the affected leg onto the lower step.**
  3. **After the affected leg is on the lower step, the client moves the unaffected leg onto the lower step.**
