## Part 1: Decoding and Hacking the Pain Signal

Pain isn’t just an "ouch." It’s the body's fire alarm system. Before we can turn off the alarm, we have to know how loud it is ringing. Since we can't see pain like a cut or a scrape, we have to be smart about how we measure it.

### 1. Measuring the Invisible: Pain Scales
Different people need different tools to tell us how much they hurt. We pick our measuring tool based on how old the patient is and how well their brain can communicate.

| Pain Scale | Who it is for | How it works |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Numeric Rating Scale** | Older kids and adults | The Numeric Rating Scale asks the client to rate pain intensity from zero to ten. A **pain score of zero represents no pain on the Numeric Rating Scale**, while a **pain score of ten represents the worst possible pain on the Numeric Rating Scale**. |
| **Wong-Baker FACES** | Kids and some adults | **The Wong-Baker FACES Pain Rating Scale uses line drawings of faces to assess pain**. This is great because **the Wong-Baker FACES Pain Rating Scale is appropriate for children as young as three years old**, and also **the Wong-Baker FACES Pain Rating Scale is appropriate for clients with cognitive impairments** (people whose brains might have trouble understanding tough questions). |
| **FLACC Scale** | Babies and people who cannot speak | An observing tool. **The FLACC behavioral pain assessment scale evaluates client facial expressions**, **evaluates client leg movements**, **evaluates client physical activity**, **evaluates client crying**, and **evaluates client consolability** (how easily you can comfort them). Because they can't talk, **the FLACC scale is used to assess pain in infants** and **the FLACC scale is used to assess pain in nonverbal clients**. |
| **PAINAD Scale** | People with severe memory loss | **The PAINAD scale evaluates pain in clients with advanced dementia** (a brain condition that makes people forget how to talk or do things). |
| **CRIES Scale** | Brand-new babies | **The CRIES scale is a pain assessment tool specifically designed for neonates** (newborns). |

### 2. Thermal Physics: The Power of Heat and Cold
Once we know the pain level, how do we fix it without medicine? We use temperature! 

**Heat therapy** is like opening up a highway for healing cells. **Heat therapy increases local blood flow to the application area**, and **heat therapy promotes muscle relaxation to relieve pain**. 

**Cold therapy** does the opposite; it shrinks things down. **Cold therapy causes vasoconstriction to reduce tissue edema** (which means it shrinks the blood tubes to stop swelling), and **cold therapy numbs the local area to decrease pain perception**. 

> **Important Temperature Rules:** 
> 1. Both **heat therapy applications must be limited to twenty minutes per session** AND **cold therapy applications must be limited to twenty minutes per session**. 
> 2. Why do we limit the cold? Because **prolonged application of cold therapy can cause tissue ischemia** (which means the cells get starved of oxygen and can die). 
> 3. Never put a block of ice right on the skin! **Ice packs must be wrapped in a protective cloth before skin application**.

### 3. Neurological Trickery: Hacking the Pain Pathways
Pain has to travel like a text message from your toe up to your brain. We can block that message!

*   **Cutaneous stimulation:** This **involves skin massage to block pain pathways** (like rubbing your elbow really fast right after you bump it; the rubbing feeling beats the pain feeling to the brain!).
*   **Transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation (TENS):** A cool little gadget that **delivers mild electrical currents to the skin to relieve pain**.
*   **Guided imagery:** A mental trick that **uses directed mental images to shift client attention away from pain** (like picturing your favorite beach).
*   **Distraction:** This **involves engaging the client in an alternate activity to reduce pain awareness** (like playing a video game so you forget your stomach hurts).

## Part 2: The Art and Science of ADLs and Hygiene

What makes us independent? Doing things for ourselves! When being sick takes that away, we step in to help with **activities of daily living** (ADLs). Remember these four big ones: **Bathing is considered an activity of daily living**, **dressing is considered an activity of daily living**, **toileting is considered an activity of daily living**, and **feeding is considered an activity of daily living**.

Before you grab a washcloth, you need to ask questions. **Assessing usual hygiene habits ensures nursing care aligns with client cultural preferences** and **assessing usual hygiene habits ensures nursing care aligns with client personal routines** (like if they prefer a shower before bed instead of in the morning). 

### The Mechanics of Bathing
You have to match the type of bath to how much energy the patient has:
*   **Complete bed bath:** This **involves washing the entire body of a dependent client in bed**. 
*   **Partial bed bath:** This is for a patient who gets tired easily. **A partial bed bath involves washing only areas that cause discomfort if left unwashed**, and **a partial bed bath involves washing only areas that cause odor if left unwashed** (usually the face, underarms, and bottom).
*   **Sitz bath:** A soothing bath that **immerses the pelvic region in warm water to soothe perineal tissues** (the bottom area).

### Perineal Care: The High-Stakes Geography
Keeping the private areas (perineal area) clean is a super important defense against bad germs.

For girls, **proper female perineal care requires wiping from the pubic area toward the anal area** (front to back). Why are we so strict about this? Because **wiping from front to back during female perineal care prevents the introduction of intestinal bacteria into the urethra** (the tube where pee comes out).

For boys, specifically those who are uncircumcised, **male perineal care for an uncircumcised client requires retracting the foreskin to wash the glans**. 

> **🚨 DANGER: THE FORESKIN RULE 🚨**
> **The foreskin of an uncircumcised male must be returned to the natural position immediately after cleaning**. Why? Because **failure to return the foreskin to the natural position after cleaning can cause tissue necrosis**. It acts like a tight rubber band, cutting off the blood supply to the skin until it dies. Never forget this!

### Oral Care: Defending the Airway and Mucosa
If a patient is knocked out (unconscious), they can't swallow their spit safely. So, **oral hygiene for an unconscious client requires positioning the client on the side**. Gravity helps us here—**positioning an unconscious client on the side during oral care prevents the aspiration of fluids** (getting water into their lungs). Also, because they breathe with their mouths open, **unconscious clients require oral care at least every two hours to maintain mucosal integrity** (keeping the inside of their mouth healthy and moist).

What about fake teeth? **Dentures must be brushed with a soft toothbrush** and **dentures must be brushed with a denture cleanser**. When they take them out, **dentures must be stored in water when not in the mouth to prevent warping** (bending out of shape).

### Diabetic Foot Care: Preventing Catastrophe
Diabetes is a condition that can damage blood vessels and numb the nerves in the feet. A tiny pebble in a shoe can cause a huge wound if they can't feel it! 
*   **Diabetic foot care requires daily inspection of the feet for cuts**. 
*   **Diabetic foot care requires daily inspection of the feet for blisters**. 
*   **Diabetic foot care requires daily inspection of the feet for redness**. 
*   Moisture is the enemy. **Clients with diabetes mellitus must dry the areas between the toes completely to prevent fungal infections**.
*   Because they can't feel if something is too hot, **clients with diabetes mellitus must avoid using heating pads on the feet due to decreased sensation**.
*   To keep nails from digging into the skin, **toenails of clients with diabetes mellitus must be filed straight across**.

## Part 3: The Architecture of Sleep

You can do everything else perfectly, but if the patient doesn't sleep, their body's battery will stay empty. **The circadian rhythm regulates the human sleep-wake cycle over a twenty-four-hour period** (it's your body's internal clock). 

**Normal human sleep cycles alternate between non-rapid eye movement sleep and rapid eye movement sleep**. Here is what they do:
1.  **Non-rapid eye movement sleep is primarily responsible for physical restoration** (fixing the body muscles and bones).
2.  **Rapid eye movement sleep is primarily responsible for cognitive restoration** (fixing the brain). Also, **rapid eye movement sleep is primarily responsible for memory consolidation** (acting like a hard drive, saving all the things you learned that day!).

**Healthy adults typically require seven to nine hours of sleep per night**. But as we get older, sleep changes: **older adults frequently experience a decrease in deep sleep stages**, which is why they might wake up more easily.

### The Chemistry of Sleep: Melatonin and the Modern World
**Melatonin is a naturally occurring hormone that regulates sleep-wake cycles** (it's the brain's "time for bed" chemical). But we live surrounded by glowing screens! **Exposure to blue light from electronic devices suppresses melatonin production**, and because of that, **suppressed melatonin production delays sleep onset** (making it take way longer to fall asleep).

### Engineering the Perfect Sleep Environment
Good sleep hygiene is just having awesome rules for sleep. **Good sleep hygiene includes maintaining a consistent bedtime every night** and **good sleep hygiene includes maintaining a consistent wake time every morning**. 

We also have to be careful with drinks:
*   **Caffeine consumption must be avoided for at least four hours before bedtime**.
*   While alcohol might seem to make people sleepy at first, **alcohol consumption before bedtime disrupts sleep continuity during the second half of the night**.

Finally, let's fix up the bedroom! **Lowering the room temperature is an environmental modification that promotes sleep**. You should make it quiet because **minimizing environmental noise promotes continuous sleep**, and **dimming bedroom lights helps facilitate sleep onset**. To wrap it all up, **creating a relaxing pre-sleep routine decreases physiological arousal to facilitate sleep onset** (meaning it calms your busy body down so you can snooze).
