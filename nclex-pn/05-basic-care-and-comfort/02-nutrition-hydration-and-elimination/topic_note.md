Welcome to the study of Nutrition, Hydration, and Elimination! Think about the [human body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_body) like a masterfully designed, dynamic engine. It needs fuel (nutrition), it needs a constant [circulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circulatory_system) of coolant (hydration), and it absolutely must clear out its exhaust and waste safely (elimination). 

As a [nurse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nurse), you are the chief engineer of this remarkable system. When it works perfectly, it’s a beautiful thing. But when a [valve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Valve) sticks, a pump slows down, or a pipe gets clogged, it’s your job to step in, understand *why* the mechanics are failing, and apply the perfect [physiological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physiology) fix. Let’s dive into the fascinating, elegant [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics) and [biology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology) of keeping your patients fueled, hydrated, and flowing!

***

## Part 1: The Input – [Swallowing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swallowing), [Diets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diet_%28nutrition%29), and the Perils of the [Airway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Respiratory_tract)

Normally, taking in fuel is simple. But what happens when the very mechanics of [swallowing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swallowing) fail? We call this **[dysphagia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dysphagia)**, which is simply the medical term for difficulty [swallowing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swallowing). 

Why do we care? Because the human throat is a busy intersection. The path to the [stomach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stomach) ([esophagus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Esophagus)) and the path to the [lungs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lung) ([trachea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trachea)) sit right next to each other. **[Dysphagia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dysphagia) significantly increases a client’s risk for [aspiration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulmonary_aspiration)**—the terrifying event where food or fluid drops into the [respiratory tract](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Respiratory_tract) instead of the [digestive tract](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gastrointestinal_tract). 

How do you spot this engine misfiring? Watch your client closely. **Signs of [dysphagia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dysphagia) include coughing during meals, pocketing food in the cheeks, and a wet-sounding voice** after [swallowing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swallowing). That "wet" sound is a red alarm; it means fluid is hovering right over the [vocal cords](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vocal_cords).

### The [Physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics) of Safe [Swallowing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swallowing)
If the [swallow reflex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swallowing) is weak, we have to use gravity and [anatomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anatomy) to our advantage. 
First, posture! **The [nurse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nurse) should instruct the client to remain upright for 30 minutes after oral meals.** 
Second, the maneuver: **Clients with [dysphagia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dysphagia) should [swallow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swallowing) with the chin tucked toward the chest.** Why does this work? It’s pure [anatomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anatomy). **The chin-tuck maneuver narrows the airway opening**, and naturally, **narrowing the airway opening reduces the risk of [aspiration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulmonary_aspiration) during [swallowing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swallowing).** You are physically closing the door to the [lungs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lung)!

### Modifying the Fuel: Liquid Consistencies
Thin liquids, like [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water), move fast. They are incredibly dangerous for a [dysphagia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dysphagia) patient because they splash right past the weak [swallow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swallowing) [muscles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muscle) and into the [lungs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lung). By adding resistance (viscosity), we slow the fluid down, giving the [muscles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muscle) time to react. Therefore, **thickened liquids are easier for clients with [dysphagia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dysphagia) to [swallow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swallowing) than thin liquids.**

| Consistency Level | [Physical Properties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_property) |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Thin** | [Water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water), coffee, plain [juice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Juice). (Moves rapidly, high [aspiration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulmonary_aspiration) risk). |
| **Nectar-thick** | Viscous enough that it **falls slowly from a spoon**, like [maple syrup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maple_syrup). |
| **Honey-thick** | Denser; it **drops from a spoon in a thick mass**. |
| **Pudding-thick** | Practically [solid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid). It **maintains its shape and must be eaten with a spoon**. |

### Diet Progressions: Texture is Everything
When a patient is recovering from illness or [surgery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surgery), we don't just hand them a steak. We introduce foods based on what the [gastrointestinal tract](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gastrointestinal_tract) can handle.

*   **Clear liquid diets**: Think "see-through." This **includes [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water), [broth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Broth), clear juices, and [gelatin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gelatin).** It leaves almost no residue in the gut.
*   **Full liquid diets**: A step up. It **includes all clear liquids plus [milk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milk), [pudding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pudding), and strained creamy [soups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soup).**
*   **[Pureed diet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pur%C3%A9e)**: For those who can't chew. It **consists of food blended to a smooth, pudding-like consistency.**
*   **Mechanical soft diet**: For those with mild chewing issues. It **includes ground meats, flaked fish, and soft cooked [vegetables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vegetable).** It’s essentially normal food that has been mechanically altered to require less jaw work.

## Part 2: The Bypass – Mastering [Enteral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enteral_administration) Tubes

Sometimes, the oral route is completely off-limits, but the gut still works perfectly. We have a saying: *"If the gut works, use it!"* To do this, we bypass the [mouth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mouth) with an [enteral feeding tube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feeding_tube). 

### Location, Location, Location!
You are sliding a tube blindly down a patient's throat. How do you know if it went into the [stomach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stomach), or if it took a wrong turn into the [lungs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lung)? This is a life-or-death question.

> **Crucial Rule:** **[Radiographic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiography) verification (an [X-ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray)) is the most reliable method to confirm initial [enteral feeding tube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feeding_tube) placement.** 

But you can't get an [X-ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray) every time you touch the tube. So, for ongoing checks, we use [chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemistry)—specifically, pH levels! **The [nurse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nurse) must check [enteral tube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feeding_tube) placement before administering any fluid, [medication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medication), or feeding.** You pull back a little fluid (aspirate) and test it:

*   **[Gastric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stomach) aspirate pH of less than 5.5 indicates correct gastric placement of an [enteral tube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feeding_tube).** The [stomach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stomach) is a vat of [hydrochloric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gastric_acid)!
*   If the tube is in the [lungs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lung), the pH will be much higher. **[Respiratory tract](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Respiratory_tract) aspirate typically has a pH greater than 6.0.**
*   If the tube slipped too far down into the [intestines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intestine), the pH shifts [alkaline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkalinity). **[Intestinal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intestine) aspirate typically has a pH greater than 7.0.**

### Keeping the Pipes Clean: The Magic of 30 mL
A clogged [feeding tube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feeding_tube) is a nightmare. It requires replacing the tube and putting the patient through the trauma of reinsertion. Prevent it with rigorous flushing!
*   **[Enteral tubes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feeding_tube) should be flushed with 30 mL of [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water) before [medication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medication) administration.**
*   **[Enteral tubes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feeding_tube) should be flushed with 30 mL of [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water) after [medication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medication) administration.**
*   If feeding around the clock, **[enteral tubes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feeding_tube) should be flushed with 30 mL of [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water) every 4 hours during continuous feeding.**

### The Dynamics of [Enteral Feeding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enteral_administration)
When you introduce the feed, you must respect the [biology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology) of the [stomach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stomach). 
First, [temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperature). **[Enteral feedings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enteral_administration) must be administered at room temperature.** Why? Because your core body temperature is warm. **Cold [enteral feedings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enteral_administration) can cause severe abdominal cramping** as the [stomach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stomach) aggressively spasms in response to the [shock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermal_shock).

Second, [hygiene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hygiene). Liquid nutrition is a perfect breeding ground for [bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacteria). Thus, **[enteral feeding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enteral_administration) bags and tubing must be changed every 24 hours to prevent bacterial growth.**

Third, capacity. Is the [stomach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stomach) actually emptying the food you are putting in? For continuous feeds, **gastric residual volume should be checked every 4 to 6 hours.** 
*   **A gastric residual volume exceeding 500 mL indicates delayed gastric emptying.** The [stomach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stomach) is full and backed up! 
*   Why is this an emergency? Because [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics) dictates that fluid takes the path of least resistance. If the [stomach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stomach) overflows, the fluid travels back up the [esophagus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Esophagus). Therefore, **delayed gastric emptying increases the risk of [pulmonary aspiration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulmonary_aspiration).**

Finally, gravity! Always use gravity to keep the food where it belongs. 
*   **The client's head of the bed must be elevated to at least 30 [degrees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_%28angle%29) during [enteral feeding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enteral_administration).**
*   For bolus meals, **the client's head of the bed must remain elevated for 30 to 60 minutes after intermittent [enteral feeding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enteral_administration).**

## Part 3: The Output – [Kidneys](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kidney), [Bladders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urinary_bladder), and Hydration

Now, let's look at the exhaust system. The [kidneys](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kidney) are magnificent filters, and their output is the ultimate gauge of the body's hydration and [cardiovascular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circulatory_system) [pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_pressure).

### The Magic Number: 30 mL/hr
If you remember one number about [urine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urine), make it this: **Normal [urine output](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urination) for an adult is approximately 30 mL per hour.** 

This is the baseline speed at which the [kidneys](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kidney) must run to clear [toxins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toxin). Therefore, **[urine output](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urination) less than 30 mL per hour indicates potential fluid volume deficit (dehydration/shock) or [renal impairment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kidney_failure).** If output drops below this, alarms in your head should go off!

### [Indwelling Catheters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foley_catheter): Fighting [Infection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infection) with Gravity
When a patient has a tube directly into their [bladder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urinary_bladder) (an [indwelling catheter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foley_catheter)), we have bypassed their natural defenses against [bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacteria). 

> **Crucial Rule:** **An [indwelling urinary catheter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foley_catheter) bag must be kept below the level of the client's [bladder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urinary_bladder).**

Why? Because **keeping the urinary catheter bag below the [bladder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urinary_bladder) prevents the backflow of [urine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urine).** Once [urine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urine) sits in that bag, it starts growing [bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacteria). If you lift the bag above the [bladder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urinary_bladder), that contaminated [urine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urine) flows right back into the sterile [bladder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urinary_bladder). **[Urine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urine) backflow into the [bladder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urinary_bladder) increases the risk of a [catheter-associated urinary tract infection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urinary_tract_infection) (CAUTI).** 

To further protect the patient, **catheter care should be performed at least every 8 hours using mild [soap](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soap) and [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water)** to clean the insertion site.

### Continuous Bladder Irrigation (CBI)
Imagine a patient just had prostate or [bladder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urinary_bladder) [surgery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surgery). The surgical site is going to bleed. If blood clots in the [bladder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urinary_bladder), it acts like a cork in a bottle, causing agonizing [urinary retention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urinary_retention). 

To solve this, we set up a dynamic wash system. **Bladder irrigation is used to flush blood clots from the [bladder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urinary_bladder) after genitourinary [surgery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surgery).** 
To do this simultaneously (putting fluid in while letting fluid out), **continuous bladder irrigation requires a three-way [urinary catheter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urinary_catheterization)** (one port for the balloon, one for fluid in, one for fluid out). 

How fast do you run the irrigation? It’s visual! **The rate of continuous bladder irrigation should be adjusted to keep the [urine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urine) drainage light pink.** If it's bright red, turn up the flow. If it's totally clear, you can slow it down.

## Part 4: The Output – Bowels, Enemas, and [Ostomies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ostomy)

Finally, we arrive at the [gastrointestinal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_gastrointestinal_tract) exhaust. The [intestines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intestine) are essentially a muscular conveyor belt moving waste out of the body. 

### Listening to the Gut
You assess this conveyor belt with your [stethoscope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stethoscope). **Normal bowel sounds occur every 5 to 15 seconds**—a happy, gurgling sound indicating peristalsis. 

But what if there is absolute silence? **Absent bowel sounds indicate a potential paralytic ileus**—a dangerous condition where the [intestinal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intestine) [muscles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muscle) have completely frozen, stopping the movement of waste. However, you cannot jump to this conclusion immediately. Because the gut can be momentarily quiet, **the [nurse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nurse) must listen for bowel sounds for a full 5 minutes before documenting them as absent.**

### The [Physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics) of Enemas
When a patient is severely [constipated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constipation), we must trigger the body's natural evacuation reflexes from the bottom up. **A cleansing enema stimulates bowel peristalsis through [intestinal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intestine) distention.** By filling the colon with fluid, we stretch the walls, which tricks the colon into contracting and pushing the waste out!

To align the [anatomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anatomy) properly (since the [descending colon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Descending_colon) sits on the left side of the body), **the client should be placed in the left lateral Sims position for enema administration.** 

Let's talk about [pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pressure). **The enema container should be held 12 to 18 inches above the client's rectum.** Gravity creates the water [pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluid_pressure). If the [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water) flows in too fast, the sudden distention causes painful spasms. The solution is simple [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics): **Lowering the enema container decreases the flow rate and reduces client cramping.** 

### [Ostomy Care](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ostomy): A Window to the Bowel
Sometimes, the lower bowel is bypassed entirely, and the [intestine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intestine) is brought directly to the abdominal wall—a [stoma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stoma_%28medicine%29). 
A [stoma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stoma_%28medicine%29) is literally exposed internal tissue, giving you a real-time look at your patient's vascular health. 
*   **Healthy [colostomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colostomy) [stomas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stoma_%28medicine%29) should appear beefy red and moist.** This means [blood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood) is flowing beautifully!
*   Conversely, **a pale or blue [colostomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colostomy) [stoma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stoma_%28medicine%29) indicates impaired blood perfusion to the bowel.** This is a [medical emergency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medical_emergency); the tissue is suffocating!

Managing the appliance is key to skin integrity. **An [ostomy pouch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ostomy_pouching_system) should be emptied when the pouch is one-third to one-half full.** If you wait until it is completely full, the weight of the waste will literally pull the adhesive off the skin, causing a caustic leak.

### Bowel Training: Routine and [Roughage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dietary_fiber)
For patients suffering from chronic bowel issues, we don't just want to rely on [medications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medication); we want to retrain the body's [biological clock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circadian_rhythm). 

**Bowel training programs require encouraging toileting at the same time every day.** The body loves a predictable rhythm. 
Specifically, **scheduling toileting 30 minutes after meals capitalizes on the gastrocolic reflex to promote [defecation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Defecation).** (This is the reflex where the [stomach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stomach) senses food and tells the colon, *"Make room, new cargo is coming!"*).

We must also ensure the waste itself is properly formed.
*   **Fluid intake should be maintained at 2000 to 3000 mL per day to prevent [constipation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constipation).** Think of a water slide—if there’s no [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water), nobody is sliding down!
*   Finally, **adequate [dietary fiber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dietary_fiber) increases fecal bulk and speeds transit time through the [intestines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intestine).** [Fiber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dietary_fiber) is the non-digestible scaffolding that sweeps the colon clean.

### The Professor's Summary
Whether you are dealing with a [pureed diet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pur%C3%A9e), a clogged [enteral tube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feeding_tube), a catheter bag, or an [ostomy pouch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ostomy_pouching_system), you are managing the extraordinary, interconnected plumbing of the [human body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_body). Respect gravity, utilize the body's natural reflexes, test your pH levels, and always, *always* ensure that what goes in is safe, and what comes out is effectively managed! Keep your eyes open, think about the [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics) behind the [physiology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physiology), and you will be an exceptional [nurse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nurse). Good luck on the [NCLEX](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NCLEX)!
