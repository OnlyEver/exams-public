Welcome to the study of [Nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nutrition), [Hydration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluid_replacement), and [Elimination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Excretion)! Think about the [human body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_body) like a masterfully designed, dynamic engine. It needs fuel ([nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nutrition)), it needs a constant [circulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circulatory_system) of coolant ([hydration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluid_replacement)), and it absolutely must clear out its exhaust and waste safely ([elimination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Excretion)). 

As a [nurse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nurse), you are the chief engineer of this remarkable system. When it works perfectly, it’s a beautiful thing. But when a [valve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Valve) sticks, a pump slows down, or a pipe gets clogged, it’s your job to step in, understand *why* the mechanics are failing, and apply the perfect [physiological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physiology) fix. Let’s dive into the fascinating, elegant [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics) and [biology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology) of keeping your patients fueled, hydrated, and flowing!

***

## Part 1: The Input – [Swallowing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swallowing), [Diets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diet_%28nutrition%29), and the Perils of the [Airway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Respiratory_tract)

Normally, taking in fuel is simple. But what happens when the very mechanics of [swallowing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swallowing) fail? We call this **[dysphagia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dysphagia)**, which is simply the medical term for difficulty [swallowing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swallowing). 

Why do we care? Because the human [throat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Throat) is a busy intersection. The path to the [stomach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stomach) ([esophagus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Esophagus)) and the path to the [lungs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lung) ([trachea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trachea)) sit right next to each other. **[Dysphagia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dysphagia) significantly increases a client’s risk for [aspiration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulmonary_aspiration)**—the terrifying event where food or fluid drops into the [respiratory tract](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Respiratory_tract) instead of the [digestive tract](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gastrointestinal_tract). 

![Sagittal view of the mouth and pharynx, illustrating the close anatomical proximity of the airway and esophagus which makes dysphagia a major risk for aspiration.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Sagittalmouth.png)

How do you spot this engine misfiring? Watch your client closely. **Signs of [dysphagia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dysphagia) include [coughing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cough) during meals, pocketing food in the cheeks, and a wet-sounding voice** after [swallowing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swallowing). That "wet" sound is a red alarm; it means fluid is hovering right over the [vocal cords](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vocal_cords).

![A barium swallow study visually capturing pulmonary aspiration, where material improperly enters the respiratory tract instead of passing safely into the stomach.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Aspiration_on_barium_swallow.jpg)

### The [Physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics) of Safe [Swallowing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swallowing)
If the [swallow reflex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swallowing) is weak, we have to use [gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity) and [anatomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anatomy) to our advantage. 
First, posture! **The [nurse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nurse) should instruct the client to remain upright for 30 minutes after oral meals.** 
Second, the maneuver: **Clients with [dysphagia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dysphagia) should [swallow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swallowing) with the chin tucked toward the chest.** Why does this work? It’s pure [anatomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anatomy). **The chin-tuck maneuver narrows the [airway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Airway) opening**, and naturally, **narrowing the [airway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Airway) opening reduces the risk of [aspiration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulmonary_aspiration) during [swallowing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swallowing).** You are physically closing the door to the [lungs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lung)!

### Modifying the Fuel: [Liquid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liquid) Consistencies
Thin liquids, like [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water), move fast. They are incredibly dangerous for a [dysphagia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dysphagia) patient because they splash right past the weak [swallow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swallowing) [muscles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muscle) and into the [lungs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lung). By adding resistance ([viscosity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viscosity)), we slow the fluid down, giving the [muscles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muscle) time to react. Therefore, **thickened liquids are easier for clients with [dysphagia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dysphagia) to [swallow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swallowing) than thin liquids.**

| Consistency Level | [Physical Properties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_property) |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Thin** | [Water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water), [coffee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coffee), plain [juice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Juice). (Moves rapidly, high [aspiration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulmonary_aspiration) risk). |
| **Nectar-thick** | Viscous enough that it **falls slowly from a [spoon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spoon)**, like [maple syrup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maple_syrup). |
| **Honey-thick** | Denser; it **drops from a [spoon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spoon) in a thick mass**. |
| **Pudding-thick** | Practically [solid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid). It **maintains its shape and must be eaten with a [spoon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spoon)**. |

### Diet Progressions: Texture is Everything
When a patient is recovering from illness or [surgery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surgery), we don't just hand them a steak. We introduce foods based on what the [gastrointestinal tract](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gastrointestinal_tract) can handle.

*   **[Clear liquid diets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clear_liquid_diet)**: Think "see-through." This **includes [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water), [broth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Broth), clear juices, and [gelatin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gelatin).** It leaves almost no residue in the gut.
*   **[Full liquid diets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liquid_diet)**: A step up. It **includes all clear liquids plus [milk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milk), [pudding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pudding), and strained creamy [soups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soup).**
*   **[Pureed diet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pur%C3%A9e)**: For those who can't chew. It **consists of food blended to a smooth, pudding-like consistency.**
*   **[Mechanical soft diet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soft_diet)**: For those with mild chewing issues. It **includes ground meats, flaked [fish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fish_as_food), and soft cooked [vegetables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vegetable).** It’s essentially normal food that has been mechanically altered to require less jaw work.

## Part 2: The Bypass – Mastering [Enteral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enteral_administration) Tubes

Sometimes, the oral route is completely off-limits, but the gut still works perfectly. We have a saying: *"If the gut works, use it!"* To do this, we bypass the [mouth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mouth) with an [enteral feeding tube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feeding_tube). 

![A patient utilizing a nasogastric (NG) feeding tube, effectively bypassing the oral cavity to deliver liquid nutrition directly into the gastrointestinal tract.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Man_with_NG_feeding_tube_wearing_a_zebra_baseball_cap.jpg)

### Location, Location, Location!
You are sliding a tube blindly down a patient's [throat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Throat). How do you know if it went into the [stomach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stomach), or if it took a wrong turn into the [lungs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lung)? This is a life-or-death question.

> **Crucial Rule:** **[Radiographic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiography) verification (an [X-ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray)) is the most reliable method to confirm initial [enteral feeding tube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feeding_tube) placement.** 

But you can't get an [X-ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray) every time you touch the tube. So, for ongoing checks, we use [chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemistry)—specifically, [pH levels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/pH)! **The [nurse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nurse) must check [enteral tube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feeding_tube) placement before administering any fluid, [medication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medication), or feeding.** You pull back a little fluid (aspirate) and test it:

*   **[Gastric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stomach) aspirate [pH](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/pH) of less than 5.5 indicates correct gastric placement of an [enteral tube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feeding_tube).** The [stomach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stomach) is a vat of [hydrochloric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gastric_acid)!
*   If the tube is in the [lungs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lung), the [pH](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/pH) will be much higher. **[Respiratory tract](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Respiratory_tract) aspirate typically has a [pH](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/pH) greater than 6.0.**
*   If the tube slipped too far down into the [intestines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intestine), the [pH](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/pH) shifts [alkaline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkalinity). **[Intestinal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intestine) aspirate typically has a [pH](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/pH) greater than 7.0.**

### Keeping the Pipes Clean: The Magic of 30 [mL](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Millilitre)
A clogged [feeding tube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feeding_tube) is a nightmare. It requires replacing the tube and putting the patient through the [trauma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_trauma) of reinsertion. Prevent it with rigorous flushing!
*   **[Enteral tubes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feeding_tube) should be flushed with 30 [mL](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Millilitre) of [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water) before [medication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medication) administration.**
*   **[Enteral tubes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feeding_tube) should be flushed with 30 [mL](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Millilitre) of [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water) after [medication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medication) administration.**
*   If feeding around the clock, **[enteral tubes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feeding_tube) should be flushed with 30 [mL](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Millilitre) of [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water) every 4 hours during continuous feeding.**

### The Dynamics of [Enteral Feeding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enteral_administration)
When you introduce the feed, you must respect the [biology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology) of the [stomach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stomach). 
First, [temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperature). **[Enteral feedings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enteral_administration) must be administered at [room temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Room_temperature).** Why? Because your [core body temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_human_body_temperature) is warm. **Cold [enteral feedings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enteral_administration) can cause severe abdominal [cramping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cramp)** as the [stomach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stomach) aggressively [spasms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spasm) in response to the [shock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermal_shock).

Second, [hygiene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hygiene). Liquid [nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nutrition) is a perfect breeding ground for [bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacteria). Thus, **[enteral feeding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enteral_administration) bags and tubing must be changed every 24 hours to prevent [bacterial growth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacterial_growth).**

Third, capacity. Is the [stomach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stomach) actually emptying the food you are putting in? For continuous feeds, **gastric residual volume should be checked every 4 to 6 hours.** 
*   **A gastric residual volume exceeding 500 [mL](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Millilitre) indicates [delayed gastric emptying](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gastroparesis).** The [stomach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stomach) is full and backed up! 
*   Why is this an emergency? Because [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics) dictates that fluid takes the [path of least resistance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Path_of_least_resistance). If the [stomach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stomach) overflows, the fluid travels back up the [esophagus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Esophagus). Therefore, **[delayed gastric emptying](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gastroparesis) increases the risk of [pulmonary aspiration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulmonary_aspiration).**

Finally, [gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity)! Always use [gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity) to keep the food where it belongs. 
*   **The client's head of the bed must be elevated to at least 30 [degrees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_%28angle%29) during [enteral feeding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enteral_administration).**
*   For [bolus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bolus_%28medicine%29) meals, **the client's head of the bed must remain elevated for 30 to 60 minutes after intermittent [enteral feeding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enteral_administration).**

## Part 3: The Output – [Kidneys](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kidney), [Bladders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urinary_bladder), and [Hydration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluid_replacement)

Now, let's look at the exhaust system. The [kidneys](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kidney) are magnificent filters, and their output is the ultimate gauge of the body's [hydration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluid_replacement) and [cardiovascular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circulatory_system) [pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_pressure).

### The Magic Number: 30 [mL](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Millilitre)/hr
If you remember one number about [urine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urine), make it this: **Normal [urine output](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urination) for an adult is approximately 30 [mL](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Millilitre) per hour.** 

This is the baseline speed at which the [kidneys](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kidney) must run to clear [toxins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toxin). Therefore, **[urine output](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urination) less than 30 [mL](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Millilitre) per hour indicates potential fluid volume deficit ([dehydration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dehydration)/[shock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shock_%28circulatory%29)) or [renal impairment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kidney_failure).** If output drops below this, alarms in your head should go off!

### [Indwelling Catheters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foley_catheter): Fighting [Infection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infection) with [Gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity)
When a patient has a tube directly into their [bladder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urinary_bladder) (an [indwelling catheter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foley_catheter)), we have bypassed their natural defenses against [bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacteria). 

> **Crucial Rule:** **An [indwelling urinary catheter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foley_catheter) bag must be kept below the level of the client's [bladder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urinary_bladder).**

Why? Because **keeping the urinary catheter bag below the [bladder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urinary_bladder) prevents the backflow of [urine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urine).** Once [urine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urine) sits in that bag, it starts growing [bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacteria). If you lift the bag above the [bladder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urinary_bladder), that contaminated [urine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urine) flows right back into the sterile [bladder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urinary_bladder). **[Urine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urine) backflow into the [bladder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urinary_bladder) increases the risk of a [catheter-associated urinary tract infection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urinary_tract_infection) (CAUTI).** 

To further protect the patient, **catheter care should be performed at least every 8 hours using mild [soap](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soap) and [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water)** to clean the insertion site.

![Cross-sectional view of a Foley catheter showing the inflatable balloon used to securely anchor the tube within the bladder, bypassing the body's natural barriers to infection.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Section_cut_-_Foley_cath.jpg)

### Continuous Bladder Irrigation (CBI)
Imagine a patient just had [prostate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prostate) or [bladder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urinary_bladder) [surgery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surgery). The surgical site is going to bleed. If [blood clots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thrombus) in the [bladder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urinary_bladder), it acts like a cork in a bottle, causing agonizing [urinary retention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urinary_retention). 

To solve this, we set up a dynamic wash system. **Bladder irrigation is used to flush [blood clots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thrombus) from the [bladder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urinary_bladder) after [genitourinary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genitourinary_system) [surgery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surgery).** 
To do this simultaneously (putting fluid in while letting fluid out), **continuous bladder irrigation requires a three-way [urinary catheter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urinary_catheterization)** (one port for the balloon, one for fluid in, one for fluid out). 

How fast do you run the irrigation? It’s visual! **The rate of continuous bladder irrigation should be adjusted to keep the [urine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urine) drainage light pink.** If it's bright red, turn up the flow. If it's totally clear, you can slow it down.

## Part 4: The Output – [Bowels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Large_intestine), [Enemas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enema), and [Ostomies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ostomy)

Finally, we arrive at the [gastrointestinal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_gastrointestinal_tract) exhaust. The [intestines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intestine) are essentially a muscular conveyor belt moving waste out of the body. 

### Listening to the Gut
You assess this conveyor belt with your [stethoscope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stethoscope). **Normal [bowel sounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bowel_sound) occur every 5 to 15 seconds**—a happy, gurgling sound indicating [peristalsis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peristalsis). 

But what if there is absolute silence? **Absent [bowel sounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bowel_sound) indicate a potential [paralytic ileus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ileus)**—a dangerous condition where the [intestinal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intestine) [muscles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muscle) have completely frozen, stopping the movement of waste. However, you cannot jump to this conclusion immediately. Because the gut can be momentarily quiet, **the [nurse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nurse) must listen for [bowel sounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bowel_sound) for a full 5 minutes before documenting them as absent.**

### The [Physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics) of [Enemas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enema)
When a patient is severely [constipated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constipation), we must trigger the body's natural evacuation [reflexes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflex) from the bottom up. **A cleansing [enema](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enema) stimulates bowel [peristalsis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peristalsis) through [intestinal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intestine) distention.** By filling the [colon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Large_intestine) with fluid, we stretch the walls, which tricks the [colon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Large_intestine) into contracting and pushing the waste out!

To align the [anatomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anatomy) properly (since the [descending colon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Descending_colon) sits on the left side of the body), **the client should be placed in the left lateral [Sims position](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sims%27_position) for [enema](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enema) administration.** 

Let's talk about [pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pressure). **The [enema](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enema) container should be held 12 to 18 inches above the client's [rectum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectum).** [Gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity) creates the water [pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluid_pressure). If the [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water) flows in too fast, the sudden distention causes painful [spasms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spasm). The solution is simple [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics): **Lowering the [enema](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enema) container decreases the flow rate and reduces client [cramping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cramp).** 

![An enema bag system suspended to utilize gravity; lowering the height of the bag alters the flow rate and effectively manages the water pressure to prevent painful spasms.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Barium_Contrast_Enema.jpg)

### [Ostomy Care](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ostomy): A Window to the Bowel
Sometimes, the lower bowel is bypassed entirely, and the [intestine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intestine) is brought directly to the [abdominal wall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abdominal_wall)—a [stoma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stoma_%28medicine%29). 
A [stoma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stoma_%28medicine%29) is literally exposed internal tissue, giving you a real-time look at your patient's vascular health. 
*   **Healthy [colostomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colostomy) [stomas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stoma_%28medicine%29) should appear beefy red and moist.** This means [blood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood) is flowing beautifully!
*   Conversely, **a pale or blue [colostomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colostomy) [stoma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stoma_%28medicine%29) indicates impaired [blood perfusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perfusion) to the bowel.** This is a [medical emergency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medical_emergency); the tissue is suffocating!

![Diagram illustrating different locations for a colostomy; regardless of placement, a healthy stoma must always appear moist and beefy red, indicating excellent blood perfusion.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Blausen_0247_Colostomy.png)

Managing the appliance is key to skin integrity. **An [ostomy pouch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ostomy_pouching_system) should be emptied when the pouch is one-third to one-half full.** If you wait until it is completely full, the weight of the waste will literally pull the adhesive off the skin, causing a caustic leak.

### [Bowel Training](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bowel_management): Routine and [Roughage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dietary_fiber)
For patients suffering from chronic bowel issues, we don't just want to rely on [medications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medication); we want to retrain the body's [biological clock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circadian_rhythm). 

**[Bowel training](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bowel_management) programs require encouraging [toileting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toilet) at the same time every day.** The body loves a predictable rhythm. 
Specifically, **scheduling [toileting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toilet) 30 minutes after meals capitalizes on the [gastrocolic reflex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gastrocolic_reflex) to promote [defecation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Defecation).** (This is the reflex where the [stomach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stomach) senses food and tells the [colon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Large_intestine), *"Make room, new cargo is coming!"*).

We must also ensure the waste itself is properly formed.
*   **Fluid intake should be maintained at 2000 to 3000 [mL](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Millilitre) per day to prevent [constipation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constipation).** Think of a water slide—if there’s no [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water), nobody is sliding down!
*   Finally, **adequate [dietary fiber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dietary_fiber) increases fecal bulk and speeds transit time through the [intestines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intestine).** [Fiber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dietary_fiber) is the non-digestible scaffolding that sweeps the [colon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Large_intestine) clean.

![Foods naturally rich in dietary fiber, such as fruits, vegetables, and grains, are essential in bowel training programs to provide optimal fecal bulk and promote healthy peristalsis.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Fruit%2C_Vegetables_and_Grain_NCI_Visuals_Online.jpg)

### The Professor's Summary
Whether you are dealing with a [pureed diet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pur%C3%A9e), a clogged [enteral tube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feeding_tube), a catheter bag, or an [ostomy pouch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ostomy_pouching_system), you are managing the extraordinary, interconnected plumbing of the [human body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_body). Respect [gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity), utilize the body's natural [reflexes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflex), test your [pH levels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/pH), and always, *always* ensure that what goes in is safe, and what comes out is effectively managed! Keep your eyes open, think about the [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics) behind the [physiology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physiology), and you will be an exceptional [nurse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nurse). Good luck on the [NCLEX](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NCLEX)!
