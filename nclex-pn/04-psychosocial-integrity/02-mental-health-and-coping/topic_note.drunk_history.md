Listen to me, Jerry. Stop looking at the jukebox and listen. The human brain isn't just some textbook diagram. It is a chaotic, high-stakes, cutthroat reality TV restaurant franchise run by rival mob families! When the kitchen is running smoothly, it's Michelin-star quality. But when the staff completely loses their minds, you, as the practical nurse health inspector, need to know exactly what kind of disaster you are looking at! 

When you walk into that restaurant, before anyone even hands you a menu, your assessment begins. Client appearance assessment includes observing personal hygiene and grooming. If the formerly immaculate head chef suddenly stumbles out of the kitchen with mashed potatoes in his unwashed hair and a filthy apron, pay attention! A sudden deterioration in personal hygiene can indicate a worsening mental health condition. His executive functioning kitchen has completely shut down!

Then you ask the chef how he’s doing. He smiles and says, "I am absolutely thrilled." That’s his mood. Mood is a self-reported subjective emotional state. It's his personal, highly biased Yelp review. But you look closely at his face, and his eyes are dead and staring blankly at a wall. That’s his affect. Affect is the objective observation of an emotional expression—it is what the health inspector actually sees! Sometimes the mismatch is wild. A blunted affect involves a significant reduction in the intensity of emotional expression. Imagine a waitress dropping a massive, six-tier wedding cake onto the floor and just giving a tiny, half-hearted shrug. Or worse, the emotional thermostat completely breaks! A labile affect involves rapid and abrupt changes in emotional expression. One second she’s sobbing uncontrollably over the ruined cake, then laughing hysterically at the icing on the ceiling, then screaming in a blinding rage at the baker, all in ten seconds!

And watch their bodies, Jerry! Look at the physical tension on the floor. Psychomotor agitation involves excessive motor activity associated with internal tension. The kitchen is practically vibrating with unreleased stress. Pacing is a behavioral indicator of psychomotor agitation, just marching back and forth by the stoves like a caged tiger, and hand-wringing is a behavioral indicator of psychomotor agitation, like they are trying to physically wring the stress right out of their own fingers. Conversely, sometimes the kitchen just bogs down in the mud. Psychomotor retardation involves a visible slowing of physical movements and speech. They are chopping carrots in slow motion, talking... like... they... are... underwater. 

Sometimes the behavior gets downright weird. You ask the busboy, "Need a towel?" and he just stares at you and says, "Need a towel, need a towel." Echolalia is a psychomotor behavior involving the meaningless repetition of another person's spoken words. Or you scratch your nose, and the hostess scratches her nose the exact same way. Echopraxia is a psychomotor behavior involving the meaningless imitation of another person's movements. They're just bizarrely mirroring the chaos around them!

Jerry, slide that bowl of peanuts over here, because we need to talk about the rival restaurant owners. First, the sad owner down the street. Major depressive disorder is characterized by a persistently depressed mood lasting at least two weeks. It's not just a bad dinner service; it is a total, weeks-long corporate bankruptcy of the soul. Anhedonia is the inability to experience pleasure from normally enjoyable activities. You hand him his absolute favorite, world-class truffles, and he tastes nothing but wet cardboard. The whole system goes haywire: symptoms of major depressive disorder include changes in appetite—he's either eating the whole pantry or starving himself—and profound changes in sleep patterns, sleeping all day in the walk-in cooler. There's also a diminished ability to concentrate; he can't even read a simple, three-step recipe. 

Now, look at me, because this is life or death. Suicidal ideation is a critical symptom of major depressive disorder requiring immediate assessment. You have to ask them point-blank if they are planning to close the restaurant permanently, if you catch my drift. And here is the most terrifying trap: a sudden improvement in the mood of a severely depressed client can indicate a decision to commit suicide. They seem incredibly peaceful, not because the kitchen is magically fixed, but because they’ve secretly decided to burn the whole building down tonight, and the agonizing inner conflict is finally over. Do not turn your back on that sudden sunshine!

Then there's the other owner. The wild one. Bipolar disorder includes depressive episodes in addition to manic episodes. He crashes into those dark depressions too, but then he slingshots into the stratosphere! Mania is characterized by a persistently elevated or irritable mood lasting at least one week. The kitchen is actively on fire, but he thinks it's a glorious fireworks show! Symptoms of mania include a decreased need for sleep—he sleeps two hours and wakes up ready to conquer the culinary world. He has absolute grandiosity or inflated self-esteem, genuinely believing he's the secret child of Gordon Ramsay and the King of France. Add in rapid and pressured speech; he is talking so intensely and aggressively about his new menu that you literally cannot interrupt him. His brain is firing so fast that flight of ideas is a symptom of mania involving rapid shifting between unrelated topics. "We are serving duck! Have you seen my shoes? The government is hiding the good paprika!"

Across town is the avant-garde diner. Complete disconnect from reality. Schizophrenia is a chronic mental disorder characterized by psychotic symptoms. We divide this into two menus: Positive and Negative. Positive symptoms of schizophrenia involve the presence of abnormal behaviors. It means adding bizarre, impossible ingredients to reality. Hallucinations are a positive symptom of schizophrenia—he is hearing non-existent food critics whispering from the ventilation shafts. Delusions are a positive symptom of schizophrenia, which are fixed, false beliefs. Delusions of persecution involve a false belief of being harmed or harassed ("The rival mob poisoned my flour!"). Delusions of grandeur involve an exaggerated sense of power or importance ("I am the sole inventor of the concept of breakfast!"). Wait, what if he sees a normal mop in the corner and screams that it's an assassin? That's not a hallucination, Jerry. Illusions are misperceptions of real external stimuli. The mop is actually there, his brain just completely scrambled the video feed!

Now, the other side of the menu. Negative symptoms of schizophrenia involve the absence of normal behaviors. Normal things are stripped entirely away from the kitchen. Alogia is a negative symptom of schizophrenia characterized by reduced speech output—getting a single word out of the waiter is like pulling teeth. Avolition is a negative symptom of schizophrenia characterized by a lack of motivation—the cooks just sit on crates, completely unable to start a task. Flat affect is a negative symptom of schizophrenia involving reduced emotional expression—total dead-eyed robot faces. 

And their language? A total linguistic blender. Neologisms are made-up words that have meaning only to the person using them ("Pass the flimflam!"). Clang associations are groupings of words based on rhyming sounds ("Bake the cake, take the steak, jump in the lake!"). And word salad is a disorganized mix of words with no logical connection ("Carrot blue sky running toaster forever"). It’s a literal linguistic food fight!

Now, what about the terrified kitchen staff? Generalized anxiety disorder involves excessive and uncontrollable worry lasting at least six months. They are constantly, endlessly terrified the health inspector is coming, every single day, for half a year! Sometimes the pressure cooker explodes. Panic attacks are sudden episodes of intense fear accompanied by physical symptoms. They think they are having a massive heart attack right by the grill because the physical symptoms of a panic attack include palpitations and shortness of breath. Some staff get so inexplicably terrified they won't even leave the kitchen. Agoraphobia is an intense fear of places or situations where escape might be difficult. They think the dining room is an inescapable trap! 

Look at the pastry chef over there. Obsessions are recurrent and persistent thoughts that cause anxiety. His brain screams, "The dough is contaminated!" over and over in a horrific loop. To fix this unbearable anxiety, he acts. Compulsions are repetitive behaviors performed in response to an obsession. He absolutely must wash his rolling pin exactly 47 times before he can touch the dough, or the world will end! And the veteran fry-cook? Post-traumatic stress disorder develops after exposure to a traumatic event, like a horrific grease fire years ago. His brain never logged the memory as "past tense." So, flashbacks are a symptom of post-traumatic stress disorder involving reliving the traumatic event. Someone drops a metal tray—BANG—and he is literally reliving the fire in present time, diving under the counter in pure, unadulterated terror.

Let's look at the management. The general manager has a massive, chaotic reality TV personality. Borderline personality disorder involves a pattern of instability in interpersonal relationships and self-image. Every day is a dramatic breakup or a passionate reunion! To survive this emotional whiplash, she uses a specific, hardcore armor. Splitting is a defense mechanism commonly used by individuals with borderline personality disorder. How does it work? Splitting involves viewing people or situations in all-or-nothing terms. You are either her glorious savior who can do no wrong, or a demonic saboteur trying to ruin her life. There is absolutely no middle ground! But the owner of the building? Ice cold. Antisocial personality disorder involves a persistent disregard for the rights of others. He will steal the waiters' tips, forge the safety logs, and ruin lives without a microscopic drop of remorse. He's a shark in a tailored suit!

How do the normal staff survive this nightmare, Jerry? They use shields. Conscious and unconscious ones. Defense mechanisms are mostly unconscious psychological strategies used to protect individuals from anxiety. The brain lies to itself so it doesn't shatter! Denial is a defense mechanism involving the refusal to accept reality. ("The restaurant isn't bankrupt, we are just aggressively rebranding!") Projection is a defense mechanism involving attributing unacceptable internal feelings to another person. ("I don't secretly want to quit, YOU want to quit!") Displacement is a defense mechanism involving redirecting emotions from a threatening target to a safer target. (The head chef screams at the sous chef, so the sous chef goes out back and kicks the dumpster). Rationalization is a defense mechanism involving the creation of logical excuses for unacceptable behavior. ("I only drank on the job because the oysters were late!") Regression is a defense mechanism involving reverting to an earlier stage of development under stress. (The grown-up sommelier sitting on the floor throwing a literal toddler tantrum). Repression is a defense mechanism involving unconsciously blocking unwanted memories from conscious awareness. (Completely, genuinely forgetting the horrific health inspection that got them shut down). Sublimation is a defense mechanism involving channeling unacceptable impulses into socially acceptable activities. (You have raging, violent anger issues? Great, you are now the primary meat tenderizer. Smash that veal!). Reaction formation is a defense mechanism involving expressing the opposite of an internal true feeling. (You violently despise the hostess, but when she walks in, you shower her with flowers and say she's your favorite person in the entire world).

Then there are conscious choices. Coping mechanisms are conscious strategies used to manage internal and external stressful situations. Effective coping mechanisms reduce stress without creating new problems. Seeking social support is an example of an effective coping mechanism—like venting to your buddy at a bar! Problem-solving is an example of an effective coping mechanism—like actually picking up a wrench and fixing the broken freezer. But the bad ones? Ineffective coping mechanisms provide temporary stress reduction. Sure, they numb the pain for a minute, but ineffective coping mechanisms create long-term negative consequences. Substance abuse is an example of an ineffective coping mechanism. You drink the stress away, but now you have a hangover and no job. Avoidance is an example of an ineffective coping mechanism. You just ignore the giant stack of unpaid meat invoices until the repo men show up at the door!

Finally, when we call in the chemical exterminators—the psychiatric medications—to fix these chaotic restaurants, there is collateral damage. Neurological side effects! Tardive dyskinesia is a medication-induced movement disorder involving involuntary facial and jaw movements. The patient starts uncontrollably smacking their lips or thrusting their tongue like they are chewing invisible gum. And worse, akathisia is a medication-induced movement disorder characterized by an intense feeling of inner restlessness. The dishwasher literally feels like his bones are on fire; he cannot stop pacing, shifting, and jumping out of his skin. It's not worsening anxiety, Jerry, it's the medication!
