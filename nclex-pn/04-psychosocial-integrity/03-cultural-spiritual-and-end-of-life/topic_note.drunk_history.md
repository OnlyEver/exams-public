Listen to me, slide that peanut bowl over here and pay attention, because what I’m about to tell you is the absolute secret sauce of nursing. You think healthcare is just about plugging IVs into arms like you're playing some biological video game? Absolutely not! Taking care of humans is like managing a high-stakes, five-family mob summit that's being filmed for a reality TV show. Every single person who walks through those hospital doors brings an invisible, iron-clad rulebook with them—their culture and their spirit. If you stomp on their rulebook, the whole deal goes up in flames.

To survive the madness and actually heal people, you have to be a culturally competent practical nurse, which means you actively adjust the plan of care to accommodate a client's specific cultural practices. You become the ultimate VIP concierge. But how do you know what they want? You ask them! Cultural assessments in nursing practice must be based on the client's self-reported cultural identity. You cannot just look at a patient's last name or skin color and guess their storyline. That is cultural stereotyping, which involves making assumptions about a client based on their racial or ethnic background. When you stereotype, you are basically writing a fake script for a reality star, and it completely prevents individualized nursing care. 

And whatever you do, do not be the arrogant Head Chef who thinks his greasy diner menu is the absolute pinnacle of culinary evolution. That is ethnocentrism, the toxic belief that one's own cultural beliefs and practices are superior to those of other cultures. As a professional, you have to check your ego at the door; healthcare providers must avoid ethnocentrism to provide unbiased client care. 

So, you pull up a chair to take their "VIP order"—which is your cultural assessment. First, a cultural assessment includes asking clients about their preferences for dietary modifications, because food isn't just fuel, it's identity! Next, a cultural assessment involves identifying the client's preferred language for medical communication. If they don't speak your language, you do not—I repeat, do not—pull their anxious teenage daughter out of the waiting room to translate. Family members should not be used as medical interpreters for clients due to potential translation inaccuracies. They will sugarcoat the bad news to protect their loved one! Instead, the use of professional medical interpreters is absolutely required for clients with limited English proficiency. You bring in the neutral, professional lawyers of language.

Now, let's talk about the physical boundaries, because modesty requirements differ significantly among various cultures. To some, the body is a fortress. Certain cultures require same-sex healthcare providers for physical assessments due to modesty beliefs. If a female patient needs a female nurse, you make the trade with your coworker. Even the way we look at each other is a minefield, because eye contact norms vary significantly across different cultural groups. In our culture, staring someone down means you're being honest. But direct eye contact is considered a sign of disrespect in some Native American and Asian cultures! If they are looking at the floor while you talk, they aren't ignoring you; they might be giving you the highest respect of the house.

And when things get rough, you have to understand the culture of pain. Understanding cultural beliefs about pain is necessary for effective pain management. Pain is a totally subjective reality show. On one channel, some cultures view stoicism during pain as a sign of inner strength. They will sit there with a shattered femur, looking like a stone statue. On the other channel, some cultures encourage the open and vocal expression of pain, screaming and crying as a totally appropriate way to let the agony out. You have to assess the physical reality, not just the volume of the broadcast!

When illness gets severe, it rattles the very foundation of a patient’s universe, causing spiritual distress. This occurs when a client's belief system is challenged by illness or suffering. They start asking the big, heavy questions. To catch this, a spiritual assessment involves asking the client if faith plays a role in their healthcare decisions. 

If it does, you better know the specific "hospitality riders" for these different belief systems. For instance, medical treatments must be adapted to align with a client's religious fasting practices when safely possible. If they can only eat at night, you move their daily pill to midnight! And there are strict rules: Jehovah's Witnesses generally refuse the transfusion of whole blood or primary blood components. You don't try to sneak it in; you use alternative fluids. What about food? Many Muslim clients require a halal diet, and Islamic faith practices may require a client to face Mecca during daily prayers. Make sure their bed can accommodate that! Then you have the kitchen rules: many Orthodox Jewish clients adhere to a strict kosher diet, and you better memorize that a kosher diet prohibits the mixing of dairy and meat at the same meal. Do not put a slice of cheddar on that beef patty, or you've ruined the whole plate! Timing is also everything: Seventh-Day Adventists typically observe the Sabbath from Friday sunset to Saturday sunset, so you keep non-emergency interruptions to an absolute minimum. And when the chips are truly down, some Roman Catholic clients may request the Sacrament of the Sick during serious illness. You get the priest on the phone, immediately.

Speaking of the chips being down, let’s talk about the final act of the show: end-of-life care. Sometimes, the grief starts before the curtain even closes. Anticipatory grieving is the process of mourning a loss before the loss actually occurs. It’s like mourning the cancellation of your favorite show before the finale even airs, and remember, anticipatory grieving can be experienced by both the dying client and the client's family members. 

We have two different ways to manage this final season, and you cannot mix them up. Palliative care focuses on relieving symptoms and improving the quality of life for clients with serious illnesses. The magic of palliative care is that it can be provided concurrently with curative medical treatments. You can fight the dragon with a sword while we give you a magical shield for the pain. But hospice care is different. Hospice care is specifically designed for clients with a terminal diagnosis and a life expectancy of six months or less. At this point, we put the sword away. Hospice care prioritizes comfort over curative medical interventions. 

When a patient faces the end, their brain goes through an absolute rollercoaster. The Kubler-Ross model identifies five stages of grief, and the five stages of grief in the Kubler-Ross model are denial, anger, bargaining, depression, and acceptance. But listen to me: clients do not necessarily experience the five stages of grief in a linear sequence! It is a chaotic pinball machine! One minute they are in denial, which involves the client rejecting the reality of a terminal diagnosis. ("The labs are lying!") The next minute they bounce to anger, and anger during the grief process may be directed at healthcare providers or family members. Do not take it personally when they yell at you! Then they might try bargaining, which involves the client attempting to negotiate for more time or a cure. ("Just let me live to see my daughter's wedding.") Then comes the heavy drop into depression, which during the grief process involves deep sadness over the impending loss. Finally, they might reach acceptance, which involves the client acknowledging the reality of the impending death and finding peace.

Your job as the nurse during this chaotic finale isn't to fix the unfixable. You use active listening, which is a therapeutic communication technique used to support grieving clients. Let them talk! And sometimes you just shut up entirely; providing presence involves sitting quietly with a dying client to offer emotional support. 

But while you are sitting there, your clinical brain must remain sharp. The practical nurse must assess a dying client's physical comfort continuously. Pain management is a primary physiological need during end-of-life care. We do not worry about creating addicts in hospice; we worry about suffering. Another huge issue is breathing; dyspnea is a common physical symptom experienced by clients at the end of life. They feel like they can't catch their breath, and you have to manage it aggressively.

Throughout all of this, the ultimate VIP guest list is the advance directive. An advance directive is a legal document outlining a client's end-of-life medical preferences. It is legally binding, and the healthcare team must honor the specifications detailed in a client's advance directive. If they have a Do Not Resuscitate order, that completely prohibits cardiopulmonary resuscitation in the event of cardiac or respiratory arrest. If their heart stops, you become the bouncer who blocks the crash cart from entering the room. You let nature take its course.

When that final breath happens, your job isn't over. First, the practical nurse provides emotional support to the family immediately following a client's death. You hold the space for their devastation. Then, you turn your attention to the body. Postmortem care includes cleaning the deceased client's body according to facility policy and cultural practices. The culture doesn't disappear just because the heartbeat did! For example, some Islamic traditions require the deceased body to be washed only by individuals of the same sex. And Orthodox Jewish traditions may require that the deceased body not be left alone until burial. You ensure someone sits vigil with them. 

You honor their culture in life, you protect their dignity in death, and you survive the madness. Now finish your ginger ale, because your shift starts in ten minutes!
