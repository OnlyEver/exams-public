Listen to me, grab your drink, and pay close attention because stepping onto a substance use disorder unit is exactly like walking onto the set of the most unhinged, high-stakes reality TV show ever created, entirely run by rival mob families. You are the sole producer trying to keep the cast alive and the cameras rolling. Before the cameras even start rolling, you have to pat down the incoming cast. Nurses should assess for hidden illicit substances or drug paraphernalia upon admission of a client with a substance use disorder, because these folks will try to smuggle their favorite toxic castmates right past security. And while you are doing that, look at them! They’ve been surviving on pure drama and chemical chaos for months, meaning nutritional status must be assessed and managed in clients with substance use disorders due to common dietary neglect. You have to feed them a real meal before you can negotiate with them. 

Now, let me introduce you to the reigning mafia dons of the brain: the Depressants. These guys—Alcohol and Benzodiazepines—run a massive, heavy-handed protection racket over the central nervous system, aggressively suppressing everything to keep the city quiet. But what happens if the Alcohol Boss suddenly gets whacked and goes missing? The brain’s henchmen absolutely panic. You can set a watch to this mutiny: alcohol withdrawal symptoms typically begin within 4 to 12 hours after the last drink. The body realizes the boss is gone, and absolute chaos erupts. Signs of alcohol withdrawal include tremors, tachycardia, diaphoresis, and elevated blood pressure—the body is literally sweating, shaking, and redlining because it has no idea how to operate without its chemical dictator. 

To keep the mutiny from tearing the whole city apart, you have to bring in a temporary boss to smooth the transition. We use an incredibly strict HR spreadsheet for this: The Clinical Institute Withdrawal Assessment for Alcohol scale dictates the dosage of benzodiazepines administered to a client. Depending on how bad the henchmen are freaking out, that scale tells you exactly how much severance pay to hand out. What kind of severance? Well, chlordiazepoxide is a long-acting benzodiazepine commonly used to prevent seizures during alcohol withdrawal. It’s the smooth-talking corporate fixer who slowly cools down the panicked brain cells. If you fail to manage this, the mutiny goes nuclear. Delirium tremens is a severe form of alcohol withdrawal characterized by hallucinations, severe agitation, and autonomic instability. The brain hallucinates rival mobs that aren't there, and the body’s vital signs wildly fluctuate until the whole system crashes. 

To prevent this meltdown, you have to manage the vibe of the room. A quiet, low-stimulus environment reduces agitation in clients experiencing substance withdrawal. Turn down the lights and shut off the noise so the brain stops jumping at shadows! And remember, the Alcohol Boss wasn't just suppressing the city; he was robbing it blind, specifically stealing all the B-vitamins. Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome is a neurological disorder caused by a severe thiamine deficiency, essentially burning holes straight through the brain's memory hard drive. To stop this irreversible data loss, thiamine replacement is administered to clients with chronic alcohol use to prevent Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome. 

When the client is finally sober, you have to keep them away from the mob. We have two crazy strategies for this. Strategy one is the petty, vengeful curse: Disulfiram is a medication used to deter alcohol consumption by causing severe nausea and vomiting upon alcohol ingestion. It is brutally unforgiving! Clients taking disulfiram must avoid all products containing alcohol including mouthwash and vanilla extract, or this medication will make them puke their guts out in the driveway over a single minty swish. Strategy two is the memory-wiper: Naltrexone is an opioid antagonist used to decrease cravings for both alcohol and opioids by simply turning off the brain's reward center, making them forget why they ever thought hanging out with the mob was fun.

Let's not forget Alcohol's shady cousin, Benzodiazepines. When someone hangs out with the Benzo family too much, they turn into a sloppy disaster. Benzodiazepine intoxication causes slurred speech, ataxia, and central nervous system depression. Because they are staggering around and losing their balance, fall precautions must be implemented for clients experiencing intoxication or withdrawal due to impaired coordination and altered mental status. If they get too deep and pass out on the couch, nurses must assess the airway and respiratory status frequently during suspected central nervous system depressant overdoses, because they will literally forget to breathe. If their breathing tanks, you call in the VIP bouncer: Flumazenil is the specific pharmacological antidote for a benzodiazepine overdose. It kicks the benzo right out of the brain's club! But here is the trap: abrupt discontinuation of chronic benzodiazepine use can precipitate life-threatening withdrawal seizures. You cannot just lock the doors on them instantly, or the brain's electrical grid explodes! Medical management of benzodiazepine withdrawal requires a gradual tapering of the medication to prevent seizures. And just to be safe, whether it's alcohol or benzos crashing out, seizure precautions must be implemented for clients undergoing severe alcohol or sedative withdrawal. Pad the bed rails, because the lightning storm could hit at any second!

Now let’s walk over to a totally different kind of drama: the Opioids. Opioids are the toxic, lazy couch surfers of the brain. When they move in, everything slows to a crawl. Signs of opioid intoxication include pinpoint pupils, respiratory depression, and a decreased level of consciousness. They just shrink their pupils to tiny dots, sink into the cushions, and refuse to inhale! To evict them before the client dies, you grab the ultimate eviction notice. Naloxone is the specific pharmacological antidote for an opioid overdose. It physically rips the opioid off the brain's receptors like tearing the blankets off a sleeping teenager. But once they are evicted, the dramatic crying starts. Opioid withdrawal symptoms include rhinorrhea, piloerection, muscle aches, diarrhea, and yawning. It is an Oscar-winning performance of misery—leaky noses, skin covered in goosebumps like a plucked bird, and incessant yawning. But here is the major plot twist you must memorize: opioid withdrawal is intensely uncomfortable and generally not life-threatening. They will act like they are dying, but unlike alcohol, it won't kill them! To judge this theatrical performance, we use a specific rubric. The Clinical Opiate Withdrawal Scale is used to determine the severity of opioid withdrawal and guide pharmacological treatment. Instead of leaving them to suffer, we swap the toxic couch surfer for a boring, stable roommate. Methadone is a long-acting synthetic opioid used for opioid maintenance therapy and withdrawal management. Or we offer the decaf version: Buprenorphine is a partial opioid agonist used to treat opioid use disorder by reducing cravings and withdrawal symptoms without the high.

Then there is the chaotic cooking show disaster: the Stimulants. Cocaine and meth are like a chef who just drank ten espresso shots and is trying to cook a five-course meal in three minutes. Stimulant intoxication presents with tachycardia, dilated pupils, elevated blood pressure, and hypervigilance. They are staring at the ceiling fan with dinner-plate pupils, waiting for the SWAT team to arrive! But the human engine isn't built to redline like that. Severe stimulant intoxication can precipitate fatal cardiac arrhythmias, seizures, and hyperthermia. They will quite literally fry their own electrical wiring and cook their organs from the inside out! Because the heart is basically trying to vibrate through the chest wall, continuous cardiac monitoring is indicated for clients experiencing severe stimulant toxicity due to the risk of arrhythmias. But what happens when the chef runs out of espresso? The crash is devastating. Stimulant withdrawal symptoms include profound fatigue, increased appetite, and severe depression. They eat everything in the fridge, collapse on the floor, and sink into a bottomless pit of despair. Because the brain is completely depleted of all its happy chemicals, the primary nursing intervention for a client experiencing stimulant withdrawal is observing the client for suicidal ideation. You cannot take your eyes off them, because the drop is incredibly steep.

So how do you actually talk to these reality stars, mob bosses, and manic chefs? Welcome to hostage negotiation. The foundation of this negotiation is your face. Nurses must maintain a non-judgmental attitude when caring for clients with substance use disorders to foster trust. If you look at them like they are trash, the negotiation is over. But you also can't let them steal your wallet, meaning setting clear boundaries is essential to maintain a professional and therapeutic nurse-client relationship.

When you do talk, you have to hand them the microphone. Therapeutic communication relies on open-ended questions to encourage the client to share thoughts and feelings. Never interrogate them! Asking why questions often makes clients feel defensive and impairs the therapeutic nurse-client relationship. If you ask "Why did you relapse?" they will immediately put up their armor. And don't you dare try to fix it with a cheap greeting card. False reassurance minimizes client feelings and blocks further therapeutic communication. Telling them "Everything is going to be okay" just proves to them that you don't understand how badly their house is burning down. Don't try to pivot, either. Changing the subject during a client expression of feelings is a non-therapeutic communication block. It is the conversational equivalent of hanging up the phone on them.

Instead, you use your physical presence as a tool. Active listening involves maintaining eye contact, leaning forward, and providing nonverbal cues of engagement. You become a verbal mirror. The technique of reflection directs client questions or feelings back to the client to encourage self-exploration. When they say, "I'm a disaster," you say, "You feel like things are out of control." Or you can be a parrot: restating involves repeating the main idea of a client statement to confirm nursing understanding. And when things get heavy, you just shut up. Silence is a therapeutic communication technique allowing the client time to organize thoughts and process emotions. Let the quiet do the heavy lifting.

Throughout all of this, you must tell them their reality makes sense. Validation acknowledges client feelings as legitimate and promotes a sense of being understood. But you must wear your emotional hazmat suit at all times! Empathy requires understanding the client perspective without absorbing the actual emotions of the client. You can look into the pit and say, "That looks incredibly dark," but if you jump into the pit with them, you both die in there! And finally, when you’ve built enough trust, you drop the reality check. Confrontation in therapeutic communication involves gently pointing out discrepancies between client words and actions. You look right at them and say, "You told me you wanted to get your kids back, but I noticed you skipped group therapy again today." You hold the mirror up, you keep them physically breathing, and you guide them out of the madness!
