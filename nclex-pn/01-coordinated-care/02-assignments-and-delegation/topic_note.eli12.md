Welcome, everyone! Pull up a chair. Today we are going to talk about being a team captain. In nursing, this is called **Assignments and Delegation**. 

A lot of people think delegation just means bossing people around or making someone else do your chores. But that’s not true at all! If you want to be an awesome Practical Nurse (PN), you have to think of yourself like the leader of a multiplayer video game or the captain of a sports team. You don't play the game alone. You have helpers, called Unlicensed Assistive Personnel (UAP), and you have other nurses on your team. How do you work together to keep your patients safe and help them get better?

Let's look at how this works in a really simple way, learn the rules of the game, and figure out how to master this for your tests and your future job.

***

## The Fundamentals: Delegation vs. Assignment

Let’s start by looking at two words that sound similar but are actually very different: delegation and assignment. 

Think about when your parents ask you to clean the whole house. If you ask your little brother to sweep the kitchen floor for you, you are delegating. **Delegation is the transfer of responsibility for the performance of a nursing task from one individual to another.** 

Notice what that means: you are giving your brother the job of actually pushing the broom. But here is the most important rule of being a leader: **The delegating nurse retains legal accountability for the overall outcome of a delegated task.** That means if your brother does a terrible job and leaves dirt everywhere, your parents are going to be mad at *you*, not him. You are still the one who gets in trouble. When you delegate a task in a hospital, you still own the results! 

An assignment is totally different. Think of it like your parents making a chore chart at the start of the week. You get the kitchen, and your sister gets the living room. **An assignment is the routine distribution of client care duties among unit staff members during a designated work period.** 

When you get to the hospital for your shift, the boss *assigns* you a few patients. They are yours to take care of. What if you need to share the work with another nurse who has the same license as you? **A practical nurse can assign the care of stable clients with predictable health outcomes to another licensed practical nurse.** You are handing over the whole job, and they become the one who is responsible if something goes wrong.

## The NCSBN Five Rights of Delegation

To make sure we don’t accidentally ask someone to do something dangerous, a big nursing group made a simple checklist. 

**The National Council of State Boards of Nursing defines five rights of delegation to ensure safe client care.** If you skip even one of these five rules, you might put a patient in danger. Let's look at how they work.

### 1. The Right Task
Can this specific helper legally do this specific job? **The right task principle requires that a delegated activity legally falls within the job description of the delegatee.** 

More importantly, it is about how much thinking the job takes. **The right task principle dictates that a delegated activity must not require independent nursing judgment.** 
*   Emptying a trash can or reading a number on a scale? That's easy and doesn't require making big decisions. *Right task.*
*   Deciding *why* a patient's stomach suddenly hurts? That takes a lot of brainpower and nursing training. *Wrong task for a helper.*

### 2. The Right Circumstances
Even if a job is usually okay to give to a helper, the current situation might make it a bad idea. **The right circumstances principle mandates that delegated tasks are only performed on clients in stable physiological condition.** 

Checking a normal blood pressure is a fine job for a helper. But what if the patient is super dizzy, sweating, and fainting? Taking their blood pressure suddenly becomes a high-level job because the patient is not stable. You don't send the helper in there; you go in there yourself!

### 3. The Right Person
You have to match the person to the job. **The right person principle requires delegating a task to an individual who possesses documented competency for that specific activity.** 

"Documented competency" just means there is a piece of paper proving they passed a test on how to do it. **A practical nurse must verify the documented competency of unlicensed assistive personnel prior to assigning a clinical task.** If the helper just started working yesterday and hasn't been tested on how to use a wheelchair lift, you can't ask them to use it, even if they say they know how!

### 4. The Right Direction and Communication
You cannot give muddy or confusing instructions. 
*   **The right direction and communication principle requires the delegator to provide a clear description of the delegated task.**
*   **The right direction and communication principle requires the delegator to specify timeframes for task completion.** (Like saying, "Please check his blood sugar and tell me the number by 7:30 AM.")
*   **The right direction and communication principle requires the delegator to specify exact abnormal findings the delegatee must report.** 

Don't make your helpers guess! If you need them to watch a patient's water drinking (intake), do the math for them so they have an exact number to report:
Step 1: Check the patient's daily water goal. Let's say it is 800 milliliters (mL).
Step 2: Decide your warning limit. Let's say you want to know if they drink less than half. Divide 800 by 2 (800 / 2 = 400).
Step 3: Give the exact instruction: "Please report back to me immediately if the patient drinks less than 400 mL of water."

### 5. The Right Supervision and Evaluation
When you give someone a job, you can't just walk away and forget about it. 
*   **The right supervision and evaluation principle requires the delegator to monitor the ongoing performance of the delegated task.** 
*   **The right supervision and evaluation principle requires the delegator to provide constructive feedback after task completion.** (Tell them they did a good job, or kindly show them how to do it better next time).

What if you see your helper moving a patient out of bed, but they forgot to lock the wheels on the bed? **A practical nurse must intervene immediately if assistive personnel are observed performing a delegated task unsafely.** You stop them right then and there to keep the patient safe!

## Making the Call: Assigning Care to Assistive Personnel

So, how do you decide what jobs to give out? **A practical nurse must assess the complexity of a client's needs before assigning care to assistive personnel.** You have to look at how complicated the patient's sickness is.

Let's draw a clear line between what Unlicensed Assistive Personnel (UAPs) can and cannot do. Think of UAPs as your extra hands, but *never* your extra brain. They can do normal, routine, everyday things. They cannot do things that require making big medical decisions.

### What UAPs CAN Do

These jobs are simple, normal, and don't involve making medical guesses:

| Approved Helper Tasks | Why it's safe to give them this job |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Basic activities of daily living (ADLs)** | **Unlicensed assistive personnel are permitted to perform basic activities of daily living for physiologically stable clients.** Helping people brush their teeth, eat, and use the bathroom are everyday habits. |
| **Routine Vital Signs** | **Unlicensed assistive personnel are permitted to measure routine vital signs on clients in stable condition.** (Remember, only if the patient is stable and not super sick!). |
| **Intake and Output (I&O)** | **Unlicensed assistive personnel are permitted to measure and record client fluid intake and output.** Writing down how many ounces of juice someone drank is just measuring. |
| **Ambulation** | **Unlicensed assistive personnel are permitted to assist physiologically stable clients with ambulation.** Walking a patient down the hall to stretch their legs is totally fine. |

### What UAPs CANNOT Do (The Danger Zones)

If you see these on a test, immediately know they are off-limits for helpers. Why? Because every single one requires *nursing brainpower*.

*   **No Assessing:** **Unlicensed assistive personnel are prohibited from performing initial client assessments.** They can't be the first person to check out a new patient or look at a patient after they fall down.
*   **No Planning:** **Unlicensed assistive personnel are prohibited from formulating nursing care plans.** Making a plan to help someone get better takes a lot of training.
*   **No Teaching:** **Unlicensed assistive personnel are prohibited from providing primary client education.** Only the nurse can teach a patient about their sickness or their medicines for the first time.
*   **No Evaluating:** **Unlicensed assistive personnel are prohibited from evaluating the physiological effectiveness of nursing interventions.** If you give a patient an ice pack for a hurt knee, you can't ask the helper to go check if the knee is healing. Checking if a treatment worked is the nurse's job.
*   **No Complex Feedings:** **Unlicensed assistive personnel are prohibited from administering enteral tube feedings.** Giving food through a tube into the stomach requires special checks to make sure the tube is in the right place. 
*   **No High-Risk Feeding:** **Unlicensed assistive personnel are prohibited from feeding clients who are on active aspiration precautions.** If a patient easily chokes on their food, feeding them is dangerous and requires a nurse to watch them carefully.
*   **No Meds:** **A practical nurse cannot delegate standard medication administration to unlicensed assistive personnel in an acute care setting.** Giving out medicine in a hospital is only for nurses.

## Navigating Changing Tides: When Assignments Must Change

Bodies are tricky. Sometimes a patient who was doing perfectly fine suddenly gets very sick. When that happens, your game plan has to change!

Imagine you asked a helper to take a patient's temperature. Suddenly, the patient starts having trouble breathing and holding their chest. **A practical nurse must reassess client assignments if a client's physiological status unexpectedly deteriorates.** That patient is no longer "stable." They need a nurse right away. You must take over!

But what if a patient gets so sick that they need a type of care you aren't legally allowed to give? 

Here is exactly what you do:
1.  **A practical nurse must notify the registered nurse supervisor immediately when a client's condition requires a change in staff assignments.** Don't try to play the hero and do things you aren't allowed to do.
2.  **A practical nurse must request an assignment change from a supervisor if an assigned client requires complex interventions outside the scope of practice of a practical nurse.** 

### Knowing Your Own Limits

Being a professional doesn't just mean knowing what you *can* do. It also means being totally honest about what you *can't* do. Let’s say the boss gives you a patient who needs a special kind of medical machine. It’s technically allowed for your job level, *but* you have never been taught how to use it. 

Do you just guess and hope for the best? No way! **A practical nurse must refuse an assignment and notify the supervisor if the practical nurse lacks the specific training to perform a required task safely.** Saying "no" when you don't know how to do something isn't being stubborn. It means you are protecting your patient. You tell the boss, "I haven't been trained on this machine, so it would be unsafe. I need a different assignment or someone to teach me."

***

## Summary: The Art of Delegation

To wrap this all up, think of delegation like putting together a winning team. Before you hand off a job, you look at the patient. Are they doing okay? Is it a normal day for them? Then, you look at your helper. Do they know how to play this part of the game? Have they passed the test for this skill? Is the job simple enough that it doesn't require making big medical decisions?

If it's a good match, you give crystal clear instructions—exact times, exact numbers. You keep an eye on how they are doing, tell them "good job," and remember that you are the one responsible for the patient's safety. And if a patient suddenly gets worse, you step back in and talk to your boss.

You aren't just bossing people around. You are leading a team to help people heal. Stick to the Five Rights, know your rules, and you will be a fantastic nurse!
