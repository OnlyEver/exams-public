Pull up a stool and let me buy you a drink, because you look like you think working the floor as a Practical Nurse is just walking around with a stethoscope and handing out ice chips. Listen to me: running a nursing unit is exactly like running the kitchen of a high-stakes, mob-owned Italian restaurant on a Saturday night. You aren't just a cook; you are the Head Chef. You have a whole brigade of sous-chefs and prep cooks under you, and if you don't know exactly how to command them, the whole place goes up in flames. Let’s talk about the absolute survival skills of this kitchen: Assignments and Delegation. 

People always mix these two up, but they are totally different beasts. Let's start with the big one. When I hand my favorite whisk to my junior prep cook and tell him to beat the eggs, that is delegation. Delegation is the transfer of responsibility for the performance of a nursing task from one individual to another. I am transferring the physical act of whipping those eggs to him. But here is the terrifying catch—if that soufflé collapses and the mob boss dining at table four gets angry, the boss doesn't yell at the prep cook. He yells at *me*. The delegating nurse retains legal accountability for the overall outcome of a delegated task. You can pass the whisk, but you can never, ever pass the blame. 

Now, don't confuse that with an assignment. An assignment is the routine distribution of client care duties among unit staff members during a designated work period. That’s just me looking at the schedule at 3:00 PM and saying, "Tony, you take the grill station tonight; Maria, you're on the fryers." You're just carving up the pie. And if my section of the restaurant is filled with easygoing customers ordering simple, predictable food, I don't have to keep it. A practical nurse can assign the care of stable clients with predictable health outcomes to another licensed practical nurse. I can just hand that entire block of tables over to another fully trained chef, lock, stock, and barrel, along with both the responsibility *and* the accountability.

But when you are passing tasks down to the unlicensed prep cooks—your Unlicensed Assistive Personnel, or UAPs—you are walking through a minefield. To keep us from poisoning the critics, the big bosses at the National Council of State Boards of Nursing defines five rights of delegation to ensure safe client care. Think of these as the Five Golden Rules of the Kitchen Mafia. If you break even one, you're finished. 

First up is the Right Task. The right task principle requires that a delegated activity legally falls within the job description of the delegatee. You absolutely do not ask the dishwasher to butcher a thousand-dollar wagyu beef! Furthermore, the right task principle dictates that a delegated activity must not require independent nursing judgment. If the task requires the prep cook to taste the soup and decide on their own if it needs more salt, they can't do it. No judgment allowed!

Second is Right Circumstances. The right circumstances principle mandates that delegated tasks are only performed on clients in stable physiological condition. You don't send the busboy out to a table if the customer is actively having a heart attack into his spaghetti! You only send them out when the dining room is calm and stable.

Third is Right Person. The right person principle requires delegating a task to an individual who possesses documented competency for that specific activity. You can't just look at a new guy and say, "Hey, you look like you know how to use the industrial meat slicer." No! A practical nurse must verify the documented competency of unlicensed assistive personnel prior to assigning a clinical task. I need to physically see the training certificate in their HR file before they touch that blade!

Fourth is Right Direction and Communication. You can't just mumble "take care of the onions" and walk away. The right direction and communication principle requires the delegator to provide a clear description of the delegated task. You have to be incredibly specific. It requires the delegator to specify timeframes for task completion—"I need these onions diced by exactly 6:30 PM." And it requires the delegator to specify exact abnormal findings the delegatee must report. You tell them, "Come get me immediately if the onions turn purple, smell like almonds, or if you find a bug in the bag." Give them the exact parameters!

Fifth, and maybe most important, is Right Supervision and Evaluation. You do not just fire and forget! The right supervision and evaluation principle requires the delegator to monitor the ongoing performance of the delegated task. You watch them chop out of the corner of your eye. And it requires the delegator to provide constructive feedback after task completion. "Next time, hold the knife like this so you don't lose a thumb." But what if you look over and they are literally juggling the cleavers right that second? A practical nurse must intervene immediately if assistive personnel are observed performing a delegated task unsafely. You tackle them to the floor before there is blood in the risotto!

So, how do you know what to hand off? Before you give a recipe to the junior staff, a practical nurse must assess the complexity of a client's needs before assigning care to assistive personnel. Is this a simple grilled cheese, or a ten-tier wedding cake? Let's talk about what your UAPs *can* do, because they are your lifeblood for the simple stuff. Unlicensed assistive personnel are permitted to perform basic activities of daily living for physiologically stable clients. Wiping down the tables, refilling water glasses, getting the customers cleaned up—totally fine. They can check the temperature on a slow-cooking roast, because unlicensed assistive personnel are permitted to measure routine vital signs on clients in stable condition. Notice the word *stable*! Unlicensed assistive personnel are permitted to measure and record client fluid intake and output. That's just counting the empty water pitchers coming back to the dish pit! And unlicensed assistive personnel are permitted to assist physiologically stable clients with ambulation. Walking a happy, steady customer to the restroom? Easy money. Go for it.

But here is the danger zone. The absolute, do-not-cross lines where you will lose your license and the restaurant gets shut down. Unlicensed assistive personnel are prohibited from performing initial client assessments. They cannot be the first one to taste the secret broth! Unlicensed assistive personnel are prohibited from formulating nursing care plans. They do not write the menu! Unlicensed assistive personnel are prohibited from providing primary client education. They absolutely cannot walk out to the VIP table and explain the complex wine pairings! Unlicensed assistive personnel are prohibited from evaluating the physiological effectiveness of nursing interventions. You never send a busboy out to ask the customer if the antacid actually cured their heartburn. That requires clinical deduction! 

And when it comes to feeding, you have to be paranoid. Unlicensed assistive personnel are prohibited from administering enteral tube feedings. Injecting a blind marinade deep into a turkey where you can't see the placement? Too risky. That requires high-level nursing assessment. Unlicensed assistive personnel are prohibited from feeding clients who are on active aspiration precautions. If the mob boss has a bad swallow reflex and is actively coughing and sputtering, you do not let the rookie feed him soup! And finally, keep the kitchen pharmacy under lock and key. A practical nurse cannot delegate standard medication administration to unlicensed assistive personnel in an acute care setting. No exceptions. Never let the prep cook sprinkle the high-blood-pressure meds onto the steaks!

But what happens when the kitchen catches fire? Biology is chaotic. Sometimes a totally stable customer suddenly drops to the floor clutching his chest. A practical nurse must reassess client assignments if a client's physiological status unexpectedly deteriorates. You can't let the busboy keep checking that guy's blood pressure; the game has completely changed! The second that happens, a practical nurse must notify the registered nurse supervisor immediately when a client's condition requires a change in staff assignments. You scream for the Executive Chef right then and there! 

And sometimes, a new ticket comes into the kitchen that is completely out of your league. Say a VIP walks in and orders fugu blowfish—a dish that requires a highly specialized license to prepare, and if you mess it up, the guy dies. A practical nurse must request an assignment change from a supervisor if an assigned client requires complex interventions outside the scope of practice of a practical nurse. You tell the boss, "This is outside my legal scope, give this table to someone else." But what if it *is* legally in your scope, but you've just never actually been trained on it? Do you watch a quick YouTube video on blowfish preparation in the bathroom? Hell no! A practical nurse must refuse an assignment and notify the supervisor if the practical nurse lacks the specific training to perform a required task safely. That isn't mutiny. That isn't being lazy. That is the ultimate act of saving the restaurant, saving your license, and saving the patient's life. 

You manage that kitchen with ironclad rules, you trust but verify every single task, and you fiercely protect your patients when the heat gets turned up. Now finish your drink, Chef. Your shift starts in ten minutes.
