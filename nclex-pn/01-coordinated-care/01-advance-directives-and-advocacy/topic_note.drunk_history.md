Listen to me, slide those nachos over and focus, because the National Council Licensure Examination for Practical Nurses—yeah, the NCLEX-PN—wants you to know exactly how to handle the most explosive, high-stakes drama in the entire hospital: Advance Directives and Advocacy. 

Imagine your patient as the ultimate Mob Boss. They are the absolute Don of their own body. As long as the Boss is awake, coherent, and legally competent, they run the entire syndicate. A legally competent adult client possesses the absolute right to refuse any medical treatment. I don’t care if refusing a life-saving surgery seems like jumping out of a perfectly good airplane without a parachute; you, as the nurse, must respect a competent client's autonomous decision to decline life-sustaining interventions. Even if they don't have a single piece of formal, notarized paperwork to their name, the absence of an advance directive does not grant healthcare providers the authority to override a competent client's current verbal instructions. If the Don says, "Stop the IV," you stop the IV. Their voice is the supreme law.

But what happens if a rival gang—let's call them "Massive Stroke" or "Severe Trauma"—takes the Don out? Who runs the family when the Boss is completely unconscious? This is where the federal feds stepped in to prevent total anarchy. The Patient Self-Determination Act is an actual federal law requiring healthcare facilities to inquire about advance directives upon client admission. It’s an absolute mandate; healthcare facilities require the practical nurse to document the client's advance directive status upon admission. We are essentially the bouncers at the front door of the casino, legally obligated to ask, "Hey, do you have a succession plan?"

This succession plan is called an advance directive, which is a legal document specifying a client's healthcare wishes in the event of future cognitive incapacitation. Now, to write this master blueprint, a client must be a legally competent adult of sound mind. You can’t be hallucinating on heavy narcotics and suddenly start rewriting the family rules! Interestingly enough, emancipated minors possess the legal right to create an advance directive too, because the law looks at those legally independent teens and says, "Congrats kid, you're a full-fledged adult in the eyes of the medical mob, write your will."

This blueprint has two main flavors. First, you’ve got the "What." A living will dictates specific medical treatments a client accepts or refuses during a terminal illness or state of permanent unconsciousness. Think of it as a highly specific, rigid catering menu for an event they sleep through. It clearly lists, "Yes to the mechanical ventilator, absolutely no to the feeding tube." But a menu can't adapt or negotiate if things get weird. That’s why you need the "Who." 

A durable power of attorney for healthcare designates a specific surrogate decision-maker for a client's medical care. You are naming an Underboss. And if you hear a family member on some trashy reality TV show screaming about being the "healthcare proxy," just roll your eyes and nod, because the term healthcare proxy is legally synonymous with a durable power of attorney for healthcare. 

Now, here is where families start acting like reality TV villains. The daughter gets named as the surrogate and immediately tries to start bossing the nurses around while the patient is sitting right there, awake and eating Jell-O. Hold up! A durable power of attorney for healthcare activates only when the client loses the cognitive capacity to make medical decisions. If the Boss can still talk and reason, the Underboss has exactly zero power. Once they *do* take over, it’s not a free-for-all power trip. A designated healthcare surrogate must make medical decisions based strictly on the client's previously expressed wishes. If the Boss never explicitly talked about what they wanted? Then a designated healthcare surrogate must act in the client's best interest if the client's specific wishes remain unknown.

Getting this paperwork signed is like securing an international peace treaty, and it has to be completely air-tight. Two independent witnesses are typically required by state laws to validate a client's signature on a living will. But who gets to witness it? You can't just pull anyone into the room. Legal relatives of the client are frequently prohibited by state law from acting as witnesses to the client's advance directive signature because, let's face it, they might have a financial stake in the inheritance! And you, the nurse? Don't even think about grabbing a pen. Healthcare employees directly involved in a client's clinical care are generally disqualified from serving as witnesses to the client's advance directive to avoid any wild accusations of an inside job or hospital conspiracy.

Once the ink is dry, the paperwork can't just sit in the patient's overnight bag. The healthcare facility must place copies of a client's executed advance directive in the client's active medical record. If a family member dramatically slides a brand-new, freshly signed directive across the bed rails to you, you have to act fast. The practical nurse must formally communicate the existence of a newly provided advance directive to the primary healthcare provider. The whole crew needs to know the new rules! And what if the Boss decides they hate the plan? Total messy breakup! A client possesses the right to revoke an advance directive at any time while retaining decision-making capacity. They can literally rip it to shreds. But remember, just like writing it, a client must be a legally competent adult to modify an advance directive.

Now, let’s talk about a major kitchen fire out in the real world. Imagine the Boss is at a grocery store, collapses, and a bystander calls 911. Paramedics bust in like a frantic kitchen brigade during a chaotic dinner rush. Do they stop to read a ten-page living will? No! Standard advance directives do not guide emergency medical personnel during out-of-hospital emergencies. They simply don't have the time to verify legal signatures while a heart is stopped. To stop the paramedics from doing chest compressions, emergency medical personnel require a specific out-of-hospital Do Not Resuscitate order to withhold emergency resuscitation. 

What is that? A Do Not Resuscitate order is a medical order written by a healthcare provider instructing staff not to attempt cardiopulmonary resuscitation. Usually, the Boss's paperwork paves the way for this exact scenario, because a client's living will can stipulate the specific conditions under which a Do Not Resuscitate order should be initiated once they interact with their doctor.

So, where do you fit into all this glorious chaos? You are the loyal Consigliere. The ultimate advocate. Nursing advocacy requires the nurse to prioritize and protect the client's healthcare rights above institutional convenience. If it delays the operating room by two hours because the Boss wants to review their options, you hold the line against the angry surgeons! Furthermore, nursing advocacy involves supporting a client's healthcare decisions entirely independent of the nurse's own personal or religious beliefs. You might totally disagree with their choice to refuse a blood transfusion, but it’s not your body, and you will fiercely defend their choice.

Sometimes, the family gets incredibly aggressive, crying and trying to guilt-trip the Boss into signing something they don't want. Stop the reality show right there. The practical nurse must ensure the client is free from coercion when making decisions regarding advance directives. You clear the room of the drama. You also need to empower the Boss to speak up for themselves. Promoting client self-advocacy involves encouraging the client to ask questions about identified treatment options. You are their guide through the underworld. The practical nurse provides objective educational information to assist the client in understanding available treatment options. No personal bias, just the raw facts of what the treatments actually do. You also play the ultimate translator. The practical nurse acts as a liaison between the client and the healthcare provider to clarify misunderstood treatment options. 

But you can't just assume they get it because they nod. The practical nurse evaluates the client's verbalized understanding of existing advance directives. You do this by getting them to talk. The practical nurse utilizes open-ended questions to assess a client's comprehension of the medical terms in their living will. You say, "Hey Boss, tell me in your own words what you think being intubated means." If they reply, "It's when they give me a nice breathing mask and a back rub," we have a massive problem. The practical nurse must report to the registered nurse if a client demonstrates a lack of understanding regarding proposed treatment options, so the whole team can regroup and re-educate. 

And finally, if the Boss turns down every single life-saving measure on the table, do we just abandon them in a dark, quiet room? Absolutely not. The practical nurse must deliver all physical care without bias regarding the client's chosen or refused treatment options. You give them the absolute best oral care, the most meticulous pain management, and the deepest human respect, because no matter what path they choose, they are the Boss until the very end.
