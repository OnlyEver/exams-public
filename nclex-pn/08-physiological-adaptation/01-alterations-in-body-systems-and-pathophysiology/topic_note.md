# The Master Mechanic of the Human Body: Alterations in Systems and [Pathophysiology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pathophysiology)

Welcome! Pull up a chair. If you are going to be a brilliant [practical nurse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Licensed_practical_nurse), you need to stop memorizing disconnected lists of [symptoms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symptom) and start looking at the [human body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_body) for what it really is: the most magnificent, intricate, interconnected machine in the [universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe). 

When you look at a [patient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patient), you are looking at a living [ecosystem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem) of plumbing, [electrical wiring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrical_wiring), and pressure dynamics. When [illness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disease) strikes, this machine doesn't just fail randomly; it fights back, it compensates, and it leaves clues. Your job is to be the detective that reads those clues.

Let’s take a walk through the wards and decode the pathophysiology behind the [signs and symptoms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Signs_and_symptoms) you will face on the [NCLEX-PN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Council_Licensure_Examination).

## 1. The Body at War: Decoding [Infection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infection)

When a [pathogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pathogen) invades the body, nature doesn't just sit back. It mounts an organized, energetic defense. 

Think about what happens when you cut your [finger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finger) and it gets infected. **[Purulent drainage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pus) from a [wound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wound) indicates a localized [tissue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tissue_%28biology%29) infection.** The [pus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pus) you see is literally a graveyard of dead [bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacteria) and [white blood cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_blood_cell)—evidence of a localized battle. 

![Purulent drainage (pus) and localized inflammation characteristic of an active tissue infection.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Dit_del_peu_gros_infectat.jpg)

But what if the invader gets into the [blood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood)? The battle goes system-wide. **[Fever](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fever) is a systemic [clinical sign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medical_sign) of an active infection.** The body intentionally turns up the core thermostat because bacteria hate the heat. But a hotter, working body demands more [oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen) and [nutrients](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nutrient). So, what does the [heart](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heart) do? It speeds up! Therefore, **[tachycardia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tachycardia) is a physiological compensatory response to systemic infection.** 

To fight this war, the body needs soldiers. 
> **The Normal Fleet:** **A normal [white blood cell count](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_blood_cell) ranges from 5,000 to 10,000 [cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_%28biology%29) per [microliter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microlitre) of blood.** 

If you draw blood and see a number jumping up to 15,000 or 18,000, you know the factories in the [bone marrow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bone_marrow) are working overtime. **An elevated white blood cell count indicates an active infectious process in the body.**

### The Stealth Invader: [Urinary Tract Infections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urinary_tract_infection)
Not all infections are obvious. If the [kidneys](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kidney) and [bladder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urinary_bladder) are under attack, the waste disposal system shows the evidence. **Cloudy [urine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urine) with a foul odor is a primary clinical indicator of a urinary tract infection.** 

But here is a fascinating quirk of [human biology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_biology): the [brains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brain) of [older adults](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old_age) are highly sensitive to [systemic inflammation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Systemic_inflammatory_response_syndrome). Long before an elderly patient complains of burning urination, their brain feels the [stress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stress_%28biology%29). **Sudden [confusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confusion) is often the first clinical sign of a urinary tract infection in older adult clients.** Remember this! If sweet Grandma suddenly doesn't know what year it is, check the urine.

![Cloudy, purulent urine (pyuria) is a classic visual indicator of a urinary tract infection.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Pyuria2011.JPG)

## 2. The Heart's Electrical Symphony

The heart is a mechanical [pump](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pump), but it runs entirely on an electrical grid. If you understand the wiring, reading an [ECG monitor strip](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrocardiography) is like reading a book.

The main power switch of the heart is the [sinoatrial (SA) node](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sinoatrial_node). **A [normal sinus rhythm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sinus_rhythm) originates from the sinoatrial node of the heart.** When this [pacemaker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardiac_pacemaker) fires beautifully, **normal sinus rhythm has a regular [heart rate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heart_rate) between 60 and 100 beats per minute.**

![A normal sinus rhythm on an electrocardiogram, displaying a regular rate with identifiable P waves preceding each QRS complex.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/SinusRhythmLabels.svg)

If the rhythm is regular but the rate drops below 60, we call it **[sinus bradycardia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sinus_bradycardia)** (defined as a regular heart rhythm with a rate below 60 beats per minute). If the regular rhythm speeds up past 100, that is **[sinus tachycardia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sinus_tachycardia)** (a regular heart rhythm with a rate above 100 beats per minute). 

### Electrical Misfires & Chaos
What happens when the electrical signal gets delayed? As the signal travels from the top of the heart ([atria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atrium_%28heart%29)) to the middle ([AV node](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atrioventricular_node)), it should be quick. **A prolonged [PR interval](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PR_interval) indicates a delay in electrical conduction through the atrioventricular node.** It’s like a traffic jam on the electrical highway.

Now, let's look at when the electricity goes completely haywire:

| Rhythm | Pathophysiology & ECG Appearance | Clinical Consequence |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **[Atrial Fibrillation (A-Fib)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atrial_fibrillation)** | The atria quiver instead of squeezing. **[Atrial fibrillation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atrial_fibrillation) presents on an [electrocardiogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrocardiography) as an irregular heart rhythm with no identifiable [P waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/P_wave_%28electrocardiography%29).** | Loss of atrial "kick," risk of [blood clots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thrombus). |
| **[Ventricular Fibrillation (V-Fib)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ventricular_fibrillation)** | The main pumping chambers just twitch chaotically. **[Ventricular fibrillation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ventricular_fibrillation) produces a chaotic [waveform](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Waveform) with no discernible [QRS complexes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/QRS_complex) on an electrocardiogram.** | **Ventricular fibrillation results in a complete absence of [cardiac output](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardiac_output).** The patient is clinically dead. Start [CPR](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardiopulmonary_resuscitation)! |
| **[Asystole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asystole)** | Total electrical failure. **[Asystole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asystole) appears as a flat baseline line on an electrocardiogram monitor strip.** | No [pulse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulse). CPR and [Epinephrine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epinephrine) required. |

![Comparison of a normal sinus rhythm (top) with atrial fibrillation (bottom), which lacks distinct P waves and has an irregular rhythm.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Atrial_Fibrillation.png)

## 3. Plumbing Dynamics: [Heart Failure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heart_failure) and Fluids

[Fluid dynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluid_dynamics) in the body are governed by a simple rule: *If a pump fails, fluid backs up into whatever feeds it.*

### Left vs. Right Heart Failure
The left side of the heart receives blood from the [lungs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lung). If the [left ventricle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Left_ventricle) fails, the blood has nowhere to go but backwards into the [pulmonary system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Respiratory_system). **Left-sided heart failure causes pulmonary venous congestion.** The fluid leaks into the lung tissue, which is why **auscultated [crackles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crackles) in the lung bases are a primary clinical sign of left-sided heart failure.**

The right side of the heart receives blood from the rest of the body. **Right-sided heart failure causes systemic venous congestion.** The fluid backs up into the [veins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vein). You can literally see the backup in the [neck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neck)—**[jugular vein distention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jugular_venous_pressure) is a primary clinical sign of right-sided heart failure.** 

![Marked jugular vein distention (JVD) caused by systemic venous congestion in right-sided heart failure.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Elevated_JVP.JPG)

[Gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity) then pulls this backed-up fluid down to the lowest points, meaning **dependent [peripheral edema](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peripheral_edema) is a common clinical manifestation of right-sided heart failure.**

![Severe dependent peripheral pitting edema resulting from fluid backup in right-sided heart failure.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Combinpedal.jpg)

### Hypovolemia
What if there just isn't enough fluid in the pipes? **[Hypovolemia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypovolemia) decreases total circulating [blood volume](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_volume).** Because there is less liquid to push against the vessel walls, **severe hypovolemia presents with a weak and thready peripheral pulse.**

## 4. Breathing for the Patient: Tracheostomies and Ventilators

When the natural [airway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Respiratory_tract) fails, we build a new one. **A [tracheostomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tracheotomy) provides a secure [artificial airway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Airway_management) through an [incision](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surgical_incision) in the anterior neck.** 

![Anatomical overview showing the correct placement of a tracheostomy tube bypassing the upper respiratory tract.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Tracheostomy_NIH.jpg)

### The Rules of Tracheostomy Care
Because we have bypassed the body's natural air filters (the [nose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_nose) and [mouth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_mouth)), the lungs are highly vulnerable. Therefore, **tracheostomy [suctioning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suction_%28medicine%29) requires strict [sterile technique](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asepsis) to prevent [lower respiratory tract infections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lower_respiratory_tract_infection).** 

Suctioning removes [mucus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mucus), but it also steals the patient's oxygen. You must prepare them! **The [nurse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nurse) must pre-oxygenate the client with 100 percent oxygen before initiating tracheostomy suctioning**, and to prevent [suffocation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asphyxia), **each individual tracheostomy suctioning pass must not exceed 10 seconds in duration.** 

For maintenance, **routine tracheostomy care requires cleaning the inner [cannula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cannula) with sterile [normal saline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saline_%28medicine%29).** 

> **The Golden Safety Rule:** What happens if the patient [coughs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cough) so hard the whole tube flies out? You need to get a new one in immediately. **An obturator must be kept at the bedside of a client with a new tracheostomy at all times.** Why? Because **the obturator is used to safely guide the reinsertion of an accidentally dislodged tracheostomy tube.**

### Mechanical Ventilators
Think of a [ventilator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ventilator) as a smart bicycle pump. **[Mechanical ventilators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanical_ventilation) provide positive pressure to inflate the lungs of a compromised client.** 

The vent is always measuring how hard it has to push ([resistance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Airway_resistance)). Understanding ventilator alarms is pure [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics):
*   **High-Pressure Alarms:** **A high-pressure ventilator alarm indicates increased airflow resistance in the airway circuit.** The machine is trying to push air in, but something is blocking the path. What causes blocks?
    *   **Excessive airway [secretions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Secretion) trigger a high-pressure ventilator alarm.**
    *   **A kinked ventilator tube triggers a high-pressure ventilator alarm.**
    *   **A client biting the [endotracheal tube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tracheal_tube) triggers a high-pressure ventilator alarm.**
*   **Low-Pressure Alarms:** **A low-pressure ventilator alarm indicates a sudden decrease in airway resistance.** The machine pushes air, and it meets zero resistance. The air is escaping!
    *   **A disconnected ventilator tubing circuit triggers a low-pressure ventilator alarm.**
    *   **An endotracheal tube cuff leak triggers a low-pressure ventilator alarm.**

*Bonus Vent Safety:* Having a tube in the [throat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Throat) allows bacteria to slide right down into the lungs. **Frequent [oral care](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oral_hygiene) with [chlorhexidine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chlorhexidine) reduces the risk of [ventilator-associated pneumonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ventilator-associated_pneumonia).**

## 5. Rerouting the Plumbing: Ostomy Care

When the [bowel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intestine) is diseased, [surgeons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surgeon) bring a piece of it through the [abdominal wall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abdominal_wall), creating a [stoma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stoma_%28medicine%29). 

### Reading the Stoma
A stoma is inside-out [intestine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intestine). It should look like the inside of your [cheek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cheek). **A healthy [ostomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ostomy) stoma appears beefy red and moist.** 

![A healthy ostomy stoma is highly vascular, appearing beefy red and moist.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/An_ostommy_opening.jpg)

If the color changes, you have a blood flow emergency. 
*   **A pale ostomy stoma indicates inadequate [tissue perfusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perfusion)** (think low [hemoglobin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hemoglobin) or poor flow).
*   **A dusky blue or black ostomy stoma indicates severe stomal [ischemia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ischemia).** This is [dying tissue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Necrosis). Alert the provider instantly!

### Pouch Mechanics & Output
Gravity is your enemy with [ostomy bags](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ostomy_pouching_system). **An ostomy appliance should be emptied when the pouch is one-third to one-half full.** Why so early? Because **emptying the ostomy pouch before the pouch becomes completely full prevents weight-induced appliance leakage.** A heavy bag pulls the [adhesive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adhesive) right off the [skin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_skin).

What comes out of the bag depends on *where* the ostomy was placed:
*   **[Ileostomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ileostomy) ([Small Intestine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Small_intestine)):** This bypassed the entire [large intestine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Large_intestine) (where [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water) is normally absorbed). Thus, **ileostomy drainage is typically liquid and continuous.** Because they are losing so much water, **clients with a new ileostomy must increase daily fluid intake to prevent [dehydration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dehydration).**
*   **[Colostomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colostomy) (Large Intestine):** If the stoma is way down the line, water has already been absorbed. **Colostomy drainage from the [descending colon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Descending_colon) is typically solid and formed.**

## 6. System-Wide Crises: Metabolic and Neurologic Derangements

Finally, let's look at how the entire body behaves when the chemistry or pressure is thrown off balance. 

**Oxygen Deprivation:** The brain is a greedy [organ](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organ_%28biology%29). It uses 20% of the body's oxygen. If oxygen drops, the brain panics long before the patient turns blue. **[Cerebral hypoxia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cerebral_hypoxia) causes early behavioral changes such as restlessness and [agitation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Psychomotor_agitation).** If your previously calm patient suddenly won't stay in bed, check their oxygen!

**Blood Sugar Battles:** 
*   **[Hypoglycemia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypoglycemia) (Low Sugar):** The body dumps [adrenaline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adrenaline) to try and free up stored [glucose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glucose). This adrenaline rush is why **hypoglycemia frequently presents with profuse [diaphoresis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perspiration) and [muscle tremors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tremor).** 
*   **[Diabetic Ketoacidosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diabetic_ketoacidosis) (High Sugar/No [Insulin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Insulin)):** When cells starve without insulin, they burn [fat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fat), creating acid ([ketones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketone_bodies)). The body tries to fix this extreme acidity by blowing off [carbon dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_dioxide) (an acid) through the lungs. This is why **diabetic ketoacidosis causes deep and rapid [Kussmaul respirations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kussmaul_breathing).** Those broken-down ketones also off-gas through the lungs, meaning **diabetic ketoacidosis produces a distinct fruity breath odor.**

![Pathological breathing patterns, including the deep and rapid Kussmaul respirations (second from bottom) seen in diabetic ketoacidosis.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Breathing_abnormalities.svg)

**[Anaphylaxis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anaphylaxis):** When an [allergic reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allergic_reaction) goes nuclear, [mast cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mast_cell) release massive amounts of [histamine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Histamine). Histamine does two terrible things simultaneously: **Anaphylaxis causes severe systemic [vasodilation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vasodilation)** (crashing [blood pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_pressure)) AND **anaphylaxis causes acute [bronchoconstriction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bronchoconstriction)** (closing the airways). 

![Systemic signs of anaphylaxis resulting from massive histamine release, leading to life-threatening vasodilation and bronchoconstriction.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Signs_and_symptoms_of_anaphylaxis.svg)

**[Increased Intracranial Pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intracranial_pressure) (ICP):** The [skull](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_skull) is a closed, rigid box. If the brain swells, pressure builds. This pressure crushes the [brainstem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brainstem), which controls [vital signs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vital_signs), resulting in a terrifying phenomenon called [Cushing's Triad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cushing_reflex):
1.  The heart tries to force blood into the highly pressurized skull, so [systolic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Systole) blood pressure shoots up while [diastolic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diastole) stays low. **Increased intracranial pressure causes a widened [pulse pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulse_pressure).**
2.  The crushed [vagus nerve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vagus_nerve) drastically slows the heart. **Increased intracranial pressure causes severe [bradycardia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bradycardia).**
3.  The [respiratory center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Respiratory_center) is compressed. **Increased intracranial pressure causes an irregular respiratory pattern.**

### Final Thoughts
Look at the [logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logic) here! Whether it's the heart pumping faster to deliver white blood cells, the lungs breathing deeply to blow off acid in [DKA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diabetic_ketoacidosis), or a ventilator screaming because of a kinked hose—none of this is random. By understanding *why* the body systems alter, you stop being just a nurse who follows orders, and you become a [critical thinker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Critical_thinking) who saves lives. 

Now, go out there, look at your patients, and read the machine.
