Imagine playing a brand-new video game. The first level is a "tutorial" where the game tells you exactly what buttons to press in a safe, quiet room. You beat it easily! But then, you step into the real game. Suddenly, there are enemies everywhere, the environment keeps changing, and there are no instructions on the screen. A lot of players freeze up because the real game looks totally different from the tutorial.

In school, we see the exact same thing happen. A student might learn a new skill in a quiet, helpful room with a teacher guiding them. But a week later, when they are asked to use that same skill in a busy, loud classroom, it feels like they forgot everything. 

Teaching a skill for the first time is just like the game tutorial. To make sure the skill actually sticks with the student forever and works in the real world, teachers rely on two big ideas: maintenance and generalization.

## The Pillars of Lasting Learning

Before we look at how teachers build their lessons, let's learn what these two important words mean.

> **Maintenance refers to a student's ability to continue performing a learned skill over time after direct instruction has ended.** Basically, it means having a great memory and keeping the skill strong long after the teacher stops teaching it.

> **Generalization occurs when a student applies a learned skill to new settings, materials, people, or situations.** It is the ability to adapt and use what you know anywhere, with anyone. 

Let's look at how this works with math! Imagine a teacher shows a student how to add up their weekly allowance. Let's say they have \$25 and earn another \$14. 

Here is how they learn the math step-by-step:
1. Stack the numbers on top of each other (25 on top, 14 on the bottom).
2. Add the numbers in the ones column on the right side: 5 + 4 = 9.
3. Add the numbers in the tens column on the left side: 2 + 1 = 3.
4. Put those together for the final answer: 39. So, they have \$39!

If the student can still do this exact math problem smoothly a month later, that is **maintenance**. 

Now, imagine that same student is at a pizza shop and has to figure out the cost of a \$25 pizza and a \$14 side of wings. If they can use their math steps to figure out the total cost while standing at the noisy counter, that is **generalization**. In fact, **applying skills learned in a special education setting to the general education curriculum is a primary goal of skill generalization.**

## The "Train and Hope" Fallacy

A long time ago, people used to teach a skill over and over until the student got it right, and then they would just cross their fingers and hope the student could use it somewhere else. 

**The phrase train and hope was coined by Stokes and Baer in 1977 to describe the ineffective practice of teaching a skill without explicitly planning for generalization.** It's like showing someone how to kick a soccer ball once and "hoping" they can instantly play in a huge tournament! 

Now we know that hoping is not enough. **Generalization must be explicitly planned and systematically taught as part of the instructional process.** Teachers have to plan for the "real game" from the very first day. 

## Engineering Maintenance: Defeating the Curve of Forgetting

When you first learn something new, it is very easy to forget. To help the brain remember long-term, teachers change how a student practices, how much help they get, and how they are rewarded.

### Practice Structures: Massed vs. Distributed
When you are trying to learn something brand new, teachers often use **massed practice, which involves concentrating practice sessions close together in time.** Think about cramming all your spelling words into one giant 30-minute study session. It helps you memorize them for a quick quiz, but your brain throws the information away shortly after.

To make the memory permanent, teachers switch to **distributed practice, which involves spreading out practice sessions over time.** Instead of one long 30-minute block, you might practice for 10 minutes on Monday, 10 minutes on Wednesday, and 10 minutes on Friday. **Distributed practice is highly effective for long-term skill maintenance** because your brain has to work to remember the skill over and over again, which makes your memory super strong.

Teachers also use something called **overlearning, which involves providing additional practice of a skill after the student has initially demonstrated mastery.** If it takes you 10 tries to perfectly learn a new basketball play, doing it 5 *more* times perfectly is overlearning. **Overlearning increases the likelihood of long-term skill maintenance** because it makes the skill totally automatic!

### The Mechanics of Reinforcement
When you first learn something hard, getting a high-five or a reward every single time feels great. **Continuous reinforcement schedules**—where you get a reward for every single correct answer—**are highly effective for initially teaching a new skill.** 

But in the real world, you don't get a prize every time you tie your shoes or raise your hand. To prepare you for the real world, teachers use an **intermittent reinforcement schedule**, which means they only reward you sometimes or randomly. **Intermittent reinforcement schedules promote the maintenance of behaviors better than continuous reinforcement schedules.** Because you don't know exactly when the reward is coming, you keep trying your best! Therefore, **shifting from continuous to intermittent reinforcement helps students maintain skills after the initial learning phase ends.**

### Fading Prompts to Foster Independence
A prompt is a hint or a helpful push from a teacher. But if a teacher gives you too many hints, you might start waiting for the hint instead of doing the work yourself. **Fading involves the gradual removal of instructional prompts.** It is just like slowly taking the training wheels off a bike. 

**Fading instructional prompts prevents students from becoming prompt-dependent** (meaning they can't do it without help). When the teacher slowly steps back, it **promotes the maintenance of skills in independent settings.**

### Verifying Retention: Probes and Boosters
Because it is normal to forget things, teachers have to do quick checks. **Maintenance probes are brief assessments conducted after instruction has ended to verify long-term skill retention.** Think of them as tiny, quick check-ups. 

If a student did great in October but forgets a little bit during a maintenance probe in December, the teacher doesn't make them redo the whole class! Instead, the teacher does a **booster session, which is a brief review of a previously learned skill provided when maintenance probes indicate a decline in student performance.** It's just a quick memory refresher.

## The Architecture of Generalization: Ensuring Transfer

Generalization is about taking a skill out of the classroom and into the big wide world. There are two main ways this happens:

1. **Stimulus generalization occurs when a learned response is triggered by a new, untrained stimulus.** (For example, you learned what the word "Level Up" looks like on your TV screen. Without any extra help, you see the words "Level Up" printed on a t-shirt in a totally different font, and you can still read it!)
2. **Response generalization occurs when a student emits a new, untrained response that serves the same function as a previously learned response.** (For example, you were taught to say "Hello" to greet your teacher. The next day, you decide to invent a cool hand-wave that does the exact same job. You changed your response, but the purpose is the same!)

Here is a table showing the special tools teachers use to help students generalize their skills:

### Strategies for Promoting Generalization

| Strategy | Definition | Why It Matters / Application |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Teaching with Multiple Exemplars** | Involves using a variety of materials and examples during instruction. | **Using multiple exemplars helps students generalize learned skills to unfamiliar materials.** If you only learn to count using red blocks, counting coins might confuse you. So, teachers use blocks, coins, and pizza slices to practice! |
| **Programming Common Stimuli** | Involves incorporating elements from the natural environment into the instructional setting. | If your big math test will be on blue lined paper, your teacher will have you practice on blue lined paper. **Programming common stimuli facilitates the transfer of skills from a special education resource room to a general education classroom.** |
| **Teaching Loosely** | Involves randomly varying noncritical aspects of the instructional setting. | A teacher might change their tone of voice, sit in a different chair, or move the desks. **Teaching loosely prevents students from associating a learned skill with a single specific instructional environment.** It proves you can do the skill anywhere! |
| **General Case Programming** | Involves analyzing the full range of situations a student will encounter for a specific skill, and selecting teaching examples that sample that full range. | **General case programming selects teaching examples that sample the full range of stimulus and response variations.** If you are learning to buy snacks, your teacher won't just show you one vending machine. They will show you machines with touchscreens, machines with buttons, and machines where you tap a card. |

## Transferring Control to the Student and the Environment

The greatest goal a teacher has is for the student to not need them anymore! To make this happen, the student and the real world need to take control.

### Behavior Trapping
At first, a teacher might give you a sticker to encourage you to talk to a new friend in class. The sticker is an artificial reward. But once you start talking to that classmate, you discover they like the exact same video games as you! Now, having fun and laughing with your new friend is the real reward.

These real-world rewards are called **natural contingencies of reinforcement, which refer to the typical consequences that naturally follow a behavior in everyday settings.** 

The magical moment when you stop caring about the teacher's sticker and start caring about the fun friendship is called **behavior trapping. Behavior trapping involves shifting control from artificial classroom reinforcers to natural contingencies of reinforcement.** Because the real world has now "trapped" you with awesome, natural rewards, **behavior trapping heavily promotes both the generalization and maintenance of target behaviors.**

### Mediating Generalization
Sometimes the environment doesn't help out, so the student has to help themselves. **Mediating generalization involves teaching a student a strategy to use independently across different settings.** 

One great way to do this is **self-monitoring, which requires a student to observe and independently record their own behavior.** For example, a student might have a tiny checklist on their desk where they give themselves a checkmark every time they stay focused. Because they do it themselves, they can take this checklist anywhere! **Self-monitoring is an effective form of mediated generalization.**

For school subjects, **providing self-instruction cue cards helps students apply complex academic skills in generalized settings.** A cue card is just a small index card with hints on it. If a student has the steps for long division written on a little card in their pocket, they can pull it out and do math in any room in the building.

### Social Generalization and Collaborative Communication
Learning to act in social situations doesn't work if you only talk to adults. That is why **using peers as instructional models in the general education classroom promotes the generalization of social skills for students with disabilities.** Watching how kids your own age act is the best way to learn natural, normal social skills!

Finally, for all of this to work, the teachers have to talk to each other. If a special education teacher does all this amazing work to teach a skill, the general classroom teacher needs to know what to look for. **Cross-setting communication between special and general educators is required to verify the generalization of targeted individualized education program skills.** When all the teachers communicate, they make sure the student can take their skills anywhere and succeed!
