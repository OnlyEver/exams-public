A long time ago, figuring out if a kid had a learning disability was like waiting for a wobbly bridge to actually break before deciding to fix it. This old way was called the **severe discrepancy model**. To get help, the severe discrepancy model required a significant statistical gap between a student's intelligence quotient score (their IQ, or brain power) and their academic achievement level (their actual school grades). Basically, it was a "wait-to-fail" system. A kid had to fail really badly before anyone would step in.

Today, schools are more like builders who test the bridge *before* anyone drives on it. Instead of waiting for a crash, teachers check for weak spots early and use proven methods to make those spots stronger. This guide explains how teachers pick the best tools, use them correctly, and track how well they work.

## The Foundation of Proof: Evidence-Based Practices

If you had a bad sore throat, you would want a doctor to give you medicine that is proven to cure it, not just some random potion they saw on the internet. School works the exact same way!

Teachers use **evidence-based practices**. Evidence-based practices are instructional techniques supported by rigorous scientific research demonstrating positive student outcomes. This means scientists have tested these teaching methods over and over to prove they actually help kids learn.

Because they work so well, they are literally part of the law:
*   **The Every Student Succeeds Act** mandates the use of evidence-based interventions in public schools. 
*   **The Individuals with Disabilities Education Act** requires special education teachers to use evidence-based practices whenever possible.

But how do teachers know which methods are actually backed by science? They look at the **What Works Clearinghouse**. Think of this like a giant, trustworthy review website for teaching. The What Works Clearinghouse is a federal database that reviews and rates the effectiveness of specific educational interventions.

## The Mechanics of Delivery: Intervention Fidelity

Having a great, science-backed plan is only step one. Step two is actually doing it right.

Imagine putting together a big Lego set. If you skip steps or swap out pieces, the final toy won't look like the picture on the box. In school, following the steps perfectly is called **intervention fidelity**. Intervention fidelity is the act of delivering an instructional program exactly as the original developers intended. 

High intervention fidelity requires strict adherence to the dosage (how many minutes) and schedule (how many days) specified by the original intervention designers. 

Let's look at the math of sticking to a schedule. Imagine a reading program that must be done for 25 minutes a day, 4 days a week. How much time is that per week?
1. Identify the daily time (dosage): 25 minutes.
2. Identify the schedule: 4 days per week.
3. Multiply the minutes by the days: 25 x 4.
4. The total weekly requirement is 100 minutes. 

> **The Golden Rule of Fidelity:** 
> Modifying a research-based intervention *before* gathering baseline data compromises the established validity of the intervention. 

What does that mean? It means if a teacher changes the rules of the program before they even see how the student was doing at the starting line (the baseline data), the program might not work anymore. You ruin the proven success!

## The Architecture of Prevention: MTSS and RTI

To make sure all these great teaching methods reach the right kids, schools use big step-by-step systems. The biggest system is the **Multi-Tiered System of Supports (MTSS)**. Think of MTSS like the whole school's safety net. Multi-Tiered System of Supports is a comprehensive educational framework encompassing both academic and behavioral student interventions.

Inside that big MTSS net is a smaller framework called **Response to Intervention (RTI)**. Response to Intervention is a preventative academic framework designed to identify and support struggling students early in the educational process.

RTI is amazing because it changes the whole game. The Response to Intervention framework serves as an alternative to the severe discrepancy model for identifying specific learning disabilities. Instead of waiting for that massive gap between IQ and grades, the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act allows schools to use a student's response to scientific interventions to identify specific learning disabilities.

Why is this awesome? Because the Response to Intervention framework is designed to prevent the over-identification of students with specific learning disabilities. By giving kids awesome, scientifically proven teaching right away, schools can prove that a kid's struggle isn't just because they had confusing teaching in the past. 

### The Three Tiers of Response to Intervention

The Response to Intervention framework typically consists of three distinct tiers of increasing instructional intensity. Think of tiers like levels in a video game that get a little tougher and require more focus as you go up.

Before putting anyone in a tier, the school does a **universal screening**. Universal screening is the systematic assessment of all general education students to identify individuals at risk for academic or behavioral difficulties. It's like checking every kid's vision at the nurse's office to see who might need glasses. Universal screening in a Response to Intervention framework typically occurs two to three times per academic school year.

Once the school has this screening data, they have to match the extra help to the exact problem. Targeted interventions must directly align with the specific academic deficits identified during universal screening and progress monitoring. If the test says you struggle with reading math word problems, they won't give you extra spelling practice!

| Tier Level | What It Is & Where It Happens | Who It Is For |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Tier 1** | Tier 1 of the Response to Intervention framework involves high-quality core instruction provided to all students in the general education classroom. | Tier 1 instruction is statistically designed to meet the academic needs of approximately **80%** of the student population. |
| **Tier 2** | Tier 2 of the Response to Intervention framework provides targeted small-group interventions for students who do not make adequate progress in Tier 1. | Tier 2 instruction is statistically designed to support approximately **15%** of the student population. |
| **Tier 3** | Tier 3 of the Response to Intervention framework provides the most intensive individualized academic interventions for students who do not respond to Tier 2 supports. | Tier 3 instruction is statistically designed to support approximately **5%** of the student population. |

If a student gets all this amazing help but still isn't catching up, the school knows it's a real learning challenge. Students who fail to respond adequately to intensive Tier 3 interventions may be referred for a comprehensive special education evaluation.

## The Feedback Loop: Data-Based Decision Making

How do teachers know if you need to move from Tier 1 to Tier 2, or if you need a different kind of help? They don't just guess. They use **data-based decision making**, which involves using objective student assessment data to guide instructional adjustments and intervention selection. It's like a sports coach looking at a player's exact score stats before deciding what drills to run at practice.

To get this data, teachers use **progress monitoring**. Progress monitoring is the frequent and ongoing assessment of a student's academic performance to evaluate the effectiveness of a specific intervention.

The main tool for this is a **curriculum-based measurement (CBM)**. Curriculum-based measurement is a standardized assessment tool frequently used for academic progress monitoring within a Response to Intervention framework. These are usually fast, simple quizzes—like seeing how many math problems you can solve in two minutes.

When teachers put these quiz scores on a graph, they look for the speed of your improvement. A student's **rate of growth** on a curriculum-based measurement dictates whether an academic intervention should be maintained or altered.

Let's look at how to calculate a rate of growth using some step-by-step math! Let's say you are saving a \$10 allowance each week, but here we are counting reading speed. A student starts out reading 40 words per minute. After 5 weeks of extra help, they read 60 words per minute.

1. First, find out how many total words they gained by subtracting the starting number from the ending number: 60 - 40 = 20 words.
2. Next, identify the number of weeks the extra help happened: 5 weeks.
3. Then, divide the words gained by the number of weeks: 20 / 5.
4. You get 4. The student's rate of growth is 4 new words per minute each week.

If that rate of growth is fast enough to help the student catch up to the rest of the class, the teacher maintains (keeps) the intervention! If the growth is flat or too slow, the teacher will alter (change) the plan. This makes school a constantly improving scientific experiment, designed perfectly to help your unique brain learn.
