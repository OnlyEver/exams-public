Think about playing your favorite video game. You don’t wait until your character has only 1 health point left before you use a healing potion, right? You keep an eye on your health bar and heal up early so you don't lose. For a long time, schools would wait until a student was failing terribly before giving them extra help. Today, teachers try to fix the problem *before* it gets bad. By setting up smart systems, we can catch problems early and help a student succeed before they ever fall behind!

## The Architecture of Prevention

We manage how to help all students by using a master plan. A Multi-Tiered System of Supports integrates academic and behavioral interventions into a unified prevention framework. That just means we combine our plan for schoolwork and our plan for behavior into one big safety net. After all, if a kid is struggling to read, they might act out in class because they feel frustrated!

Inside this big safety net is a step-by-step system. The Response to Intervention framework typically consists of three tiers of increasingly intensive instructional support. Think of these "tiers" like levels in a game:

*   **Tier 1:** Tier 1 interventions provide high-quality core instruction to all students in the general education classroom. If the teaching is great, Tier 1 supports typically meet the academic and behavioral needs of approximately 80 percent of the student population. 

    Let's use math to see what that 80 percent looks like in a school of 500 kids!
    Step 1: Write down the total number of students (500).
    Step 2: Change the percentage (80 percent) into a decimal by dividing by 100 (80 / 100 = 0.80).
    Step 3: Multiply the total number of students by the decimal (500 x 0.80 = 400).
    So, 400 kids do just fine with only Tier 1 teaching!

*   **Tier 2:** For the kids who need a little extra help, Tier 2 interventions provide targeted, small-group support for students who do not adequately respond to Tier 1 instruction. Tier 2 supports are typically required for approximately 10 to 15 percent of the student population.
*   **Tier 3:** Finally, Tier 3 interventions are designed for the 1 to 5 percent of students who do not respond to Tier 1 and Tier 2 supports. This is super focused, extra-strong help.

Across all levels, early intervention aims to provide targeted support before academic or behavioral learning gaps become severe. The law actually wants schools to do this! The Individuals with Disabilities Education Act allows school districts to use up to 15 percent of special education funds for early intervening services. For example, if a school gets \$10,000 for special education, they are allowed to use up to \$1,500 of it just to stop problems before they start!

## Finding the Cracks: Universal Screening and Warning Signs

To step in early, we have to figure out who needs help. Universal screening involves assessing all students to identify individuals at risk for academic or behavioral difficulties. Think of it like a metal detector at an airport that checks everybody.

For these screeners to work well, they have to be accurate. 
*   First, screening measures must possess high sensitivity to accurately identify students who are truly at-risk for failure. (Like a metal detector, it *always* beeps when someone has actual metal).
*   Also, screening measures must possess high specificity to avoid misidentifying typically developing students as at-risk. (It shouldn't beep for a plastic button). If the test tells us everyone is struggling when they aren't, teachers waste time helping kids who are already doing fine.

### Decoding the Warning Signs

Teachers are always looking for clues that a student might need backup.

**Academic Warning Signs:**
*   When kids are little, a lack of phonemic awareness is an early warning sign for potential reading failure in primary grades. Phonemic awareness just means being able to hear and play with the individual sounds in words.
*   Also, difficulty with rapid letter naming is an early predictor of subsequent reading disabilities. If you struggle to name your letters really fast, it might mean your brain processes things a bit slower.
*   We can't just hope a student figures it out later. First-grade reading failure is a statistically strong predictor of continued reading difficulties in later elementary grades. 

**Behavioral Warning Signs:**
*   Missing school is a huge clue. Chronic absenteeism is an early warning indicator for long-term academic failure and high school dropout. You can't learn if you aren't there!
*   Also, frequent office disciplinary referrals serve as a behavioral warning sign indicating the need for targeted Tier 2 interventions. Getting sent to the principal once is a mistake; going all the time means the student needs a better plan.
*   Sometimes, kids hold bad feelings inside. Behavioral warning signs of internalizing disorders include social withdrawal and frequent unprovoked crying. These kids might just hide in the corner and cry silently.
*   Other times, kids let it all out. Behavioral warning signs of externalizing disorders include physical aggression and property destruction, like hitting a classmate or breaking a pencil.

## Tier 1: Engineering the Core Environment

Let’s look at Tier 1, which works for 80 percent of kids. A great Tier 1 classroom stops problems before they even happen.

### Academic Prevention Strategies
For schoolwork, differentiated instruction proactively adjusts instructional content, processes, and products to accommodate diverse learner needs. Think of it like a pizza place that offers gluten-free crust or extra toppings so everyone gets a pizza they can actually enjoy!

When learning something tough, explicit instruction utilizes clear modeling and guided practice to prevent academic failure in at-risk learners. A basketball coach doesn't just hand you a ball and say "figure it out"; they show you *exactly* how to shoot. If it's still too hard, task analysis breaks complex academic skills into smaller instructional steps to prevent learner frustration. It’s like breaking a massive video game boss fight into small, easy stages.

During this, instructional scaffolding provides temporary support to enable an at-risk student to complete a task independently. Just like training wheels on a bike, the teacher takes the extra help away once you learn how to ride. Finally, high rates of opportunities to respond decrease disruptive behavior by keeping students actively engaged in instruction. If you are constantly answering questions or playing the game, you don't have time to be bored and act out!

### Proactive Behavioral Management
For behavior, Positive Behavioral Interventions and Supports uses data-based decision making to establish a proactive school-wide disciplinary framework. Basically, Positive Behavioral Interventions and Supports focuses on explicitly teaching expected behaviors rather than relying on punitive discipline. Instead of just grounding you when you mess up, teachers show you the right way to act. In class, Tier 1 behavior management relies on explicitly teaching a small number of positively stated classroom rules (like saying "Please walk" instead of "NO RUNNING").

Teachers have cool tricks to keep peace in the room:
*   Active supervision requires teachers to continuously scan the classroom and interact frequently with students to prevent off-task behavior. (Just walking around checking on everyone).
*   Teacher proximity control uses physical distance to prevent minor disruptive behaviors from escalating. Just standing near a whispering student often makes them quiet down!
*   Pre-correction involves reminding students of behavior expectations immediately before a challenging task or transition. (Reminding you to be quiet right *before* you walk down the hallway).
*   Behavior-specific praise reduces off-task behavior by explicitly stating the positive action a student performed. (Saying "Great job raising your hand!" instead of just "Good boy!").

For the whole class, The Good Behavior Game is a Tier 1 interdependent group contingency strategy used to prevent disruptive classroom behavior. Kids work in teams to earn a prize by acting right. Also, token economies are classroom prevention systems that reinforce expected behavior with exchangeable secondary reinforcers. This is exactly like earning tickets at an arcade to buy a toy!

## Tier 2: Deploying Targeted Interventions

When Tier 1 isn't quite enough, we bring in Tier 2 interventions.

### Academic Approaches
When kids need help with schoolwork, schools usually fix it in one of two ways:

| Intervention Model | Description |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Standard Treatment Protocol** | A standard treatment protocol involves using a predetermined research-based intervention for all students facing a specific academic difficulty. (If your bike gets a flat tire, the mechanic uses the exact same patch kit every single time). |
| **Problem-Solving Models** | Problem-solving models in Response to Intervention individualize early intervention strategies based on specific student deficits. (If your bike makes a weird clicking noise, a team has to investigate and build a custom fix just for you). |

### Behavioral Approaches
For behavior, Check-In/Check-Out is a Tier 2 behavioral intervention involving daily adult feedback on specific student goals. You meet with a mentor in the morning to set goals, and in the afternoon to see if you met them. Because of this, Check-In/Check-Out interventions statistically reduce office discipline referrals for students exhibiting mild problem behaviors.

For kids who struggle with friends, social skills training groups are a common Tier 2 intervention for students demonstrating mild interpersonal difficulties. It lets kids practice how to share or talk in a safe group. Lastly, self-monitoring interventions teach students to independently observe and record their own academic or behavioral performance. You use your own checklist to track your actions, making you the boss of your own behavior!

## Measuring the Impact: Data and Fidelity

None of this works if we don't check to see if the student is actually getting better! 

While teaching, formative assessment provides ongoing feedback to help teachers adjust instructional strategies during the learning process. It's like tasting soup while you are cooking it to see if it needs more salt. But for big interventions, progress monitoring uses brief and frequent assessments to evaluate the effectiveness of an implemented intervention. (Like taking a fast mini-quiz every week).

To know if you improved, you need a starting point. A baseline data point represents a student's current level of performance prior to the implementation of an intervention. (Your high score before you started practicing). Then, curriculum-based measurement is a standardized method for tracking student progress in basic academic skills over time. (Using the exact same type of reading test every Friday).

Let's do some math to see how teachers track progress!
Step 1: Record the baseline data point (Example: reading 40 words per minute).
Step 2: Record the new curriculum-based measurement score after two weeks of extra help (Example: reading 52 words per minute).
Step 3: Subtract the baseline score from the new score to find the total growth (52 - 40 = 12).
The student grew by 12 words per minute!

However, none of this data matters if we don't follow the rules. Fidelity of implementation refers to the accurate and consistent delivery of an intervention as intended by its developers. It means following the Lego instruction manual exactly step-by-step. 

Poor fidelity of implementation can lead to false conclusions about an intervention's effectiveness. If you are supposed to practice piano 30 minutes a day, but you only practice 5 minutes, you might fail your recital. The piano lessons aren't broken—you just didn't follow the plan! Teachers always make sure the plan was followed perfectly before they ever decide a student is failing.
