Think about how you act in class. It is not just random! A student ripping up a paper, getting out of their seat, or not talking isn't doing it for no reason. They are reacting to what is happening around them. They are trying to tell you what they need in the fastest way they know how. For a teacher, understanding this changes everything. Instead of getting stressed out and just handing out punishments, managing behavior becomes a cool science of setting up the room and teaching new skills. When we look at behavior as something that follows a predictable pattern, we can change our classrooms for the better!

## The Architecture of the Environment: Proactive Management

Before a teacher even thinks about stopping bad behavior, they have to look at the room itself. The best tricks happen before a student even walks in!

**Proactive classroom management involves structuring the physical environment to minimize crowding and visual distractions.** Imagine trying to play a video game with people bumping your elbows and flashing lights in your eyes. It's too much! A room with clear walking paths and neat walls keeps everyone calm. But space is only half the puzzle; time is just as important.

Chaos loves free time. **Establishing clear classroom routines decreases unassigned time that often leads to disruptive behavior.** Plus, **establishing clear classroom routines reduces transition times between instructional activities**. This means kids move smoothly from one task to the next without waiting around and getting bored.

Teachers also need to teach their expectations super clearly. Sometimes teachers make a giant list of "Do Nots." But actually, **teachers should establish a small number of broad classroom rules rather than an exhaustive list of specific behavioral prohibitions.** How many? **The IRIS Center recommends establishing between three and five positively stated classroom rules.** Most importantly, **teachers should state classroom rules in positive terms to explicitly instruct students on expected behaviors.** If a coach just yells, "Don't drop the ball!" you still don't know *how* to catch it. Tell them what *to* do!

Once the room is set up, the teacher has to be active. **Active supervision requires teachers to continuously move around the classroom and visually scan the environment.** During a lesson, **increasing a student's opportunities to respond during instruction directly decreases off-task behavior.** If you are answering questions and playing the game, you don't have time to mess around.

When students do a great job, how we cheer for them matters. Saying "Good job" is nice, but kind of blurry. Instead, we use **behavior-specific praise**, which **identifies the exact desired behavior a student is demonstrating in the moment** (like, "I really like how you raised your hand before giving the answer!"). The facts show that **behavior-specific praise is more effective at increasing desired behaviors than general verbal praise.**

Finally, we try to guess where trouble might happen. **Precorrection involves anticipating a predictable behavior problem and providing a prompt about expected behavior before the problem occurs.** If a teacher knows the walk to the lunchroom is always messy, they pause the class a minute early to remind everyone exactly how to walk in the hallway.

## The Macro-Level Systems: PBIS

Looking at the whole school, great schools use a master plan for everyone. **Positive Behavioral Interventions and Supports (PBIS) is a three-tiered framework for improving student behavior across a school.**

Think of PBIS like a sports training camp with three levels:
1. **Tier 1 of Positive Behavioral Interventions and Supports involves universal behavior expectations applied to all students across all school settings.** This is the basic training—everyone gets the same clear rules, routines, and high-fives.
2. **Tier 2 of Positive Behavioral Interventions and Supports provides targeted group interventions for students who do not respond adequately to Tier 1 supports.** This is like a special practice group for players who need a little extra help, like a daily check-in with a coach.
3. **Tier 3 of Positive Behavioral Interventions and Supports involves individualized intensive interventions for students with significant behavioral challenges.** This is totally personalized, one-on-one coaching to help students who are really struggling.

## The Physics of Behavior: Applied Behavior Analysis

To really help kids at Tier 3, a teacher needs to know why humans act the way they do. **Applied Behavior Analysis relies on the premise that behavior is learned and influenced by environmental consequences.** Basically, we learn how to act based on what happens around us after we do something.

To figure this out, we use the **Antecedent-Behavior-Consequence model**, which **is a direct observation tool used to analyze the environmental events surrounding a specific behavior.**

> **The ABCs of Behavior:**
> *   **Antecedent:** **An antecedent is an event or stimulus that occurs immediately before a target behavior.** It is the spark or the trigger. 
> *   **Behavior:** The actual thing the student does.
> *   **Consequence:** **A consequence is an environmental event or stimulus that occurs immediately after a target behavior.** 

In this science, "consequence" isn't a bad word like "punishment." It just means whatever happens next! **Behavioral consequences determine the future probability of a specific behavior occurring again.** If the consequence is awesome, you'll probably do it again!

### The Four Forces: Functions of Behavior
Why do kids act out? They are trying to get one of four things. **The four primary functions of behavior are sensory stimulation, escape, access to attention, and access to tangibles.** 

**Special educators commonly use the acronym SEAT to remember the four primary functions of behavior:**
*   **S**ensory (This feels good, like bouncing your leg or humming a tune.)
*   **E**scape (I want to get away from this hard math or this loud room!)
*   **A**ttention (Look at me! I want my friends or teacher to notice me.)
*   **T**angibles (I want a specific thing, like an iPad, a toy, or a slice of pizza.)

### Influencing the Trajectory: Reinforcement and Punishment

When we react to behavior, we either want to see it happen more or happen less. 

**Reinforcement is a behavioral consequence that always increases the future frequency of a target behavior.** (It makes the behavior happen *more*.)
**Punishment is a behavioral consequence that always decreases the future frequency of a target behavior.** (It makes the behavior happen *less*.)

To make these changes, we can either *add* something (Positive) or *take something away* (Negative). Let's look at how this works:

| Action | Reinforcement (Makes behavior happen MORE) | Punishment (Makes behavior happen LESS) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Positive (Add something)** | **Positive reinforcement involves adding a desirable stimulus after a behavior to increase the future frequency of that behavior.** (Like giving you \$5 for doing your chores.) | **Positive punishment involves adding an aversive stimulus after a behavior to decrease the future frequency of that behavior.** (Like giving you extra chores because you yelled.) |
| **Negative (Take something away)** | **Negative reinforcement involves removing an aversive stimulus after a behavior to increase the future frequency of that behavior.** (Like saying you don't have to do the dishes tonight because you were so helpful earlier.) | **Negative punishment involves removing a desirable stimulus after a behavior to decrease the future frequency of that behavior.** (Like taking away your video game time because you hit your brother.) |

## Building and Dismantling Behaviors

Sometimes we have to teach a brand-new, tricky skill. **Shaping involves reinforcing successive approximations of a desired target behavior until the final behavior is achieved.** Think of it like leveling up in a game. If a kid is too scared to go into the lunchroom, first you might reward them just for walking past the door. Next time, they get a reward for standing inside the door. Finally, they get a reward for sitting at a table for one minute. 

For big tasks, like washing hands or doing a math problem, we use **task analysis**, which **is the process of identifying the specific sequence of discrete steps required to complete a complex skill.** Once we know the steps, we use **chaining**, which **involves breaking a complex multi-step skill into smaller sequential steps to be taught one at a time.**
*   **Forward chaining teaches the first step of a behavioral sequence first while prompting the student through the remaining steps.** 
*   **Backward chaining teaches the last step of a behavioral sequence first while prompting the student through the preceding steps.** This is awesome because the student always gets the thrill of finishing the job at the very end!

To keep kids excited about learning, teachers might use a **token economy**, which **is a behavior management system where students earn symbolic rewards that can be exchanged for backup reinforcers.** It is exactly like earning tickets at an arcade to trade in for a giant stuffed bear! 

Here is a quick math example of calculating points in a token economy:
Suppose a student earns 3 tokens every hour for 4 hours, and then loses 2 tokens for running in the hall. How many tokens do they have to spend at the prize store? Let's solve this step-by-step:
Step 1. Find the total earned tokens: 3 tokens per hour multiplied by 4 hours = 12 tokens.
Step 2. Subtract the lost tokens: 12 - 2 = 10 tokens.
Step 3. The final amount they can spend is 10 tokens.

On the flip side, if a behavior is really bad, we might use **extinction**, which **involves completely withholding the reinforcement that previously maintained a target behavior.** If a student screams just so you will look at them, and you totally ignore the screaming, you are using extinction. But watch out! **An extinction burst is a temporary increase in the frequency or intensity of a behavior immediately after an extinction procedure is implemented.** The student is basically thinking, "Hey, screaming usually gets me attention! I better scream way louder!"

## The Art of Replacement: Differential Reinforcement

You can't just make a bad behavior disappear; you have to replace it with a good one! This is called differential reinforcement. It means you reward the good stuff and ignore the bad stuff.

*   **Differential Reinforcement of Alternative Behavior (DRA) involves reinforcing a specific acceptable behavior that serves the exact same function as the target problem behavior.** If a student shouts to get your attention, you reward them when they raise their hand instead. It's an alternative way to get the *exact same* thing!
*   **Differential Reinforcement of Incompatible Behavior (DRI) involves reinforcing a specific behavior that physically cannot be performed at the exact same time as the target problem behavior.** You can't run around the room if you are sitting nicely on an exercise ball. So, you reward them for sitting on the ball, making running impossible!
*   **Differential Reinforcement of Other Behavior (DRO) involves providing reinforcement when a target problem behavior does not occur during a predetermined time interval.** For example, "You went 10 whole minutes without saying a bad word! Here is your token." 

## The Investigative Process: Assessment and Measurement

To make these cool plans work, we have to measure what's actually happening. We can't just guess! 

*   **Frequency recording counts the exact number of times a specific behavior occurs within a defined observation period.** (Like keeping a tally of how many times a kid taps their pencil).
*   **Duration recording measures the total amount of time a student engages in a specific continuous behavior.** (Like using a stopwatch to see exactly how long a crying fit lasts).
*   **Latency recording measures the amount of time between the delivery of an instructional prompt and the initiation of the student's corresponding behavior.** (Like measuring the seconds between when a teacher says, "Open your book," and when the kid actually opens it).
*   **Interval recording divides an observation period into smaller blocks of time to document whether a specific behavior occurred during each individual block.** This is great for things that happen too fast to count one by one!

When a behavior is super tough, we put on our detective hats. **A Functional Behavior Assessment (FBA) is a systematic process used to identify the underlying purpose of a specific problem behavior.** 

This is actually a rule by law! **The Individuals with Disabilities Education Act requires a Functional Behavior Assessment when a student's behavior significantly impedes the student's learning.** Plus, **the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act requires a Functional Behavior Assessment when a student faces a disciplinary change of placement for behavior related to the student's disability** (like if they are sent home for more than 10 days).

## The Blueprint for Change: The Behavior Intervention Plan

The FBA tells us *why* a kid is acting out. The next step is figuring out *what* to do about it! 

**A Behavior Intervention Plan (BIP) is a customized document outlining specific strategies to alter a student's environment.** Also, **a Behavior Intervention Plan outlines specific instructional strategies to teach a student appropriate replacement behaviors.**

A BIP isn't just a list of punishments. **Educators must base a Behavior Intervention Plan directly on the findings of a comprehensive Functional Behavior Assessment.** If our detective work shows a student is throwing pencils to *escape* super hard math, the plan shouldn't offer them extra recess as a reward for being good. Recess is a tangible item/activity, but what they really need is a way to escape the hard math! 

This brings us to the biggest rule in behavior science: **A replacement behavior taught in a Behavior Intervention Plan must serve the exact same function as the student's target problem behavior.** If a student rips up paper to escape a hard worksheet, we have to teach them a safe and polite way to ask for a break (which gives them that escape). When we give them a better way to get what they need, the bad behavior just disappears!
