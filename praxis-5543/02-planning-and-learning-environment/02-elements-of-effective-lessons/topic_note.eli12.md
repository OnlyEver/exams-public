Imagine trying to beat a really hard level in a video game without a map or a tutorial. It would be super frustrating! For some kids, especially those who learn differently, school can feel just like that. That is why teachers use a style of teaching called **explicit instruction**. This is **a highly structured teacher-directed approach essential for students with learning disabilities**. It is basically a clear, step-by-step guide where the teacher leads the way so you never get lost. Teachers also make sure they use **evidence-based practices that have been proven effective through rigorous scientific research**. This means they use methods that scientists and experts have tested and know for a fact will work to help your brain learn.

## The Blueprint: Precision in Objectives and Individualization

Before you build a huge LEGO set, you need to know what it is supposed to look like at the end. Teachers do this too by writing goals for the day! 

An awesome lesson plan starts with super clear goals. **Lesson objectives must clearly state the expected observable student behavior**—meaning the teacher writes down exactly what they want to see you do, like writing a sentence or solving a puzzle. 

To make these goals perfect, teachers build them with three special parts:

| Component | Function | Real-World Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Conditions** | **Lesson objectives must specify the conditions under which the student behavior will occur.** This means exactly what tools you will be given. | *"Given a calculator and a worksheet..."* |
| **Behavior** | The exact action the teacher will see you do. | *"...the student will write the answers..."* |
| **Criteria** | **Lesson objectives must include the criteria for acceptable performance.** This is your winning score! | *"...getting 9 out of 10 right."* |

Because every student is unique, teachers have to plan for special helpers, too. **Special education lesson plans must explicitly list the accommodations required by students Individualized Education Programs.** If your special learning plan (called an IEP) says you need extra time on a test, or an audiobook, the teacher has to write those exact helpful tools right into their daily plan.

## Igniting the Engine: Review and the Anticipatory Set

Have you ever watched a "Previously on..." video recap before watching a new episode of your favorite TV show? Teachers do the same thing! 

**Daily review of prior learning is an essential component of effective lesson planning for students with cognitive delays.** Reviewing what you learned yesterday helps warm up your brain so you are totally ready for today's new stuff.

Once yesterday's lesson is fresh in your mind, the teacher throws out a hook. **An effective lesson plan for explicit instruction begins with an anticipatory set to activate prior knowledge.** This is a fun sneak peek that gets your brain ready to connect what you already know to the new lesson. A famous teacher named **Madeline Hunter developed the instructional model containing elements such as anticipatory set modeling and guided practice.** She knew that **an anticipatory set focuses student attention before the main lesson begins**. It is just like a sports warm-up that gets you totally focused before the big game starts.

## The Core Architecture: "I Do, We Do, You Do"

At the heart of Madeline Hunter's plan is a way to share the work between the teacher and you. **The I do we do you do sequence is a common framework for explicit instruction.** Here is how it works:

### The "I Do" Phase (Modeling)
First, the teacher shows you how to play. **The I do phase involves the teacher modeling the target skill through step-by-step demonstration.** But they don't just quietly show you what to do; they share their secret thoughts! **Teachers must use think-alouds during the modeling phase to make internal cognitive processes visible to students.** For example, if a teacher is baking, they will say out loud, "Hmm, I need 2 cups of flour. Let me grab my measuring cup so I don't pour too much." They let you hear exactly how they figure things out.

### The "We Do" Phase (Guided Practice)
Next, you try it together. **The we do phase consists of guided practice with significant teacher support.** The teacher is right there cheering you on and helping you out. If you make a tiny mistake, **teachers must provide immediate corrective feedback during guided practice.** That means they stop and help you fix the error right at that exact second, before it becomes a bad habit. 

When are you ready for the next phase? **Research recommends an 80 to 90 percent accuracy rate during guided practice before moving students to independent practice.** You need to be getting an A or a B on your practice tries with the teacher before you are ready to do it all by yourself.

### The "You Do" Phase (Independent Practice)
Finally, the training wheels come off! **The you do phase involves independent practice without teacher assistance.** 

> **Warning:** Remember the golden rule: **Independent practice should only occur after students achieve a high rate of success during guided practice.** You shouldn't be asked to play a solo game until you've proven you know the controls!

## The Geometry of Sequencing

If you want to climb to the top of a building, you have to use a staircase, not just jump! **Instruction for students with mild to moderate disabilities should progress from simple concepts to more complex concepts.** 

**Logical sequencing requires breaking complex skills into smaller manageable steps.** This makes learning feel easy and doable. **Breaking complex skills into smaller instructional steps is known as task analysis.** If the goal is making a pizza, the task analysis steps would be: 1) roll the dough, 2) spread the sauce, 3) sprinkle the cheese, and 4) bake it.

### The Concrete-Representational-Abstract (CRA) Sequence
Learning math can feel like reading an alien language, so teachers use a special staircase for it. **The Concrete-Representational-Abstract sequence is an evidence-based logical progression for math instruction.** 

1. **Concrete:** **The concrete phase of math instruction involves using physical manipulatives to solve problems.** This means using real objects you can touch, like putting 3 actual blocks next to 2 actual blocks.
2. **Representational:** **The representational phase of math instruction involves using drawings or pictures to solve problems.** Now, instead of real blocks, you just draw little circles or pictures on your paper.
3. **Abstract:** **The abstract phase of math instruction involves using numbers and symbols exclusively to solve problems.** This is when you just use regular math symbols. 

Let's look at an abstract math problem, like solving 14 + 15. We don't just guess the answer; we follow exact steps!
Step 1: Write the number 14, and write the number 15 right below it, lining up the sides perfectly.
Step 2: Look at the column on the right (the ones). Add 4 + 5. The answer is 9. Write 9 at the bottom.
Step 3: Look at the column on the left (the tens). Add 1 + 1. The answer is 2. Write 2 at the bottom next to the 9.
Step 4: Put them together, and your final answer is 29!

## Velocity and Engagement: Pacing and OTRs

If a movie moves too slowly, you might get bored and start looking around the room. Classrooms are the exact same way! **Instructional pacing must be brisk to maintain student engagement.** Brisk just means moving at a quick, energetic speed.

**Brisk instructional pacing reduces off-task behavior in students with attention deficits.** When the lesson moves fast, your brain stays busy, and you simply don't have time to get distracted.

To keep the class moving quickly, **effective lesson plans include frequent Opportunities to Respond.** 
**An Opportunity to Respond is a teacher prompt that requires a student to actively engage with the instructional material.** It means the teacher asks you to do something right then, like holding up a mini-whiteboard, giving a thumbs-up, or shouting out the answer together with your friends. 

**High rates of Opportunities to Respond are linked to increased academic achievement and decreased disruptive behavior.** The more you are asked to participate, the more you learn, and the less likely anyone is to goof off!

## Structural Supports: Scaffolding and Differentiation

When someone is learning to ride a bike, they use training wheels. In school, **scaffolding provides temporary instructional support to help a student master a new skill.** This might be a cheat sheet for times tables or a paragraph where you just fill in the blanks.

But you can't leave the training wheels on forever! **Teachers must gradually withdraw scaffolding as student competence increases.** As you get better, they help you a little less. **The gradual withdrawal of instructional support is known as fading.** 

Also, teachers know that everyone's brain works differently. **Differentiated instruction requires planning multiple pathways for students to access the learning content.** If you find reading a textbook really hard, the teacher might give you an awesome podcast or a video to learn the exact same facts. On the flip side, **differentiated instruction requires planning multiple pathways for students to demonstrate their acquired knowledge.** If writing a long essay is tough for you, your teacher might let you build a model or give a fun speech to prove how much you learned!

## Sealing the Concrete: Assessment, Closure, and Long-Term Memory

How does a teacher know if you are actually learning the cool new stuff? **Formative assessment must be embedded throughout the lesson plan to check for student understanding.** These are just quick, tiny checks—like asking the class to hold up a green card if they get it, or a red card if they are confused.

At the end of class, you don't just pack up and run when the bell rings. **Lesson closure provides a structured review of the key concepts taught during the lesson.** It is a quick, neat wrap-up of what you did today and why it matters.

### The Physics of Long-Term Memory
To make sure you remember things for a long time, teachers use two special practice tricks:

1. **Spaced Practice:** **Spaced practice involves reviewing material across multiple time periods to improve long-term retention.** Instead of cramming for three hours the night before a test, you practice for just 15 minutes a day over a whole week.
2. **Interleaved Practice:** **Interleaved practice involves mixing different types of problems within a single practice session.** This is like a sports practice where you mix up catching, throwing, and running, instead of just throwing for an hour straight.

Why mix it up? Because your brain has to work harder to figure out which rule to use for each problem! **Interleaved practice improves problem-solving skills for students with learning disabilities more effectively than massed practice** (which is doing the exact same thing over and over). By mixing up the problems, your memory becomes super strong!
