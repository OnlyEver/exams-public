Imagine trying to group all the players in a massive online video game based on how they play. In schools, a law called the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act attempts a remarkably similar feat to help students who learn differently. To give students the right power-ups and support, the law must first create a common rulebook. Therefore, the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act defines thirteen specific disability categories under which children may be eligible for special education services. 

However, before we look at these thirteen categories, we have to understand the golden rule of special education. A student might have a note from a doctor with a medical diagnosis, but that alone doesn't automatically unlock special education. Every disability category under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act requires that the student's condition adversely affects their educational performance to qualify for services. We don't create special learning plans just to treat a medical label; we create them to help the student actually succeed in class!

## The Cognitive and Processing Profiles

To understand how a child learns, we have to look at how their brain processes, stores, and brings up information. Two categories deal with how the brain thinks, but they are very different from each other.

### Specific Learning Disability (SLD)
Think of the brain as an amazing, high-speed computer. For a student with a Specific Learning Disability, 95% of the computer might run perfectly, but one specific wire—maybe the one that sounds out words—gets jammed. 

By definition, a specific learning disability is a disorder in one or more of the basic psychological processes involved in understanding or using language. This "jam" in the brain's wiring means a specific learning disability may manifest in an imperfect ability to listen, think, speak, read, write, spell, or do mathematical calculations. Because an SLD is super specific to one area, it includes famous learning differences you might have heard of. Dyslexia (trouble with reading) is classified as a specific learning disability. Dysgraphia (trouble with writing) is classified as a specific learning disability. Dyscalculia (trouble with math) is also classified as a specific learning disability. 

To see how an imperfect math ability might look, let's do a simple math calculation for managing an allowance:
Step 1: The student starts with \$10.
Step 2: They want to buy a snack for \$3, so they need to set up the problem 10 - 3.
Step 3: They subtract 3 from 10 to get an answer of 7.
Step 4: They realize they have \$7 left over.
A student with dyscalculia might find these exact numbers and steps incredibly confusing, even if they are super smart in reading or science!

> **The SLD Exclusions:** To make sure schools don't give a student the wrong label, there are strict rules about what *isn't* an SLD. A specific learning disability diagnosis requires ruling out primary sensory or motor disabilities as the main cause of the learning problem (for example, a student who is failing reading just because they need glasses). It also requires ruling out intellectual disability as the primary cause of the learning problem. Finally, to be fair to everyone, a specific learning disability diagnosis requires ruling out environmental, cultural, or economic disadvantage as the primary cause of the learning problem. If a kid is failing simply because they've never had a good teacher, they do not have an SLD.

### Intellectual Disability (ID)
If SLD is a glitch in just one specific wire, Intellectual Disability is like the computer's main processor running much slower overall. Intellectual disability involves significantly subaverage general intellectual functioning (meaning they score very low on an IQ test).

But an IQ test is just a piece of paper, and real life is way more complicated! Because of this, an intellectual disability diagnosis requires concurrent deficits in adaptive behavior. This just means the student also struggles with real-life, everyday skills, like talking to others, getting dressed, or handling money safely. Plus, the characteristics of an intellectual disability must manifest during a child's developmental period to meet eligibility criteria. This means it has to show up while they are growing up as a kid, not suddenly happen to an adult.

## The Behavioral, Emotional, and Social Spectra

How a student acts in their environment, controls their feelings, and makes friends makes up the next two major categories.

### Autism
Autism is like having a totally different, but still awesome, operating system in your brain—like a Mac instead of a PC! Under the law, autism is a developmental disability significantly affecting verbal and nonverbal communication. It is also a developmental disability significantly affecting social interaction, which means making friends or reading body language can be tricky. 

Because it's related to how a kid develops, the characteristics of autism are generally evident before a child reaches age three. As you hang out with friends, you might notice some unique behaviors. A child diagnosed with autism often exhibits repetitive activities. Similarly, a child diagnosed with autism often exhibits stereotyped movements, like rocking back and forth or flapping their hands. Instead of thinking of this as weird, know that it's a great way for them to calm themselves down! 

Also, resistance to environmental change or daily routines is a common characteristic of autism. A predictable schedule makes them feel safe. You will also notice that unusual responses to sensory experiences are frequently observed in children with autism. A student might cover their ears because a normal lightbulb sounds incredibly loud to them, or they might love the feeling of a super heavy weighted blanket.

### Emotional Disturbance (ED)
Emotional Disturbance can be tricky to figure out because feelings are hard to measure. At its core, an inability to learn that cannot be explained by intellectual, sensory, or health factors is a defining characteristic of emotional disturbance. It means the student's heavy emotions are building a wall that blocks them from learning, and it’s not because they are sick or struggling with reading.

To fit this category, a student has to show these signs for a long time:
1. An inability to build or maintain satisfactory interpersonal relationships with peers and teachers.
2. Inappropriate types of behavior or feelings under normal circumstances (like laughing when something is really sad).
3. Emotional disturbance can manifest as a general pervasive mood of unhappiness or depression.
4. A tendency to develop physical symptoms or fears associated with personal or school problems (like throwing up every day because they are so anxious about math class).

**Important Distinctions within ED:** 
The Individuals with Disabilities Education Act category of emotional disturbance includes children diagnosed with schizophrenia. But there is a huge rule you have to know: The emotional disturbance category excludes children who are socially maladjusted without a concurrent determination of emotional disturbance. What does that mean? If a kid totally understands the rules, can control their feelings, but simply *chooses* to break the rules to look cool for their friends, they are "socially maladjusted." They don't get special education unless they *also* actually have an emotional disturbance.

## The Physical, Health, and Neurological Realities

Physical limits and health conditions can drain a student's energy or make it hard to sit in a normal classroom.

### Other Health Impairment (OHI)
The name of this category sounds kind of vague! Other health impairment involves having limited strength, vitality, or alertness. But here is a wild twist that tricks a lot of people: Other health impairment includes a heightened alertness to environmental stimuli that results in limited alertness with respect to the educational environment. 

Think of a student with ADHD. Their brain is *so* alert to a tiny fly buzzing near the window that they have limited alertness to the teacher's lesson! This is exactly why Attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder is classified under the Other health impairment category.

An Other health impairment classification requires the student's limited alertness or vitality to be due to chronic or acute health problems. Besides ADHD, Tourette syndrome is classified under the Other health impairment category, because physical tics take up a lot of energy. Common sicknesses like asthma and diabetes are classified under the Other health impairment category. Also, epilepsy is classified under the Other health impairment category.

### Orthopedic Impairment
This category is all about the physical bones and muscles of the body. Orthopedic impairment includes impairments caused by congenital anomalies (which means physical defects a baby is born with, like a missing foot). Orthopedic impairment includes physical impairments caused by diseases such as poliomyelitis (polio). Lastly, orthopedic impairment includes physical impairments from causes such as cerebral palsy or amputations. Basically, if a physical body issue makes it hard to access the school, it belongs here.

### Traumatic Brain Injury (TBI)
How the injury happened means everything here. Traumatic brain injury is an acquired injury to the brain caused by an external physical force (like hitting your head hard in a skateboarding crash). A traumatic brain injury classification requires the injury to result in total or partial functional disability or psychosocial impairment. 

Because the rule says it must come from an "external force" after you are born, the exceptions are super strict. The traumatic brain injury category excludes congenital brain injuries (injuries you are born with). The traumatic brain injury category excludes degenerative brain injuries (diseases that slowly hurt the brain over time). Surprisingly, the traumatic brain injury category excludes brain injuries induced by birth trauma. Those other injuries might get help under different categories, but they are not legally considered TBIs.

## The Sensory and Communication Gateways

How information goes into the brain (seeing, hearing) and how it comes out (talking) make up the next few categories.

### Speech or Language Impairment
Speech or language impairment is defined as a communication disorder. It's about how you form words and use language. For example, stuttering is an example of a speech or language impairment. Impaired articulation (when someone physically can't make the "R" or "S" sound with their mouth) is an example of a speech or language impairment. Finally, a voice impairment (like speaking with a really weird pitch or volume you can't control) is an example of a speech or language impairment.

### Hearing Impairment vs. Deafness
Both of these deal with ears, but the law separates them based on how well the student can hear spoken words. 
*   **Deafness** is defined as a hearing impairment so severe that the child is impaired in processing linguistic information through hearing. If they can't use their hearing to understand words—even with hearing aids—they are considered deaf.
*   **Hearing impairment** refers to a hearing deficit that adversely affects educational performance without meeting the severity definition of deafness. Interestingly, a hearing impairment under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act can be a permanent condition. But, a hearing impairment under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act can be a fluctuating condition (meaning it comes and goes, like getting bad ear infections that temporarily block sound).

### Visual Impairment (Including Blindness)
Visual impairment including blindness means an impairment in vision that adversely affects a child's educational performance even with correction. This means if you have terrible eyesight, but putting on a regular pair of glasses fixes it, you do not qualify for special education! Also, the category of visual impairment under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act includes partial sight, so you don't have to be completely in the dark to get help.

### Deaf-Blindness
Deaf-blindness involves concomitant hearing and visual impairments (concomitant just means they happen at the exact same time). This isn't just simple math where deafness + blindness = deaf-blindness. It's way harder! The combination of impairments in deaf-blindness causes severe communication needs, because the student loses both of their main ways to gather information from far away. 

Because it is so incredibly tough, a child with deaf-blindness cannot be accommodated in special education programs designed solely for children with deafness. Likewise, a child with deaf-blindness cannot be accommodated in special education programs designed solely for children with blindness. They need a special class that handles *both* at the same time.

### Multiple Disabilities
Multiple disabilities involve concomitant impairments such as intellectual disability and orthopedic impairment happening at the same time. Just like the last category, the combination of impairments in multiple disabilities causes severe educational needs that transcend the services for one individual impairment. However, the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act category of multiple disabilities explicitly excludes deaf-blindness, so that deaf-blind students keep their own special funding and category.

## The Diagnostic Waiting Room: Developmental Delay

We finally get to the trickiest part of kids growing up. Young kids' brains change super fast, and slapping a lifelong label on a 4-year-old is really hard to do perfectly. 

The developmental delay category acknowledges that precise disability diagnoses can be difficult to determine accurately in young children. By contrast, specific disability categories require precise, long-term diagnostic criteria distinct from the broader developmental delay category.

To make sure little kids get help while doctors figure things out, the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act allows states to use the term developmental delay for children aged three through nine. Developmental delay applies to children experiencing delays in physical, cognitive, communication, social, emotional, or adaptive development. 

Basically, a developmental delay classification allows a young child to receive special education services without being labeled with a specific disability category. It is like a temporary bridge!

But a bridge has to end. Because this label is temporary, a student classified with a developmental delay must be formally re-evaluated by age ten. 

Let's look at a math calculation to see exactly how long a kid can keep this temporary label if they get it right on their 3rd birthday:
Step 1: Start with the maximum age allowed, which is 9.
Step 2: Subtract the starting age of 3 (9 - 3 = 6).
Step 3: Add 1 to count the starting year too (6 + 1 = 7).
Step 4: The child can have this label for a total of 7 years maximum.

When time is up, they have to move on. To continue receiving special education services after age nine, a child previously classified with a developmental delay must qualify under one of the thirteen specific disability categories. 

Learning all these rules isn't about trapping kids in boxes. It's about knowing exactly how they play the game of life so you can hand them the perfect controller to win!
