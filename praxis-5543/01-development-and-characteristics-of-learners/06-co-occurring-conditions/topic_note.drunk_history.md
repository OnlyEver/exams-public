Listen to me, slide those stale pretzels over here and pay attention, because you’re about to walk into your first special education classroom thinking these kids are going to fit into neat, color-coded diagnostic folders. They won't. Neurology doesn't care about your filing system. You are going to be dealing with comorbidity, my friend, which refers to the simultaneous presence of two or more chronic diseases or conditions in a single individual. But don't think of it like two quiet roommates splitting rent in a brain. No, it’s a reality TV house. Co-occurring disabilities interact to create educational needs far greater than the sum of the individual conditions. One disability plus one disability doesn't equal two; it equals a burning mansion, a stolen car, and a lawsuit.

Let’s talk about the absolute superstar of this reality show: Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder. ADHD is the ultimate neurological co-conspirator. For instance, approximately one-third to one-half of children with Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder also have a Specific Learning Disability. When these two team up, it’s a total heist movie. The executive functioning deficits inherent in Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder exacerbate the academic challenges of a Specific Learning Disability. Imagine trying to pick a highly complex lock on a bank vault (that's the SLD) while someone is simultaneously blasting an airhorn in your ear and spinning your chair (that’s the ADHD). Specifically, the working memory deficits associated with Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder compound the decoding difficulties seen in dyslexia. A kid is trying to sound out a word, but their working memory drops the first syllable down a bottomless elevator shaft before they can even look at the second syllable. Because of this absolute chaos, your interventions have to do double duty. Accommodations for a student with both a Specific Learning Disability and Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder must simultaneously support academic skill deficits and self-regulation. You can't just give them a graphic organizer for the lock-picking and ignore the airhorn; you have to fix both or the heist fails. 

And ADHD’s guest list doesn't stop there. Oppositional Defiant Disorder frequently co-occurs with Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder. The impulsivity of ADHD kicks the door open, and the defiance of ODD walks right in to tell you to go away. Moods get hijacked, too; bipolar disorder has a high rate of comorbidity with Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder in children and adolescents, turning the classroom into a high-stakes emotional rollercoaster. ADHD even crashes other neurological parties. Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder frequently co-occurs with Autism Spectrum Disorder. And over in the tic disorder neighborhood? Individuals with Tourette syndrome frequently present with co-occurring Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder. Speaking of Tourette's, those physical tics often come with mental ones, as a high percentage of individuals with Tourette syndrome also experience Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder. The brain gets trapped in a loop, both physically and psychologically.

Now, let's look at the Specific Learning Disabilities. They run in packs. If the bridge is collapsing, the road underneath is probably crumbling too. Dyslexia frequently co-occurs with dysgraphia—if the brain's reading wires are crossed, the writing and motor-orthographic wires are usually sparking right next to them. It doesn't stop with letters, either. Dyscalculia frequently co-occurs with dyslexia, because if symbolic processing is crashing in the language center, it's probably crashing in the math center, too. And here is where it gets devastatingly tricky: Specific Learning Disabilities in reading frequently co-occur with expressive language disorders. This is the ultimate communication trap, because co-occurring language disorders and reading disabilities severely impair reading comprehension. It’s like trying to translate a secret code into a language you physically struggle to speak. 

With all this going on, you have to be terrified of the glaring spotlight effect. In our world, diagnostic overshadowing occurs when an evaluator attributes a student's symptoms entirely to an existing primary diagnosis. It’s a magic trick: the magician waves a flashy red scarf, and you completely miss him pocketing your watch. Because people are so blinded by the primary label, diagnostic overshadowing often prevents the identification of secondary or co-occurring disabilities in students. You see this all the time with behavior. The presence of a severe behavioral disorder alongside a learning disability often leads teachers to overlook the underlying academic deficit. The teacher just sees a kid flipping a desk and thinks, "Wow, what a defiant kid." No! That desk-flip is a smokescreen because they are a sixth-grader reading at a first-grade level and you just asked them to read out loud! If you fall for the smokescreen, you fail as a teacher, because addressing a behavioral challenge without remediating a co-occurring learning disability usually results in failed behavioral interventions. You cannot sticker-chart a kid into knowing phonics.

This misdiagnosis has tragic downstream effects. We aren't just talking about bad grades; we are talking about broken spirits. Repeated academic failures related to an unaccommodated learning disability significantly increase the risk of secondary anxiety and depression. Year after year of drowning academically takes a massive psychological toll, which is exactly why depression is a common secondary emotional condition for students with chronic learning disabilities. And if you ignore the behavioral warning signs? Conduct Disorder often develops in adolescents who have a history of untreated Oppositional Defiant Disorder. The villain origin story solidifies. 

You see similar massive secondary struggles in kids with severe developmental and medical complexities. Anxiety disorders are highly prevalent in children diagnosed with Autism Spectrum Disorder—imagine how terrifying the world is when sensory inputs feel like physical assaults. Plus, the electrical wiring of the brain often overlaps in dangerous ways. Epilepsy frequently co-occurs with Autism Spectrum Disorder, and similarly, epilepsy is a common co-occurring medical condition in individuals with Intellectual Disabilities. Brain wiring also links speech and movement, which is why developmental coordination disorders frequently co-occur with Speech and Language Impairments. 

To stop this madness, you need a full crew. No lone wolves allowed. Multidisciplinary team evaluations are strictly required to accurately identify the presence of overlapping disabilities. You need the school psychologist, the speech pathologist, the occupational therapist, everyone triangulating the data. Once you have the data, the law—the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act—takes over. Under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act, Multiple Disabilities is a specific, distinct disability category. But you don't get that label just for having two random things. The Multiple Disabilities category classification requires the simultaneous presence of impairments that cause severe educational needs. This is the heavyweight division. To qualify, students classified under Multiple Disabilities have educational needs that cannot be accommodated in a special education program solely for one of the impairments. You try treating the severe orthopedic impairment without addressing the severe intellectual disability, and the whole system crashes. However, there is one massive exception: the Multiple Disabilities category does not include deaf-blindness under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act. Because the absolute sensory isolation of that combination is so uniquely profound, deaf-blindness is treated as its own independent disability category under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act. 

Regardless of what primary label wins the legal lottery, the paperwork has to cover the whole kid. An Individualized Education Program must comprehensively address all of a student's disability-related needs regardless of the primary disability label. If their label is Emotional Disturbance but they have a speech impediment, you are legally on the hook for that speech goal. Because of this intertwined reality, interventions for multiple disabilities necessitate close coordination among special educators and related service providers. You all have to steer the ship together. 

And finally, when the ship hits an iceberg and you need to figure out why the kid is melting down, you do your FBA. But functional behavioral assessments for students with multiple disabilities must account for the impact of both cognitive deficits and sensory impairments on behavior. You cannot just stand there with a clipboard tallying how many times they hit their head; you have to be a detective who asks, "Is this kid in sensory agony from the fluorescent lights, but their cognitive deficit prevents them from verbally telling me?" 

Look at the whole messy, interacting, beautiful reality of the kid. Don't be blinded by the first label you see. Now finish your drink, you've got lesson plans to write.
