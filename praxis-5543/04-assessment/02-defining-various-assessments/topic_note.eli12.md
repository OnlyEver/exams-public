Imagine playing a hard video game where your screen is completely blacked out. You can’t see your health bar, your map, or your score. You would only know if you did a good job when the game suddenly yelled "GAME OVER" or "YOU WIN!" 

In a classroom, if a teacher tries to teach without ever checking to see what you understand, it is exactly like playing a game blindfolded. Tests and quizzes aren't just there to give you a grade or make you stressed. They exist so the teacher can figure out exactly what your brain is ready to learn next. Testing is a helpful tool that shows your teacher exactly what kind of hints or tricks you need to level up. For a teacher helping kids learn, knowing exactly which kind of test to use—like knowing whether to use a quick checklist or a big official test—is the secret to helping everyone succeed.

## The Axes of Evaluation: Standardization vs. Timing

Sometimes the words teachers use for tests can be confusing. It helps to think about tests in two totally different ways:

1.  **The Rulebook (Formal vs. Informal):** *How* is the test given? Is it super strict like a referee, or relaxed like practicing in the backyard?
2.  **The Clock (Formative vs. Summative):** *When* is the test given, and *why* are we taking it? Is it a quick practice run, or the final championship game?

Let's break these down so you can easily understand exactly how they work!

---

## The Standardization Axis: Formal vs. Informal Assessments

When we ask if a test is formal or informal, we are really asking: "How strict are the rules for this test?" 

### Formal Assessments
Think of formal assessments like an official sports tournament. **Formal assessments follow strict standardized procedures for administration.** This means the teacher must give the test exactly the same way to every single student. The instructions they read, the time limit, and the quiet room must be completely identical. Why? Because it wouldn't be fair to compare scores if one kid got extra time and another didn't! 

Because the rules are so strict, **formal assessments follow strict standardized procedures for scoring.** A teacher can't just give you bonus points because they know you tried really hard. 

Since the math of grading is so exact, **formal assessments yield quantitative data for statistical comparison.** "Quantitative data" is just a fancy way of saying "number scores" that let teachers compare your math or reading level to thousands of other kids.

There are two main types of formal tests:
*   **Norm-referenced tests are a primary category of formal assessment.** These compare your score against a "norm" group of kids your exact age. It’s like looking at a video game leaderboard to see if your high score is in the top 15% of all players.
*   **Criterion-referenced tests are categorized as formal assessments.** These measure if you hit a specific goal or learned a specific skill, no matter how anyone else did. It’s like earning a specific badge in a game once you collect 50 coins.

> **Important Example:** **The Woodcock-Johnson Tests of Achievement is a formal assessment.** This is a very famous test teachers use to figure out exactly where a student needs extra help. The teacher has to give this test following a very strict instruction manual.

### Informal Assessments
On the other hand, **informal assessments are non-standardized methods of evaluating student performance.** This means they toss out the strict laboratory rules! Instead of a quiet testing center, **informal assessments provide flexible data about student learning in natural classroom settings.** 

Because there isn't a giant, strict rulebook for grading these, **informal assessments rely heavily on teacher judgment for interpretation.** Your teacher uses their own brain and experience to decide if you are understanding the lesson, just by watching you work.

Common tools teachers use for this include:
*   **Teacher-made checklists are a form of informal assessment.** If a teacher wants to see if you remember to put periods at the end of your sentences, they might just carry around a clipboard and check off a box when they see you do it.
*   **Student portfolios are classified as informal assessments.** A portfolio is just a folder of your best drawings or writing over the whole school year. The teacher looks through it to see how much you've grown!
*   **Running records used during reading instruction are informal assessments.** This is when a teacher sits next to you, listens to you read a book out loud, and jots down notes if you accidentally mix up words (like saying "bat" instead of "cat"). 

| Feature | Formal Assessments | Informal Assessments |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **How it's given** | Very strict, exact rules | Relaxed and adaptable |
| **Type of Data** | Number scores to compare kids | Descriptions of how you work |
| **Where it happens**| Usually a quiet, structured room | Normal, everyday classroom |
| **How it's graded** | Strict grading rulebooks | The teacher's own judgment |
| **Examples** | Woodcock-Johnson, Norm-referenced tests | Portfolios, Checklists, Running records |

## The Chronological Axis: Formative vs. Summative Assessments

If "formal" and "informal" describe the rules of the test, "formative" and "summative" describe *when* the test happens.

### Formative Assessments: The Steering Wheel
**Formative assessments are ongoing evaluations conducted during the instructional process.** Think of the word *forming*. You are still in the middle of forming your brain and learning the topic! It is like tasting a soup *while* you are cooking it to see if it needs more salt.

The best thing about these tests is that **teachers use formative assessments to immediately adjust their teaching strategies.** 

Let's look at a math example. Imagine the teacher wants to see if the class knows how to add fractions with different bottom numbers (denominators). The teacher asks everyone to solve 1/2 + 1/4 on a whiteboard. 
Here are the steps to solve it:
1. Find a common bottom number (denominator) for 2 and 4, which is 4.
2. Change 1/2 into an equal fraction with 4 on the bottom by multiplying the top and bottom by 2. (This makes it 2/4).
3. Set up the new problem: 2/4 + 1/4.
4. Add the top numbers together: 2 + 1 = 3.
5. Keep the bottom number exactly the same: 4. The final answer is 3/4.

If the teacher looks around the room and sees everyone got the wrong answer, they will instantly stop and teach the steps in a new, easier way! 

Because they happen in real-time, **formative assessments provide continuous feedback to both educators and students.** You find out what you are confused about, and the teacher finds out what they need to explain better.

> **Everyday Examples:**
> *   **Exit tickets are classified as formative assessments.** This is when your teacher hands you a tiny slip of paper with one math problem to solve right before the lunch bell rings. It tells them exactly what to teach tomorrow.
> *   **Classroom questioning strategies serve as formative assessments.** When a teacher pauses their lesson to ask a question out loud to the room, they are checking to see who raises their hand and who looks totally lost.

### Summative Assessments: The Finish Line
Now for the big ones. **Summative assessments evaluate student learning at the conclusion of an instructional period.** Think of the word *summary*. It summarizes everything you learned at the very end of a unit. 

**Summative assessments measure student achievement against a specific standard or benchmark.** Did you learn all the big goals for the year? Because they happen at the very end, it's too late for the teacher to change how they teach you that specific topic. Because of this, **summative assessments are typically high-stakes evaluations used for grading or accountability.** These are the big grades that go on your final report card.

> **Everyday Examples:**
> *   **End-of-unit exams are examples of summative assessments.** This is the big history test you take when the whole chapter is finished.
> *   **Annual state standardized tests are classified as summative assessments.** These are the giant tests everyone in the state takes in the spring to prove to the government that the school is doing a good job teaching.

## Curriculum-Based Measurement (CBM): The Engine of Progress Monitoring

For a teacher, the most important job is connecting daily classroom practice with those giant end-of-year goals. How do we know if extra help is actually working before the end of the school year? We use something called Curriculum-Based Measurement, or CBM.

**Curriculum-Based Measurement is an objective method of monitoring student progress in basic academic skills.** "Objective" just means it is based on clear, fair facts, not guesses. It helps teachers track exactly how much better you are getting at reading or math.

### The Mechanics of CBM
Unlike a massive end-of-year state test that takes all morning, **Curriculum-Based Measurement utilizes brief timed probes to evaluate academic performance.** A "probe" is just a mini-test, and they usually only take one to three minutes! 

Because they are so fast, **Curriculum-Based Measurement probes are administered at frequent regular intervals.** You might take one every single Friday. 

Also, **Curriculum-Based Measurement tests material directly drawn from the student's daily curriculum.** It doesn't ask you random trick questions; it tests you on the exact spelling words or math facts you are working on in class right now.

Because it happens so often and matches your daily work, **Curriculum-Based Measurement can be used as a formative assessment to guide daily instructional decisions.** It acts like a dashboard in a car, telling the teacher exactly how fast your brain is cruising!

> **Common CBM Tools You Might Know:**
> *   **Oral reading fluency probes are a widely used type of Curriculum-Based Measurement.** This is when you read a story out loud for exactly one minute to see how many words you can read correctly. 
>     *Here is the step-by-step math a teacher uses to find your score:*
>     1. The teacher counts the total number of words you read in one minute (Let's say 90 words).
>     2. The teacher counts how many mistakes you made (Let's say 4 mistakes).
>     3. The teacher subtracts the mistakes from the total words (90 - 4 = 86).
>     4. Your final score is 86 words correct per minute!
> *   **Dynamic Indicators of Basic Early Literacy Skills (DIBELS) is a common Curriculum-Based Measurement system.** This is a very popular set of one-minute reading games that schools use to make sure younger kids are learning their letters and sounds.

### The Role of CBM in Broader Frameworks
If a kid is struggling in class, the school uses a step-by-step game plan to give them extra help. This game plan is called "Response to Intervention" (RtI). **Curriculum-Based Measurement is a standard assessment tool used within the Response to Intervention framework.** 

When you get extra tutoring in a small group, **Curriculum-Based Measurement is used to determine a student's responsiveness to specific instructional interventions.** That means the teacher uses the one-minute mini-tests to see if the tutoring is actually working. If your scores go up, the extra help is working beautifully! If your scores stay flat, the teacher knows they need to try a brand-new trick.

## The Geometry of Growth: Graphing and Analyzing CBM Data

Looking at a huge list of numbers can make anyone's head spin. That's why **teachers graph Curriculum-Based Measurement data to visually track student progress over time.** Turning numbers into a picture with dots and lines makes it super easy to see if you are leveling up.

When a teacher makes a graph for you, they draw two very important lines:

1.  **The Aimline:** **An aimline on a Curriculum-Based Measurement graph represents the expected rate of student learning.** This is the "Goal Line." It is a straight line drawn from where you started all the way to where we want you to be by the end of the year. 
2.  **The Trendline:** **A trendline on a Curriculum-Based Measurement graph represents the student's actual rate of progress.** This is the line that connects your *actual* weekly scores. It shows the real path you are traveling on.

### The Golden Rule of Intervention Adjustment
Here is the most important rule teachers follow when looking at your graph. They constantly compare your real path (the trendline) to the goal path (the aimline). 

There is a strict rule to make sure no one gets left behind: **A teacher must adjust instruction if a student's Curriculum-Based Measurement trendline falls below the aimline for three to four consecutive data points.** 

Why three or four weeks in a row? Because everyone has a bad day! Maybe you scored low one week because you had a tummy ache, or you didn't sleep well. That is totally normal. But if your actual score stays below the goal line for *three or four tests in a row*, it is no longer just a bad day. It means the teaching strategy isn't working! When that happens, the teacher *must* change things up—like putting you in a smaller group or explaining the math in a totally new way.

By understanding all these tools—formal tests, informal checks, formative practice, summative finish lines, and CBM graphs—teachers don't have to guess if you are learning. They know exactly where you are, and exactly how to help you win the game!
