Look at you, clutching that exam prep book like it’s a life preserver. Drink your water, rookie, and listen to me, because teaching mild to moderate special education without understanding the taxonomy of assessments is exactly like trying to run a Michelin-star restaurant kitchen blindfolded during a Friday night dinner rush. You’re going to burn the building down, you’re going to poison somebody, and the health inspector—your district admin—is going to revoke your license. You need instruments. You need thermometers. You need to know exactly when to taste the soup and when to plate the dish. 

Let’s start with the timing, the chronological axis of the kitchen: when and why are you evaluating the food? 

First up, formative assessments are ongoing evaluations conducted during the instructional process. You are literally standing over the boiling pot, tasting the sauce as it reduces. Why do we do this? Because teachers use formative assessments to immediately adjust their teaching strategies! If you taste that sauce and it’s bland, you don’t just shrug and wait for the customer to send it back. You grab the salt! You pivot! Formative assessments provide continuous feedback to both educators and students. You learn you need more garlic, and your student learns that, oops, they forgot to carry the one in two-digit addition. 

What does this look like in the real world of your classroom? Exit tickets are classified as formative assessments. It’s like having a patron write down exactly what they thought of the appetizer on a tiny napkin before they walk out the door. You look at that napkin—two quick math problems solved on a sticky note—and you know exactly what you need to cook up for tomorrow’s lesson. Furthermore, classroom questioning strategies serve as formative assessments. When you’re mid-lecture, stop and yell, "Who can tell me the main character's motive?!" You look around the room. Who is making eye contact? Who is suddenly intensely interested in the texture of their eraser? That’s real-time, formative data!

But eventually, the dinner service ends. The plate leaves the kitchen. You can't un-burn the steak now. That brings us to the final judgment: summative assessments evaluate student learning at the conclusion of an instructional period. The root word is "sum." It’s the summary, the finale. Summative assessments measure student achievement against a specific standard or benchmark. Did you successfully create the exact dish the corporate menu demanded? Yes or no. Because it’s too late to fix the food, summative assessments are typically high-stakes evaluations used for grading or accountability. 

End-of-unit exams are examples of summative assessments. You finish the chapter on fractions, you drop the test on their desk, and you find out who survived. And those terrifying, anxiety-inducing annual state standardized tests are classified as summative assessments. That is the ruthless food critic showing up once a year to write a review that determines your school's funding. High stakes, final judgments.

Now, wipe your face. We need to talk about the *nature* of the tools you are using. The standardization axis. Are you acting like a mad scientist in a sterile lab, or an organic artist in the wild?

Let's talk lab conditions. Formal assessments follow strict standardized procedures for administration. Imagine a heist movie where you have to walk exactly three paces, wait two seconds, and swipe the keycard to avoid the laser grid. If you deviate, the alarms blare. You must read the manual verbatim. You set the timer perfectly. Why? Because formal assessments follow strict standardized procedures for scoring. You cannot give Jimmy partial credit just because he tried really hard and gave you a sweet, gap-toothed smile. Strict administration and strict scoring mean formal assessments yield quantitative data for statistical comparison. You get cold, hard numbers that let you rank and compare your student against the universe. 

There are two major heavyweights in this lab. First, norm-referenced tests are a primary category of formal assessment. This compares your student’s test score against a "norm" group of peers. It tells you if your kid is in the 15th percentile of all culinary students nationwide. Second, criterion-referenced tests are categorized as formal assessments. These don't care about other students; they measure a student's performance against a rigid, predetermined criteria. Did they jump exactly four feet high? 

If you are walking into an initial IEP meeting to secure services for a kid, you are bringing the heavy artillery. For instance, the Woodcock-Johnson Tests of Achievement is a formal assessment. It is the massive, standardized diagnostic bible. You do not improvise with the Woodcock-Johnson. You administer it like a robot. 

But we aren't always robots, right? Sometimes you just need to watch a kid work. Informal assessments are non-standardized methods of evaluating student performance. You strip away the lab coat, turn off the lasers, and observe the kid in the wild. Informal assessments provide flexible data about student learning in natural classroom settings. Because there is no corporate scoring manual, informal assessments rely heavily on teacher judgment for interpretation. You are the expert chef. You are observing the way they hold their pencil, the way they sound out vowels, the sweat on their forehead. 

What's in this flexible toolkit? Teacher-made checklists are a form of informal assessment. Are you tracking whether Sarah remembers to put a period at the end of her sentences? Just make a chart with Monday through Friday on it and check the boxes. Beautiful, natural data. Student portfolios are classified as informal assessments. Instead of a sterile test, you collect all of their rough drafts and final essays over a semester. It’s a qualitative scrapbook of their expanding brain! And when you sit right next to a kid, listening to them read a paragraph out loud, noting every time they substitute a "b" for a "d"? Running records used during reading instruction are informal assessments. Highly flexible, deeply informative, purely driven by your clinical judgment.

Okay, take a breath. Lean in. I am about to give you the golden ticket, the absolute bridge between the daily grind and the high-stakes final exam. What happens when you need the precision of a formal assessment but the daily flexibility of an informal one? 

Curriculum-Based Measurement. CBM. 

Curriculum-Based Measurement is an objective method of monitoring student progress in basic academic skills. It is the ultimate digital meat thermometer of the special education kitchen. You aren't locking a kid in a room for four hours. Instead, Curriculum-Based Measurement utilizes brief timed probes to evaluate academic performance. I’m talking one minute! Three minutes! In, out, nobody cries. Because they are so incredibly fast, Curriculum-Based Measurement probes are administered at frequent regular intervals. You probe them every Tuesday. Zap. Check the temp. 

The genius of it? The absolute beauty? Curriculum-Based Measurement tests material directly drawn from the student's daily curriculum. You aren't testing them on abstract, generalized knowledge; you are testing them on the exact sight words or multiplication tables you taught on Monday. Because it is so hyper-relevant to what you are doing right now, Curriculum-Based Measurement can be used as a formative assessment to guide daily instructional decisions. You check the one-minute probe, realize the kid still doesn't understand subtraction with regrouping, and you change tomorrow's lesson plan. 

And you don't just leave these numbers in a folder! Teachers graph Curriculum-Based Measurement data to visually track student progress over time. You need to see the mountain they are climbing. When you set up this graph, you draw the dream scenario. An aimline on a Curriculum-Based Measurement graph represents the expected rate of student learning. It is the glorious, straight trajectory from where they started to passing their IEP goal. 

But kids aren't straight lines. Kids are chaotic little pinballs. So every week, you plot their score, creating the reality line. A trendline on a Curriculum-Based Measurement graph represents the student's actual rate of progress. 

Now, hear me, because this is the rule that separates the professionals from the amateurs. You are comparing the aimline to the trendline. A teacher must adjust instruction if a student's Curriculum-Based Measurement trendline falls below the aimline for three to four consecutive data points! One bad week? The kid had a cold. Two bad weeks? There was a full moon and they lost their favorite eraser. But three to four consecutive drops?! The intervention is failing! The kitchen is on fire! You don't just stand there watching it burn; you adjust the instruction, lower the group size, change the curriculum, do something!

Because CBM is this incredibly sensitive early-warning radar, it’s the backbone of systemic school interventions. Curriculum-Based Measurement is a standard assessment tool used within the Response to Intervention framework. When a kid starts drowning in general ed, and you pull them into a Tier 2 reading group, Curriculum-Based Measurement is used to determine a student's responsiveness to specific instructional interventions. You give them the intervention, you run the CBM probes, and if the line goes up, you're a hero. If the line stays flat, you swap interventions before it's too late. 

You'll see these specific thermometers everywhere. Oral reading fluency probes are a widely used type of Curriculum-Based Measurement. Hand a kid a passage, start a stopwatch, count the words they read correctly in exactly one minute. Bam. Fluency data. And if your school wants a whole pre-packaged system for this, Dynamic Indicators of Basic Early Literacy Skills is a common Curriculum-Based Measurement system. That’s DIBELS! It's an entire battery of one-minute probes to track if a kindergartener is figuring out their phonics or if they are totally lost in the sauce. 

So there it is. Formative to taste the soup, summative to face the judge. Formal for the strict lab data, informal for the flexible artistry. And CBM to track the pulse of it all, week by sweaty week. Memorize it, pass that exam, and don't burn down your classroom! Now, are you going to finish those fries or what?
