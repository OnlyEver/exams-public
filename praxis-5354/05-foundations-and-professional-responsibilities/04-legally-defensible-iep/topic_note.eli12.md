Think about starting a massive, open-world video game. You don't just run straight at the final boss hoping you win. You check your character's starting stats, plan which skills to level up, and follow a map. In school, for students who need extra help, the Individualized Education Program (or IEP) is that map. **The Individuals with Disabilities Education Act mandates specific components that must be included in every Individualized Education Program.** This law ensures the IEP is a solid plan that helps a student level up from where they are right now to where they need to be. Understanding how to build this plan is super important. 

## The Dual Nature of Compliance

When teachers and parents check an IEP to see if it is legal and right, they look at it in two different ways. It has to pass two tests, kind of like baking a pizza. It must have **procedural** compliance and **substantive** compliance. 

Think of procedural compliance as following the recipe's steps, and substantive compliance as the pizza actually tasting good and filling you up. If you follow the recipe perfectly but use cardboard instead of cheese, the pizza still fails!

| Type of Compliance | Definition | Practical Application |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Procedural Compliance** | **Procedural compliance in an Individualized Education Program requires the school to successfully execute all legal steps mandated by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act.** It also **requires the school to adhere to all legal timelines mandated by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act.** | Having the meeting before the deadline, inviting all the right people, and filling out the paperwork correctly. |
| **Substantive Compliance** | **Substantive compliance in an Individualized Education Program means the content of the program provides the child with a free appropriate public education** (often called FAPE). | Writing goals and picking helps that actually let the student learn and grow in real life. |

For a long time, people argued about how much a student actually needed to grow. Did schools just have to do the bare minimum? In 2017, the rules of the game changed. 

> **The Endrew F. Standard:**
> **The Supreme Court ruling in Endrew F. v. Douglas County School District established that an Individualized Education Program must be appropriately ambitious in light of a child's unique circumstances.** 

This means schools can't just aim for a passing grade; they have to push the student to do the absolute best they can!

## The Foundation: The PLAAFP Statement

You can't use a GPS to get to the movie theater if the GPS doesn't know where your car is right now. That is why **an Individualized Education Program must include a statement of the student's Present Levels of Academic Achievement and Functional Performance.** We use a long abbreviation for this: **The acronym PLAAFP stands for Present Levels of Academic Achievement and Functional Performance.** 

A good PLAAFP isn't just a teacher saying, "Johnny gets distracted." It uses hard numbers. **A legally defensible PLAAFP statement must include current baseline data regarding the student's academic performance** (like "Johnny reads 45 words per minute"). It also means **a legally defensible PLAAFP statement must include current baseline data regarding the student's functional performance** (like "Johnny stays in his seat for 4 minutes before needing a reminder"). 

Why are these starting numbers so important? Because **baseline data in a PLAAFP statement serves as the necessary starting point for measuring a student's progress toward annual goals.** If you don't know your starting score, you can't tell if you are winning!

A complete, legal PLAAFP statement does five big things:
1. **Describe academic strengths:** **A legally sound PLAAFP statement describes the student's specific academic strengths.** (What subjects are they naturally good at?)
2. **Describe functional strengths:** **A legally sound PLAAFP statement describes the student's specific functional strengths.** (Are they great at organizing their desk or making friends?)
3. **Identify needs:** **A legally sound PLAAFP statement identifies the student's specific educational needs resulting from the student's disability.**
4. **Explain curriculum involvement:** **A legally sound PLAAFP statement explicitly describes how a student's disability affects the student's involvement in the general education curriculum.** (How does the disability make it hard to join in on the regular class activities?)
5. **Explain curriculum progress:** **A legally sound PLAAFP statement explicitly describes how a student's disability affects the student's progress in the general education curriculum.** (How does it slow down their learning in normal subjects?)

## The Blueprint: Measurable Annual Goals

Once you know your starting stats, it's time to set targets. **An Individualized Education Program must include a statement of measurable annual academic goals for the student**, and it also **must include a statement of measurable annual functional goals for the student.** 

Here is the most important rule for these goals:
> **Measurable annual goals in an Individualized Education Program must be directly linked to the needs identified in the PLAAFP statement.** 

If the PLAAFP doesn't say the student has trouble holding a pencil, you can't suddenly make a goal about handwriting! Everything has to match.

Also, you have to prove the student is getting better. **An Individualized Education Program must include a description of how the student's progress toward annual goals will be measured** (like taking a math quiz every Friday). The school has to share this info, too. **An Individualized Education Program must specify when periodic reports on the student's progress toward annual goals will be provided to the student's parents** (usually around report card time).

## The Scaffolding: Services, Aids, and LRE

Goals tell us *what* the student will do. The next part tells us *how* the school will help. The team has to list three types of help:

1. **Special Education Services:** **An Individualized Education Program must include a statement of the specific special education services to be provided to the student.** This is the actual customized teaching they get.
2. **Related Services:** **An Individualized Education Program must include a statement of the related services to be provided to the student.** These are extra helps, like speech therapy or special bus rides, that make it possible for the student to learn.
3. **Supplementary Aids:** **An Individualized Education Program must include a statement of the supplementary aids to be provided to the student.** This means extra tools, like a special calculator or a magnifying glass.

### The Law of the Least Restrictive Environment (LRE)
Why do we give students these extra tools? Because kids should get to learn with their friends whenever possible. **Supplementary aids and services are provided in regular education settings to enable children with disabilities to be educated with nondisabled children to the maximum extent appropriate.** 

Since the normal classroom is the default choice, the school has to explain if a student needs to leave it. **An Individualized Education Program must include a statement explaining the extent to which the child will not participate with nondisabled children in the regular class.**

### The Mathematics of Delivery: DFLD
You can't just say a student will get help "sometimes." That would break the procedural rules! For the teaching and the extra helps, the IEP has to list four exact things:
* **An Individualized Education Program must state the projected calendar date for the beginning of the approved special education services.** (And the start date for related services, too).
* **An Individualized Education Program must state the anticipated frequency of the provided special education services**, and **must state the anticipated frequency of the provided related services.** (How many days a week?)
* **An Individualized Education Program must state the anticipated physical location of the provided special education services**, and **must state the anticipated physical location of the provided related services.** (In the normal classroom or a special room?)
* **An Individualized Education Program must state the anticipated duration of the provided special education services**, and **must state the anticipated duration of the provided related services.** (How many minutes?)

Let's look at how we calculate the exact time a student spends getting related services each week. Just like figuring out how many weeks it takes to save \$60 if you get a \$15 allowance, we use step-by-step math:

1. Find the anticipated duration of one session (for example, 30 minutes of speech therapy).
2. Find the anticipated frequency of sessions per week (for example, 3 times per week).
3. Multiply the duration by the frequency to find the total minutes (30 x 3 = 90).
4. State your final answer: The student will receive exactly 90 minutes of speech therapy per week.

## Measuring the System: Assessments and Accommodations

Schools give big state tests to see how everyone is doing. But forcing a student with severe ADHD to sit totally still for three hours isn't fair. 

Because of this, **an Individualized Education Program must include a statement of any individual accommodations necessary to measure the academic achievement of the child on state assessments.** It also **must include a statement of any individual accommodations necessary to measure the functional performance of the child on districtwide assessments.** Accommodations are just helpful tweaks—like getting extra time or sitting in a quiet room—so the test is fair.

But what about students with very severe disabilities who can't take the normal test, even with tweaks? 
* **An Individualized Education Program team may determine a child must take an alternate assessment instead of a particular regular state assessment.** (An alternate assessment is a different, special kind of test).
* If they choose this, they have to explain why. **If a child requires an alternate assessment, the Individualized Education Program must include a statement explaining why the child cannot participate in the regular assessment.**
* They also have to explain their new choice. **If a child requires an alternate assessment, the Individualized Education Program must include a statement explaining why the particular alternate assessment selected is appropriate for the child.**

## The Horizon: Transition Services

An IEP isn't meant to keep a kid in school forever. It's meant to get them ready for the real world! 

**The Individuals with Disabilities Education Act requires transition services to be included in an Individualized Education Program beginning no later than the first IEP to be in effect when the child turns 16.** (Transition services are plans for life after high school).

Just like we used baseline data to make school goals, we need data to make life goals. **Measurable postsecondary goals in an Individualized Education Program must be based upon age-appropriate transition assessments.** These are quizzes that find out what jobs the student likes or what life skills they need to practice.

Using those quiz results, **transition services in an Individualized Education Program must include appropriate measurable postsecondary goals.** These goals explain exactly what the student wants to do after they graduate—like going to college, getting a job, or living in their own apartment. 

When you read an IEP, it should make total sense from start to finish. Everything connects, from their starting stats to their final goals for adulthood!
