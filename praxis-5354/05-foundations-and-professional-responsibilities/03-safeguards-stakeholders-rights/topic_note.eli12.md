Imagine you are playing a multiplayer video game, but one player gets to make all the rules in secret. That wouldn't be very fair, right? In schools, sometimes there is a big imbalance of power between the school and a family. To make sure things are completely fair, a law called **The Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA)** creates special rules. The Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) mandates procedural safeguards to protect the rights of students with disabilities and the parents of these students. 

Think of these safeguards like the official rulebook in a board game. Procedural safeguards ensure that parents are equal partners in the special education decision-making process for a child. A school cannot just make major choices all by itself. 

As a teacher, you are the person who helps protect these rights every day. Understanding this rulebook helps you work honestly and fairly with the family and the student.

## The Foundation of Transparency: Notice and Records

If you are playing a game, you need to be able to see the scoreboard. If the school kept all the information hidden, families couldn't help make good choices. 

First, parents have the right to review all of the child's educational records under the **Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act (FERPA)** and IDEA. This means if a school uses a test score, a behavior log, or an email to make a decision about a student, the parents are allowed to see it. 

Second, parents need to know their rights. Schools must provide parents with a written copy of the procedural safeguards notice at least once every school year. But just giving it once a year isn't enough, especially during stressful moments. Because of this, schools must provide the procedural safeguards notice upon:
1. The initial referral for a special education evaluation.
2. The first filing of a due process complaint in a school year.
3. The first filing of a state complaint in a school year.

By handing parents the rulebook at these exact moments, the school makes sure the family knows how to navigate the process when things get serious.

## The Circuit Breaker: Prior Written Notice (PWN)

Imagine you are a passenger in a car, and the driver suddenly yanks the steering wheel to turn without using a blinker. You would be shocked! In special education, the school can't just suddenly turn the wheel. They must use their turn signal. That turn signal is called **Prior Written Notice (PWN)**.

> **Prior Written Notice (PWN)** is a formal communication from the school detailing proposed or refused actions regarding a student's special education program.

Schools can't make secret changes. Schools must issue a Prior Written Notice before proposing to initiate or change the identification of a child. Similarly, schools must issue a Prior Written Notice before proposing to initiate or change the evaluation or educational placement of a child. 

It is also important for the school to explain when they say "no." Schools must issue a Prior Written Notice before refusing to initiate or change the identification of a child. Also, schools must issue a Prior Written Notice before refusing to initiate or change the evaluation or educational placement of a child. 

To make sure parents actually understand this notice, a Prior Written Notice must be written in a language understandable to the general public. That means no confusing teacher jargon! Also, a Prior Written Notice must be provided in the native language of the parent or other mode of communication used by the parent (like Braille or sign language). 

## The Power of the Signature: Informed Consent

While PWN is the school turning on its blinker to say what it *wants* to do, **informed consent** is the parent actually giving the green light. 

Informed consent requires that parents are fully informed of all information relevant to the specific special education activity for which consent is sought. You can't just slide a piece of paper across a desk and say, "Sign this." You have to explain exactly what is going to happen, how it will happen, and why it matters.

### When is Consent Required?
Consent is an absolute must before starting major steps. Schools must obtain informed written consent from a parent before:
* Conducting an initial evaluation of a child.
* The initial provision of special education and related services.
* Conducting a reevaluation of a child.

### The Nuances of Consent
Understanding the rules of consent helps keep everyone on the same page:

* **Separate Steps:** Parental consent for an initial evaluation does not constitute consent for the initial provision of special education services. Think of it like taking a car to the mechanic: agreeing to let them check the engine does not mean you automatically agree to pay for the repairs. They are two separate steps!
* **Your Choice:** A parent's consent for special education services is voluntary. They never have to say yes.
* **Changing Your Mind:** A parent may revoke consent for special education services in writing at any time. If they want to stop the services, they hold the power to do so.
* **No Time Travel:** If a parent stops the services, what happens to the past? Revoking parental consent for special education services is not retroactive. This means revoking parental consent does not negate actions that occurred while the consent was active. It's like eating a slice of pizza—you can decide to stop eating it, but you can't magically un-eat the bites you already took.

## The Right to Disagree: Independent Educational Evaluations (IEE)

What happens if the school tests a student, and the parents feel the test results are completely wrong? Parents have the right to obtain an **Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE)** if the parents disagree with the evaluation obtained by the school district. Think of this like asking a second referee for their opinion on a bad call in sports.

Here is an interesting rule about who pays. If a parent requests an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at public expense (meaning the school pays for it), the school district must either provide the IEE or file a due process complaint. 

The school cannot just say "no" and ignore the parents. A school district filing a due process complaint after an Independent Educational Evaluation request must prove to a hearing officer that the district's evaluation is appropriate. 

## The Spectrum of Dispute Resolution

When the school and the family can't agree, IDEA gives them ways to figure it out fairly.

### 1. State Complaints
State complaint procedures allow any individual or organization to file a written complaint alleging that a public educational agency has violated a requirement of IDEA. You don't even have to be a parent to do this! 

When a state complaint is filed, there is a strict math timeline to finish it:
1. Identify the exact date the state receives the complaint (let's say March 1st).
2. Add exactly 60 days to that date.
3. The answer is the final deadline. 

Because of this rule, state educational agencies must resolve a state complaint within 60 days of the complaint being filed.

### 2. Mediation
Before going to a big legal hearing, the two sides can try **mediation**. Mediation is a voluntary dispute resolution process where an impartial third party helps parents and the school district reach an agreement. Because the school wants to make this easy for families, the school district must bear the entire cost of the mediation process in special education disputes. The family pays \$0.

### 3. Due Process
When mediation doesn't work, things get formal. A **due process complaint** is a formal written request to resolve a dispute involving a student's special education identification, evaluation, or placement. 

Because these formal arguments take a lot of time, IDEA forces the school and parents to try talking one last time. A **resolution meeting** must occur within 15 days of a school district receiving notice of a parent's due process complaint. 

Here is how you calculate that deadline:
1. Find the date the school gets the complaint notice (for example, October 5th).
2. Add the 15 allowed days (5 + 15 = 20).
3. The resolution meeting must happen by October 20th.

The purpose of a resolution meeting is to give the school district an opportunity to resolve the due process complaint before a formal hearing begins.

If they still can't agree, they go to a **due process hearing**. A due process hearing is a formal legal proceeding overseen by an impartial hearing officer. 

At this hearing, parents have strong legal rights. During a due process hearing:
1. Parents have the right to be accompanied and advised by counsel (like a lawyer).
2. Parents have the right to present evidence.
3. Parents have the right to compel the attendance of witnesses (which means forcing people to show up and answer questions).
4. Parents have the right to cross-examine witnesses.

### The Anchor in the Storm: The Stay-Put Provision
If the parents and the school are arguing in a hearing, where does the kid go to school in the meantime? The law fixes this with the **stay-put provision**. The stay-put provision ensures that a child remains in the child's current educational placement during the pendency of any due process proceedings. It's basically a "pause" button so the student's life isn't disrupted while the adults argue. 

There is only one way to change this. The stay-put provision can be overridden if the parent and the school district mutually agree to a different educational placement during proceedings.

## The Arc of Advocacy: Surrogates and Transitions

Who gets to use these rights if a parent isn't around, or if the student grows up?

### Surrogate Parents
Sometimes a child does not have a parent who can be found. In these situations, **surrogate parents** are appointed to represent the educational interests of a child with a disability when no parent can be identified or located. A surrogate is like a stand-in.

To make sure this stand-in parent is totally fair, the rules are strict. A surrogate parent cannot be an employee of the state educational agency, the local educational agency, or any other agency involved in the education or care of the child. It has to be someone entirely independent.

### The Age of Majority
These rights do not belong to the parents forever. Under IDEA, educational rights transfer from the parents to the student when the student reaches the age of majority in the student's state (usually when they turn 18). 

Because becoming your own boss is a huge deal, schools must notify both the student and the parents of the impending transfer of educational rights at least one year before the student reaches the age of majority. 

## Summary for the Practitioner

As you study for your exams and get ready to teach, remember that these rules aren't meant to be scary. They are tools to build trust.

| Rule | What it Does | Your Daily Job |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **PWN** | School communicates intent to propose or refuse an action. | You use your "turn signal" before changing an IEP or saying "no." |
| **Consent** | Parent grants permission for eval or initial services. | You make sure parents fully understand *what* they are signing. |
| **Stay-Put** | Freezes the student's placement during a formal dispute. | The student stays safely in their normal routine while adults disagree. |

When you issue a Prior Written Notice in words everyone can understand, and when you make sure parents know their rights, you help prove that school is a fair place for everyone to learn.
