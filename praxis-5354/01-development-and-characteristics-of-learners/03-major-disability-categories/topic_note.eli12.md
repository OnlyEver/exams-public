Imagine playing a massive multiplayer online game, but every player has completely different controller settings. Some players can’t hear the game’s music, some have buttons that get stuck, and others are getting super laggy screen glitches. You can't just yell at them to play better; you have to figure out their exact settings so you can help them play the game! In schools, the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) outlines 13 major disability categories. These categories act like a guide to help teachers figure out exactly what kind of "settings" a student has, so we can make learning work perfectly for them. 

Let's look at how kids' brains, bodies, and feelings might work differently in the classroom.

## The Cognitive and Developmental Profiles

### Autism Spectrum Profiles
Autism is a developmental disability significantly affecting verbal communication (like speaking). It is also a developmental disability significantly affecting nonverbal communication (like making eye contact or using hand gestures). And finally, autism is a developmental disability significantly affecting social interaction (like knowing how to join a group of kids playing). We usually notice these signs when kids are very little; characteristics of autism are generally evident before a child reaches age three.

To understand a kid with autism, you need to know about something called "theory of mind." Think about playing hide-and-seek. You know your friend is hiding in the closet because you saw them run in there, and you *know* that they *believe* they are hidden. Individuals with autism often experience difficulties with theory of mind. Theory of mind is the cognitive ability to understand that other people have different beliefs, desires, and intentions. If a student struggles with this, they might not realize that you are feeling sad just by looking at your face. 

Sometimes, a classroom can feel way too loud or chaotic for a student with autism. Because of this, you might see common behavioral markers of autism as the student tries to feel comfortable:
*   Engagement in repetitive activities (like lining up their pencils over and over).
*   Stereotyped movements (like flapping their hands, which feels calming).
*   Resistance to environmental change (like getting very upset if their daily schedule suddenly changes).
*   Unusual responses to sensory experiences (like feeling actual pain from the quiet buzzing of a school lightbulb).

When it comes to talking, you might hear echolalia, which is the repetition of vocalizations made by another person. Echolalia is a common communication characteristic of autism. If you say, "Do you want juice?" the student might just say "Want juice?" back to you to help their brain process the words.

### Specific Learning Disability (SLD)
Think of a brain like a smartphone. Sometimes the phone is super fast, but just one single app keeps crashing. A specific learning disability involves a disorder in a psychological process used to understand spoken language. Or, it involves a disorder in a psychological process used to understand written language. Because of this "crashing app," a specific learning disability may manifest in an imperfect ability to read. It may manifest in an imperfect ability to write. Or, it may manifest in an imperfect ability to do mathematical calculations.

IDEA lists a few main types of learning disabilities:
*   **Dyslexia:** Dyslexia is classified as a specific learning disability under IDEA. It's not just "seeing letters backwards." Dyslexia primarily involves difficulties with accurate word recognition, and it primarily involves difficulties with fluent word recognition. Dyslexia typically results from a deficit in the phonological component of language. That means the brain has a really hard time matching the sound of a letter to the actual letter on the page.
*   **Dyscalculia:** Dyscalculia is classified as a specific learning disability under IDEA. Dyscalculia involves difficulties in processing numerical information, and it involves difficulties in learning arithmetic facts. For example, let's look at how someone might calculate the math to buy pizza using a \$10 allowance. Here is the step-by-step math:
    1. First, start with your total money: \$10.
    2. Second, subtract the \$3 cost of a soda: 10 - 3 = 7.
    3. Third, divide that \$7 by the \$1 cost of a pizza slice: 7 / 1 = 7 slices.
    A kid with dyscalculia might completely understand what buying pizza means, but those actual numbers and steps just get jumbled up in their mind!
*   **Dysgraphia:** Dysgraphia is classified as a specific learning disability under IDEA. Dysgraphia is a learning disability that affects handwriting and fine motor skills. It takes so much brain power for them to physically hold the pencil and draw the letters that it's hard to think about the story they are trying to write.

Here is a big rule: a specific learning disability excludes learning problems primarily resulting from visual disabilities. It excludes learning problems primarily resulting from hearing disabilities. It excludes learning problems primarily resulting from motor disabilities. It excludes learning problems primarily resulting from intellectual disability. Finally, it excludes learning problems primarily resulting from environmental disadvantage. (For example, if you can't read simply because you need glasses or missed a lot of school, you do *not* have a specific learning disability!)

### Intellectual Disability (ID)
If a specific learning disability is just one broken app, an intellectual disability is like the whole phone processing things a bit slower. Intellectual disability involves significantly subaverage general intellectual functioning. When they take an IQ test, a student with an intellectual disability typically has an IQ score below 70 or 75.

But a test score isn't the whole story. An intellectual disability diagnosis requires concurrent deficits in adaptive behavior. Also, the deficits in adaptive behavior associated with intellectual disability manifest during the developmental period (meaning they show up while the kid is still growing up, not when they are an old adult). 

What is adaptive behavior? It's all the normal stuff you do every day to take care of yourself:
1.  Adaptive behavior includes conceptual skills such as language and literacy.
2.  Adaptive behavior includes social skills such as interpersonal communication.
3.  Adaptive behavior includes practical skills such as daily living activities (like making a sandwich, crossing the street, or tying shoes).

## The Behavioral and Emotional Profiles

### Emotional Disturbance (ED)
Sometimes a kid's feelings are so overwhelmingly huge that they block out the ability to learn anything. Emotional disturbance involves an inability to learn that is unexplainable by intellectual factors. It involves an inability to learn that is unexplainable by sensory factors. And it involves an inability to learn that is unexplainable by health factors. The kid's brain works fine, and they can hear and see perfectly, but deep emotional pain gets in the way.

To get this kind of help, a student will show some of these signs:
*   Emotional disturbance involves an inability to build satisfactory interpersonal relationships with peers (meaning they can't make friends).
*   It involves an inability to maintain satisfactory interpersonal relationships with teachers.
*   Emotional disturbance includes inappropriate types of behavior under normal circumstances.
*   It includes inappropriate types of feelings under normal circumstances.
*   It includes a general pervasive mood of unhappiness or depression.
*   Emotional disturbance includes a tendency to develop physical symptoms associated with personal problems (like getting a real, terrible stomach ache just because you are nervous about going to class).

Also, a serious condition called schizophrenia is classified under the emotional disturbance category in IDEA. 

Here is what it does *not* include: The emotional disturbance category does not apply to children who are solely socially maladjusted. That means if a kid completely understands the school rules, feels perfectly fine, but simply chooses to break the rules to act tough or look cool, they do not fall under this category.

## The Physical and Health Profiles

### Other Health Impairment (OHI)
This category is for kids dealing with medical issues that drain their energy or focus. Other health impairment involves having limited strength due to chronic or acute health problems. It involves having limited vitality due to chronic or acute health problems. And it involves having limited alertness due to chronic or acute health problems.

Here is a wild fact about how the brain works: other health impairment includes a heightened alertness to environmental stimuli that results in limited alertness in the classroom. Think of it this way: if your brain is paying 100% attention to a tiny fly buzzing near the window, you have 0% attention left to listen to your math teacher!

Because of this, Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD) is categorized under other health impairment in IDEA. Kids with ADHD show a few specific behaviors:
*   Hyperactivity in ADHD manifests as excessive motor activity when physical movement is not appropriate (like running around the room when everyone is supposed to be sitting).
*   Impulsivity in ADHD refers to hasty actions occurring in the moment without forethought (like jumping off the top of a slide before checking if someone is down there).
*   Inattention in ADHD involves wandering off task.

Other sicknesses fall here, too. Asthma is categorized under other health impairment in IDEA. Epilepsy is categorized under other health impairment in IDEA. Tourette syndrome is categorized under other health impairment in IDEA. 

### Orthopedic Impairments
This category is all about the physical body, like the bones and muscles. Orthopedic impairments under IDEA include physical impairments caused by congenital anomalies (which means something you are born with, like a differently shaped foot). 

It can also come from illnesses. Bone tuberculosis is an example of an orthopedic impairment under IDEA. Also, cerebral palsy is classified as an orthopedic impairment under IDEA. Sometimes it comes from an accident, which is why amputations are classified as orthopedic impairments under IDEA.

### Traumatic Brain Injury (TBI)
If you get hit really hard in the head while playing sports or in a car crash, it can hurt your brain. Traumatic brain injury refers to an acquired injury to the brain caused by an external physical force. 

This covers two types of hits. Traumatic brain injury applies to open head injuries resulting in impairments (where the skull breaks). Traumatic brain injury applies to closed head injuries resulting in impairments (like a really bad concussion where the skull doesn't break, but the brain gets bruised inside).

Because this is all about getting hurt from the *outside*, traumatic brain injury does not apply to brain injuries that are congenital (you can't be born with a TBI). Traumatic brain injury does not apply to brain injuries that are degenerative (which means sicknesses that slowly get worse over time). And finally, traumatic brain injury does not apply to brain injuries induced by birth trauma.

## The Sensory and Communication Profiles

### Speech or Language Impairment
Talking is how we share ideas! A speech or language impairment means a communication disorder that adversely affects a child's educational performance. 
*   Stuttering is classified as a speech or language impairment under IDEA.
*   Impaired articulation is classified as a speech or language impairment under IDEA (like a kid who just cannot make the "r" sound properly).
*   A voice impairment is classified as a speech or language impairment under IDEA (like someone whose voice is always extremely raspy or way too quiet to hear).

### Deafness vs. Hearing Impairment
There are two different categories for kids who have trouble hearing. 
*   Deafness is a hearing impairment so severe that the child cannot process linguistic information through hearing. Even with super loud hearing aids, their ears just cannot send the spoken words to their brain.
*   Hearing impairment refers to an impairment in hearing that adversely affects educational performance but is not included under deafness. Kids with a hearing impairment usually have some hearing left, and hearing aids can help them understand words.

### Visual Impairment including Blindness
Just like hearing, visual impairment including blindness means an impairment in vision that adversely affects a child's educational performance. 
*   The IDEA category of visual impairment includes partial sight (like needing a magnifying glass or super giant text to read).
*   The IDEA category of visual impairment includes blindness (where a kid cannot see at all, and might read using their fingers on bumpy dots called Braille).

## The Complex and Compounding Profiles

### Deaf-Blindness
Imagine trying to play a video game with the screen turned off AND the sound turned off. It's incredibly hard! Deaf-blindness involves the simultaneous presence of a hearing impairment and a visual impairment. 

Because they are missing two big ways to learn about the world, students with deaf-blindness cannot be accommodated in special education programs designed solely for deafness. They also cannot be accommodated in special education programs designed solely for blindness. They need a totally unique, specialized plan that handles both challenges at the exact same time!

### Multiple Disabilities
Multiple disabilities involve simultaneous impairments that cause severe educational needs. This means a kid might have an intellectual disability AND an orthopedic impairment at the same time. The severe educational needs associated with multiple disabilities cannot be accommodated in programs designed solely for one of the impairments. 

Wait, doesn't deaf-blindness count as a multiple disability? Actually, no! Because kids with deaf-blindness need such incredibly specific help, the IDEA category of multiple disabilities explicitly excludes deaf-blindness. Deaf-blindness gets its very own special category.
