This is your chronological security tape. First, you have the trigger. An antecedent is the event or stimulus that occurs immediately before a behavior. It’s the laser tripwire getting activated—maybe you handed out a math worksheet, or the kid next to them sneezed. Next comes the crime itself. Now, a behavior in the ABC model must be an observable and measurable action. You cannot write "Timmy felt frustrated" on a legal document! You can’t arrest someone for a vibe! Did he throw a chair? If Danny Ocean robs your casino and gets away with a million bucks, he’s coming back. If he gets sprayed by a skunk on the way out, he’s retiring. 

So, what’s the motive? Why pull the job? We break it down into four primary functions of behavior: sensory stimulation, escape, access to attention, and access to tangibles. Just remember you are sitting in a SEAT. Let’s look at the suspects. First, you have your escape artists. Escape-maintained behavior occurs to avoid a demand or undesirable situation. When a kid’s heist becomes dangerous or chronic, you don’t just guess their motive. You call in the CSI unit. The Individuals with Disabilities Education Act requires a Functional Behavioral Assessment when a student's behavior results in a disciplinary change of placement, like getting suspended for too many days. Therefore, a Behavior Intervention Plan must include strategies to teach and reinforce replacement behaviors. 

Now, let's talk about how you manipulate their reality using consequences: the four levers of operant conditioning. Pay attention, because people mess this up all the time. "Positive" means you are adding something to the room. "Negative" means you are sucking something out of the room like a vacuum. "Reinforcement" means you want the behavior to happen *more*. "Punishment" means you want the behavior to happen *less*. Got it? Good. 

Positive reinforcement involves adding a desirable stimulus after a behavior to increase the future frequency of that behavior. Kid does his work, you hand him a sticker. Boom, added a sticker, behavior goes up. Negative reinforcement involves removing an aversive stimulus after a behavior to increase the future frequency of that behavior. Kid asks politely for quiet, you take away the blaring classroom music. You removed the annoyance, and now he’s highly likely to ask politely again tomorrow. Negative reinforcement is NOT punishment! It’s relief! Now, on the flip side, positive punishment involves adding an aversive stimulus after a behavior to decrease the future frequency of that behavior. Kid runs in the hall, you slap them with extra homework. You added garbage to their life to stop the running. Negative punishment involves removing a desirable stimulus after a behavior to decrease the future frequency of that behavior. Kid swears, you take away their recess. You removed the fun to kill the swearing. 

But how often do you pay out? If you're teaching an operative a totally new vault-cracking technique, you need a heavy payout. Continuous reinforcement provides a reinforcer every single time the target behavior occurs. Unsurprisingly, continuous reinforcement is most effective when teaching a completely new skill or behavior. But once they know it? You don't want to go bankrupt. You switch up the payout. Intermittent reinforcement provides a reinforcer only some of the times the target behavior occurs. It keeps them guessing, like a slot machine. Intermittent reinforcement is most effective for maintaining an already learned behavior over time. To manage all these payouts without losing your mind, you set up your own internal casino currency. A token economy is a behavior modification system where students earn tokens for appropriate behaviors. The tokens are useless plastic, but the magic happens because tokens earned in a token economy can be exchanged for backup reinforcers, like computer time or a giant plush shark.

What happens when you need to shut down a black-market behavior? You cut the power. Extinction is the process of completely withholding reinforcement for a previously reinforced behavior. If a kid is acting out for your attention, and you go stone-cold silent and look away, that's extinction. The goal of extinction is to decrease the frequency of a target behavior. But brace yourself, because humans are stubborn. If you put a dollar in a vending machine and nothing comes out, do you walk away gently? No! You shoulder-check the glass! An extinction burst is a temporary increase in the intensity of a behavior immediately following the implementation of extinction. They will scream louder, test you harder, shake the machine. Hold the line! A very specific version of this is planned ignoring, which is an extinction strategy used specifically for behaviors maintained by adult attention. You just stare at the wall while they perform their one-act play. But you can't just be a brick wall all day. You pair it with a secret weapon. Differential reinforcement involves reinforcing a desired behavior while simultaneously withholding reinforcement for an undesired behavior. You ignore the screaming, but the absolute microsecond they take a quiet breath and raise their hand, you rain down the praise and tokens! 

The absolute best teachers, though? They don't run around putting out fires. Reactive behavior management involves responding to challenging behaviors after the challenging behaviors have already occurred. That's exhausting. Proactive behavior management focuses on altering the learning environment to prevent challenging behaviors from occurring. You fireproof the casino! Schools do this through a massive, three-tiered public health model. Positive Behavioral Interventions and Supports is a multi-tiered framework for establishing school-wide social culture and behavioral supports. At the base, Tier 1 of Positive Behavioral Interventions and Supports provides universal behavioral expectations for all students. This is the general admission—everyone gets the same school-wide rules and assemblies. But some kids need a VIP pass. Tier 2 of Positive Behavioral Interventions and Supports provides targeted interventions for small groups of students exhibiting at-risk behaviors. This is where you bring in the silver-tier systems. Check-In Check-Out is a Tier 2 behavioral intervention involving daily feedback from a mentor regarding specific behavioral goals. The kid meets with a cool coach in the morning to get hyped up, and checks out in the afternoon to count up their daily points. Finally, for the true renegades, Tier 3 of Positive Behavioral Interventions and Supports provides intensive interventions for students with the most severe behavioral challenges. This is your highly customized FBA and BIP.

Ultimately, your greatest security system is your teaching. Idle hands are the devil's playthings, my friend! Establishing clear classroom routines reduces transition times.
