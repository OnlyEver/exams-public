A great classroom is kind of like a perfect multiplayer video game. If you don't know the rules, it looks like everyone is just running around doing random things. But if you look closely, there is a reason behind every single move. A teacher's job isn't to squash kids' energy; it's to figure out why kids act the way they do and set up the classroom "game" so that winning (learning) is super easy and fun!

To make a classroom run smoothly, we have to understand the science behind how people act, and then use that science to make a great daily plan.

## The Invisible Forces: Theories of Classroom Management

If a student keeps interrupting a reading lesson, how the teacher responds depends on how they understand the student's behavior. Over the years, experts have given us different ways to understand why kids do what they do.

### The Mechanics of Consequence: Behaviorism
At the base of all behavior science is a famous idea. **B.F. Skinner's behaviorism theory posits that human behavior is shaped and maintained by its consequences.** In plain words: what happens right *after* you do something decides if you will do it again. 

Skinner said there are three main ways to change behavior:
1. **Positive reinforcement involves adding a desirable stimulus immediately following a behavior to increase the future frequency of that behavior.** Imagine getting a \$5 allowance right after you take out the trash. That awesome reward makes you want to do the chore again!
2. **Negative reinforcement involves removing an aversive stimulus immediately following a behavior to increase the future frequency of that behavior.** Think of your car's seatbelt alarm. That annoying beeping goes away the second you buckle up. Removing the bad noise makes you buckle up faster next time. 
3. **Punishment is any consequence presented immediately following a behavior that decreases the future frequency of that behavior.** For example, losing your phone privileges right after you break a rule makes you less likely to break that rule again.

In schools, teachers use these ideas in a system called ABA. **Applied Behavior Analysis applies behaviorist principles to systematically change socially significant behaviors in educational settings.** A super common ABA trick is the token economy. **A token economy is a behavior management system where students earn symbolic tokens for desired behaviors.** It is just like an arcade where you earn tickets for playing games well. And just like at an arcade, **students exchange tokens earned in a token economy for preferred backup reinforcers**, like extra recess time or a cool prize!

### The Internal Engine: Motivation and Belonging
Behaviorism explains *how* we form habits, but *why* do we want things in the first place? 

**William Glasser's Choice Theory asserts that all human behavior is driven by the internal desire to satisfy five basic psychological needs.** 

> **The Five Basic Needs (Choice Theory)**
> 1. Survival
> 2. Love and belonging
> 3. Power
> 4. Freedom
> 5. Fun

**The five basic needs in Choice Theory are survival, love and belonging, power, freedom, and fun.** If a student feels powerless during a really hard math test, they might crumple up their paper to try and feel free again. **Teachers applying Choice Theory create learning environments that help students fulfill their basic psychological needs in positive ways.** Instead of just punishing the student, the teacher might let the student *choose* between two different math assignments so they feel a sense of power!

Another expert, Rudolf Dreikurs, looked at how kids fit in. **Rudolf Dreikurs proposed that all misbehavior is the result of a child's mistaken belief about how to gain a sense of belonging.** When a kid acts out, they are basically thinking, *"I only matter if people are looking at me!"* **Rudolf Dreikurs identified attention-getting, power-seeking, revenge, and displaying inadequacy as the four mistaken goals of misbehavior.** (Inadequacy means just giving up).

To help fix these wrong beliefs, Dreikurs liked using consequences that make sense instead of just random punishments:
* **Logical consequences are disciplinary measures directly and rationally related to the student's misbehavior.** For example, if you purposely spill juice on the floor, you have to mop it up. 
* **Natural consequences occur without adult intervention as a direct result of a child's action.** For example, if you refuse to wear a jacket in the snow, you will feel cold!

### The Architecture of Authority and Communication
How a teacher talks about the rules sets the vibe for the whole room. **Lee and Marlene Canter developed the Assertive Discipline model.** The main rule here is fairness: **The Assertive Discipline model emphasizes the teacher's right to teach and the student's right to learn without disruption.** To make this work, **assertive teachers clearly communicate expectations and consistently follow through with established consequences for student choices.** There is no arguing—the rules are the rules every single time.

But being strict doesn't mean being mean. **Haim Ginott's theory of Congruent Communication emphasizes addressing the specific situation rather than criticizing the student's character.** Instead of saying, "You are so messy," a teacher should say, "Your books need to be put away." Building on this friendly way of talking, **Thomas Gordon's Teacher Effectiveness Training focuses on active listening and using I-messages to resolve classroom conflicts without damaging relationships.** For example, a teacher might say, "I feel distracted when there is talking during silent reading time."

### The Physics of the Classroom: Jacob Kounin
Instead of just reacting to bad behavior, **Jacob Kounin identified specific proactive teacher behaviors that prevent classroom management problems before they occur.** He realized the best teachers stop trouble before it even starts! 

* **Withitness is a teacher's ability to remain continuously aware of all student activity in the classroom.** It's like having eyes in the back of your head!
* **Overlapping is a teacher's ability to attend to multiple classroom events and student needs simultaneously.** This is like a superpower where a teacher can hand a kid a pencil while still reading a book to the class.
* **Momentum in classroom management refers to keeping lessons moving at a brisk pace without interruptions or slowdowns.** If the class is fast and fun, no one has time to get bored and act out.
* **The ripple effect occurs when a teacher corrects one student and the surrounding students change their behavior accordingly.** If the teacher tells one student to sit down, the kids next to them usually sit down, too!

## The Framework: Positive Behavioral Interventions and Supports (PBIS)

Today, schools put all these theories together into one big playbook. **Positive Behavioral Interventions and Supports is a multi-tiered framework for establishing a positive school culture.** Think of it like a ladder of help.

| PBIS Tier | Scope & Definition | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Tier 1** | **Tier 1 of Positive Behavioral Interventions and Supports includes universal behavioral expectations explicitly taught to all students.** | School-wide assemblies where everyone learns the basic "Be Safe, Be Kind" rules. |
| **Tier 2** | **Tier 2 of Positive Behavioral Interventions and Supports provides targeted, small-group interventions for at-risk students.** | A small group of kids who have trouble in the morning getting extra practice with a mentor. |
| **Tier 3** | **Tier 3 of Positive Behavioral Interventions and Supports offers intensive, individualized interventions for students with severe behavioral needs.** | One-on-one, super specific help for a student who really struggles. |

## Engineering Your Classroom Management Plan

Knowing the science is cool, but teachers have to put it into action! The best tool a teacher has is their plan. **A comprehensive classroom management plan defines the behavioral expectations, routines, and consequences for the learning environment.** 

### 1. Spatial and Procedural Foundation
Before kids even walk in, the room needs to be set up right. **The physical arrangement of a classroom must allow the teacher to see all students from any location in the room.** Also, **high-traffic areas in the classroom must be kept free of physical congestion to prevent peer conflicts and behavioral incidents.** You don't want a traffic jam by the pencil sharpener!

Next come the rules:
* **Classroom rules must be stated in positive terms to tell students exactly what behavior is expected of them.** (Say "Walk in the halls" instead of "Don't run.")
* **Effective classroom management plans limit the number of overarching classroom rules to between three and five.** Nobody can remember 20 rules! 
* **Classroom rules must be explicitly taught, modeled, and practiced at the beginning of the school year to ensure student understanding.** You have to practice them on day one.
* While rules are for general behavior, **classroom procedures outline the specific steps students must take to successfully complete recurring daily tasks**, like how to ask to use the bathroom. 
* **Establishing clear routines minimizes downtime during transitions between instructional activities.** Switching from math to reading needs to be smooth so kids don't get bored. 

### 2. Instructional Dynamics
The best way to keep kids out of trouble is to make the lesson super interesting. **Increased academic engagement directly correlates with a significant decrease in disruptive classroom behaviors.** If you are having fun learning, you aren't messing around!

To keep kids engaged, **providing frequent opportunities to respond increases student engagement during whole-group instruction.** Teachers should let kids hold up whiteboards, do thumbs-up signs, or talk to a partner! Also, **active supervision requires teachers to constantly monitor the classroom through moving, scanning, and interacting with students.** Teachers shouldn't just sit at a desk; they need to walk around and be part of the action.

### 3. The Feedback Loop
Sometimes, kids will still break rules. **A hierarchy of consequences details a graduated progression of teacher responses to minor rule violations.** This means punishments go step-by-step, like getting a warning before losing free time. It keeps things predictable.

But a classroom shouldn't be all about rules and warnings! **Teachers must deliver positive feedback more frequently than corrective feedback to maintain a supportive classroom climate.** A great rule of thumb is the 4 to 1 ratio: four compliments for every one correction. 

Here is how you calculate that balance:
Step 1: Count how many times you had to correct a student (let's say you gave 3 corrections today).
Step 2: Multiply the number of corrections by 4 to find your target number of compliments (3 x 4 = 12).
Step 3: Make sure you give that student those 12 pieces of positive feedback to keep them feeling happy and encouraged!

## When the Plan Isn't Enough: Individualized Behavior Management

Sometimes, even with the best plan, a student might still have a really hard time. When a car makes a weird noise, a mechanic doesn't just hit the engine with a hammer; they run a test to figure out *why* it's making the noise. 

In school, this test is an FBA. **A Functional Behavior Assessment identifies the underlying purpose of a student's persistent problem behavior.** It's like playing detective to find out if a kid is throwing chairs because they want attention, or because they want to escape a hard math worksheet.

Once the teacher knows the "why," they make a new plan. **A Behavior Intervention Plan uses data from a Functional Behavior Assessment to teach positive replacement behaviors.** If the detective work shows the student throws chairs because they are overwhelmed by math, the new plan teaches them to hold up a "Break Card" when they need a rest. This gives them a safe way to get what they need without disrupting the class!
