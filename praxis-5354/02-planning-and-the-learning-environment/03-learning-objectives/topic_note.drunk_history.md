If you do that, the federal big boss—the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act—is going to drop a piano on your head. They do not fund "vibes." The Individuals with Disabilities Education Act requires Individualized Education Program goals to be rigidly, undeniably measurable. And you can't just write it on a dusty scroll and leave it in a tomb; the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act requires Individualized Education Program goals to be updated at least annually. It is a living, breathing blueprint for a cognitive bank heist, and every year, you have to plan a new job!

To pull off this heist, your blueprint needs three non-negotiable structural pillars, starting with the exact moves your crew will make. A measurable learning objective must state a specific target behavior. You can't tell your getaway driver to "feel good" about driving; you need a specific, observable action. That’s right, the target behavior in a measurable learning objective must be entirely observable. How do you know if it's observable? Imagine the security cameras at the casino. An observable behavior is one that can be directly seen, heard, counted, or timed by an educator. If I can't catch it on tape or clock it with a stopwatch, it's garbage! 

This is why you have to choose your words like a neurosurgeon. Action verbs like 'write', 'point', or 'state' represent observable behaviors suitable for learning objectives. I can look at a kid and definitively see them write a fraction or point to a picture. On the flip side, verbs like 'understand', 'know', or 'appreciate' describe unobservable internal states. You cannot physically witness "knowing," just like you can't see the air inside a vault. Because you can't see or time a feeling, these unobservable verbs cannot serve as the primary action in a measurable learning objective. If you write "the student will understand math," I will personally come to your classroom and confiscate your laminator!

But your crew doesn't just act in a void. We need to set the scene. A measurable learning objective must specify the condition under which the target behavior will occur. Are they doing this blindfolded? While juggling chainsaws? The condition of a learning objective defines the materials, setting, or level of assistance provided to the student. If the goal is "given a graphic organizer and one verbal prompt," that’s your condition. It’s the exact gear and environment they have when the alarms start ringing.

Then comes the getaway threshold, the ultimate test of survival. A measurable learning objective must include a performance criterion. A performance criterion establishes the required accuracy level for the student to achieve the learning objective—like, "bypassing the firewall with 80% accuracy." Alternatively, this performance criterion can also establish the required frequency of the behavior for the student to achieve the objective, such as "will initiate a conversation three times per lunch period." It’s the exact metric that proves the job was a success!

How do we remember this beautifully manic blueprint? We use a survival code. The acronym SMART is a widely used framework for writing educational goals and objectives. The letter S in the SMART framework stands for Specific; we are targeting a precise skill, not a vague wish. The letter M in the SMART framework stands for Measurable, meaning it has that observable behavior and strict criteria we just yelled about. The letter A in the SMART framework stands for Attainable or Action-oriented, because we aren't planning to steal the moon, we're planning a job the kid can actually pull off. The letter R in the SMART framework stands for Relevant or Realistic, making sure this mission actually matters to the student's life and independence. And finally, the letter T in the SMART framework stands for Time-bound. A time-bound learning objective specifies the exact date or timeframe by which the student must achieve the goal. No "whenever they get around to it." We need to hit the vault by May 15th!

But before you plan the route, you have to case the joint. You need to know exactly where you are starting from. Educators use the student's Present Levels of Academic Achievement and Functional Performance statement—the PLAAFP—to establish baseline performance data. This is your initial recon. Can they currently crack a three-number lock, or are they still trying to eat the lock? Baseline performance data is absolutely required to determine the appropriate starting point for a learning objective. Without it, you’re flying blind. 

Once you have that baseline, you build your map. Special education learning objectives must directly align with the student's annual Individualized Education Program goals. And these annual goals have two massive legal burdens strapped to their backs. First, the Individualized Education Program goals must address the unique educational needs resulting from the student's disability. Second, the Individualized Education Program goals must enable the student to make progress in the general education curriculum. 

Wait, what exactly is the general education curriculum? It’s the master floor plan of the casino! State academic content standards define the expectations of the general education curriculum for each grade level. You can't just invent your own standards. Educators must align special education learning objectives with the student's enrolled grade-level state academic standards. Even if your 5th-grade student is reading at a 2nd-grade level, your goal's ultimate north star must align to the 5th-grade standards, properly scaffolded. 

Now, how hard do we push? We use the Goldilocks zone of heist-planning. An appropriately challenging learning objective requires the student to demonstrate measurable growth beyond their established baseline data. You can't set the goal at what they already know how to do; that's just loitering! A challenging learning objective maintains high expectations while remaining realistically attainable within the specified timeframe. If they grew three reading levels last year, a goal of four levels is appropriately challenging. A goal of ten levels is a suicide mission.

Sometimes, though, the annual goal is just too massive. It’s an epic, multi-stage casino infiltration. Back in the day, everyone had to break these down, but the law shifted. Now, the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act requires short-term objectives only for students taking alternate assessments aligned to alternate achievement standards. Who are these elite operatives? Students with the most significant cognitive disabilities may be assessed using alternate achievement standards. These standards adapt the complexity of the task, but crucially, these alternate achievement standards must maintain alignment with the state's general academic content standards. The route may change, but the vault is in the same building.

For these students, we use two tracking devices to make sure the mission doesn't go off the rails. First, short-term objectives break down an annual Individualized Education Program goal into smaller instructional steps. It’s the literal sequence: Step 1, cut the cameras; Step 2, disable the laser grid; Step 3, blow the vault door. They master each discrete sub-skill in order. Second, benchmarks establish expected performance levels at specific intervals across the school year to monitor progress toward an annual goal. These are your radio check-ins. If the goal is 80% accuracy by May, a benchmark says, "Bravo team, you should be at 40% accuracy by November, and 60% by February."
