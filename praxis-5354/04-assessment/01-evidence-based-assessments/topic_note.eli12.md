Imagine trying to build a skateboard ramp, but your tape measure is made out of a stretchy rubber band. One time you measure a piece of wood, it says 20 inches. You measure it again, and the tape stretches to say 22 inches. You have no idea how long the wood actually is! 

In schools, teachers make huge decisions to help kids learn. If we use bad, stretchy tests to figure out what a student needs, it’s like building that skateboard ramp with a rubber band. We need tools that give us exact, trustworthy answers.

## The Hallmarks of Precision: Validity and Reliability

To trust a test, we have to make sure it has two main things: validity and reliability. Think of them as the accuracy and the consistency of your tool.

**Validity refers to the degree to which an assessment measures what the assessment claims to measure.** If you take a math test, but the questions are super long, tricky word puzzles, a kid who is great at math but struggles with reading might fail it. Did that test actually measure their math skills, or did it just measure their reading skills? If it claims to be a math test but tests reading instead, it lacks validity! 

There are three main types of validity to look for:
*   **Construct validity refers to how accurately an assessment measures a theoretical trait such as intelligence or reading comprehension.** You can't open up someone's head and measure "smarts" with a ruler. So, we build tests (constructs) like puzzles to capture those invisible skills perfectly.
*   **Content validity indicates whether the items on an assessment adequately cover the specific instructional domain the assessment intends to evaluate.** If your teacher says there is a test on fractions, but the test only has addition problems like 5 + 2 = 7, your test has bad content validity.
*   **Predictive validity evaluates how well the results of an assessment forecast a student's future academic performance or behavior.** It’s like a sports drill at the start of the season that correctly predicts how many goals you will score by the end of the year!

**Reliability refers to the consistency of an assessment's results across different administrations, raters, or test items.** A reliable test is like a video game controller that does the exact same thing every single time you press the 'A' button. 

*   **Test-retest reliability measures the stability of assessment scores when the exact same test is administered to the same student at two different times.** If you take a test on Monday and get an 85, and take the exact same test on Tuesday without learning anything new, you should still get an 85. 
*   **Inter-rater reliability measures the degree of scoring agreement between two or more different evaluators grading the same assessment.** If Mr. Smith grades your essay and gives it a B, and Ms. Jones grades the exact same essay, she should also give it a B!

## Evidence-Based Tools vs. Flexible Observations

We need different kinds of tests for different situations.

**Evidence-based assessments are evaluation tools that have undergone rigorous scientific research demonstrating their accuracy for specific populations.** This means scientists have tested these tools over and over to prove they work perfectly for kids just like you. 

To make sure everything is perfectly fair, we use **standardized assessments**. **Standardized assessments require strict adherence to specific administration and scoring procedures to ensure uniformity across all test-takers.** That means the teacher has to read the exact same instructions and set the exact same timer for every single kid who takes it. 

On the other hand, **non-standardized assessments encompass flexible evaluation methods such as teacher observations, student portfolios, and informal checklists.** These are relaxed! A teacher might just watch how you solve a puzzle on a Tuesday afternoon or look through a folder of your coolest drawings.

> **Where to verify technical adequacy?** 
> How do teachers know which tests to trust? **The National Center on Intensive Intervention provides independent reviews evaluating the technical adequacy of various evidence-based assessment tools.** It’s like a giant review website that tells teachers which tests are actually good!

## The Legal Imperative: IDEA Mandates

Testing isn't just about grades; there are strict laws about it to protect students. **The Individuals with Disabilities Education Act requires that assessments used for special education evaluation must be technically sound.** This means it is literally the law to use good, solid tests! 

We also have to make sure tests are fair to everyone, no matter who they are or where they come from.
*   **The Individuals with Disabilities Education Act mandates that assessment materials must not be racially or culturally discriminatory.** 
*   **The Individuals with Disabilities Education Act requires administering assessments in the student's native language or primary mode of communication.** If a kid’s main way of talking is Sign Language, giving them a test in spoken English is totally unfair and against the law.

To follow these rules, teachers must use a **culturally responsive assessment process**, which **requires verifying that the test's normative sample includes individuals from the student's demographic background.** "Normative sample" just means the original group of kids they tried the test out on. If they only tried the test out on kids who live on farms, is it fair to use it on a kid who grew up in a giant city? Probably not!

## Choosing the Right Instrument

You have to pick the right tool for the job. **Educators must match the selected assessment tool to the student's specific developmental level, chronological age, and suspected disability.** You wouldn't give a kindergarten coloring test to a high schooler!

When we do give formal tests, they usually fall into two types:

| Assessment Type | Core Function | Example Scenario |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Norm-referenced tests** | **Norm-referenced tests evaluate a student's performance by comparing the student's scores to a representative sample of peers.** | Like a video game leaderboard! Finding out you scored better at reading than 85% of other kids your age across the country. |
| **Criterion-referenced tests** | **Criterion-referenced tests measure a student's performance against a fixed set of predefined learning standards or objectives.** | Like beating a specific level in a game. It doesn't matter what other kids did, you just have to prove you know your multiplication tables! |

Sometimes, a kid is having a really hard time, and we need to figure out exactly why. That's when we use **diagnostic assessments**. **Diagnostic assessments are evidence-based tools designed to identify specific learning difficulties and pinpoint a student's precise academic deficits.** It’s like a mechanic plugging a special computer into a car to find out exactly which wire is broken.

### The Physics of Poor Measurement: Ceiling and Floor Effects

Imagine using an outdoor thermometer in Antarctica that only goes down to 30 degrees. It will just say "LOW" every single day. That is a bad tool! 

In schools, this happens when tests are poorly matched to the students: 
*   **Floor effects occur when an assessment is too difficult.** Imagine giving a college math test to third graders. **Floor effects result in a cluster of students scoring at the lowest possible level on an assessment.** Everyone gets a zero, and you learn absolutely nothing about what they *do* know.
*   **Ceiling effects occur when an assessment is too easy.** Imagine giving an ABC alphabet test to a group of 8th graders. **Ceiling effects result in a cluster of students scoring at the highest possible level on an assessment.** Everyone gets a 100%, but you have no idea how smart they actually are beyond knowing their ABCs! 

## Tracking Learning in Motion

Testing doesn't stop once a plan is made. We have to keep checking to see if our teaching is actually working!

*   **Formative assessments are ongoing evaluations used to adjust instruction during the learning process.** This is like checking your map while you are walking. A teacher might look over your shoulder to see if you get the math problem, so they know if they need to teach it again tomorrow.
*   **Summative assessments evaluate student learning at the conclusion of an instructional period by comparing the learning against a standard.** This is like the final boss battle at the end of a long video game level. 

To connect those quick checks with big goals, we use **Curriculum-Based Measurement**. **Curriculum-Based Measurement is an evidence-based assessment method used to continuously track student progress in core academic skills.** For example, a teacher might track how many words you can read in one minute every single Friday to watch your reading speed go up on a graph!

Sometimes, we need to step outside the regular testing room entirely:
*   **Ecological assessment evaluates a student's behavior and academic performance within the student's natural physical and social environments.** Instead of testing you in a quiet room, a teacher watches how you handle the noisy, crowded cafeteria.
*   **Authentic assessment evaluates a student's ability to apply acquired knowledge and skills to perform real-world tasks.** Instead of a multiple-choice question about money, the teacher asks you to go to the school store, buy a snack, and figure out the exact change. 

Let's look at the math steps for an authentic assessment where you buy that snack:
Imagine you have a \$10.00 bill and want to buy a slice of pizza that costs \$3.50.
1. Write down your starting amount: \$10.00.
2. Subtract the cost of the pizza: \$10.00 - \$3.50.
3. Calculate the difference: \$6.50.
4. You successfully proved you know how to calculate your change in real life!

## Preserving the Measurement: Accommodations vs. Modifications

Finally, we have to talk about how teachers help kids take tests without accidentally ruining the test itself. There is a huge difference between an accommodation and a modification.

**Test accommodations alter how a student accesses an assessment without changing the fundamental construct being measured.** If you know a lot about science but have trouble seeing the tiny words on the paper, letting you wear glasses or reading the test out loud to you is an accommodation. The test is still measuring your science knowledge! 

**Test modifications alter the actual content or the learning expectations of an assessment.** If the teacher takes that same science test, crosses out the hard questions, and makes it a super easy test, they have modified it. It’s like changing a video game from "Normal" mode to "Super Easy" mode. 

Why does this matter so much? Because of those standardized tests we talked about! **Applying test modifications to an evidence-based standardized assessment invalidates the normative data of that assessment.** If you play a game on "Super Easy" mode, you can't compare your high score to a kid who played on "Normal" mode. Once you modify a standardized test, the results are broken, and you can't compare the scores to other kids anymore. You are back to measuring with a stretchy rubber band!
