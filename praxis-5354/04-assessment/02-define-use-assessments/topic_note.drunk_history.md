Listen to me, slide that coaster over, ignore the jukebox, and pay attention, because you are about to walk into your first special education classroom, and it is not a room—it is a high-stakes, multi-million-dollar heist being filmed live for a reality TV cooking show. You are the mastermind, the head chef, and the ringleader all rolled into one, and if you walk in there just guessing what your students know, your soufflé is going to explode and the vault alarms are going to trigger simultaneously. You need telemetry. You need intel. You need assessments.

Before you even step foot in the kitchen or unlock the vault door, you have to do your reconnaissance. This is where diagnostic assessments come into play. These are administered before instruction begins to identify a student’s prior knowledge and their deeply held, potentially disastrous misconceptions. If you don't know that a kid thinks you carry a zero by literally putting it in your pocket, your math lesson is doomed before it starts. By doing this recon, teachers use diagnostic assessments to establish a baseline for measuring future academic growth. You can't prove you grew the kid's brain if you didn't measure it before you started stretching it!

Alright, the clock is ticking, the cameras are rolling, and you are actively teaching. You do not—I repeat, do *not*—wait until the dish is plated and served to the judges to find out if it tastes like battery acid. You grab a spoon, and you taste the sauce while it’s still boiling! That is what formative assessments are for. Formative assessments are used to monitor student learning and provide ongoing feedback. Because you are getting real-time intel, teachers use formative assessments to adjust instructional strategies during a lesson. Did half the room give you a blank stare when you explained fractions? Boom, you pivot immediately. Because this is just a taste-test mid-recipe, formative assessments typically carry little or no point value toward a student's final grade. Examples of formative assessments include exit tickets, think-pair-share activities, and quick quizzes. If you just rolled out a brand-new behavior or math strategy, formative assessment data helps special educators determine if a current instructional intervention is effective right there in the trenches. 

But eventually, time is up. Hands off the tools. The food critic is sitting at the table, napkin tucked in, ready to judge your life’s work. Welcome to summative assessments, which evaluate student learning at the end of an instructional unit. We are no longer tasting the sauce to fix it; we are deciding if you win the prize money. Summative assessments compare student performance against a standard or benchmark to see if they actually mastered the content. Because this is the finale, summative assessments are typically high-stakes measures of learning, and unlike your friendly little exit tickets, summative assessments generally account for a significant portion of a student's final grade. Examples of summative assessments include midterm exams, final projects, and standardized state tests.

Now, we have to talk about *how* we gather this intel, because there are two wildly different vibes: the health inspector and the seasoned sous-chef.

When you need cold, hard, mathematically defensible proof that can stand up in a court of law, you need formal assessments. These bad boys follow strict administration and scoring procedures to ensure reliable data. You cannot deviate from the script, or you ruin the experiment. Because of this rigidity, formal assessments yield quantitative data regarding student performance, giving you actual numbers to crunch. Formal assessments compare student achievement to broader populations or specific predetermined criteria. Standardized intelligence tests and state-mandated achievement tests are types of formal assessments.

But you and I know a classroom is a living, breathing ecosystem, and numbers don't tell the whole story. You also need the vibe check, the daily grind data. Informal assessments are content-driven and performance-driven measures that lack standardized grading criteria. Instead of rigid numbers, informal assessments provide qualitative data about a student's daily academic performance and behavior. We are talking about teacher observations, student portfolios, and running records—those are all examples of informal assessments. A portfolio assessment consists of a curated collection of student work gathered over time to evaluate growth and achievement, like a chef's lookbook of their finest dishes over the season! And when a kid inexplicably throws a chair because the wind blew the wrong way? You need anecdotal records, which are brief written notes describing a student's behavior or performance in a specific situation. If you need to track how often that happens, you level up to rating scales, which are assessment tools used to indicate the degree or frequency of a student's behavior or skill demonstration.

Now, how are we comparing these kids once we have the data? What is our frame of reference? 

Sometimes, you need to know how your kid stacks up against the rest of the country. Norm-referenced assessments compare a single student's performance to the performance of a larger representative peer group. Think of it as a nationwide bake-off. Because you're grading on a massive curve, norm-referenced test scores are often reported as percentiles, stanines, or standard scores. The Wechsler Intelligence Scale for Children is an example of a norm-referenced assessment—it literally tells you where a kid's cognitive ability sits relative to thousands of other kids their exact age.

But other times, we don't care about the other kids! If you are taking your driving test, I don't care if you drive better than Timmy, I care if you stop at the red light! Criterion-referenced assessments measure a student's performance against a predetermined set of learning standards or objectives. The beauty here is that criterion-referenced assessments do not compare a student's performance to the performance of other students. A spelling test assessing specific weekly spelling words is an example of a criterion-referenced assessment. You either spelled "dinosaur" right, or you didn't. 

And here is my absolute favorite, the underdog's secret weapon: ipsative assessments. These compare a student's current performance with the student's *own* previous performance. It’s like a gym training montage where you're only trying to beat your own high score! Because you remove the crushing weight of peer comparison, ipsative assessments are useful for motivating special education students by highlighting personal academic growth.

Because we are special educators, we have a highly specialized toolkit. To make sure no kid is slipping through the cracks of the entire school system, we cast a massive net. Universal screening assessments are used to identify students who may be at risk for academic or behavioral difficulties. Once we've caught them, we don't just hope for the best until May. Benchmark assessments are administered periodically to track student progress toward end-of-year academic goals. Fall, Winter, Spring—boom, boom, boom, checking the milestones.

But for our specific students, we need a microscope, not just a net. Curriculum-based measurement is a standardized method of evaluating basic skills in reading, math, and writing. Because it's tied directly to what you're teaching, special education teachers use curriculum-based measurement for continuous progress monitoring of Individualized Education Program goals. And because we don't have time to waste, curriculum-based measurements are typically brief assessments lasting one to five minutes. 

Sometimes, a static test is useless because it only shows what a kid *can't* do alone. Enter the interactive heist: dynamic assessment. Dynamic assessment is an interactive approach that measures a student's ability to learn new skills with scaffolding. It typically follows a test-teach-retest format. You test them, realize they are stuck, teach them how to hold the proverbial lockpick, and immediately test them again to see if they absorbed the coaching! 

And we must remember that kids act differently in different rooms. Ecological assessment involves evaluating a student's functioning within various environments like the classroom, home, or community. Maybe they can follow a recipe in your room but panic in the cafeteria. We also need to know if they can survive in the wild. Authentic assessments require students to perform real-world tasks to demonstrate meaningful application of essential knowledge—like giving them \$20 and making them successfully navigate a grocery store scenario. Because life doesn't have A-B-C-D bubbles, authentic assessments are often evaluated using a scoring rubric rather than a standardized test score. 

Finally, for a very specific, fiercely protected group of our kids, standard state testing is like asking them to fly a helicopter when they're in a submarine. Alternative assessments are designed for students with severe cognitive disabilities who cannot participate in standard state testing. To ensure they are still participating in accountability but in a valid way, alternative assessments often utilize modified academic achievement standards. 

Here is the absolute golden rule, the thing that keeps you from failing the mission and losing your license: selecting an assessment tool requires matching the assessment's primary purpose to the specific learning objective. You don't use a yardstick to measure the temperature of a turkey! And above all else, legally, ethically, and morally, special education teachers must select assessment tools that align with a student's Individualized Education Program accommodations. If their IEP says they need extra time and a quiet room, and you give them a pop quiz in the middle of a chaotic pep rally, your data is garbage and you broke the law.
