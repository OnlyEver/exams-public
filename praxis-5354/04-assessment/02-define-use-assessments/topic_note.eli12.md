Think of a video game. You don't play the whole game blindfolded and only find out your score at the very end. Instead, the game constantly shows you your health, your points, and a map so you can adjust how you play in real-time. Similarly, in a classroom, testing isn't just about handing out a grade when it's all over. Assessments are ongoing tools that help teachers figure out exactly what you need to learn next. To teach well, special education teachers need to understand all the different types of tools they can use to track a student's progress.

## The Timing and Purpose of Measurement

To measure learning accurately, we have to look at the difference between tests used to *guide* the learning process and tests used to *judge* the final result.

### Establishing the Baseline: Diagnostic Assessments
Before you can start a race, you need a starting line. Diagnostic assessments are administered before instruction begins to identify a student's prior knowledge and misconceptions. By seeing exactly what you already know—and maybe what you are confused about—teachers use diagnostic assessments to establish a baseline for measuring future academic growth. Without this starting point, it's impossible to see how far you've come!

### Guiding the Process: Formative Assessments
Once the lesson is rolling, teachers need to know if you are keeping up. Formative assessments are used to monitor student learning and provide ongoing feedback. These are quick, low-stress checks. Formative assessments typically carry little or no point value toward a student's final grade. 

Their power is in how fast they work. Teachers use formative assessments to adjust instructional strategies during a lesson. For example, if a teacher tries explaining fractions and it isn't making sense, formative assessment data helps special educators determine if a current instructional intervention is effective. If it isn't working, they can instantly try a different way of teaching it. Examples of formative assessments include exit tickets, think-pair-share activities, and quick quizzes.

### Evaluating the Outcome: Summative Assessments
When a whole topic is finished, it is time for the boss battle. Summative assessments evaluate student learning at the end of an instructional unit. Unlike the quick, low-stress checks, summative assessments are typically high-stakes measures of learning. Because they test everything you learned over a long time, summative assessments generally account for a significant portion of a student's final grade.

Summative assessments compare student performance against a standard or benchmark to see if you mastered the topic. Examples of summative assessments include midterm exams, final projects, and standardized state tests.

## The Nature of the Data: Formal vs. Informal

Besides *when* we test, we also have to look at *how strict* the test is.

### Formal Assessments
When teachers need hard facts and exact numbers, they use formal tests. Formal assessments follow strict administration and scoring procedures to ensure reliable data. Because everyone takes the test under the exact same rules, formal assessments yield quantitative data regarding student performance (meaning cold, hard numbers). 

Formal assessments compare student achievement to broader populations or specific predetermined criteria. Because the rules are so strict, standardized intelligence tests and state-mandated achievement tests are types of formal assessments.

### Informal Assessments
Classrooms are lively, busy places, and sometimes numbers don't tell the whole story. For everyday learning, teachers use informal tests. Informal assessments are content-driven and performance-driven measures that lack standardized grading criteria. Instead of just giving a number grade, informal assessments provide qualitative data about a student's daily academic performance and behavior (meaning descriptions and observations). 

Teacher observations, student portfolios, and running records are examples of informal assessments. 
*   Portfolio assessments consist of a curated collection of student work gathered over time to evaluate growth and achievement. 
*   Anecdotal records are brief written notes describing a student's behavior or performance in a specific situation.
*   Rating scales are assessment tools used to indicate the degree or frequency of a student's behavior or skill demonstration (like rating how often a student raises their hand on a scale of 1 to 5).

## Frames of Reference: How We Compare Data

If you get a score of "85" on a test, what does that actually mean? It depends on who or what you are being compared to.

### Norm-Referenced Assessments
Sometimes, we want to know how a student is doing compared to other kids their age. Norm-referenced assessments compare a single student's performance to the performance of a larger representative peer group. To show where you stand in the crowd, norm-referenced test scores are often reported as percentiles, stanines, or standard scores. If you score in the 80th percentile, it means you did as well or better than 80 percent of the kids in that group. The Wechsler Intelligence Scale for Children is an example of a norm-referenced assessment.

### Criterion-Referenced Assessments
Other times, it doesn't matter what anyone else is doing. Criterion-referenced assessments measure a student's performance against a predetermined set of learning standards or objectives. The main point is that criterion-referenced assessments do not compare a student's performance to the performance of other students. A spelling test assessing specific weekly spelling words is an example of a criterion-referenced assessment. You are only graded on whether or not you know those exact words!

### Ipsative Assessments
Imagine playing a video game and trying to beat your own high score. Ipsative assessments compare a student's current performance with the student's own previous performance. Because it takes away the stress of comparing yourself to other people, ipsative assessments are useful for motivating special education students by highlighting personal academic growth.

## Specialized Measurements in Special Education

Special education teachers need extra specific tools to help their students succeed and to track their special goals. 

### Progress Monitoring: Curriculum-Based Measurement (CBM)
To track how well a student is learning basic skills week by week, teachers use CBMs. Curriculum-based measurement is a standardized method of evaluating basic skills in reading, math, and writing. Because it directly tests what is being taught in class, special education teachers use curriculum-based measurement for continuous progress monitoring of Individualized Education Program goals. Curriculum-based measurements are typically brief assessments lasting one to five minutes, so they don't take up too much class time.

### Systematic Screening: Universal and Benchmark Assessments
Schools want to make sure nobody gets left behind. Universal screening assessments are used to identify students who may be at risk for academic or behavioral difficulties. Once those kids get a little extra help, teachers check in on them like checking the scoreboard during a game. Benchmark assessments are administered periodically to track student progress toward end-of-year academic goals.

### Evaluating Potential: Dynamic Assessment
Normal tests usually only show what you can do all by yourself. But what if you just need a hint? Dynamic assessment is an interactive approach that measures a student's ability to learn new skills with scaffolding (a fancy word for helpful hints and support). Dynamic assessment typically follows a test-teach-retest format. The teacher tests you, helps teach you the parts you missed, and then tests you again to see how well you learn with a little coaching.

### Contextual and Real-World Evaluation
*   **Ecological Assessment:** Sometimes a student acts totally different at recess than they do during math class. Ecological assessment involves evaluating a student's functioning within various environments like the classroom, home, or community.
*   **Authentic Assessment:** Memorizing flashcards is fine, but can you use that knowledge in real life? Authentic assessments require students to perform real-world tasks to demonstrate meaningful application of essential knowledge. 

Let's look at an example of an authentic assessment for math. Imagine your real-world task is to buy snacks on a budget. You have \$20. You buy a pizza for \$12 and a juice for \$3. How much money do you have left? Here is how you solve it step-by-step:
1. First, find the total cost of the food by adding the price of the pizza and the juice: \$12 + \$3 = \$15.
2. Next, look at the amount of money you started with: \$20.
3. Subtract the total cost of the food from your starting money: \$20 - \$15.
4. The final answer is your leftover change: \$5.

Because these real-world tasks can be solved in many different ways, authentic assessments are often evaluated using a scoring rubric rather than a standardized test score.

### Accommodating Severe Needs: Alternative Assessments
State testing is required for almost everyone, but standard tests just don't work for some students. Alternative assessments are designed for students with severe cognitive disabilities who cannot participate in standard state testing. To make sure the test is fair and helpful, alternative assessments often utilize modified academic achievement standards.

## The Capstone: Selecting the Right Assessment Tool

Teachers can't just pick a test out of a hat. Selecting an assessment tool requires matching the assessment's primary purpose to the specific learning objective. If you want to test how fast someone can run, you use a stopwatch, not a ruler! 

Most importantly, special education teachers must select assessment tools that align with a student's Individualized Education Program accommodations. If a student's IEP plan says they are allowed to have test questions read out loud to them, the teacher has to provide that support. By picking the exact right tool for the job, teachers can gather the perfect clues to help every student learn better.
