Slide that margarita over here, Brenda, and listen to me closely because you are staring at those thirty graded math exams like they are going to bite you. The principal wants to know how the class did, and you can’t just throw thirty pieces of paper at his face! You need to boil that entire chaotic mess of students down into one, single, digestible reality TV star. You need a measure of central tendency! This isn't just math, Brenda, this is survival. A measure of central tendency provides a single summary value representing the center point of a data distribution. It’s the ultimate spokesperson for your unruly mob of data. 

But to pick your spokesperson, you have to choose between three completely unhinged personalities: the Mean, the Median, and the Mode. 

Let’s start with the ultimate martyr, the arithmetic mean. Out on the streets, the arithmetic mean is commonly referred to as the average. He is a total people-pleaser. He literally takes on everyone's baggage. The arithmetic mean is calculated by finding the sum of all values in a data set and dividing the sum by the total number of values. He absorbs all the joy and all the trauma in the room and splits the burden equally among everyone. 

Now, this guy operates under a couple of aliases depending on his mob rank. If he is the godfather, representing absolutely everyone in your universe, the formula for a population mean is represented in statistics by the Greek letter mu. Sounds intimidating, right? Don Mu! But if he’s just a mid-level capo representing a tiny sliver of the gang, the formula for a sample mean is represented in statistics by the letter x with a horizontal bar over the letter. Like a little flat hat. 

But here is Don Mu’s fatal flaw: he has zero boundaries. The arithmetic mean is sensitive to extremely high or extremely low values in a data set. We call these chaotic, rule-breaking freaks outliers. Extremely high or extremely low values in a data set are called outliers, and they ruin everything! Imagine your class is happily sitting at a 75, and suddenly an absolute mega-genius scores a 105, or a kid eats his test and gets a zero. An outlier pulls the arithmetic mean toward the outlier's numerical value! The mean gets dragged out of the center, kicking and screaming, toward that extreme freak. He just can't stand his ground!

That is exactly why you sometimes need to call in the enforcer: The Median. The median represents the exact middle numerical value of a given data set. But he demands absolute order! You can’t just point into a rioting crowd and find him. To find the median, the values in a data set must first be arranged in ascending or descending numerical order. You have to line them all up against the wall, shortest to tallest, or tallest to shortest! 

Once they are lined up, the Enforcer makes his move. If you have an odd number of students, it’s a piece of cake. For a data set with an odd number of values, the median is the single middle value in the ordered list. Just grab the kid in the dead center. But what if you have an even number of students? There’s no single middle guy; there are two guys standing there sweating. The Enforcer compromises. For a data set with an even number of values, the median is the arithmetic mean of the two middle values in the ordered list. You just add them up and slice them in half. 

And here is why the Median is an absolute unit: he does not care about the drama at the edges of the room! Because he only looks at the dead center of the line, the median is highly resistant to extreme values, or outliers, in a data set. That billionaire at the high end of the line can scream all he wants; the median doesn't flinch. 

Then we have the high school Prom Queen of the group: The Mode. The mode is the value or values occurring most frequently in a data set. It is a pure popularity contest. Who wore the same outfit? What score showed up the most times? 

But she is incredibly petty, Brenda. If every single person in the room wears a completely unique outfit—meaning every value in the data set appears with the exact same frequency—then the party is ruined, and a data set has no mode! On the flip side, what if exactly two distinct values share the highest frequency of occurrence? Two girls wear the exact same designer dress to the dance! It’s a total catastrophe! A data set is considered bimodal. And heaven forbid it’s a massive turf war where more than two distinct values share the highest frequency of occurrence—then a data set is considered multimodal. Total chaos!

You might ask, why do we even invite the Mode to the party? Because she has one glorious superpower. The mode is the only measure of central tendency applicable to categorical, non-numerical data! You try calculating the arithmetic average of "blonde," "brunette," and "redhead." You can't do it! You try lining up favorite pizza toppings from lowest to highest. It makes no sense! For pure categories, the Mode is the only royalty that reigns.

Now, sip your drink, because the Reality TV producers—the statisticians—are about to step in and manipulate the game. They decide to bless, or curse, every single person in the house equally. The math here is shockingly obedient. Whatever you do to the mob, you do to the center! 

Let's say you curve the test and give everyone 5 bonus points. Adding a constant value to every number in a data set increases the arithmetic mean by that exact constant value. It also increases the median by that exact constant value. And yes, adding a constant value to every number in a data set increases the mode by that exact constant value! The whole house just floats up. Conversely, if you dock everyone 5 points for talking during the exam, subtracting a constant value from every number in a data set decreases the arithmetic mean by that exact constant value, decreases the median by that exact constant value, and decreases the mode by that exact constant value. It’s perfect synchronization!

What if they amplify the drama and double everything? Multiplying every number in a data set by a constant value multiplies the arithmetic mean by that exact constant value, multiplies the median by that exact constant value, and multiplies the mode by that exact constant value! Everything explodes proportionally! And if you slice the scores in half? Dividing every number in a data set by a non-zero constant value divides the arithmetic mean by that exact constant value, divides the median by that exact constant value, and divides the mode by that exact constant value! Just don't divide by zero, Brenda, or the spacetime continuum collapses into your margarita.

Finally, we have to look at the shape of the drama—the grand finale of our data distribution! 

If the house is completely at peace, in a perfectly symmetrical, unimodal data distribution, the arithmetic mean, median, and mode share the exact same numerical value. They are all holding hands right at the very peak of the bell curve. Perfect, boring harmony.

But there is always a tug-of-war! Imagine a Right-Skewed data distribution. This is when a couple of outrageously high-scoring divas pull the "tail" of the graph way off to the right side. And who gets dragged by outliers? The Mean! The weak-willed Mean gets pulled toward the drama, dragging him higher than the Enforcer. Therefore, in a right-skewed data distribution, the arithmetic mean is typically greater than the median. 

And if it’s Left-Skewed? A couple of miserable, ultra-low scores drag the tail of the graph to the left. The Mean gets pulled down into the mud. So, in a left-skewed data distribution, the arithmetic mean is typically less than the median. The sturdy Median just stands there in the middle, shaking his head at the Mean's absolute lack of spine.

So there it is! Choose your reality star wisely, Brenda. Tell the principal about the Mean if you want to include everyone’s baggage, use the Median if you need a sturdy bouncer to ignore the freaks, and call on the Mode if you just want to know what’s popular. Now, let's grade these tests before happy hour ends!
