# [Praxis Core](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Praxis_test) Math Study Guide: [Correlation vs. Causation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Correlation_does_not_imply_causation)

Hello there! Let’s talk about one of the most beautiful, dangerous, and frequently misunderstood ideas in all of [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics) and everyday [logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logic). 

Imagine you are a [detective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Detective), or perhaps an [alien](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extraterrestrial_life) looking down at [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth), trying to figure out the rules of how our world operates. You start measuring things, and you notice a pattern: whenever a town has more [fire trucks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fire_engine) at a scene, there is more fire damage! You leap to a conclusion: *Aha! Fire trucks must be causing the damage!* 

Now, you and I know that’s ridiculous. But mathematical [data](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data) doesn't know it. Data just sees two numbers moving together. Understanding the difference between numbers that merely move together and numbers where one actually *forces* the other to move is the entire difference between good [science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Science) and utter nonsense. 

For the [Praxis Core Mathematics exam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Praxis_test), you must master this distinction. We are going to explore how to read these [statistical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistics) patterns, how to find the hidden tricks [nature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nature) plays on us, and how to prove what is actually causing what. Let’s dive in.

## Part 1: The Dance of Variables (Understanding [Correlation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Correlation_and_dependence))

Before we talk about cause and effect, we have to talk about [observation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observation). When we measure the world, we often look at how two things—two *[variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dependent_and_independent_variables)*—behave relative to each other. 

> **[Correlation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Correlation_and_dependence)** is a statistical relationship indicating that two variables fluctuate together. 

It simply means that when Variable A does something, Variable B tends to do something predictable, too. They are dancing to the same beat. But correlation comes in a few different flavors:

| Type of Correlation | What It Means | Real-World Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **[Positive Correlation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Correlation_and_dependence)** | A positive correlation means that as one variable increases, the other variable also increases. | As the number of hours you spend studying increases, your exam score increases. |
| **[Negative Correlation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Correlation_and_dependence)** | A negative correlation means that as one variable increases, the other variable decreases. | As the miles driven on your car's tires increase, the amount of [tread](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tire_tread) remaining on the tires decreases. |

### Visualizing the Dance: [Scatterplots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scatter_plot)
How do we actually *see* this dance? We graph it. **[Scatterplots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scatter_plot)** visually display the statistical correlation between two [quantitative variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantitative_research). By plotting one variable on the [x-axis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system) and the other on the [y-axis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system), you get a cloud of dots. 
* If the dots drift upward from left to right, you are looking at a positive correlation.
* If the dots drift downward from left to right, you have a negative correlation.
* If the dots look like a random blast from a [shotgun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shotgun), there is little to no correlation.

![A real-world scatterplot demonstrating a positive correlation. As the wait time between Old Faithful's eruptions increases along the x-axis, the duration of the eruption on the y-axis also tends to increase.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Oldfaithful3.png)

### Measuring the Dance: The [Correlation Coefficient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pearson_correlation_coefficient)
But [scientists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientist) and [mathematicians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematician) don’t just like looking at pictures; we like [precision](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accuracy_and_precision). We want to know exactly *how strong* the relationship is. 

To do this, we use a beautiful mathematical tool. **The [correlation coefficient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pearson_correlation_coefficient) mathematically measures the strength of a [linear relationship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linearity) between two variables.** 

> **The [Correlation Coefficient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pearson_correlation_coefficient) (r)** is a numerical value ranging strictly from negative one to positive one ($-1 \le r \le 1$). 

* An $r$ of **+1** means a perfect positive correlation. They move in lockstep.
* An $r$ of **-1** means a perfect negative correlation. Like a perfect [seesaw](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seesaw): one goes up, the exact other goes down.
* An $r$ of **0** means absolutely zero linear relationship. Total [chaos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Randomness).

![Different scatterplot patterns and their corresponding Pearson correlation coefficients. The top row demonstrates how the r-value indicates the strength and direction of a linear relationship.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Correlation_examples2.svg)

## Part 2: The Holy Grail (Understanding [Causation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Causality))

Now we reach the most critical rule in [statistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistics)—a rule so important it should be [tattooed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tattoo) on the arm of every [researcher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Researcher). 

> **The presence of a [correlation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Correlation_and_dependence) between two variables [does not prove that one variable causes the other to change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Correlation_does_not_imply_causation).**

Why? Because correlation is merely a description of *what* is happening. **[Causation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Causality)**, on the other hand, indicates that a change in one variable *directly produces* a specific effect in another variable. Causation is the *why*. 

If you want to argue that Variable A causes Variable B, **a strong mathematical correlation is logically insufficient on its own to justify a claim of causation.** Just because two things happen together doesn't mean one is pulling the strings. 

Let's look at the fascinating, tricky ways the [universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe) can fool us into seeing a cause where none exists.

---

## Part 3: When the Universe Lies to You (The Pitfalls)

If [correlation doesn't mean causation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Correlation_does_not_imply_causation), what else could be going on? When you see two variables highly correlated, there are three major statistical traps you must watch out for.

### Trap 1: The [Confounding Variable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confounding)
This is the most common trick in the book. **[Confounding variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confounding)** are unmeasured external factors that independently affect both of the correlated variables. 

Think of a [puppeteer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Puppetry) holding two puppets. Puppet A and Puppet B are moving in perfect harmony (a strong correlation). But Puppet A isn't moving Puppet B. The unmeasured external factor—the puppeteer—is moving *both*. **The presence of a confounding variable can create a false appearance of a direct causal relationship between two observed variables.**

![A diagram illustrating a confounding variable (Z). Variables X and Y may appear correlated in data, not because one causes the other, but because they are both independently caused by Z.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Simple_Confounding_Case.svg)

**The Classic Example:**
Let's look at real data. **[Ice cream](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ice_cream) sales and [sunburn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunburn) rates exhibit a positive statistical correlation in real-world data.** The more ice cream a city buys, the more sunburns [hospitals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hospital) treat. They rise and fall together perfectly. 

Now, imagine someone looking at this data and declaring: *"My god! Eating ice cream causes humans to burst into sunburns! We must ban ice cream!"* 

**Assuming ice cream consumption causes sunburns based on their positive correlation is a common [logical fallacy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fallacy).** Why? Because of a confounding variable. **A confounding variable like hot [weather](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weather) can independently cause increases in both ice cream sales and sunburn rates.** When it's hot outside, people buy more ice cream. When it's hot outside, people go to the [beach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beach) and get sunburnt. The heat is the hidden puppeteer driving both variables.

### Trap 2: [Reverse Causation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reverse_causality)
Sometimes there *is* a causal relationship, but you have it completely backward. **[Reverse causation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reverse_causality) occurs when the assumed direction of cause and effect between two variables is actually the opposite of reality.**

Remember our fire truck example? More fire trucks at a scene is positively correlated with more fire damage. But the trucks don't cause the damage; the size of the [fire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fire) causes the [dispatcher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dispatcher) to send more trucks. The effect is actually the cause!

### Trap 3: [Random Coincidence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coincidence)
If you have enough data, the universe will occasionally throw you a curveball that means absolutely nothing at all. **Two entirely unrelated variables can exhibit a strong statistical correlation purely by [random coincidence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spurious_relationship).**

Statistical researchers have famously pointed out that the [per capita](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Per_capita) consumption of [cheese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cheese) in the [United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States) has mathematically correlated with the number of people who died by becoming tangled in their [bedsheets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bed_sheet). Does eating cheese make your sheets hostile? No! If you measure millions of random variables over time, just by sheer mathematical [luck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luck), some of them will line up perfectly. It is pure, glorious nonsense.

## Part 4: The Detective's Toolkit (How We Know for Sure)

So, if correlation doesn't prove causation, how does a scientist or a mathematician *ever* prove that one thing causes another? We have to rely on the right tools. We must understand the profound difference between just looking at the world, and actively poking it.

### [Observational Studies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observational_study): Looking, Not Touching
**[Observational studies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observational_study) gather data without manipulating the variables.** You just sit back with your [clipboard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clipboard) and record what is naturally happening in the world. 

* **What they CAN do:** **Observational studies can establish mathematical correlations between variables.** They are great for finding patterns, like the link between [smoking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tobacco_smoking) habits and [lung capacity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lung_volumes) over decades. 
* **What they CANNOT do:** Because you are just watching, you aren't controlling for hidden puppeteers. Therefore, **observational studies cannot definitively establish causation between variables.**

### [Controlled Experiments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_control): Poking the Universe
If you want to prove causation, you have to get your hands dirty. **[Controlled experiments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_control) measure the effect of an actively manipulated [independent variable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dependent_and_independent_variables) on a [dependent variable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dependent_and_independent_variables).** 

You don't just ask people if they eat ice cream and measure their sunburns. You take two groups of people in a windowless room. You forcefully feed one group ice cream (manipulating the independent variable) and give the other group nothing. Then you shine identical [UV lights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultraviolet) on them to measure sunburns (the dependent variable). Because *you* controlled the environment, the weather can no longer act as a confounding variable!

The [gold standard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gold_standard_%28test%29) of this process relies on [randomness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Randomness). **[Randomized controlled experiments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Randomized_controlled_trial) are the primary statistical method used to establish true causation between variables.** By [randomly assigning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Random_assignment) subjects to different groups, you mathematically ensure that all the hidden confounding variables (like [genetics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genetics), [diet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diet_%28nutrition%29), [sleep](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sleep)) are evenly distributed, isolating the pure causal effect of your test variable.

![A standard flowchart for a randomized controlled trial. By randomly allocating subjects into an intervention group and a control group, researchers can control for outside variables and definitively test for causation.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Flowchart_of_Phases_of_Parallel_Randomized_Trial_-_Modified_from_CONSORT_2010.png)

## Part 5: Passing the Praxis (Evaluating Scenarios)

On the Praxis Core exam, you won't just be asked for definitions. You will be given real-world scenarios and asked to evaluate them. 

When you are reading a test question where someone claims "Variable A causes Variable B," put on your detective hat. **Evaluating a scenario for causation requires identifying [alternative explanations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alternative_hypothesis) for the observed relationship.** 

Ask yourself:
1. *Is there a [confounding variable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confounding)?* (What else could cause both of these to happen?)
2. *Could the causation be reversed?* (Is B actually causing A?)
3. *Was this an [observational study](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observational_study), or a [randomized controlled experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Randomized_controlled_trial)?*

To legitimately claim causation, you can't just find an alternative explanation; you have to destroy it. **Evaluating a scenario for causation requires ruling out alternative explanations for the observed relationship.** If an experiment doesn't rule out the confounding variables, then the causal claim is unjustified.

### Summary Checklist for Exam Day:
* **Fluctuate together?** That's [correlation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Correlation_and_dependence).
* **Directly produces an effect?** That's [causation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Causality). 
* **Just a strong math link?** Not enough to prove causation.
* **Look but don't touch?** [Observational study](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observational_study) (correlation only).
* **Actively manipulate and randomize?** [Controlled experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_control) (proves causation).

The universe is full of fascinating patterns. As an [educator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Educator) and a mathematical thinker, your job is to look past the superficial dance of the numbers and uncover the hidden truth of *why* things happen. When you see a correlation, admire it—but demand [proof](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_proof) before you call it a cause. Good luck!
