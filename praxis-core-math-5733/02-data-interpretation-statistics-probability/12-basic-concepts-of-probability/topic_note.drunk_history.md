You see, probability isn’t just some psychic parlor trick; probability is a mathematical measure of the likelihood that a specific event will occur. It’s the raw, unfiltered Vegas odds of reality. It’s how we measure the exact chance of my sister’s latest terrible boyfriend, Chad, ruining Thanksgiving. 

To figure out the odds of Thanksgiving being ruined, we first have to map out the battleground. Every time we observe a situation, we are conducting a statistical experiment. The absolute boundaries of this experiment—every single thing that could possibly happen—is called the sample space. The sample space is the complete set of all possible distinct outcomes of an experiment. It is the entire sprawling buffet of disasters. If a scenario isn’t on the menu of our sample space, it literally cannot happen in our universe. Inside this kitchen of chaos, we are usually looking for a specific piece of reality TV drama to unfold. That’s our event. An event is a defined set of outcomes resulting from a statistical experiment. 

Now, let’s say the event we are watching for is "Chad knocks over the gravy boat." The specific outcomes inside our sample space that make this happen are what we call favorable outcomes. Favorable outcomes are the specific outcomes from a sample space that satisfy the defined conditions of a target event. Now don't get it twisted—"favorable" doesn't mean it’s a *good* thing. Getting coated in hot turkey grease is a tragedy! But mathematically, it’s "favorable" because it’s the exact target mob hit we are looking to track. And if our event boils down to just one single, solitary, isolated thing happening—like specifically Uncle Leo taking the gravy to the face, and nothing else—that is a simple event. A simple event is a mathematical event that consists of exactly one single outcome. Just Leo. Bam.

So, how do we calculate the chance of the gravy hit? We use theoretical probability! It’s the golden rule of the universe. The theoretical probability of an event equals the number of favorable outcomes divided by the total number of possible outcomes. Favorable divided by total. Boom. But hold on, put your drink down, because there is a massive catch! This formula only works if the universe isn't rigging the game. Theoretical probability requires the assumption that all possible outcomes in a given sample space are equally likely to occur. If Chad bribed the waiter, or if the floor is tilted, the math falls apart because the outcomes aren't equally likely anymore!

And let me tell you about the absolute laws of physics here. You can’t have a negative chance of a breakup, and you can’t give 110% outside of a sports movie! The probability of any event is mathematically restricted to a numerical value between 0 and 1, inclusive. If an event has a probability of exactly 0, it is impossible and cannot occur. Like my ex apologizing. Zero. Never happening. On the flip side, an event with a probability of exactly 1 is absolutely certain to occur. Like me crying when listening to sad pop music on the drive home. Certain! Because the value is trapped between 0 and 1, a numerical probability value can be mathematically represented as a fraction, like one-half. It can be mathematically represented as a decimal, like zero-point-five. And it can be mathematically represented as a percentage, like fifty percent! It’s all the same drama, just wearing different outfits.

Here is the most beautiful part. If you take every possible disaster that could happen at Thanksgiving and add up their probabilities, the sum of the individual probabilities of all distinct possible outcomes within a single sample space equals exactly 1. Total, 100% certainty that *something* from the sample space will happen! And because of that perfect balance, we have a shortcut called the complement. The complement of an event includes every possible outcome in the sample space that does not belong to the target event. It’s the anti-event! It’s the Yin to the Yang! If the target event is "Chad ruins dinner," the complement is literally every other scenario where dinner is saved. Because everything must add up to 1, the mathematical probability of the complement of an event equals 1 minus the probability of the target event occurring.

But wait! Theoretical math is just what *should* happen in a perfect world. We live in the mud, my friend! What happens when we actually get our hands dirty? That’s experimental probability! You divide the 80 collapsed souffles by the 100 total trials. The kitchen doesn't lie!

And what if our sample space isn't made of countable things like cards, coins, or souffles? What if the disaster takes up physical space? Like, what if you drop a giant pot of marinara sauce on the floor? We use geometric probability! Geometric probability calculates likelihood by dividing a specific target geometric area by the total available area. If your priceless white rug is 10 square feet, and your total kitchen floor is 100 square feet, the chance of a randomly flying meatball hitting the rug is 10 divided by 100! But what if the disaster is linear? Like a tightrope walk! Geometric probability calculates likelihood by dividing a specific target geometric length by the total available length. If a tightrope is 50 feet long, and your enemies greased a 5-foot section of it with butter, the chance of you randomly stepping on the buttered section is 5 divided by 50!
