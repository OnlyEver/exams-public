Think of a stem-and-leaf plot as the FBI’s ultimate wiretap interrogation table. It displays quantitative data by splitting each numerical value right down the middle into a stem and a leaf. In this gritty underworld, the stem represents the leading digit or digits of a numerical data value. These are your mob bosses, the heavyweights holding down the territory. Meanwhile, the leaf represents the final, trailing digit of a numerical data value. These are the low-level foot soldiers doing the dirty work out on the street. 

Why did Tukey love this method so much? Because a stem-and-leaf plot preserves the exact individual data points from the original dataset! Unlike a bar chart that throws everyone into a giant, anonymous prison cell where you can't tell who is who, here, nobody loses their identity. Count the rap sheet, and you'll realize each individual leaf in a stem-and-leaf plot represents exactly one data point in the dataset. One leaf, one suspect. Period. And these criminals are strictly disciplined. To keep the family organized, the leaves in a stem-and-leaf plot are listed in ascending numerical order outward from the central stem. The foot soldiers literally line up by rank, smallest to largest, radiating outward from their boss!

But wait, if you find a smuggled ledger that just says "Stem 4" and "Leaf 2," what does that actually mean? Are they moving 4.2 million dollars, or 42 crates of stolen ziti? You have no idea unless you have the wiretap decoder! A stem-and-leaf plot requires a key to explain the specific place value of the stem and the leaf. Reconstructing a specific data point from a stem-and-leaf plot requires combining the stem with one corresponding leaf according to the plot's key. If the key says "4 | 2 equals 42," boom, you've reconstructed the exact value of the crime! And here is my absolute favorite trick: imagine grabbing that entire table of mobsters and tipping it over in a bar brawl. When a stem-and-leaf plot is rotated 90 degrees counterclockwise, the plot's shape visually resembles a histogram! You can't track ten thousand individual street thugs with a stem and a leaf. You need the big picture, the brutal, high-level corporate summary of the syndicate. Enter the boxplot. A boxplot is a graphical representation of a dataset based on a five-number summary. Because of its sprawling, gangly shape reaching across the page, a boxplot is also commonly referred to as a box-and-whisker plot—like some sort of villainous mastermind's pet cat. 

This five-number summary is the VIP list of the organization. It consists of the minimum, first quartile, median, third quartile, and maximum values of a dataset. Let's talk about the absolute dead center of this criminal empire: the median. The median represents the 50th percentile of an ordered dataset. It’s the Godfather. Exactly half the syndicate is below him, half is above him. In a boxplot, a vertical line drawn inside the rectangular box marks the median value of the dataset, dividing the turf right down the middle. 

Then you have his underbosses. The first quartile represents the 25th percentile of an ordered dataset—the capo running the lower ranks. The third quartile represents the 75th percentile of an ordered dataset—the elite capo running the upper echelon. Now, you lock these bosses in a room. In a boxplot, a rectangular box is drawn spanning from the first quartile to the third quartile. This central box contains the middle 50 percent of the dataset's values. It is the absolute thunderdome of the organization, the core where all the real heavy-hitting drama happens! The sheer size of that room matters immensely. The length of the rectangular box in a boxplot represents the interquartile range of the dataset. You figure out just how much turf this core group controls because the interquartile range is calculated by subtracting the first quartile value from the third quartile value. You take Q3, rip away Q1, and whatever is left is your prime real estate.

But what about the rest of the gang? Outward from this thunderdome, lines called whiskers extend outward from the central box to the minimum and maximum values. These whiskers are the long arms of the mob, reaching all the way down to the lowest street hustler (the minimum) and all the way up to the highest-earning associate (the maximum).

We don't want his psychotic behavior warping our map of the family's normal turf. So, statisticians handle him like bouncers. Some boxplots use individual dots or asterisks plotted beyond the whiskers to represent outlier data points. We literally kick them out of the main visual! When you exile them like that, what happens to the arms of the organization? When a boxplot includes outlier dots, the whiskers extend only to the lowest and highest data points that are not mathematically considered outliers. The whiskers shrink back to the last sane, predictable criminal in the lineup!

Finally, how do you know if the whole syndicate is fundamentally lopsided? You look for skewness! A skewed data distribution is visually indicated in a boxplot when the median line is noticeably closer to one end of the central box than the other. If the Godfather is huddling way up against the third quartile, the power dynamic is totally warped! And you can see it on the streets too. An asymmetrical whisker length in a boxplot indicates that the data distribution is skewed.
