# The [Mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanics) of [Mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics): Understanding How [Numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number) Behave

Hello there! Let's talk about [math](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics). 

Too often, [math](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics) is taught like a dusty old law book filled with complicated Latin names you're supposed to memorize. Commutative this, [associative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associative_property) that. Let me tell you a secret: **nature doesn't care about the names.** The [universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe) doesn't know what "[distributive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distributive_property)" means. 

What matters—what *truly* matters, especially for the [Praxis Core exam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Praxis_test)—is understanding **how the [numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number) behave**. [Math](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics) is just a game of patterns, and once you understand the rules of the game, you can play it beautifully. You don't need to memorize a dictionary to understand that if you have three [apples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apple) and I give you two more, it's the exact same as if you had two [apples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apple) and I gave you three. The result is five [apples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apple) either way. 

Today, we are going to look under the hood of basic operations. We’re going to play with [addition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Addition), [subtraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subtraction), [multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication), and [division](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_%28mathematics%29). By the end of this, you won't just be [calculating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculation); you'll be *thinking* in [math](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics).

## Part 1: The Fast-Forward Button (What is [Multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication), Really?)

Before we start moving [numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number) around, we have to agree on what our tools actually do. We all understand [addition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Addition). It’s [counting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Counting) forward. But what is [multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication)? 

Some people think [multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication) is a completely different beast, a whole new set of rules. It isn't! **[Multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication) represents the [mathematical process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics) of repeatedly [adding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Addition) a specific [number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number) a specified amount of times.** It is simply a fast-forward button for [addition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Addition). 

> **Fact to remember:** The [mathematical expression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expression_%28mathematics%29) **5 times 4** represents [adding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Addition) the [number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number) 5 four separate times. 

Instead of exhaustively writing out $5 + 5 + 5 + 5 = 20$, some incredibly clever human centuries ago decided, "Let's just write $5 \times 4$ and save some ink." That's all it is! Whenever you get lost in an [algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra) problem, remind yourself of this simple truth: [multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication) is just repeated addition in a clever disguise.

## Part 2: The Freedom of Movement (Swapping and Grouping)

Now, let's talk about the physical space of a [math](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics) problem. Can you move the furniture around without breaking the house? 

### The Swapping Rule
Imagine I hand you a [\$10 bill](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_ten-dollar_bill) and then a \$5 bill. You have $15. 
Now imagine I hand you a $5 bill first, and then a [\$10 bill](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_ten-dollar_bill). You still have \$15! 

**Swapping the order of [numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number) in an [addition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Addition) problem does not change the final [sum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Summation).** 

Because [multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication) is just repeated addition, it shares this wonderful flexibility. If $5 \times 4$ is five added four times (20), what is $4 \times 5$? It's four added five times ($4 + 4 + 4 + 4 + 4 = 20$). The result is exactly the same! **Swapping the order of [numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number) in a [multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication) problem does not change the final [product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Product_%28mathematics%29).** 

But watch out! This freedom doesn't apply to everything. What happens if we mess with [subtraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subtraction) or [division](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_%28mathematics%29)? 

If you have \$10 and you owe me \$2 ($10 - 2$), you have \$8 left. But what if we swap it? What is \$2 - 10$? Now you are \$8 in [debt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Debt) (-\$8). The results are entirely different. Similarly, \$10 \div 2 = 5$, but \$2 \div 10 = 0.2$. 

Let's summarize this reality:

| Operation | Can I swap the order of the numbers? | Result |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **[Addition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Addition) (+)** | YES | Unchanged |
| **[Multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication) ($\times$)** | YES | Unchanged |
| **[Subtraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subtraction) (-)** | **NO** | **Changes the final result** |
| **[Division](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_%28mathematics%29) ($\div$)** | **NO** | **Changes the final result** |

### The Grouping Rule (The "[VIP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Very_important_person) Section")
In [math](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics), parentheses `( )` are like the [VIP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Very_important_person) section at a party. They tell you, "Do the [math](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics) in here *first*." But does the [VIP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Very_important_person) section actually matter for the final outcome?

Imagine you are [adding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Addition) up the cost of a [burger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamburger) (\$5), [fries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_fries) (\$3), and a [soda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soft_drink) (\$2). 
Does it matter if you add the [burger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamburger) and [fries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_fries) first, and then the [soda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soft_drink)? 
$(5 + 3) + 2 = 8 + 2 = 10$

What if you add the [fries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_fries) and [soda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soft_drink) first, and then the [burger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamburger)?
$5 + (3 + 2) = 5 + 5 = 10$

**Changing the grouping of [numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number) being [added](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Addition) together does not change the final [sum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Summation).** 

Once again, [multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication) plays by the same relaxed rules. **Changing the grouping of [numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number) being [multiplied](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication) together does not change the final [product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Product_%28mathematics%29).** $(2 \times 3) \times 4$ is exactly the same as $2 \times (3 \times 4)$. Both equal 24.

But what about our grumpy friends, [Subtraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subtraction) and [Division](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_%28mathematics%29)? 
Let's try grouping [subtraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subtraction): 
$10 - (5 - 2)$ means we do $5 - 2$ first, which is $3$. Then $10 - 3 = 7$.
Now let's change the group: 
$(10 - 5) - 2$ means we do $10 - 5$ first, which is $5$. Then $5 - 2 = 3$. 
Seven does not equal three! 

**Changing the grouping of [numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number) in a [subtraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subtraction) problem changes the final result.** And exactly the same goes for [division](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_%28mathematics%29): **Changing the grouping of [numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number) in a [division](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_%28mathematics%29) problem changes the final result.**

You don't need to know the official names (commutative and [associative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associative_property)). Just intuitively remember: **[Addition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Addition) and [Multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication) are free and flexible. [Subtraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subtraction) and [Division](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_%28mathematics%29) are rigid and strict.**

## Part 3: The Delivery Driver (The Art of [Distributing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distributive_property))

Now we get to one of the most powerful tools in [algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra): [Distribution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distributive_property). 

Imagine you are a teacher handing out supplies. You have a bag containing 1 pencil and 2 [erasers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eraser). If I tell you to give a bag to 3 different students, you have to [multiply](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication) *everything* inside the bag by 3. You don't just give out 3 pencils and ignore the [erasers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eraser)!

In [math](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics), when a [number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number) sits directly outside a parenthesis, it is waiting to be handed out ([multiplied](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication)) to *everything* inside. 

**[Distributing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distributive_property) a [multiplier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication) across a grouped [sum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Summation) means [multiplying](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication) each grouped term by the [multiplier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication) before [calculating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculation) the final [addition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Addition).**
If you see **$4(x + 3)$**, you hand the 4 out to the $x$, and you hand the 4 out to the $3$:
*   $4 \times x = 4x$
*   $4 \times 3 = 12$
*   Result: **$4x + 12$**

The same exact logic applies to [subtraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subtraction). **[Distributing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distributive_property) a [multiplier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication) across a grouped difference means [multiplying](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication) each grouped term by the [multiplier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication) before [calculating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculation) the final [subtraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subtraction).**
If you see **$5(y - 2)$**, you [multiply](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication) the 5 by the $y$, and the 5 by the $2$, keeping the [subtraction sign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plus_and_minus_signs) intact:
*   Result: **$5y - 10$**

### The "Negative Ninja" (Warning!)
There is a trap hidden in the [distributive rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distributive_property), and the test-makers *love* to see if you fall for it. What happens if the delivery driver outside the parentheses is a [negative sign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plus_and_minus_signs)?

A [negative sign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plus_and_minus_signs) is basically a command that says: "Do the exact opposite." 

> **Crucial Rule:** **[Multiplying](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication) a [negative sign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plus_and_minus_signs) across terms inside parentheses reverses the [mathematical sign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sign_%28mathematics%29) of every individual term inside the parentheses.**

If you have **$-(2x + 4)$**, that [negative sign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plus_and_minus_signs) hits the positive $2x$ and turns it negative ($-2x$). It hits the positive $4$ and turns it negative ($-4$). 
Result: **$-2x - 4$**

If you have **$-(3y - 7)$**, the negative hits the $3y$ to make it $-3y$. But when it hits the $-7$, the "opposite of an opposite" becomes a positive! It flips to $+7$. 
Result: **$-3y + 7$**

Never let the negative ninja sneak past you. When a [minus sign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plus_and_minus_signs) is outside the brackets, *everything* inside flips!

## Part 4: The Ghost [Numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number) ([Zero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/0) and [One](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1))

In the [universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe) of [numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number), [0](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/0) and [1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1) have magical properties. They act like ghosts, slipping through [equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equation) and leaving things untouched, or occasionally annihilating things entirely. Let's look at how they behave.

### The Magic of [Zero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/0)
[Zero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/0) is the ultimate symbol of "nothing." 
If you have a million dollars and I give you [zero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/0) dollars, you still have a million dollars. **[Adding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Addition) [zero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/0) to any [numerical value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Value_%28mathematics%29) leaves the original value completely unchanged.**

But what happens when you *[multiply](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication)* by nothing? Remember our rule: [multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication) is repeated addition. If I give you [zero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/0) dollars, five million times in a row... how much money do you have? You're still broke! **[Multiplying](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication) any [numerical value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Value_%28mathematics%29) by [zero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/0) always results in [zero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/0).** It is the great [eraser](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eraser). 

### The Magic of [One](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1)
While [zero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/0) leaves [addition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Addition) untouched, the [number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number) *[one](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1)* leaves [multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication) untouched.
If I tell you to take the [number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number) 85 and [multiply](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication) it by [1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1), you are taking "85, [one](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1) time." 
**[Multiplying](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication) any [numerical value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Value_%28mathematics%29) by the [number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number) [one](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1) leaves the original value completely unchanged.**

This ghost-like behavior also works in [division](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_%28mathematics%29). If you have 10 slices of [pizza](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pizza) and you [divide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_%28mathematics%29) them evenly among [1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1) person (just yourself), you get all 10 slices. **[Dividing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_%28mathematics%29) any [number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number) by the [number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number) [one](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1) results in the original unchanged [number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number).**

But [division](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_%28mathematics%29) has [one](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1) more beautiful trick. What happens if you [divide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_%28mathematics%29) a [number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number) by *itself*? If you have 10 slices of [pizza](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pizza) and 10 people at the party, everybody gets exactly [1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1) slice. No matter how large or strange the [number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number) is—even a [billion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1%2C000%2C000%2C000), or $x$, or 3.14—**[dividing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_%28mathematics%29) any non-zero [number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number) by itself always results in the [number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number) [one](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1).**

## Part 5: Wrangling the Alphabet (Combining Like Terms)

Finally, we arrive at [Algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra). People get nervous when letters start showing up in [math](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics), but a letter (a [variable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variable_%28mathematics%29)) is just an empty box waiting for a [number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number) to be put inside. 

Imagine you are doing [inventory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inventory) at a fruit stand. You have [apples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apple) ($A$) and bananas ($B$). 
If you have 3 [apples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apple) and I give you 4 more [apples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apple), how many [apples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apple) do you have? You have 7 [apples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apple). 
Mathematically, $3A + 4A = 7A$. 

But what if you have 3 [apples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apple) and I give you 4 *bananas*? Do you have 7 "apple-bananas"? No! You just have 3 [apples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apple) and 4 bananas. You can't combine them into one word. 

This is the [golden rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden_Rule) of [algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra): **Terms in an algebraic expression can only be [added](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Addition) together if the terms share the exact same [variable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variable_%28mathematics%29) letters.**
*   $5x + 2x = 7x$ (Valid!)
*   $5x + 2y$ = Cannot be combined. They just stare at each other.

But the rule goes even deeper. The letters must not only be the same; their *[exponents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponentiation)* (the tiny [numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number) up top) must also be identical! An $x^2$ is fundamentally a different shape than an $x$. If $x$ is a [line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_%28geometry%29), $x^2$ is a square. You can't just mash [lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_%28geometry%29) and squares together. 

**Terms in an algebraic expression can only be [added](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Addition) together if the terms share the exact same [variable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variable_%28mathematics%29) [exponents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponentiation).**
*   $4x^2 + 3x^2 = 7x^2$ (Valid! We are combining squares with squares.)
*   $4x^2 + 3x$ = Cannot be combined. (You can't add squares to [lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_%28geometry%29).)

## Putting It All Together: Thinking Like a [Mathematician](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematician)

Let's look at a classic [Praxis Core](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Praxis_test) problem and tear it apart using nothing but the intuitive rules we just learned. 

**Simplify the [expression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expression_%28mathematics%29): $3(2x^2 + 4x - 1) - 2x^2 + 5x(1)$**

At first glance, it looks like a mess of [numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number) and letters. But you know exactly how these [gears](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gear) turn. 

**Step 1: The Delivery Driver ([Distribute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distributive_property))**
We have a $3$ sitting outside the parentheses. We must hand it out to *every* term inside. 
*   $3 \times 2x^2 = 6x^2$
*   $3 \times 4x = 12x$
*   $3 \times -1 = -3$
Now our [expression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expression_%28mathematics%29) is: **$6x^2 + 12x - 3 - 2x^2 + 5x(1)$**

**Step 2: The Ghost [Number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number) ([Multiply](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication) by [1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1))**
Look at the end: $5x(1)$. We know that [multiplying](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication) any [numerical value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Value_%28mathematics%29) by [one](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1) leaves the original value unchanged. So $5x(1)$ is just $5x$. 
Now our [expression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expression_%28mathematics%29) is: **$6x^2 + 12x - 3 - 2x^2 + 5x$**

**Step 3: The Freedom to Swap (Rearrange)**
Because [addition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Addition) is flexible (and [subtraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subtraction) can be viewed as [adding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Addition) a negative), we can swap the order to put the similar shapes next to each other. Let's group the $x^2$'s together, the $x$'s together, and the plain [numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number) together.
**$6x^2 - 2x^2 + 12x + 5x - 3$**

**Step 4: Wrangel the Alphabet (Combine Like Terms)**
We can only [add](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Addition) or [subtract](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subtraction) terms with the *exact same [variable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variable_%28mathematics%29) letters and exact same [exponents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponentiation)*.
*   Combine the $x^2$ terms: $6x^2 - 2x^2 = 4x^2$
*   Combine the $x$ terms: $12x + 5x = 17x$
*   The $-3$ has no friends to combine with, so it stays as is.

**Final Answer: $4x^2 + 17x - 3$**

Look at that! You didn't recite "The Commutative Property of Addition" or "The Multiplicative Identity Property." You just remembered that you can swap things around, that [multiplying](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication) by [one](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1) does nothing, that you have to hand out the delivery to everyone in the brackets, and that you can't add [apples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apple) to bananas. 

[Math](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics) isn't a [dead language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinct_language) to be memorized. It's a living [machine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Machine). Once you understand how the parts move, you can build anything. Good luck on the [Praxis Core](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Praxis_test)!
