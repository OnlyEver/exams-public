[Mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics) is, at its core, a [language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formal_language) of unparalleled [precision](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accuracy_and_precision). When we look at a [real-world problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_model)—calculating the [cost](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cost) of a fleet of buses, [distributing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resource_allocation) supplies among students, or determining the necessary [speed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed) to reach a destination on time—we are faced with a messy [paragraph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paragraph) of [human language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_language). The task of the [algebraist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra) is to act as a [translator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Translation), stripping away the [ambiguity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ambiguity) of [conversational English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colloquialism) and mapping it onto the crisp, unforgiving architecture of [mathematical symbols](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_notation). 

![Mathematics operates as a formal language. Just as English grammar distinguishes between proper sentences and nonsense, algebra distinguishes between well-formed logical formulas and invalid mathematical statements.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Formal_languages.svg)

This [translation process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Translation) requires understanding not only the [vocabulary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vocabulary) of [mathematical operations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_%28mathematics%29) but also the [grammatical structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grammar) of [mathematical logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_logic). To succeed on the [Praxis Core Mathematics exam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Praxis_test), you must learn to read an [English sentence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_language) and write an [algebraic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra) [truth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Truth_value).

## The Basic [Vocabulary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vocabulary) of [Algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra)

Before we can build complex sentences, we must define our [parts of speech](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Part_of_speech). In [algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra), our primary tool for representing the unknown is the **[variable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variable_%28mathematics%29)**. A variable is a [symbol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symbol)—typically a letter like $x$, $y$, or $n$—used to represent an unknown [numerical value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Value_%28mathematics%29) in an [algebraic expression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_expression) or [equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equation). 

[Variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variable_%28mathematics%29) combine with [numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number) and [operations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_%28mathematics%29) to form the two foundational [structures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_structure) of algebra, which are fundamentally different in [mathematical grammar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formal_grammar):

![The anatomical parts of an algebraic equation. Variables act as placeholders for unknown quantities, combining with numerical coefficients, constants, and operators to form mathematical phrases.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Algebraic_equation_notation.svg)

> **[Algebraic Expression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_expression):** An algebraic expression is a [mathematical phrase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expression_%28mathematics%29) containing numbers, variables, and operation symbols *without* an [equals sign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equals_sign). Think of an [expression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expression_%28mathematics%29) as a mathematical [fragment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sentence_fragment) or phrase (e.g., $3x + 5$). It has a [value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Value_%28mathematics%29), but it does not make a [claim](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statement_%28logic%29).

> **[Algebraic Equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equation):** An algebraic equation is a [mathematical statement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statement_%28logic%29) asserting [equality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equality_%28mathematics%29) between two algebraic expressions. The inclusion of the equals sign makes it a complete sentence, proposing that the expression on the left balances perfectly with the expression on the right (e.g., $3x + 5 = 14$).

![An algebraic equation acts like a physical balance scale. The equals sign serves as the verb "to be," asserting that the mathematical expression on the left balances perfectly in value with the expression on the right.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Balance_scale.svg)

## The [Rosetta Stone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rosetta_Stone) of [Operations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_%28mathematics%29)

To [translate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Translation) real-life scenarios into expressions and equations, we must map specific [English words](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_language) to mathematical operations. 

![Just as the Rosetta Stone was the historical key to translating ancient Egyptian hieroglyphs, memorizing how specific English vocabulary maps to mathematical operations is the key to decoding algebraic word problems.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Rosetta_Stone_BW.jpeg)

### [Addition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Addition) and [Subtraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subtraction)
Addition is the act of combining. The word **"[sum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Summation)"** indicates the mathematical operation of addition. Similarly, the phrases **"more than"** and **"increased by"** indicate the mathematical operation of addition. 

Subtraction is the act of removing or finding a gap. The word **"[difference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Difference_%28mathematics%29)"** indicates the mathematical operation of subtraction, as do the phrases **"decreased by"** and **"less than."** 

However, subtraction introduces a critical complication that addition lacks: *[order matters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutativity)*. While $5 + 3$ is exactly the same as $3 + 5$, $5 - 3$ is profoundly different from $3 - 5$. [English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_language) often tries to trick us here. 
* The phrase **"subtracted from"** reverses the standard [left-to-right](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Order_of_operations) order of [operands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operand) in a subtraction expression. If a problem says "5 subtracted from $x$," you do not write $5 - x$; you must write $x - 5$.
* Similarly, the [verbal phrase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phrase) **"x less than y"** translates algebraically to the expression $y - x$. The [quantity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantity) $x$ is being taken away from the starting quantity, $y$. 

### [Multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication) and [Division](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_%28mathematics%29)
Multiplication represents [repeated addition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication_and_repeated_addition) or [scaling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_multiplication). The word **"[product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Product_%28mathematics%29)"** indicates the mathematical operation of multiplication, as does the word **"times."** Often, we encounter specific scaling words: the word **"twice"** indicates the mathematical operation of multiplying a quantity by the [number two](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2). 

One of the most easily missed translations involves [fractions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fraction) and [percentages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Percentage). The word **"of"** immediately following a fraction or percentage indicates the mathematical operation of multiplication. For instance, "[one-half](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One_half) *of* the students" translates to $\frac{1}{2}s$, and "20% *of* the [budget](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Budget)" translates to $0.20b$.

Division is the act of [partitioning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partition_of_a_set). The word **"[quotient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quotient)"** indicates the mathematical operation of division. You will also see the phrase **"divided by."** In many [scientific](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Science) or comparative contexts, the word **"[ratio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ratio)"** typically indicates the mathematical operation of division (the ratio of $a$ to $b$ is $\frac{a}{b}$).

### The [Verb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Verb) "To Be": [Equality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equality_%28mathematics%29)
If expressions are phrases, equations are complete sentences, and every sentence needs a verb. In algebraic translation, the word **"is"** translates to an [equals sign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equals_sign) in an algebraic equation. Action verbs do this as well: the phrase **"yields"** translates to an equals sign in an algebraic equation, and the phrase **"results in"** also translates to an equals sign.

*Example: "The product of twice a number and three yields twelve."*
[Translation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Translation): $2x \cdot 3 = 12$.

![The first recorded use of the equals sign in 1557 by Welsh mathematician Robert Recorde. He chose two parallel lines of the same length because "no two things can be more equal," providing mathematical translation with its most essential verb.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/First_Equation_Ever.png)

## [Syntax](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syntax) and [Grammar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grammar): Grouping [Operations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_%28mathematics%29)

In English, we use [punctuation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Punctuation) like [commas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comma) to clarify meaning. "Let's eat, Grandma!" means something very different from "Let's eat Grandma!" In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics), we use [grouping symbols](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket) to clarify the [order of operations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Order_of_operations). 

**[Parentheses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket)** are used in algebraic expressions to group operations that must be performed first according to the standard order of operations. 

![Grouping symbols like parentheses explicitly dictate the order of operations. They override the default left-to-right calculation rules to ensure the math accurately reflects the intended grammatical structure of the English phrase.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Order_of_operations.svg)

Consider the verbal phrase: **"the sum of x and y, multiplied by z."** 
If we simply wrote $x + y \cdot z$, the rigid rules of algebra ([PEMDAS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Order_of_operations)) would force us to multiply $y$ and $z$ first, which is incorrect based on the English phrasing. Therefore, this phrase requires placing the sum of $x$ and $y$ inside parentheses before multiplying the result by $z$. 

*Correct translation:* $(x + y)z$.

## [Inequalities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inequality_%28mathematics%29): Setting [Boundaries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Upper_and_lower_bounds)

Not every mathematical sentence asserts perfect equality. Often, the real world deals with boundaries and [limits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limit_%28mathematics%29). 

![Unlike equations that pinpoint a single exact solution, inequalities establish boundaries. They define a "feasible region" (shown here in green) of multiple possible values that satisfy the required conditions.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Linear_Programming_Feasible_Region.svg)

We must be extremely careful to distinguish between subtraction and inequality. We learned earlier that "less than" means subtraction. But the addition of the verb "is" changes everything. The phrase **"is less than"** indicates a mathematical inequality rather than an operation of subtraction. 
* "$5$ less than $x$" $\rightarrow x - 5$
* "$5$ is less than $x$" $\rightarrow 5 < x$

Many practical problems involve [minimums](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxima_and_minima) or [maximums](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxima_and_minima). 
* The phrase **"at least"** translates to a [greater-than-or-equal-to symbol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inequality_%28mathematics%29) ($\ge$) in an algebraic inequality. If a [theater](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theater) must sell *at least* 50 tickets to [break even](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Break-even_%28economics%29), then tickets sold $t \ge 50$.
* The phrase **"at most"** translates to a [less-than-or-equal-to symbol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inequality_%28mathematics%29) ($\le$) in an algebraic inequality. If an [elevator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elevator) can hold *at most* 10 people, then occupants $p \le 10$.

## [Modeling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_model) the Real World

With our vocabulary and grammar established, we can now model classic scenarios tested on the Praxis Core. 

### [Linear Cost Models](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_equation)
Imagine you are renting a [banquet hall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Banquet). There is usually a flat reservation fee, plus a per-plate charge for the food. This is a classic [linear model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_function).

*   **[Fixed costs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fixed_cost)** in a real-world linear model are represented by the *[constant term](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constant_term)* in an algebraic equation. This cost exists no matter how many items you produce or people you feed.
*   **[Variable costs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variable_cost)** in a real-world linear model are represented by a *[numerical coefficient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coefficient) multiplied by a variable*.
*   **[Total cost](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Total_cost)** in a linear algebraic model is calculated by adding the fixed cost to the product of the variable rate and the number of items.

![In a linear cost model, the total cost is calculated by adding the unchangeable fixed cost to the variable cost, which scales upward depending on the total quantity produced.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/CVP-TC-FC-VC.svg)

> **[Linear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_function) Cost [Formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formula):** $Total = ([Rate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rate_%28mathematics%29) \cdot Items) + Fixed Cost$
> *Example:* A phone plan costs \$40 a month plus \$0.10 per minute. The total cost for $m$ minutes is $C = 0.10m + 40$.

### [Consecutive Integers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer)
[Number-theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_theory) problems often ask you to represent [sequences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sequence) of numbers that follow one another. 
*   **Consecutive integers** are represented algebraically by a sequence such as $x, x + 1, x + 2$, and so forth. If the first integer is 5, the next is $5+1=6$, then $5+2=7$.
*   **Consecutive [even](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parity_%28mathematics%29) integers** skip by twos (e.g., 8, 10, 12). They are represented algebraically by a sequence such as $x, x + 2, x + 4$. 
*   **Consecutive [odd](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parity_%28mathematics%29) integers** (e.g., 7, 9, 11) are *also* represented algebraically by a sequence such as $x, x + 2, x + 4$. Why? Because if $x$ is an odd number (like 7), you must still add 2 to leap over the even number in between to get to the next odd number (9).

### [Kinematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematics): [Distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distance), [Rate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rate_%28mathematics%29), and [Time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time)
The [physical motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Motion_%28physics%29) of [objects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_object) moving at a [constant speed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed) follows a beautiful, simple law. **Distance traveled** is modeled by the algebraic equation representing Distance equal to Rate multiplied by Time. 

> $D = RT$

If a train travels at 60 [miles per hour](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Miles_per_hour) for $t$ [hours](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hour), the distance is $60t$. 

### [Percentage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Percentage) Shifts
Scaling quantities up or down by percentages requires a two-step mental process: finding the [fractional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fraction) piece, and applying it to the whole. 

*   A **[percentage increase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relative_change_and_difference)** problem requires multiplying the original amount by the percentage in [decimal form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal) and *adding* that product to the original amount. For an item that costs $x$ [marked up](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Markup_%28business%29) by 15%, the new price is $x + 0.15x$ (which elegantly [simplifies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simplification) to $1.15x$).
*   A **[percentage decrease](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relative_change_and_difference)** problem requires multiplying the original amount by the percentage in decimal form and *subtracting* that product from the original amount. If a jacket priced at $P$ is on sale for 20% off, the sale price is $P - 0.20P$ (which simplifies to $0.80P$).

To master algebraic translation is to master the art of looking past the [conversational](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conversation) clutter of a [word problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Word_problem_%28mathematics_education%29) to find the [logical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logic) skeleton underneath. When you spot words like "of," "less than," "is," or "sum," you are no longer reading English—you are reading the [blueprints](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blueprint) of an equation waiting to be assembled.
