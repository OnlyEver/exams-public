## Part 1: The Grammar of Mathematics

To speak a language, you must understand its basic grammar. In algebra, we have nouns, phrases, verbs, and sentences. 

> **The Variable: The Ultimate Placeholder**
> A **variable** is simply a symbol (usually a letter, like $x$ or $y$) used to represent an unknown numerical value. Think of a variable like an empty box. We don't know what number is inside the box yet, but we can still carry the box around and do math with it!

Now, let's look at the difference between a mathematical phrase and a mathematical sentence.

An **algebraic expression** is a mathematical phrase containing numbers, variables, and operation symbols without an equals sign. It represents a *value*, but it doesn't make a complete claim.
*   *Example:* "Three more than a number" translates to the expression $x + 3$. Notice there is no equals sign. It's just a phrase.

An **algebraic equation**, on the other hand, is a mathematical statement asserting equality between two algebraic expressions. It tells you that the stuff on the left weighs exactly the same as the stuff on the right. 
*   *Example:* "Three more than a number is ten" translates to the equation $x + 3 = 10$.

### The Verbs of Algebra
How do you know when you've moved from an expression to an equation? You look for the verb! In English, verbs drive the action. In algebra, the verb translates to an equals sign ($=$). 

When you are reading a word problem, the word **"is"** translates directly to an equals sign in an algebraic equation. Similarly, if you see the phrase **"yields"** or the phrase **"results in"**, these also translate to an equals sign. They are simply different ways of saying "balances out to."

## Part 2: The Rosetta Stone of Operations

Most of translation is simply knowing your vocabulary. Words in English map directly to operations in math. Let's build our dictionary.

### Addition and Subtraction
Addition is the act of bringing things together. You'll know it's time to add when you see specific keywords:
*   The word **"sum"** indicates the mathematical operation of addition.
*   The phrase **"more than"** indicates the mathematical operation of addition.
*   The phrase **"increased by"** indicates the mathematical operation of addition.

Subtraction is the act of taking away or finding the gap between two values:
*   The word **"difference"** indicates the mathematical operation of subtraction.
*   The phrase **"decreased by"** indicates the mathematical operation of subtraction.
*   The phrase **"less than"** indicates the mathematical operation of subtraction.

**🚨 THE REVERSAL TRAP! (Pay Close Attention Here)**

Addition is commutative, meaning $5 + 3$ is the same as $3 + 5$. Subtraction, however, is notoriously stubborn. $5 - 3$ is NOT the same as $3 - 5$. Therefore, the order in which you write your terms matters immensely!

> **The Reversal Rule**
> The phrase **"subtracted from"** reverses the standard left-to-right order of operands in a subtraction expression. 

If I say "Take 5 subtracted from 20," you don't write $5 - 20$. You write $20 - 5$. The "from" tells you what you started with!

The exact same reversal happens with "less than." Think about money: If I have $x$ dollars less than your $y$ dollars, how do we write my wealth? We start with yours, and take away mine. Therefore, the verbal phrase **"x less than y"** translates algebraically to the expression $y - x$. Do not write $x - y$!

### Multiplication and Division
Multiplication is just incredibly fast addition. 
*   The word **"product"** indicates the mathematical operation of multiplication.
*   The word **"times"** indicates the mathematical operation of multiplication.
*   The word **"twice"** indicates the mathematical operation of multiplying a quantity by the number two ($2x$).

There is also a hidden multiplication word that sneaks past people all the time: the word **"of"**. When the word "of" immediately follows a fraction or percentage, it indicates the mathematical operation of multiplication. If I want "half *of* ten", I calculate $\frac{1}{2} \times 10$. If I want "20% *of* $x$", I calculate $0.20x$. 

Division is the act of splitting things into groups.
*   The word **"quotient"** indicates the mathematical operation of division.
*   The phrase **"divided by"** indicates the mathematical operation of division.
*   The word **"ratio"** typically indicates the mathematical operation of division (the ratio of $a$ to $b$ is written as $\frac{a}{b}$).

### The Dictionary at a Glance

| English Concept | Mathematical Operation | Example | Translation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Sum, more than, increased by** | Addition ($+$) | The sum of a number and 4 | $x + 4$ |
| **Difference, decreased by** | Subtraction ($-$) | A number decreased by 7 | $x - 7$ |
| **Less than, subtracted from** | Subtraction ($-$ REVERSED) | 10 less than a number | $x - 10$ |
| **Product, times, of** | Multiplication ($\times$) | The product of 3 and $y$ | $3y$ |
| **Quotient, divided by, ratio** | Division ($\div$) | The ratio of $z$ to 5 | $\frac{z}{5}$ |

## Part 3: Punctuation Matters (The Power of Parentheses)

In English, punctuation saves lives. There is a massive difference between "Let's eat, Grandma!" and "Let's eat Grandma!" 

In algebra, our punctuation marks are **parentheses**. Parentheses are used in algebraic expressions to group operations that must be performed first according to the standard order of operations (PEMDAS). 

Let’s look at a classic translation error. Imagine a problem asks you to write an expression for: 
*"The sum of $x$ and $y$, multiplied by $z$."*

If you translate this lazily, left to right, you might write: 
$x + y \times z$

But wait! According to the order of operations, multiplication comes before addition. A mathematician reading $x + yz$ will multiply $y$ and $z$ first, and *then* add $x$. That completely violates the English sentence, which wanted you to find the *sum* first!

To fix this, we use algebraic punctuation. The phrase **"the sum of x and y, multiplied by z"** requires placing the sum of $x$ and $y$ inside parentheses before multiplying the result by $z$. 

Correct Translation: **$(x + y)z$**

Whenever you see a phrase ask for a "sum" or a "difference" and then immediately ask you to multiply or divide *that result*, wrap the sum or difference in parentheses!

## Part 4: Inequalities (When Things Aren't Exactly Equal)

Sometimes the universe isn't perfectly balanced. Sometimes, things are heavier, taller, or more expensive than other things. When a relationship is not an equation, it is an **inequality**.

Earlier, we learned that "less than" means subtraction ($3$ less than $x$ is $x - 3$). But what happens if we add our verb "is" into the mix? 

The phrase **"is less than"** indicates a mathematical inequality rather than an operation of subtraction. 
*   "3 less than $x$" $\rightarrow x - 3$ (An expression)
*   "3 **is** less than $x$" $\rightarrow 3 < x$ (An inequality)

Do you see how one tiny word changes the entire universe of the math? 

We also have limits and boundaries in the real world. Think about riding a roller coaster. A sign might say, "You must be at least 48 inches tall to ride." Does that mean you have to be exactly 48 inches? No, you can be 48 inches, or 50 inches, or 60 inches! Therefore, the phrase **"at least"** translates to a greater-than-or-equal-to symbol ($\ge$) in an algebraic inequality. Let $h$ be your height: $h \ge 48$.

Conversely, think of a weight limit on an elevator. "Capacity: at most 2,000 lbs." Can it hold 2,000 lbs? Yes. Can it hold 1,500 lbs? Yes. Can it hold 2,001 lbs? Snap. Therefore, the phrase **"at most"** translates to a less-than-or-equal-to symbol ($\le$) in an algebraic inequality. Let $w$ be the weight: $w \le 2000$.

## Part 5: Modeling the Real World

Now we arrive at the crown jewel of algebra: building real-world models. This is where we take a practical scenario and turn it into a flawless algebraic machine. 

### 1. The Business Model (Linear Costs)
Imagine you are starting a business printing custom t-shirts. 
Before you print a single shirt, you have to buy a printing press for $500. You have to pay this no matter what. In algebra, **fixed costs** in a real-world linear model are represented by the **constant term** in an algebraic equation. (It's a constant because it doesn't change, no matter how many shirts you make).

Then, for every t-shirt you actually print, you have to buy the blank shirt and the ink, which costs $3 per shirt. In algebra, **variable costs** in a real-world linear model are represented by a **numerical coefficient multiplied by a variable**. If $x$ is the number of shirts, your variable cost is $3x$.

How do we find your total bill? **Total cost** in a linear algebraic model is calculated by adding the fixed cost to the product of the variable rate and the number of items. 

$$\text{Total Cost} = \text{Fixed Cost} + (\text{Variable Rate} \times \text{Number of Items})$$
$$C = 500 + 3x$$

### 2. The Motion Model (Road Trips!)
If you are driving down the highway at 60 miles per hour, and you drive for 2 hours, how far did you go? 120 miles! You intuitively know this. Algebraically, we say that **distance traveled** is modeled by the algebraic equation representing Distance equal to Rate multiplied by Time.

$$D = R \times T$$
*(Or simply $d = rt$)*

If a Praxis question says, "A train leaves Chicago traveling at $x$ miles per hour for 4 hours, and covers 300 miles," you can snap that right into the model: $300 = x \cdot 4$, or $4x = 300$. 

### 3. The Percentage Models (Taxes and Sales)
Percentages are everywhere, from sales tax to Black Friday discounts. 

A **percentage increase** problem (like adding tax or a tip) requires multiplying the original amount by the percentage in decimal form and adding that product to the original amount.
Let $x$ be the original price. If there is an 8% tax, the tax is $0.08x$. 
The total you pay is: $x + 0.08x$. 
*(Note: Because $1x + 0.08x = 1.08x$, you can also just multiply the original amount by $1.08$!)*

A **percentage decrease** problem (like a clearance sale) requires multiplying the original amount by the percentage in decimal form and subtracting that product from the original amount.
If a $50 jacket is 20% off, the discount is $0.20 \times 50$. 
The new price is the original minus the discount: $50 - (0.20 \times 50)$. 

### 4. The Number Theory Riddles
Finally, the Praxis exam loves number riddles. They usually look something like this: "The sum of three consecutive integers is 42..." 

To solve these, you need to know how to represent sequences algebraically.
When you count normally (1, 2, 3, 4...), each number is exactly 1 larger than the last. Therefore, **consecutive integers** are represented algebraically by a sequence such as $x$, $x + 1$, $x + 2$, and so forth.

But what if the test specifies "even" or "odd" integers? Let's think about even numbers: 2, 4, 6, 8. How far apart are they? They are separated by *two*. Therefore, **consecutive even integers** are represented algebraically by a sequence such as $x$, $x + 2$, $x + 4$.

Now, what about odd integers? 1, 3, 5, 7. 
Students often stumble here. They think, *"Because they are odd, I should use $x+1$ and $x+3$!"* 
Stop and look at the numbers! How do you get from 1 to 3? You add 2! How do you get from 3 to 5? You add 2! The gap between odd numbers is exactly the same as the gap between even numbers. Therefore, **consecutive odd integers** are ALSO represented algebraically by a sequence such as $x$, $x + 2$, $x + 4$. 

(The only difference is that for odd integers, $x$ itself will be an odd number. But the *jumps* between the numbers are always even jumps of 2!)
