## Part 1: The Grammar of Mathematics

To speak a language, you must understand its basic grammar. In algebra, we have nouns, phrases, verbs, and sentences. 

> **The Variable: The Ultimate Mystery Box**
> In math, a **variable** is a symbol used to represent an unknown numerical value in an algebraic expression or equation. Usually, we use a letter like x or y. Think of a variable like a mystery box in a video game. We don't know exactly what number is inside the box yet, but we can still carry the box around and play the game!

Now, let's look at the difference between a math phrase and a full math sentence.

An **algebraic expression** is a mathematical phrase containing numbers, variables, and operation symbols without an equals sign. It represents an amount of something, but it doesn't give you the full story.
*   *Example:* "Three more than a number" translates to the expression x + 3. Notice there is no equals sign anywhere. It's just a phrase!

An **algebraic equation**, on the other hand, is a mathematical statement asserting equality between two algebraic expressions. It tells you that the stuff on the left side of the teeter-totter weighs exactly the same as the stuff on the right side. 
*   *Example:* "Three more than a number is ten" translates to the equation x + 3 = 10.

### The Verbs of Algebra
How do you know when you've moved from an expression (a phrase) to an equation (a full sentence)? You look for the verb! In English, verbs drive the action. In algebra, the verb is the equals sign (=). 

When you are reading a word problem, the word **"is"** translates to an equals sign in an algebraic equation. Similarly, the phrase **"yields"** translates to an equals sign in an algebraic equation, and the phrase **"results in"** translates to an equals sign in an algebraic equation. They are simply different ways of saying "balances out to."

## Part 2: The Rosetta Stone of Operations

Most of translating is simply knowing your vocabulary. Words in English match up perfectly with actions in math. Let's build our dictionary!

### Addition and Subtraction
Addition is the act of bringing things together, like adding more blocks to your Lego tower. You'll know it's time to add when you see specific keywords:
*   The word **"sum"** indicates the mathematical operation of addition.
*   The phrase **"more than"** indicates the mathematical operation of addition.
*   The phrase **"increased by"** indicates the mathematical operation of addition.

Subtraction is the act of taking away, like eating slices of a pizza:
*   The word **"difference"** indicates the mathematical operation of subtraction.
*   The phrase **"decreased by"** indicates the mathematical operation of subtraction.
*   The phrase **"less than"** indicates the mathematical operation of subtraction.

**🚨 THE REVERSAL TRAP! (Pay Close Attention Here)**

Addition is super easygoing. 5 + 3 is exactly the same as 3 + 5. Subtraction, however, is very stubborn! 5 - 3 is NOT the same as 3 - 5. Therefore, the order in which you write your numbers matters a ton!

> **The Reversal Rule**
> Watch out! The phrase **"subtracted from"** reverses the standard left-to-right order of operands in a subtraction expression. (An "operand" is just a fancy math word for the number or letter you are using!) 

If I say "Take 5 subtracted from 20," you don't write 5 - 20. You write 20 - 5. The word "from" tells you what number you started with in your pocket!

The exact same reversal happens with the phrase "less than." Think about your allowance. If I have x dollars less than your y dollars, how do we figure out my money? We start with yours, and take away mine. Therefore, the verbal phrase **"x less than y"** translates algebraically to the expression y - x. Do not write x - y!

### Multiplication and Division
Multiplication is just incredibly fast addition. 
*   The word **"product"** indicates the mathematical operation of multiplication.
*   The word **"times"** indicates the mathematical operation of multiplication.
*   The word **"twice"** indicates the mathematical operation of multiplying a quantity by the number two (written as 2 * x, or just 2x).

There is also a hidden multiplication word that sneaks past people all the time: the word **"of"**. The word "of" immediately following a fraction or percentage indicates the mathematical operation of multiplication. If I want "half of ten", I multiply 1/2 by 10. If I want "20% of x", I multiply 0.20 by x. 

Division is the act of sharing things equally, like splitting a bag of candy with your friends.
*   The word **"quotient"** indicates the mathematical operation of division.
*   The phrase **"divided by"** indicates the mathematical operation of division.
*   The word **"ratio"** typically indicates the mathematical operation of division (the ratio of a to b is written as a / b).

### The Dictionary at a Glance

| English Concept | Mathematical Operation | Example | Translation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Sum, more than, increased by** | Addition (+) | The sum of a number and 4 | x + 4 |
| **Difference, decreased by** | Subtraction (-) | A number decreased by 7 | x - 7 |
| **Less than, subtracted from** | Subtraction (- REVERSED) | 10 less than a number | x - 10 |
| **Product, times, of** | Multiplication (*) | The product of 3 and y | 3 * y |
| **Quotient, divided by, ratio** | Division (/) | The ratio of z to 5 | z / 5 |

## Part 3: Punctuation Matters (The Power of Parentheses)

In English, punctuation is super important! There is a huge difference between "Let's eat, Grandpa!" and "Let's eat Grandpa!" One is a nice dinner, and the other is a zombie movie.

In algebra, our punctuation marks are **parentheses**. Parentheses are used in algebraic expressions to group operations that must be performed first according to the standard order of operations (you might know this as PEMDAS). 

Let’s look at a classic mistake. Imagine a problem asks you to write an expression for: 
*"The sum of x and y, multiplied by z."*

If you just write it left to right without thinking, you might write: 
x + y * z

But wait! In math, multiplication always happens before addition. A calculator reading x + y * z will multiply y and z first, and *then* add x. That completely ruins the English sentence, which wanted you to find the *sum* first!

To fix this, we use parentheses. The phrase **"the sum of x and y, multiplied by z"** requires placing the sum of x and y inside parentheses before multiplying the result by z. 

Here is step-by-step how to write it correctly:
1. First, write the sum part: x + y
2. Second, put that whole group in parentheses so it happens first: (x + y)
3. Third, multiply that entire group by z: (x + y) * z

Correct Translation: **(x + y) * z**

## Part 4: Inequalities (When Things Aren't Exactly Equal)

Sometimes the world isn't perfectly balanced. Sometimes, things are heavier, taller, or more expensive than other things. When a relationship is not perfectly equal, it is called an **inequality**.

Earlier, we learned that "less than" means subtraction (like saying 3 less than x is x - 3). But what happens if we add our verb "is" into the sentence? 

The phrase **"is less than"** indicates a mathematical inequality rather than an operation of subtraction. 
*   "3 less than x" means x - 3 (This is just a phrase: an expression).
*   "3 **is** less than x" means 3 < x (This is a full sentence comparing two things: an inequality).

Do you see how one tiny word changes the entire math problem? 

We also have limits and boundaries in the real world. Think about riding a big roller coaster. A sign might say, "You must be at least 48 inches tall to ride." Does that mean you have to be *exactly* 48 inches? No, you can be 48 inches, or 50 inches, or 60 inches! Therefore, the phrase **"at least"** translates to a greater-than-or-equal-to symbol (>=) in an algebraic inequality. If h is your height: h >= 48.

Now, think of a weight limit on a small elevator. A sign says: "Capacity: at most 2,000 pounds." Can it hold exactly 2,000 pounds? Yes. Can it hold 1,500 pounds? Yes. Can it hold 3,000 pounds? Snap! The cables might break. Therefore, the phrase **"at most"** translates to a less-than-or-equal-to symbol (<=) in an algebraic inequality. If w is the weight: w <= 2000.

## Part 5: Modeling the Real World

Now we arrive at the coolest part of algebra: building real-world models. This is where we take a practical, everyday situation and turn it into a flawless math machine. 

### 1. The Business Model (Linear Costs)
Imagine you are starting a business selling custom skateboards. 
Before you build a single board, you have to buy a bunch of tools for \$100. You have to pay this no matter what, even if you sell zero skateboards. In algebra, **fixed costs** in a real-world linear model are represented by the constant term in an algebraic equation. (It's a "constant" number because it stays the same, constantly!)

Then, for every skateboard you actually build, you have to buy the wheels and the wood, which cost \$20 per board. In algebra, **variable costs** in a real-world linear model are represented by a numerical coefficient multiplied by a variable. If x is the number of boards you make, your variable cost is 20 * x.

How do we find your total bill? **Total cost** in a linear algebraic model is calculated by adding the fixed cost to the product of the variable rate and the number of items. 

Let's do this step-by-step. Imagine you build 5 skateboards. What is your total cost?
1. Start with your formula: Total Cost = Fixed Cost + (Variable Rate * Number of Items).
2. Plug in your Fixed Cost: 100.
3. Plug in your Variable Rate: 20.
4. Plug in your Number of Items: 5.
5. Multiply the variable rate by the number of items: 20 * 5 = 100.
6. Add your fixed cost to that product: 100 + 100 = 200.
7. Your Total Cost is \$200!

### 2. The Motion Model (Road Trips!)
If your family is driving down the highway at 60 miles per hour, and you drive for 2 hours, how far did you go? 120 miles! You totally know this already! In math talk, **distance traveled** is modeled by the algebraic equation representing Distance equal to Rate multiplied by Time. (Distance = Rate * Time).

Let's do a step-by-step road trip:
1. Identify your Rate (speed): Let's say it's 50 miles per hour.
2. Identify your Time: Let's say you drive for 3 hours.
3. Multiply the Rate by the Time: 50 * 3.
4. Find your final Distance: 150 miles!

### 3. The Percentage Models (Taxes and Sales)
Percentages are everywhere, from sales tax on a video game to awesome holiday discounts. 

A **percentage increase** problem (like adding tax or a tip) requires multiplying the original amount by the percentage in decimal form and adding that product to the original amount.
Let's figure out the tax on a \$40 video game, with a 10% tax.
1. Identify the original amount: \$40.
2. Change the percentage into a decimal: 10% becomes 0.10.
3. Multiply the original amount by the decimal: 40 * 0.10 = 4. (This \$4 is your tax!)
4. Add that product to the original amount: 40 + 4 = 44. Your total is \$44!

A **percentage decrease** problem (like a clearance sale) requires multiplying the original amount by the percentage in decimal form and subtracting that product from the original amount.
Let's buy a \$50 pair of sneakers that are on sale for 20% off.
1. Identify the original amount: \$50.
2. Change the percentage into a decimal: 20% becomes 0.20.
3. Multiply the original amount by the decimal: 50 * 0.20 = 10. (This \$10 is your discount!)
4. Subtract that product from the original amount: 50 - 10 = 40. Your new price is \$40!

### 4. The Number Theory Riddles
Sometimes math tests like to give you little number riddles. They usually look something like this: "The sum of three consecutive integers is 42..." 

To solve these, you just need to know how to write number patterns using variables. 
When you count normally (1, 2, 3, 4...), each number is exactly 1 larger than the last one. Therefore, **consecutive integers** are represented algebraically by a sequence such as x, x + 1, x + 2, and so forth.

But what if the test specifies "even" or "odd" numbers? Let's look at even numbers: 2, 4, 6, 8. How many steps does it take to jump from 2 to 4? It takes *two* steps! Therefore, **consecutive even integers** are represented algebraically by a sequence such as x, x + 2, x + 4.

Now, what about odd numbers? 1, 3, 5, 7. 
A lot of people get tricked here! They think, *"Because they are odd words, I should use x + 1 and x + 3!"* 
Stop and look at the odd numbers for a second! How many steps does it take to get from 1 to 3? You add 2! How many steps to get from 3 to 5? You add 2! The jumps between odd numbers are exactly the same size as the jumps between even numbers. Therefore, **consecutive odd integers** are represented algebraically by a sequence such as x, x + 2, x + 4. 

(The only difference is that for odd integers, the starting "x" will be an odd number. But the *jumps* between the numbers are always even jumps of 2!)
