Let's play a little math game. Usually, math gives you a starting point and asks you to find the finish line. Like, "Take the number 7, multiply it by itself, what do you get?" Easy. You get 49. 

But what if we play the game in reverse? What if I give you the final score and ask how you got there? What if I say, "I am thinking of a secret number. When I multiply this number by itself, the answer is 49. What is my secret number?"

Welcome to a cool part of math called quadratic equations!

At its core, a simple quadratic equation can be written in the form x^2 = c, where x is a variable (the secret number we want to find) and c is a constant (a plain, regular number like 49). 

Solving this is like solving a mystery puzzle. Solving the equation x^2 = c requires finding all numerical values of x that produce the constant c when multiplied by themselves. It sounds like a super simple puzzle, right? But math sometimes hides neat little surprises in simple places. Let's look closer.

---

## The Two Paths to the Same Destination

Let's think about what happens when we multiply numbers together.

If you have a positive number, like 3, and you square it (which just means multiplying it by itself like this: 3 * 3), you get 9. This is a rule that never changes: the square of any positive real number is always a positive real number. 

But here is where the puzzle gets interesting. What happens when we use negative numbers, like when you owe someone money? What is -3 * -3? The answer is also a positive 9. Why? Because of the rules of math, the two negative signs cancel each other out. Multiplying a negative real number by itself always results in a positive real number product.

Think of it like walking to the pizza shop. You can walk 3 blocks north or 3 blocks south, but the total distance you walked is still a positive amount of blocks. Both the positive path and the negative path give us a positive result when multiplied by themselves!

### Unlocking x^2 = 49

Let's go back to our first mystery puzzle: x^2 = 49. 

To win this game, we are asking: what real numbers can I multiply by themselves to get exactly 49? 

Because of the two different paths to the pizza shop we just talked about, an equation of the form x^2 = c has exactly two distinct real solutions when the constant c is a positive number. 

Let's solve the quadratic equation x^2 = 49 step-by-step to show why it has exactly two real number solutions:

Step 1. First, we test the positive path. We know that 7 * 7 = 49. So, the positive integer 7 is a valid solution to the equation x^2 = 49.
Step 2. Next, we test the negative path. We know that negative signs cancel out. Squaring the number -7 yields the positive number 49.
Step 3. We look at our results. Both 7 and -7 worked perfectly. Because of this, the negative integer -7 is a valid solution to the equation x^2 = 49.

## The Machinery Under the Hood: Absolute Values

Let's look at how to actually solve this step-by-step on paper. The quick reaction is to just "take the square root of both sides." But if you do that too fast, you might fall into a trap.

> **Warning: A Big Trap to Avoid!**  
> A common algebraic error when solving equations of the form x^2 = c is omitting the negative square root solution. If you just write down the positive answer and forget the negative one, you only get half the points!

Let's look at how the math actually works inside the machine. 

When you take the square root of x^2, the math kind of gets "amnesia." The square root symbol only spits out a positive distance. It completely forgot if the starting number was positive or negative before it was squared! Because of this amnesia, the algebraic expression √(x^2) simplifies to the absolute value of x, written as |x|. Absolute value is just how far a number is from zero, like checking your score in a game to see how far you are from zero points.

Because of this rule, taking the square root of both sides of the equation x^2 = c yields the intermediate algebraic equation |x| = √c.

Let's use our numbered steps to see this safely in action for x^2 = 49:

Step 1. Write the original problem: x^2 = 49.
Step 2. Put a square root symbol over both sides: √(x^2) = √49.
Step 3. Remember that the left side turns into absolute value: |x| = 7.
Step 4. Ask yourself: What numbers have a distance of 7 from zero? The answers are 7 and -7.

Boom! The math perfectly protects both answers. We didn't just guess; we proved it!

## The Language of Mathematicians: Shorthand and Symbols

Math experts love shortcuts. Writing out "x is the positive square root, or x is the negative square root" every single time takes way too long. So, they created a shortcut rule.

> **The Square Root Property**  
> The square root property states that if x^2 = c for a positive number c, the solutions are x = √c and x = -√c. 

To say this using formal math words: the two solutions to the equation x^2 = c for a positive constant c are the principal square root of c and the negative square root of c. (Don't let the word "principal" scare you; it's just a fancy word for the main, positive one).

To write this super fast, math experts invented a special symbol: ±. 
The mathematical symbol ± is read as "plus or minus". 

Instead of writing two whole equations, the mathematical notation x = ±√c is a shorthand representation for the two independent solutions x = √c and x = -√c.

So, let's solve x^2 = 49 like a pro:

Step 1. Write the problem: x^2 = 49.
Step 2. Use the shortcut symbol: x = ±√49.
Step 3. Solve the root: x = ±7.

Fast, easy, and completely correct!

## The Three Universes of x^2 = c

Depending on what the number c is, our puzzle acts like it lives in three different video game levels or "universes." Let's explore them.

### Universe 1: The Constant is Positive (c > 0)
We just played a lot in this universe! As we already learned, an equation of the form x^2 = c has exactly two distinct real solutions when the constant c is a positive number. 

Step 1. The problem is: x^2 = 25.
Step 2. We apply our plus/minus shortcut: x = ±√25.
Step 3. The two answers are: x = 5 and x = -5.

### Universe 2: The Constant is Zero (c = 0)
What if I ask you, "What number multiplied by itself gives a score of exactly zero?" 
There is only one number in the whole world that works. Zero isn't positive or negative. Because of this, an equation of the form x^2 = c has exactly one real solution when the constant c is equal to zero. 

Step 1. The problem is: x^2 = 0.
Step 2. Think about what times itself is 0. (0 * 0 = 0).
Step 3. We find that the only real number solution to the equation x^2 = 0 is the number 0. (x = 0).

### Universe 3: The Constant is Negative (c < 0)
Now, what if the game asks you to solve x^2 = -16? 
Wait a minute. We know that a positive times a positive is positive. And we know that a negative times a negative is positive. There is no real number that can be squared to produce a negative product. 

Let's look at it in steps:
Step 1. The problem is: x^2 = -16.
Step 2. Try to think of a number that multiplies by itself to make -16. (4 * 4 = 16, and -4 * -4 = 16).
Step 3. Realize it's impossible! If you try to put √-16 into a calculator, it will glitch out and say "ERROR." 

Because of this fact, an equation of the form x^2 = c has no real number solutions when the constant c is a negative number.
