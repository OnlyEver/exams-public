## Part I: The Atoms and the Molecules

When you try to arrange items—like trading cards or blocks—into a perfect rectangle, a number like 12 gives you lots of options. You can make 3 rows of 4, 2 rows of 6, or 1 long row of 12. But if you have exactly 7 blocks, you're stuck. You can only make one straight line of 7. It cannot be broken down into smaller even groups. This points us to two basic types of numbers.

**A prime number is a whole number strictly greater than 1 that has exactly two distinct positive divisors: 1 and the number itself.**

Think of primes as the unbreakable blocks in a video game. They stand alone! If you try to divide them up evenly, you fail, unless you just use the number itself or the number 1. 

On the other hand, numbers like 12 are built out of smaller pieces:

**A composite number is a positive integer that has at least one positive divisor other than 1 and the number itself.**

### The Oddballs: 0, 1, and 2
Now, you might be wondering about the smallest numbers. What about zero and one? 

By our rule, a prime must have *exactly* two positive divisors. The number 1 only has *one* divisor (just the number 1). Therefore, **the number 1 is neither prime nor composite.** It's just doing its own thing!

And what about zero? You can’t divide anything by zero, and every number divides into zero to just make zero. Because it acts so weirdly, **the number 0 is neither prime nor composite.**

Then we have the number 2. It has exactly two divisors: 1 and 2. So, it is a prime number! But wait—every other even number in the universe can be cut in half (divided by 2). Because of this, **the number 2 is the only even prime number.** It’s super unique! 

To be super fast at math, you should memorize the smallest of these unbreakable blocks. **The first ten prime numbers are 2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13, 17, 19, 23, and 29.** Get to know them like you know the stats of your favorite sports players. They are going to help you a lot.

## Part II: The Fundamental Theorem and the Factor Tree

Now we arrive at a super cool rule in math.

**The Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic states that every integer strictly greater than 1 is either a prime number itself or can be uniquely represented as a product of prime numbers.**

Think about what that means! Every single big composite number is really just a specific recipe of prime numbers multiplied together. It's like finding out exactly which Lego pieces were used to build a giant spaceship.

So, how do we find this recipe? We use a helpful process! **Prime factorization is the mathematical process of determining which prime numbers multiply together to yield a specific original number.** 

To do this, we don't just guess randomly; we draw a picture. **A factor tree is a visual diagrammatic tool used to systematically break down a number into the prime factors of that number.**

Let’s build a factor tree for the number 60 together:
1. Write down the number 60 at the top of your page.
2. Think of any two numbers that multiply to make 60. Let’s use 6 and 10. Draw two lines (branches) pointing down from 60 to the numbers 6 and 10.
3. Look at 6 and 10. Neither is an unbreakable prime block, so we keep going!
4. Break down 6 into 2 x 3. Since 2 and 3 are prime numbers (unbreakable!), circle them. That branch is done.
5. Break down 10 into 2 x 5. Since 2 and 5 are prime numbers, circle them. That branch is done.
6. Gather all your circled prime numbers: 2, 2, 3, and 5.

If we multiply those circled numbers back together (2 x 2 x 3 x 5), we get exactly 60! To make this look cleaner, kind of like grouping identical items in your video game inventory, we use a shortcut. **The prime factorization of a number can be written using exponents to compactly group identical prime factors.** 

Here is how we do that for 60:
1. Look at our list of prime factors: 2, 2, 3, 5.
2. We have two 2s. We can group those together by writing 2^2.
3. Bring down the other numbers and write it beautifully as: 2^2 x 3 x 5.

## Part IV: The Greatest Common Divisor (GCD)

Numbers don't just exist all by themselves; they team up and interact. Imagine you are trying to cut two different lengths of rope into equal pieces, and you want the absolute biggest pieces possible.

**The Greatest Common Divisor of two or more integers is the largest positive integer that divides each of the integers without leaving a remainder.**

*(A quick note for your test: **The term Greatest Common Divisor is mathematically synonymous with the term Greatest Common Factor**. They mean the exact same thing!)*

How do we find this master-key number? We use our prime block recipes! 
**The Greatest Common Divisor of two numbers is calculated by multiplying all the common prime factors shared by both numbers at their lowest exponent levels.**

Let's find the GCD of the numbers 24 and 36:
1. Write down the prime factorization of 24, which is 2^3 x 3^1.
2. Write down the prime factorization of 36, which is 2^2 x 3^2.
3. Look for the base numbers they both share. They both have a base of 2 and a base of 3!
4. For the shared base 2, pick the one with the *lowest* exponent. That is 2^2.
5. For the shared base 3, pick the one with the *lowest* exponent. That is 3^1.
6. Multiply your picks together: 2^2 x 3^1.
7. Calculate 2^2 (which is 2 x 2 = 4) and multiply it by 3.
8. 4 x 3 = 12. The GCD is 12! It's the absolute largest number that divides perfectly into both 24 and 36.

### Strangers in Math: Relatively Prime
What happens if you compare two numbers, like 8 and 15, and they share absolutely no prime blocks? (8 = 2^3, and 15 = 3 x 5). Their only shared divisor is the number 1. 

We have a special term for this: **Two numbers are considered relatively prime if the Greatest Common Divisor of the two numbers is exactly 1.** They don't share any mathematical building blocks at all!

## Part V: Stepping Forward (The Least Common Multiple)

While divisors are about breaking numbers down, multiples look forward to what a number can grow into. 

**A multiple of a given number is the product of that given number and any integer.**

Think of a multiple like saving up your weekly allowance. If you get 4 dollars a week, your multiples are 4, 8, 12, 16, 20...
Now imagine you save 4 dollars a week, and your friend saves 6 dollars a week. When will you both have the exact same amount of money? That's what we are finding next!

**The Least Common Multiple of two or more integers is the smallest positive integer that is divisible by each of the original integers.**

Just like we used the prime recipe to find the GCD, we can use it to find the LCM, but with a fun twist! **The Least Common Multiple of two numbers is calculated by multiplying the highest power of each distinct prime factor present in the prime factorizations of those two numbers.**

Let's use 24 and 36 again to find the LCM:
1. Write the prime factorization of 24: 2^3 x 3^1.
2. Write the prime factorization of 36: 2^2 x 3^2.
3. Make a list of every single distinct base number you see across both recipes. We see 2s and 3s.
4. Look at the base 2s. This time, pick the one with the *highest* exponent. That is 2^3.
5. Look at the base 3s. Pick the one with the *highest* exponent. That is 3^2.
6. Multiply your picks together: 2^3 x 3^2.
7. Calculate 2^3 (which is 2 x 2 x 2 = 8).
8. Calculate 3^2 (which is 3 x 3 = 9).
9. Multiply 8 x 9 = 72. The LCM is 72! If you count by 24s and count by 36s, 72 is the very first number they both land on.

## Part VI: The Grand Symmetrical Equation

Here is an awesome secret hidden in math. The GCD (the pieces numbers share inside) and the LCM (the place numbers meet up later on) are perfectly connected! 

**The product of two positive integers is equal to the product of the Greatest Common Divisor and the Least Common Multiple of those same two integers.**

Let's prove it with our numbers, 24 and 36:
1. Take our two original numbers (24 and 36) and multiply them together: 24 x 36 = 864.
2. Now, take their GCD (which we found was 12) and their LCM (which we found was 72).
3. Multiply the GCD and the LCM together: 12 x 72 = 864.
4. Look at that! Both answers are exactly 864!
