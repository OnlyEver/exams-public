## Part 1: Integers — The World of Debts and Assets

What is an integer? It's pretty simple. **Integers include all positive whole numbers, negative whole numbers, and zero.** That means no fractions and no messy decimals—just the solid, whole numbers you see on a standard number line. 

Right in the exact middle of this number line is **zero**. Zero is a really special number. **Zero is an integer that is neither positive nor negative.** It’s the perfect balance point, like the center of a seesaw. 

When we talk about an integer, sometimes we just want to know its raw "size," without worrying if it is positive or negative. We call this its **absolute value**. Think about walking: **the absolute value of an integer represents the distance of that specific integer from zero on a number line.** Because you can't walk a "negative" distance, absolute value is always positive (or zero). The absolute value of -5 is 5. The absolute value of 5 is also 5. They both sit exactly five steps away from zero!

### Adding and Subtracting Integers

Imagine you have a piggy bank. Positive numbers are your allowance (money you get), and negative numbers are money you owe a friend (debt). 

*   **Adding two positive integers always results in a positive sum.** 
    *Example: 5 + 5*
    1. You get 5 dollars.
    2. You get another 5 dollars.
    3. You have 10 dollars (a positive sum).
*   **Adding two negative integers always results in a negative sum.**
    *Example: -3 + (-2)*
    1. You owe your friend 3 dollars.
    2. You borrow 2 more dollars.
    3. You now owe 5 dollars total (-5).

But what happens when the positive and negative signs clash? What if you have some allowance money, but you also owe your friend? 

> **The Clash Rule:**
> **To add integers with different signs, subtract the smaller absolute value from the larger absolute value.**

Why do we do this? Because the money you have cancels out the money you owe! The winner of this clash tells you if your answer will be positive or negative. **When adding integers with different signs, the final sum takes the sign of the integer with the larger absolute value.** 

*Example: -10 + 4*
1. Find the absolute values: The absolute value of -10 is 10. The absolute value of 4 is 4.
2. Subtract the smaller absolute value from the larger one: 10 - 4 = 6.
3. Keep the sign of the number with the larger absolute value: Since 10 is bigger than 4, and the 10 was originally negative, our answer is negative. 
4. Answer: -6.

Now, look at a perfect tie. **The sum of any integer and the mathematical opposite of that exact integer is always equal to zero.** 
*Example: 7 + (-7)*
1. You have 7 dollars.
2. You owe exactly 7 dollars.
3. When you pay it back, you are left with exactly 0 dollars.

What about subtraction? Here is a super helpful trick. **Subtracting an integer is mathematically equivalent to adding the opposite of that integer.** If your friend forgives the 50 dollars you owe them (subtracting -50), it’s exactly the same as if they just handed you 50 dollars (adding +50). 

*Example: 10 - (-3)*
1. Start with the problem: 10 - (-3).
2. Change the subtraction sign to an addition sign.
3. Change the -3 to its mathematical opposite, which is +3.
4. Add them together: 10 + 3 = 13.

### Multiplying and Dividing Integers

Multiplying is just a really fast way of adding. The rules for the signs act like a neat pattern:

1.  **Multiplying two integers with the exact same sign always results in a positive product.** 
    *Example: -4 * -2*
    1. Check the signs: Both are negative (exact same sign).
    2. Multiply the numbers: 4 * 2 = 8.
    3. Make the answer positive: 8.
2.  **Multiplying two integers with different signs always results in a negative product.** 
    *Example: 3 * -4*
    1. Check the signs: One positive, one negative (different signs).
    2. Multiply the numbers: 3 * 4 = 12.
    3. Make the answer negative: -12.

Because division is just multiplication played in reverse, the exact same sign rules apply!
1.  **Dividing two integers with the exact same sign always results in a positive quotient.** 
    *Example: -20 / -4*
    1. Check signs: Both negative (same sign).
    2. Divide: 20 / 4 = 5.
    3. Make it positive: 5.
2.  **Dividing two integers with different signs always results in a negative quotient.** 
    *Example: -20 / 5*
    1. Check signs: One negative, one positive (different signs).
    2. Divide: 20 / 5 = 4.
    3. Make it negative: -4.

### The Peculiar Case of Zero

Zero behaves like a mathematical pause button. 
*   **The product of any integer and zero is always equal to zero.** 
    *Example: 50 * 0*
    1. Multiply the number by zero.
    2. Answer is always 0. (Fifty times nothing is still nothing!)
*   But be very careful here! **Division of any non-zero integer by the number zero is mathematically undefined.** 

> 🛑 **Feynman's Warning:** Why is division by zero undefined? Imagine trying to divide 10 slices of pizza into groups of zero slices. How many groups would you have? You could keep making "zero-slice groups" forever and never use up your 10 slices! It just breaks the rules of logic. Never divide by zero.

## Part 2: Fractions — Slicing Up the Universe

Integers are awesome for counting whole things, like whole video games or whole pizzas. But sometimes you only want half a pizza. **A mathematical fraction explicitly represents a part of a whole quantity.**

### The Anatomy of a Fraction
Every fraction has two really important parts:
1.  **The Numerator:** This is **the top number of a mathematical fraction.** What does it tell us? **The numerator of a fraction represents the specific number of parts being considered in a problem.**
2.  **The Denominator:** This is **the bottom number of a mathematical fraction.** What is its job? **The denominator of a fraction represents the total number of equal parts that make up a whole entity.**

If you have 3/8 of a pizza:
1. Look at the bottom number (8). This tells you the whole pizza was cut into 8 equal slices.
2. Look at the top number (3). This tells you that you get to eat 3 of those slices.

And remember our rule about zero? **A fraction possessing a zero in the denominator position is mathematically undefined.** You simply cannot have a pizza sliced into zero total pieces!

### Adding and Subtracting Fractions (Finding Common Ground)

If I give you some slices from a pizza cut into 3 pieces, and then slices from a pizza cut into 4 pieces, you can't easily add them up. The slices are totally different sizes! 

Because of this:
*   **Adding mathematical fractions requires all involved fractions to possess a common denominator.**
*   **Subtracting mathematical fractions requires all involved fractions to possess a common denominator.**

You have to make sure the slices are the same size before you mix them. How? **The least common multiple of two denominators serves as the lowest common denominator for fraction addition.** 

Once your slices are all the same size, the rule is so easy:
*   **To add fractions sharing a common denominator, calculate the sum of the numerators and keep the original denominator exactly the same.** 
*   **To subtract fractions sharing a common denominator, calculate the difference of the numerators and keep the original denominator exactly the same.**

*Example: Add 5/12 + 2/12*
1. Check the bottoms: They are both 12 (common denominator).
2. Add the top numbers (numerators): 5 + 2 = 7.
3. Keep the bottom number exactly the same: 12.
4. Answer: 7/12. (You don't add 12 + 12, because the 12 just tells you the *size* of the pizza slice).

### Multiplying and Dividing Fractions

Multiplying fractions is actually way easier than adding them! You don't even need common denominators. 
*   **Multiplying fractions requires multiplying the numerators together to calculate the new numerator.**
*   **Multiplying fractions requires multiplying the denominators together to calculate the new denominator.**

*Example: Multiply 2/3 * 4/5*
1. Multiply the top numbers straight across: 2 * 4 = 8.
2. Multiply the bottom numbers straight across: 3 * 5 = 15.
3. Write your new fraction: 8/15.

For division, we need a cool tool called a reciprocal. **The reciprocal of a fraction is created by directly swapping the numerator position with the denominator position.** (Basically, just flip it upside down! The reciprocal of 3/4 is 4/3). 

Here is a neat fact: **The product of any non-zero fraction and the exact reciprocal of that fraction is always equal to one.** 
*Example: 3/4 * 4/3*
1. Multiply tops: 3 * 4 = 12.
2. Multiply bottoms: 4 * 3 = 12.
3. 12/12 equals 1!

How do we divide fractions? We use that flipped fraction!
*   **Dividing a first fraction by a second fraction requires multiplying the first fraction by the reciprocal of the second fraction.** 

*Example: Divide 1/2 / 3/4*
1. Keep the first fraction the same: 1/2.
2. Change the division sign to multiplication: *.
3. Flip the second fraction (find its reciprocal): 3/4 becomes 4/3.
4. Multiply straight across: (1 * 4) / (2 * 3) = 4/6.

### Mixed Numbers and Improper Fractions

Sometimes we have more than just one whole pizza. 
*   **A proper fraction has a numerator that is strictly less than the denominator.** (Like 3/4. It's smaller than a whole pizza).
*   **An improper fraction has a numerator that is mathematically greater than or equal to the denominator.** (Like 11/4. It's more than one whole pizza).
*   **A mixed number consists of a whole number combined with a proper fraction.** (Like 2 3/4. Two whole pizzas, plus 3 extra slices). 

How do we switch between the two? 

**Mixed to Improper:** 
**To convert a mixed number to an improper fraction, multiply the whole number by the denominator and add the numerator.** 

*Example: Convert 2 3/4 to an improper fraction*
1. Multiply the whole number by the bottom number: 2 * 4 = 8 slices.
2. Add the top number (the extra slices): 8 + 3 = 11 slices total.
3. Put that new top number over the original bottom number.
4. Answer: 11/4.

**Improper to Mixed:**
**To convert an improper fraction to a mixed number, divide the numerator by the denominator to determine the whole number portion.** 

*Example: Convert 11/4 to a mixed number*
1. Divide the top by the bottom: 11 / 4. 
2. 4 fits into 11 exactly 2 times (2 whole pizzas). This is your whole number.
3. Find the leftovers. 4 * 2 = 8, so 11 - 8 = 3 slices left over. 
4. **When converting an improper fraction to a mixed number, the mathematical remainder from the division step becomes the new numerator.** Put the 3 over the original 4. 
5. Answer: 2 3/4.

### The Art of Equivalency and Simplification

Numbers can wear disguises! **Equivalent fractions are structurally different numerical fractions that represent the exact same underlying mathematical value.** For example, 1/2 is the exact same amount of food as 4/8. 

How do we make these disguises? **Multiplying both the numerator and denominator of a fraction by the exact same non-zero number generates a valid equivalent fraction.** 

*Example: Make an equivalent fraction for 1/2*
1. Pick a random number, like 4.
2. Multiply the top by 4: 1 * 4 = 4.
3. Multiply the bottom by 4: 2 * 4 = 8.
4. Answer: 4/8. (It looks different, but it's the same amount!)

To take off the disguise, we simplify it. **Simplifying a fraction involves dividing both the numerator and the denominator by the greatest common factor of the two numbers.** 

*Example: Simplify 12/16*
1. Find the biggest number that divides perfectly into both 12 and 16. That number is 4.
2. Divide the top by 4: 12 / 4 = 3.
3. Divide the bottom by 4: 16 / 4 = 4.
4. Answer: 3/4.

## Part 3: Decimals — Fractions in Disguise

Here is a big secret: decimals are just fractions in disguise! **Decimals represent fractions with denominators that are explicit powers of ten** (like 10, 100, or 1000). 

Instead of writing 7/10, we write 0.7. It’s just a faster way to write it!

Let's look at the names of the spots to the right of the decimal point:
*   **The first place value directly to the right of a decimal point is called the tenths place.** (This means pieces out of 10).
*   **The second place value directly to the right of a decimal point is called the hundredths place.** (Pieces out of 100).
*   **The third place value directly to the right of a decimal point is called the thousandths place.** (Pieces out of 1000).

### Adding and Subtracting Decimals

Because decimals are just fractions, we still have to follow the golden fraction rule: *you can only mix pieces that are the same size.* You can't add tenths straight to thousandths!

How do we make sure sizes match? We line them up! 
*   **Adding decimals requires aligning the decimal points vertically before adding the corresponding digits.**
*   **Subtracting decimals requires aligning the decimal points vertically before subtracting the corresponding digits.**

If one number is longer than the other (like 3.5 and 1.42), don't worry. **Trailing zeros can be appended to the right end of a decimal number without altering the mathematical value of the number.** Writing 3.5 as 3.50 is totally fine! It just makes it easier to line everything up.

*Example: Subtract 3.5 - 1.42*
1. Write 3.5 and put 1.42 right underneath it.
2. Line up the dots exactly on top of each other.
3. Add a trailing zero to 3.5 so they are the same length: 3.50.
4. Subtract straight down like normal whole numbers: 3.50 - 1.42 = 2.08.

### Multiplying and Dividing Decimals

When multiplying, we can ignore the dots for just a second. 
*   **To multiply decimals, calculate the product of the numeric values exactly as if the numbers were whole numbers.** 

But remember, we have to put the decimal point back when we finish! 
*   **The product of two decimals must contain a total number of decimal places equal to the sum of the decimal places in the original factors.**

*Example: Multiply 0.2 * 0.03* 
1. Ignore the decimals and just multiply the whole numbers: 2 * 3 = 6.
2. Count the decimal places in the original problem. 0.2 has 1 place. 0.03 has 2 places. 
3. Add them up: 1 + 2 = 3 total places.
4. Put the decimal point 3 spots to the left of your 6. 
5. Answer: 0.006.

Division takes a neat trick. Dividing by a decimal looks confusing, so we just change it!
*   **To divide a number by a decimal divisor, move the decimal point of the divisor to the right to create a whole number.**
*   To keep everything fair, **when converting a decimal divisor to a whole number, the decimal point of the dividend must be moved to the right by the exact same number of places.**

*Example: Divide 1.25 / 0.5*
1. Look at the number you are dividing by (0.5). Move its decimal 1 spot to the right to make it a whole number: 5.
2. Since you moved it 1 spot for the first number, you MUST move it 1 spot for the other number (1.25). It becomes 12.5.
3. Now divide normally: 12.5 / 5 = 2.5.

### Bridging the Gap: Decimals to Fractions and Back

Since decimals and fractions are just different ways to write the same thing, you should know how to swap between them.

**Fractions to Decimals:**
The line in a fraction is actually just a division symbol! **To convert a mathematical fraction to a decimal value, divide the numerator by the denominator.** 

*Example: Convert 3/4 to a decimal*
1. Take the top number (3).
2. Divide it by the bottom number (4).
3. 3 / 4 = 0.75.

**Decimals to Fractions:**
Let's go backward. **To convert a terminating decimal into a fraction, place the entire decimal digit sequence over the corresponding power of ten and simplify the result.** 

*Example: Convert 0.45 to a fraction*
1. Read the number out loud. It ends in the second spot, so it is "forty-five hundredths."
2. Write the digits (45) over the power of ten (100) to make a fraction: 45/100.
3. Simplify it! Find the biggest number that divides into both (which is 5). 
4. Divide the top by 5 (45 / 5 = 9). Divide the bottom by 5 (100 / 5 = 20).
5. Answer: 9/20.
