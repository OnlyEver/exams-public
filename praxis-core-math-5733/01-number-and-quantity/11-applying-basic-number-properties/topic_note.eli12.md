If you know the rules, you don't have to grind the hard way; you can work smart. You can move numbers around, team them up, and break them apart so that the math practically solves itself in your head. 

Let's look at how we can play around with numbers to make our lives way easier!

## 1. The Art of Rearranging: The Commutative Property

Have you ever packed a backpack for school? If you put your books in first and then your lunchbox, you get a packed backpack. If you put your lunchbox in first and then your books, you end up with the exact same packed backpack. The order doesn't change what is inside. 

This brings us to our first rule. **The commutative property of addition states that changing the order of addends does not change the resulting sum.** (Addends are just the numbers you are adding together).

> **The Rule of Moving Things Around (Addition)**
> **The algebraic representation of the commutative property of addition is a plus b equals b plus a.** 
> Written simply: a + b = b + a

The exact same thing happens with multiplication. Imagine 3 rows of 4 slices of pizza on a giant tray. If you spin the tray, it looks like 4 rows of 3 slices. The total amount of pizza is exactly the same! **The commutative property of multiplication states that changing the order of factors does not change the resulting product.** (Factors are the numbers you multiply).

> **The Rule of Moving Things Around (Multiplication)**
> **The algebraic representation of the commutative property of multiplication is a times b equals b times a.**
> Written simply: a * b = b * a

### Why do we care? 
We care because this property is your first major shortcut. **The commutative property allows numbers in an addition expression to be rearranged to combine compatible numbers that sum to a multiple of ten.** 

Let's say you have this problem: 13 + 48 + 7. Instead of adding the hard way, we can use step-by-step logic:
1. Look at the numbers you need to add: 13, 48, and 7.
2. Rearrange the order so the 13 and the 7 are next to each other: 13 + 7 + 48.
3. Add 13 and 7 together first, which makes a nice, clean 20.
4. Add the 20 to the 48, which gives you 68. 

You just solved a bigger problem in your head super fast!

### ⚠️ The "Watch Out" Warning
Does this trick work for everything? What about putting on your socks and then your shoes? The order definitely matters there! If you put your shoes on first, your socks won't fit!

Math has rules like this, too:
* **The operation of subtraction does not satisfy the commutative property.** 
  1. Try doing 5 - 3. That equals 2.
  2. Try flipping it to 3 - 5. That equals -2. 
  3. Since 2 is not the same as -2, flipping is not allowed here.
* **The operation of division does not satisfy the commutative property.**
  1. Try doing 10 / 2. That equals 5.
  2. Try flipping it to 2 / 10. That equals 1/5.
  3. Since 5 is not the same as 1/5, flipping is not allowed here.

## 2. The Art of Friendship: The Associative Property

If the first rule was about physically moving things around, this next rule is about *who is teaming up with whom*. 

> **Order of Operations Context**
> Before we make teams, remember a golden rule of math: **Grouping symbols such as parentheses dictate which arithmetic operations must be performed first within an algebraic expression.** Whatever is in the parentheses goes first!

Now, imagine playing an online multiplayer game with three friends: Alex, Bailey, and Chris. If Alex and Bailey team up in a squad first, and then Chris joins them, it’s the exact same gaming party as if Bailey and Chris teamed up first and then Alex joined. The little groups changed, but the final team is exactly the same.

**The associative property of addition states that changing the grouping of addends does not change the resulting sum.**

> **The Rule of Grouping (Addition)**
> **The algebraic representation of the associative property of addition is that the grouped sum of a and b plus c equals a plus the grouped sum of b and c.**
> Written simply: (a + b) + c = a + (b + c)

It works for multiplying, too! **The associative property of multiplication states that changing the grouping of factors does not change the resulting product.**

> **The Rule of Grouping (Multiplication)**
> **The algebraic representation of the associative property of multiplication is that the grouped product of a and b times c equals a times the grouped product of b and c.**
> Written simply: (a * b) * c = a * (b * c)

### Why do we care?
Because it saves you from doing terrible math! **The associative property allows a string of multiplication operations to be regrouped to form easier partial products without changing the final result.**

Look at this annoying problem: 4 * (25 * 17). Let's use our shortcut!
1. Normally, parentheses tell us to do 25 * 17 first. That is too hard to do in our heads.
2. Instead, change the grouping (re-associate) the numbers like this: (4 * 25) * 17.
3. Multiply the 4 and the 25 together first. Think of 4 quarters: that makes 100!
4. Multiply that 100 by 17.
5. The final answer is 1700. Easy!

### ⚠️ The Second "Watch Out" Warning
Just like before, subtraction and division don't like to play along.
* **The operation of subtraction does not satisfy the associative property.**
  1. Try (10 - 5) - 2. That is 5 - 2, which equals 3.
  2. Try 10 - (5 - 2). That is 10 - 3, which equals 7. They don't match!
* **The operation of division does not satisfy the associative property.**
  1. Try (16 / 4) / 2. That is 4 / 2, which equals 2.
  2. Try 16 / (4 / 2). That is 16 / 2, which equals 8. They don't match!

### Summary of Moving vs. Grouping

| Property | What it does | Addition Example | Multiplication Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Commutative** | Moves the items around | x + y = y + x | x * y = y * x |
| **Associative** | Changes the parentheses | (x + y) + z = x + (y + z) | (x * y) * z = x * (y * z) |

## 3. The Ultimate Mental Math Hack: The Distributive Property

This is the coolest trick you will learn. The first two properties only let you do one thing at a time (all adding, or all multiplying). But the **distributive property** is an awesome bridge that lets you mix multiplication with addition or subtraction.

**The distributive property of multiplication over addition allows evaluating the product of a value and a sum by first multiplying the value by each addend separately and adding the resulting products.**

> **The Bridge (Addition)**
> **The algebraic representation of the distributive property over addition is a times the grouped sum of b and c equals a times b plus a times c.**
> Written simply: a(b + c) = a * b + a * c

It works with subtraction as well. **The distributive property of multiplication over subtraction allows evaluating the product of a value and a difference by first multiplying the value by each term separately and subtracting the resulting products.**

> **The Bridge (Subtraction)**
> Written simply: a(b - c) = a * b - a * c

### Why do we care?
Because it basically turns you into a human calculator. **Applying the distributive property enables mental math calculations by breaking down a difficult multiplication problem into the sum (or difference) of two easier multiplication problems.**

Imagine you are saving your allowance to buy 6 video games that cost \$98 each. How much money do you need?
1. The math problem is 6 * 98.
2. 98 is really close to 100. So let's write 98 as a small subtraction problem: (100 - 2).
3. Now our problem looks like this: 6 * (100 - 2).
4. Distribute (hand out) the 6 to both numbers inside by multiplying: (6 * 100) and (6 * 2).
5. Do those two easy multiplication problems: 600 and 12.
6. Subtract the answers: 600 - 12.
7. The total is 588. 

What about an addition example, like 7 * 43? 
1. Break 43 apart into an easy sum: (40 + 3).
2. Write the whole problem out: 7 * (40 + 3).
3. Distribute the 7 to both numbers inside: (7 * 40) and (7 * 3).
4. Do the easy multiplication parts: 280 and 21.
5. Add those together: 280 + 21.
6. The total is 301.

## 4. The Unsung Heroes: Identities, Zeros, and Inverses

To really master math, you need to know a few special "anchor" numbers. These rules might seem super obvious, but they are the glue that holds all of math together.

### Doing Nothing Gracefully: The Identity Properties
An "identity" in math is a rule that lets you do something to a number, but leaves it completely unchanged.

* **The additive identity property states that the sum of any given number and zero is the original given number.** If you add 0 to 7, you just get 7.
* **The multiplicative identity property states that the product of any given number and one is the original given number.** If you multiply 14 by 1, you just get 14. 

### The Annihilator: Property of Zero
While zero does nothing when you are adding, it is a total boss when you are multiplying.
* **The multiplicative property of zero states that the product of any number and zero is exactly zero.** Think of zero like a black hole here. Even if you have 1,000,000 * 0, the answer gets sucked up and becomes 0.

### Pushing the Undo Button: The Inverse Properties
If you do something in a video game and mess up, you want an undo button. Inverses are math's undo buttons. They reset you back to your identity numbers!

**The additive inverse of a given number is the unique number that yields zero when added to the given number.** If you take 5 steps forward in a game, how do you get back to the start (zero)? You take 5 steps backward!

> **Fact: The additive inverse of a positive number is the negative counterpart of the identical magnitude.** (Magnitude just means the size of the number). 
> Here is how you do it:
> 1. Pick a positive number, like 12.
> 2. Find its exact opposite: -12.
> 3. Add them together: 12 + (-12). 
> 4. The result is exactly 0. 

**The multiplicative inverse of a given nonzero number is the unique number that yields one when multiplied by the given number.** If you multiply a number by 4, how do you undo it to get back to 1? 

> **Fact: The multiplicative inverse of a mathematical fraction is the reciprocal of that specific fraction.** (Reciprocal is just a fancy word for flipping a fraction upside down).
> Here is how you do it:
> 1. Start with a fraction, like 2/3.
> 2. Flip the top number and the bottom number to find the reciprocal: 3/2.
> 3. Multiply them straight across: (2 * 3) / (3 * 2).
> 4. That gives you 6/6.
> 5. Any number divided by itself is 1! You did it!

## Conclusion: Putting it all Together for the Praxis Core

When you are taking a big test, you will see math problems that look really scary at first. Your brain might tell you to just start doing the hard math. *Don't do it!* 

Look at the numbers like a puzzle. Can you **commute** the numbers to put friendly pieces together? Can you change the parentheses to **associate** groups into easy tens or hundreds? Can you break apart an ugly number and **distribute** your multiplication to make two super easy problems? 

You don't even need to shout out, "I am using the associative property!" You just need to know how the groups work, remember that subtraction and division don't like to be flipped, and use these tricks to make your test a breeze. Math is just a playground. Once you know the rules, it's actually really fun to play!
