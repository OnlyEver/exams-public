Consider the mundane act of counting a handful of mixed [coins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coin). You do not mechanically add each value in the exact sequence it rests in your palm; you instinctively group the [quarters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quarter_%28United_States_coin%29), pair [dimes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dime_%28United_States_coin%29) and [nickels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nickel_%28United_States_coin%29) to create neat multiples of ten, and leave the [pennies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Penny_%28United_States_coin%29) for the end. This intuitive mental choreography is possible only because [arithmetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic) obeys a set of universal architectural rules. Basic number properties are not arbitrary vocabulary words to be memorized; they are the fundamental permissions that allow us to bend, reorder, and simplify numbers without breaking the mathematical truth of the universe. For an educator preparing for the [Praxis Core](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Praxis_test), mastering these properties means looking past the rote steps of a [calculation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculation) to see the invisible scaffolding holding the numbers together. 

![When sorting a handful of mixed coins, we naturally apply mathematical properties to efficiently group and combine compatible values.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Contando_Dinheiro_(8228640).jpg)

Understanding these properties transforms arithmetic from a rigid set of instructions into a flexible tool. It allows you to recognize how [operations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_%28mathematics%29) are structured, manipulate expressions effortlessly, and teach students to view [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics) as a fluid, logical language rather than a punishing obstacle course. 

## The [Hierarchy of Operations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Order_of_operations): Reading the Mathematical Sentence

Before we can begin moving numbers around, we must understand how mathematical operations are bundled. Mathematics is read much like a language, but instead of [punctuation marks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Punctuation) like [commas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comma) and [periods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Full_stop), it utilizes structural [brackets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket).

**Grouping symbols such as [parentheses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket) dictate which arithmetic operations must be performed first within an [algebraic expression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_expression).** When you encounter an expression like $3 \times (4 + 5)$, the parentheses are an absolute command: evaluate the relationship between 4 and 5 before allowing the [multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication) to interact with them. Understanding these boundaries is essential, because the properties we are about to explore are largely tools for either respecting, navigating, or legally circumventing these parentheses.

![The standard order of operations dictates the sequence in which mathematical expressions are resolved, serving as the foundational grammar of algebraic structures.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Order_of_operations.svg)

## The [Commutative Property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_property): The Freedom to Rearrange

The word "commute" means to travel or move around. In mathematics, the [commutative property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_property) governs whether numbers can physically trade places without altering the final reality of the calculation. 

### [Addition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Addition) and Multiplication

Nature is highly cooperative when we are simply pooling quantities together or [scaling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scaling_%28geometry%29) them up. 

> **The [Commutative Property of Addition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_property)** states that changing the order of [addends](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Addition) does not change the resulting [sum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Summation). 
> *Algebraic Representation:* $a + b = b + a$

If you pour 3 [ounces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ounce) of water into a [beaker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beaker_%28laboratory_equipment%29) and then add 5 ounces, you possess 8 ounces. If you pour 5 ounces first and then 3 ounces, you still possess 8 ounces. **The commutative property allows numbers in an addition expression to be rearranged to combine compatible numbers that sum to a [multiple](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiple_%28mathematics%29) of ten.** For instance, faced with $17 + 46 + 13$, a clever mathematical mind rearranges the expression to $17 + 13 + 46$. The $17$ and $13$ instantly snap together to form $30$, turning a tedious calculation into an effortless $30 + 46 = 76$.

![The commutative property of addition visually demonstrated: changing the sequence in which objects are grouped does not alter the total sum.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Commutative_Addition.svg)

Multiplication shares this exact same spatial freedom.

> **The [Commutative Property of Multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_property)** states that changing the order of [factors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divisor) does not change the resulting [product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Product_%28mathematics%29). 
> *Algebraic Representation:* $a \times b = b \times a$

A [rectangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle) that is 4 units wide and 5 units long contains exactly the same 20 square units of [area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area) as a rectangle that is 5 units wide and 4 units long. You are merely viewing the same rectangle from a different angle.

![Because multiplication is commutative, treating the length as the multiplier and width as the multiplicand yields the exact same total area as reversing their roles.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Illustration_for_the_area_of_a_rectangle.svg)

### The Strict Limits of [Subtraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subtraction) and [Division](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_%28mathematics%29)

We must be equally precise about where these freedoms fail. **The operation of [subtraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subtraction) does not satisfy the commutative property.** If you have \$10 in your [bank account](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bank_account) and write a [check](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cheque) for \$2, you have \$8. If you have \$2 and attempt to write a check for \$10, you have a financial disaster. Order dictates reality here: \$10 - 2 \neq 2 - 10$.

Similarly, **the operation of [division](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_%28mathematics%29) does not satisfy the commutative property.** Sharing 10 pizzas among 2 hungry students yields a joyous 5 pizzas per student (\$10 \div 2$). Attempting to share 2 pizzas among 10 students leaves everyone wanting more ($2 \div 10 = 0.2$). The roles of the [dividend](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_%28mathematics%29) (what is being broken up) and the [divisor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divisor) (how many ways it is being broken) are entirely non-interchangeable. 

## The [Associative Property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associative_property): The Power to Regroup

While the commutative property is about *moving* numbers, the [associative property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associative_property) is about *grouping* them. The root word "associate" means to join or partner with. If the order of the numbers remains perfectly static, does it matter which two numbers decide to interact first?

> **The [Associative Property of Addition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associative_property)** states that changing the grouping of addends does not change the resulting sum.
> *Algebraic Representation:* $(a + b) + c = a + (b + c)$

Imagine a line of three people holding [cash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cash): Person A has \$5, Person B has \$10, and Person C has \$15. If Person A and B combine their money first (\$15) and then add Person C's money, the total is \$30. If A waits while B and C combine theirs first (\$25), and then A hands over their \$5, the total is still \$30. The brackets shift; the [sequence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sequence) of numbers does not.

![The associative property allows us to shift the grouping of addends without changing the total amount, illustrated here with segmented rods: $2 + (1 + 3) = (2 + 1) + 3$.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/AdditionAsc.svg)

This works exactly the same way for scaling factors.

> **The [Associative Property of Multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associative_property)** states that changing the grouping of factors does not change the resulting product.
> *Algebraic Representation:* $(a \times b) \times c = a \times (b \times c)$

This is an immensely powerful tool for [mental arithmetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mental_calculation). **The associative property allows a string of multiplication operations to be regrouped to form easier partial products without changing the final result.** 
Consider evaluating \$14 \times 5 \times 2$. 
If we group the first two: $(14 \times 5) \times 2$, we are forced to calculate $70 \times 2 = 140$. 
But if we group the last two: \$14 \times (5 \times 2)$, the calculation becomes \$14 \times 10$. The answer is instantly identifiable as \$140$. We manipulated the parentheses to create a friendly multiple of ten.

### Where Association Fails

Just as with the commutative property, directional operations like subtraction and division reject associative freedoms. **The operation of subtraction does not satisfy the associative property.**
Take $(10 - 5) - 2$. The parentheses tell us to evaluate \$10 - 5$ first, yielding $5$. Then $5 - 2 = 3$. 
Now shift the grouping: \$10 - (5 - 2)$. Here, $5 - 2 = 3$, and \$10 - 3 = 7$. Because $3 \neq 7$, the associative property utterly fails for subtraction.

Likewise, **the operation of division does not satisfy the associative property.** Division demands a strict adherence to [left-to-right](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Order_of_operations) processing precisely because shifting the parentheses wildly distorts the [quotient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quotient).

## The [Distributive Property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distributive_property): Bridging the Operations

The commutative and associative properties deal with one operation at a time—all addition, or all multiplication. But what happens when multiplication and addition are forced to occupy the same space? Enter the [distributive property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distributive_property), arguably the most important mechanical lever in all of [algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra).

**The [distributive property of multiplication over addition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distributive_property) allows evaluating the product of a value and a sum by first multiplying the value by each addend separately and adding the resulting products.**

> *Algebraic Representation:* $a \times (b + c) = (a \times b) + (a \times c)$

Imagine you are ordering 3 [combo meals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Value_meal). Each meal consists of a \$5 [burger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamburger) and a \$2 [soda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soft_drink). 
You can calculate the cost in two ways:
1. Find the total cost of one combo meal first: $(5 + 2) = 7$. Then multiply by the 3 meals: \$3 \times 7 = 21$.
2. Multiply the quantity of each item separately: \$3 \times 5 = 15$ for the burgers, and \$3 \times 2 = 6$ for the sodas. Combine them: \$15 + 6 = 21$.

The [multiplier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication) is *distributed* to every [term](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Term_%28mathematics%29) inside the parentheses.

This property holds true even when subtracting inside the parentheses. **The [distributive property of multiplication over subtraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distributive_property) allows evaluating the product of a value and a [difference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subtraction) by first multiplying the value by each term separately and subtracting the resulting products.** 
$$a \times (b - c) = (a \times b) - (a \times c)$$

**Applying the distributive property enables [mental math](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mental_calculation) calculations by breaking down a difficult multiplication problem into the sum of two easier multiplication problems.** 
If a student encounters \$6 \times 98$, the traditional [vertical algorithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication_algorithm) takes time and scratch paper. But a mathematician sees \$98$ as $(100 - 2)$. 
By applying the distributive property:
$$6 \times (100 - 2) = (6 \times 100) - (6 \times 2)$$
$$600 - 12 = 588$$
The complex operation has been instantly demystified into a simple mental subtraction.

## Identities and [Zero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/0): The Anchors of Value

Every [mathematical system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_structure) requires reference points—elements that allow us to step forward without moving at all. These are known as [identity elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Identity_element).

**The [additive identity property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Additive_identity) states that the sum of any given number and [zero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/0) is the original given number.** Zero is the mirror of addition; it reflects back exactly what you gave it. ($a + 0 = a$).

![The additive identity property in action: adding zero to any quantity leaves its value perfectly unaltered.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/AdditionZero.svg)

**The [multiplicative identity property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplicative_identity) states that the product of any given number and [one](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1) is the original given number.** When you multiply by 1, you are taking "one copy" of a number, which leaves its [magnitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnitude_%28mathematics%29) entirely unchanged. ($a \times 1 = a$).

However, zero plays a drastically different role when exposed to multiplication. **The [multiplicative property of zero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero-product_property) states that the product of any number and zero is exactly zero.** Think of multiplication as scaling an object. If you take zero copies of an object, no matter how massive that object initially was, it ceases to exist in your [inventory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inventory). ($a \times 0 = 0$).

## [Inverses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse_element): Undoing the Action

In [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics), every action has an [equal and opposite reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_laws_of_motion). In mathematics, this concept is captured by "inverses." An [inverse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse_element) is a mathematical undo button; it is an operation combined with a specific number designed to return you to the identity elements we just discussed.

### [Additive Inverses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Additive_inverse)

**The [additive inverse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Additive_inverse) of a given number is the unique number that yields zero when added to the given number.** It destroys the original value to return to the additive identity (zero). 

**The additive inverse of a [positive number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positive_real_numbers) is the [negative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Negative_number) counterpart of the identical magnitude.** If you walk 5 steps forward (a value of \$+5$), the only way to return to your starting point of zero is to walk 5 steps backward (a value of \$-5$). 
$$5 + (-5) = 0$$

![On a standard number line, a value and its additive inverse are located at identical distances from zero but in opposite directions.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Number_line_with_numbers_-3_to_3.svg)

### [Multiplicative Inverses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplicative_inverse)

Multiplication has its own method of returning to baseline. **The [multiplicative inverse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplicative_inverse) of a given nonzero number is the unique number that yields one when multiplied by the given number.** Notice that it returns us to the *multiplicative identity* (one), not zero. 

How do we turn a number like \$4$ into \$1$ purely through multiplication? We multiply it by its [fractional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fraction) opposite. **The multiplicative inverse of a mathematical fraction is the [reciprocal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplicative_inverse) of that specific fraction.** 
To find the reciprocal, we invert the [numerator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fraction) and the [denominator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fraction). The number \$4$ can be written as $\frac{4}{1}$. Its multiplicative inverse is $\frac{1}{4}$. 
$$4 \times \frac{1}{4} = \frac{4}{4} = 1$$
If you have the fraction $\frac{2}{3}$, its multiplicative inverse is $\frac{3}{2}$, because $\frac{2}{3} \times \frac{3}{2} = \frac{6}{6} = 1$.

![A visual representation of one-fourth. The integer 4 and the fraction 1/4 act as multiplicative inverses; combining four quarters returns us exactly to the multiplicative identity of 1 whole.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Cake_quarters.svg)

## Summary Comparison of Properties

To synthesize these structural laws, review the table below. Notice how the shape of the expression provides the greatest hint about which mathematical law is in play.

| Property | Addition Example | Multiplication Example | Core Function |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **[Commutative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_property)** | \$4 + 7 = 7 + 4$ | $6 \times 3 = 3 \times 6$ | **Reordering.** The sequence of numbers physically shifts. |
| **[Associative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associative_property)** | $(2 + 3) + 4 = 2 + (3 + 4)$ | $(2 \times 3) \times 4 = 2 \times (3 \times 4)$ | **Regrouping.** Order stays identical; parentheses move. |
| **[Distributive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distributive_property)** | N/A | \$5(x + 2) = 5x + 10$ | **Expanding.** A multiplier is applied to all terms in a group. |
| **[Identity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Identity_element)** | \$8 + 0 = 8$ | $8 \times 1 = 8$ | **Maintaining.** Value remains totally unchanged. |
| **[Inverse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse_element)** | $8 + (-8) = 0$ | $8 \times \frac{1}{8} = 1$ | **Undoing.** Combining values to reach $0$ (add) or $1$ (multiply). |

By deeply absorbing these properties, you do far more than pass a mathematics exam. You develop an intuitive [fluency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluency) with numbers. When you recognize that \$8 \times 25$ is really just \$8 \times (20 + 5)$, or that \$12 + 89 + 88$ is merely $100 + 89$ in disguise, you stop doing arithmetic and start conducting it.
