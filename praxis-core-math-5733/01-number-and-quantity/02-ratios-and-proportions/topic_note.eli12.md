## 1. What Exactly is a Ratio?

If you have 10 video games and your friend has 5, you could just say, "I have 5 more games than you." That's basic math. But what if we want to talk about how your collections compare in size? 

That's where ratios come in! **A ratio is a mathematical relationship comparing the sizes or amounts of two or more quantities.** 

Think of it as a snapshot. **A ratio acts as an independent mathematical expression representing a single comparison.** It stands all on its own, showing exactly how one pile of stuff compares to another pile of stuff. 

### The Language of Ratios
We have a few easy ways to write down these comparisons. If we are comparing your 10 games to your friend's 5 games, we can write it in three different ways:
1. **Ratios can be expressed verbally using the word 'to', such as 'a to b'.** (For example, "10 to 5").
2. **Ratios can be expressed using a colon, written in the format a:b.** (For example, 10:5).
3. **Ratios can be expressed as a fraction, written in the format a/b.** (For example, 10/5).

### The Golden Rule of Ratios: Order Dictates Everything
If I tell you the ratio of "controllers to players" is 1 to 4, that makes sense. It means there is one controller for four people. That's a lot of waiting for your turn! But if you swap that around and say the ratio is 4 to 1, suddenly you have four controllers for just one player. Awesome for you, but very different! 

> **Warning: The Order Matters!**
> **The order of numbers in a ratio must strictly match the order described in the ratio's corresponding verbal language.** Because of this rule, **the ratio a:b is not mathematically equal to the ratio b:a unless quantity a is exactly equal to quantity b.**

### Units: Apples to Apples, or Apples to Oranges?
Ratios are amazing because you can use them to compare almost anything. 
* **Same Things:** **Ratios can compare quantities possessing the exact same units of measurement.** For example, comparing 3 slices of pepperoni pizza to 2 slices of cheese pizza. 
* **Different Things:** **Ratios can also compare quantities possessing completely different units of measurement.** For example, comparing 150 miles to 3 hours of driving.

When you do this, it gets a special name. **A ratio comparing two quantities with entirely different units of measurement is specifically classified as a rate.** If you look at a car's dashboard, "miles per hour" is a rate! 

We can make rates even simpler. **A unit rate is a specific type of ratio where the second quantity in the comparison is exactly one.** 
Here are the steps to find a unit rate:
1. Start with your rate: 150 miles / 3 hours.
2. Divide the top number by the bottom number: 150 / 3 = 50.
3. Write your new rate over 1: 50 miles / 1 hour. 
Now you know the unit rate is 50 miles per hour!

## 2. Parts, Wholes, and Expanding the Cast

Ratios can compare different parts of a group. Let's pretend you have a sports team with 12 girls and 8 boys. That means there are 20 kids total on the team.

| Type of Ratio | Definition | Example from our Team |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Part-to-Part Ratio** | **A part-to-part ratio compares one specific subset of a group to another specific subset of the same group.** | The ratio of girls to boys is 12:8. |
| **Part-to-Whole Ratio** | **A part-to-whole ratio compares one specific subset of a group to the entire group.** | The ratio of girls to total kids is 12:20. |

### The Three-Part Harmony
Sometimes you are mixing more than two things together. Think about making a giant fruit smoothie: you might need strawberries, bananas, and yogurt. 

**Ratios comparing three quantities are typically written using colons in the format a:b:c.** 
If your smoothie needs 1 cup of strawberries, 2 cups of bananas, and 3 cups of yogurt, your ratio is 1:2:3. 

How much stuff is in the blender? 
**The total number of parts in an a:b:c ratio is calculated by adding the values of a, b, and c together.** 
Let's calculate it step-by-step:
1. Look at your ratio parts: 1 (strawberries), 2 (bananas), and 3 (yogurt).
2. Add them all up: 1 + 2 + 3 = 6.
3. Every "batch" of this smoothie makes 6 parts total!

## 3. Equivalent Ratios and The Magic of Scaling

Imagine zooming in on a picture on your tablet. The picture gets larger, but it doesn't look stretched or weird. The width and the height grow together perfectly. This is what we call equivalent ratios. **Equivalent ratios are distinct ratio expressions that represent the exact same relationship between quantities.** 

How do we make them? We stretch or shrink them evenly!
* **Multiplying every term of a ratio by the same non-zero number creates an equivalent ratio.** (For example, 1:2 turns into 3:6 if we multiply both sides by 3).
* **Dividing every term of a ratio by the same non-zero number creates an equivalent ratio.** (For example, 10:20 turns into 1:2 if we divide both sides by 10).

The secret number you use to multiply or divide has a special name. **A scale factor is the single multiplier used to scale all quantities in a given ratio up or down proportionally.** 

### Stripping it Down to the Studs
In math, we like things neat and simple. Just like fractions, **ratios are simplified to their lowest terms by dividing all quantities within the ratio by their greatest common factor** (which is the biggest number that divides perfectly into both parts). 

Let's simplify the ratio 18:24 step-by-step:
1. Find the biggest number that divides evenly into both 18 and 24. That number is 6.
2. Divide the first part: 18 / 6 = 3.
3. Divide the second part: 24 / 6 = 4.
4. Write your new, clean ratio: 3:4.
It means the exact same thing as 18:24, but it is way easier to read!

## 4. Solving Real-World Mixture Puzzles (The "One Part" Trick)

Sometimes you will get a puzzle like this:
*You and your friends earn \$50 total from doing yard work. You agree to split the money between three people (Alex, Taylor, and Sam) using the ratio 2:3:5. How much money does Taylor get?*

Don't stress! We use the "One Part" trick. 

Here are the steps to find Taylor's share:
1. Find the total parts: Add the ratio numbers together. 2 + 3 + 5 = 10 total parts.
2. Find the value of one part: **To find an unknown part-to-whole quantity using a ratio, divide the real-world total quantity by the sum of the ratio parts to find the value of one single ratio part.** 
   * \$50 / 10 parts = \$5 for each part.
3. Scale it up for Taylor: Taylor gets 3 parts. Multiply Taylor's parts by the value of one part. 
   * 3 parts x \$5 = $15. Taylor gets $15!

> **Working Backwards:**
> What if you only knew that one "part" of the yard work money was worth \$5, and you wanted to figure out how much the whole group made? That's simple too! **The total real-world quantity of a mixture defined by a ratio is calculated by multiplying the value of a single ratio part by the sum of all parts in the ratio.**
> Let's do it step-by-step:
> 1. Find the sum of all parts in the ratio: 2 + 3 + 5 = 10 parts.
> 2. Multiply the total parts by the value of one single part: 10 parts x \$5 = \$50 total.

## 5. Proportions: The Grand Equation

Now we are leveling up. We've talked about ratios, which are just single comparisons. But what happens if we put an equal sign between two ratios? We create a proportion.

**A proportion is a mathematical equation stating that two different ratios are equal to each other.** 

Pay close attention to the difference: **A ratio acts as an independent mathematical expression representing a single comparison.** But **a proportion acts as a full equation connecting two independent ratio expressions.** 

### a/b = c/d

**A proportion is typically written in the fractional format a/b = c/d.** 

It is just math's way of saying, "The rule that connects a to b is the exact same rule that connects c to d." 

---

## 6. The Elegance of Cross-Multiplication

What if you have a proportion, but you are missing a piece of the puzzle? Like 3/4 = x/20?

Because a proportion is a real equation, we can use a cool trick to find the missing piece. **Cross-multiplication is an algebraic method used to solve for an unknown variable within a proportion.** 

### a x d = b x c

Here is the rule: **In the proportion a/b = c/d, the product of a and d equals the product of b and c according to cross-multiplication rules.** ("Product" is just the math word for your answer when you multiply).

Let's use this rule to solve our missing variable puzzle, 3/4 = x/20, step-by-step:
1. Multiply the top left by the bottom right (a x d): 3 x 20 = 60.
2. Multiply the bottom left by the top right (b x c): 4 x x (which we just write as 4x).
3. Set them equal to each other: 60 = 4x.
4. Solve for the missing letter by dividing: 60 / 4 = 15.
5. So, x = 15! 

### The Ultimate Litmus Test
Cross-multiplication isn't just for finding missing numbers. It's also a great way to check if two fractions are actually equal! 

**Two ratios form a true proportion only if their resulting cross-products are perfectly equal.**

If someone asks you, "Is 5/7 equal to 7/9?", you don't have to guess. Just cross-multiply step-by-step!
1. Multiply the first diagonal: 5 x 9 = 45.
2. Multiply the second diagonal: 7 x 7 = 49.
3. Compare the answers: 45 does not equal 49.
Because the answers are different, those two ratios do *not* form a true proportion!

## The Takeaway

Whenever you are figuring out math problems, remember that ratios and proportions are all about fairness and seeing how things connect! 
* Keep your **order** strict.
* Remember that **ratios** are the single comparisons, while **proportions** are the equal signs bringing them together. 
* Use the **"one part" trick** to solve puzzles with mixtures.
* Use **cross-multiplication** to easily find missing pieces.

Math is just a secret code we use to figure out the world—from video game stats to slicing a pizza perfectly. Now you have the tools to crack the code. Keep practicing and have fun!
