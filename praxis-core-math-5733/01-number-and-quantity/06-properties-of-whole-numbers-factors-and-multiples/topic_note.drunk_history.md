Bartender, leave the pitcher! Listen to me, slide that coaster out of the way and look at my eyes. You’re taking the Praxis Core math test tomorrow, and you’re treating numbers like they’re just... math. Dead little symbols on a page. They’re not! They are the most cutthroat, dramatic, reality-TV-meets-mob-syndicate characters on the planet! Once you understand the street politics of numbers, you won't have to memorize a single thing for that exam. 

Let’s talk about the anatomy of the crime family. First, you have your factors and your multiples. Think of a whole number as a giant pile of heist money. A factor of a whole number is an integer that divides the whole number without leaving a remainder. It’s a henchman who can carve up the loot perfectly, leaving absolutely zero loose change—no messy fractions—for the feds to find. Now, in this underworld, there are two universal laws of loyalty. First, the number 1 is the Godfather. The number 1 is a factor of every whole number. He takes a piece of everyone’s action. Second, every whole number is a factor of itself. The boss always takes a cut of his own operation! 

But a mob boss doesn't just sit in a basement slicing up pies; they expand! A multiple of a whole number is the product of that whole number and any integer. It’s what happens when the boss takes their operation and multiplies it across different city blocks using different street thugs (integers). And of course, the boss’s very first piece of turf is their own front yard, meaning every whole number is a multiple of itself. 

Now, when you look at the bosses, they fall into two distinct psychological profiles: the lone wolf assassins and the reality TV divas. 

A prime number is a whole number greater than 1 that has exactly two distinct positive factors. That’s the lone wolf. The only two distinct positive factors of a prime number are 1 and the prime number itself. They only talk to the Godfather (1) and themselves in the mirror. No entourage. Just clean, untraceable hits.

On the flip side, a composite number is a whole number greater than 1 that has more than two distinct positive factors. These are the gossip-hungry divas. They’ve got entourages, makeup artists, hangers-on. You want to divide 12? You gotta deal with 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, and 12. It’s a total circus! 

Wait, what about the Godfather? What about 1? Listen closely because this is the biggest scandal in the numerical universe: The number 1 is neither a prime number nor a composite number. Why? Because to be prime, you need *exactly two distinct* factors. The number 1 only talks to itself. It has only one factor. It’s too isolated to be prime, and far too solitary to be composite. It exists in a total vacuum of power!

Now we pivot to the romantic drama. Casa Amor. Evens and odds. Parity is just a question of who gets a rose and who gets dumped on national television. An even number is any integer that is evenly divisible by 2. It’s the perfect couple. Nobody is left out. An odd number is any integer that leaves a remainder of 1 when divided by 2. It’s a total messy breakup! Everyone pairs off, but one person—that tragic remainder of 1—is left crying by the pool. 

And don’t let the Praxis test fool you into questioning zero. The number 0 is an even number! Why? Because if you have zero contestants, and you divide them into two groups, you get zero. Nobody is left crying by the pool! It is a perfectly clean, drama-free split. 

Let's look at what happens when these people start mingling at the bar. Addition and subtraction are just social math. 
The sum of two even numbers is always an even number, and the difference between two even numbers is always an even number. Think about it: a happy couple meets a happy couple, or a couple leaves. Everyone still has a partner. No drama.
Now, the sum of two odd numbers is always an even number! And the difference between two odd numbers is always an even number! This is the magic of reality TV! Two lonely, sobbing singles (the remainders) run into each other, realize they are perfect for each other, and boom! They pair up! The remainder vanishes. It’s a beautiful, even romance.
But... the sum of an even number and an odd number is always an odd number. And the difference between an even number and an odd number is always an odd number. You put a happy couple in a room with a crying, newly dumped single person? Total awkward third-wheel situation. The oddness infects the room.

But multiplication? Multiplication is not dating. Multiplication is a genetic mutation. Think of an even number as carrying the "2" virus. The product of two even numbers is always an even number—they’re just passing the virus back and forth. But watch this: the product of an even number and an odd number is always an even number! The even number sneaks its "2" DNA into the mix and turns the whole offspring even! The only way to survive the virus? Total quarantine. The product of two odd numbers is always an odd number. There are no 2s in the first number, no 2s in the second. It is mathematically impossible to breed an even number out of pure oddness! 

Okay, wipe that spilled beer, we are moving to the bouncers! The Praxis is going to ask you to figure out if massive numbers are divisible by smaller numbers, and you don’t have time to do the paperwork. You gotta know the club rules.

Rule 1: Judging by the shoes. The bouncers for 2, 5, and 10 do not care about your entourage, they only look at the guy standing at the very back of the line—the final digit.
A whole number is divisible by 2 if the final digit of the whole number is 0, 2, 4, 6, or 8. Just checking for even shoes!
A whole number is divisible by 5 if the final digit of the whole number is exactly 0 or 5. 
A whole number is divisible by 10 if the final digit of the whole number is exactly 0. That’s the ultra-VIP lounge.

What about 4? The bouncer for 4 is slightly more suspicious. A whole number is divisible by 4 if the last two digits of the whole number form a number that is divisible by 4. The bouncer literally cuts off the last two guys in line and says, "If you two can divide by 4, the whole crew gets in." 

Then you have the bouncers with the magic scales: 3 and 9. They don't care who is in the back. They make the *entire crew* step on the scale. 
A whole number is divisible by 3 if the sum of all the digits in the whole number is divisible by 3. 
A whole number is divisible by 9 if the sum of all the digits in the whole number is divisible by 9. 

And the bouncer for 6? He’s just lazy. He delegates. A whole number is divisible by 6 if the whole number is divisible by both 2 and 3. If you pass the shoe check for 2, and the scale check for 3, the bouncer for 6 just waves you through the velvet rope!

Finally, the epic turf wars. When two giant numbers clash, they have to sit down and negotiate. That’s your GCF and LCM. 
The Greatest Common Factor of two whole numbers is the largest positive integer that divides both whole numbers without leaving a remainder. It’s the highest-ranking double-agent they *both* employ. It’s looking backward into their organizations to find the biggest shared asset. 
But the Least Common Multiple? That is looking into the future! The Least Common Multiple of two whole numbers is the smallest positive integer that is a multiple of both whole numbers. It’s the absolute first time their expanding territories are going to crash into each other on the map.
