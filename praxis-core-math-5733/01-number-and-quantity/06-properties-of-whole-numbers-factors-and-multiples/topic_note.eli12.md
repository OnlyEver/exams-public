## Part 1: Anatomy of a Number: Factors and Multiples

Imagine you are building with LEGOs. You have a big tower, let's say it's made of 12 blocks. How did you build it? You could snap together two 6-block pieces, or three 4-block pieces, or four 3-block pieces. The smaller pieces you use to build a number are called its factors. The giant towers you can build *using* that number are called its multiples.

### The Factors (The Ingredients)

> **A factor of a whole number is an integer that divides the whole number without leaving a remainder.**

Think of factors as the exact ingredients that fit perfectly inside a number. Let's find the factors of 12 together:

1. Pick a number to divide 12 by, like 3.
2. Divide 12 by 3. The answer is exactly 4.
3. Check for leftovers (decimals or fractions). There are no leftovers!

Because it fits perfectly, that means 3 and 4 are both factors of 12.

There are two freebies you should always remember in the math world:
* **The number 1 is a factor of every whole number.** If you have 10 slices of pizza, you can easily put them all in 1 giant box. You can divide any number by 1!
* **Every whole number is a factor of itself.** If you have 10 slices of pizza, you can give 1 slice to 10 friends. You can always divide a number by itself!

### The Multiples (The Structures)

If factors are the ingredients, multiples are the giant things you build with them. 

> **A multiple of a whole number is the product of that whole number and any integer.**

Let's say our starting number is 5. What are its multiples? We find them by multiplying:

1. Multiply 5 by 1 to get 5.
2. Multiply 5 by 2 to get 10.
3. Multiply 5 by 3 to get 15.

So, 5, 10, and 15 are multiples of 5! Do you see the very first step there? 5 x 1 = 5. This shows us a cool rule: **Every whole number is a multiple of itself.**

### Primes and Composites: Atoms vs. Molecules

When we look closely at numbers to find their factors, we notice they fall into two teams: prime numbers and composite numbers.

> **A prime number is a whole number greater than 1 that has exactly two distinct positive factors.**

What are those two factors? Let's check the number 7:

1. Try to divide 7 by 2, 3, 4, 5, or 6. None of them work perfectly!
2. Divide 7 by 1. That works perfectly (the answer is 7).
3. Divide 7 by 7. That works perfectly (the answer is 1).

**The only two distinct positive factors of a prime number are 1 and the prime number itself.** They are the rarest items in the math game. You can't break them down into smaller pieces!

> **A composite number is a whole number greater than 1 that has more than two distinct positive factors.**

Take the number 8. Let's find its factors:

1. Divide 8 by 1. It works! Factor is 1.
2. Divide 8 by 2. It works! Factor is 2.
3. Divide 8 by 4. It works! Factor is 4.
4. Divide 8 by 8. It works! Factor is 8.

Because it has 1, 2, 4, and 8 as factors, it is composite. It's made up of more than just two pieces.

*Wait a minute,* you might ask, *what about the number 1?* 

This is a super famous math fact. **The number 1 is neither a prime number nor a composite number.** Why? A prime number needs *exactly two* different positive factors. But the number 1 only has *one* factor: itself! It just doesn't have the right pieces to join the prime or composite teams.

## Part 2: X-Ray Vision: The Divisibility Rules

On your test, you will need to know if a big number fits perfectly inside another number. You definitely don't have time to do long division for every single choice! Luckily, there are cheat codes. We can test divisibility just by looking at the digits. 

### Rule 1: Judging a Number by Its Tail

For some numbers, we only need to look at the very end of the big number. Let's learn the rules:

*   **Divisible by 2:** A whole number is divisible by 2 if the final digit of the whole number is 0, 2, 4, 6, or 8.
*   **Divisible by 5:** A whole number is divisible by 5 if the final digit of the whole number is exactly 0 or 5.
*   **Divisible by 10:** A whole number is divisible by 10 if the final digit of the whole number is exactly 0.

What about the number 4? 
*   **Divisible by 4:** A whole number is divisible by 4 if the last two digits of the whole number form a number that is divisible by 4.

Let's test the number 3,724 step-by-step:

1. Look at the whole number: 3,724.
2. Ignore everything except the last two digits. The last two digits are 24.
3. Ask yourself: Is 24 divisible by 4? Yes, 24 divided by 4 is exactly 6.

So, 3,724 is easily divisible by 4!

### Rule 2: The Sum of the Parts

What if we want to check for 3 or 9? Looking at the last digit won't help. Instead, we have to add up the digits!

*   **Divisible by 3:** A whole number is divisible by 3 if the sum of all the digits in the whole number is divisible by 3. 

Let's test the number 111:

1. Add the digits together: 1 + 1 + 1 = 3.
2. Is 3 divisible by 3? Yes! So 111 is divisible by 3.

*   **Divisible by 9:** A whole number is divisible by 9 if the sum of all the digits in the whole number is divisible by 9.

Let's test the number 819:

1. Add the digits together: 8 + 1 + 9 = 18.
2. Is 18 divisible by 9? Yes, 18 divided by 9 is exactly 2. So 819 is divisible by 9.

### Rule 3: The Combination Lock

*   **Divisible by 6:** A whole number is divisible by 6 if the whole number is divisible by both 2 and 3. 

Since 6 is made by multiplying 2 x 3, any number that passes both the rule for 2 AND the rule for 3 is guaranteed to be a multiple of 6. Let's test the number 24:

1. Does it end in 0, 2, 4, 6, or 8? Yes, it ends in 4. (It passes the rule for 2).
2. Do the digits add up to a multiple of 3? Let's add: 2 + 4 = 6. Since 6 is divisible by 3, yes! (It passes the rule for 3).

Since it passes both checks, 24 is divisible by 6.

## Part 3: The Oddities of Evens and Odds

Let's talk about evens and odds. At its core, this is just about whether things can be paired up with a buddy, like in a game of dodgeball.

> **An even number is any integer that is evenly divisible by 2.**

If you have an even number of players, everyone gets a partner. Nobody is left out!

> **An odd number is any integer that leaves a remainder of 1 when divided by 2.**

If you have an odd number of players, you pair everyone up until one player is left totally alone. That lone "remainder of 1" makes the group odd.

Now, a big question: *Is zero even or odd?* 
Let's use our rule:

1. Divide 0 by 2.
2. The answer is 0.
3. Are there any remainders or leftover players? No!

Because there are no remainders, **the number 0 is an even number.**

### Parity Arithmetic: Don't Memorize, Visualize!

Your test will ask what happens when you add, subtract, or multiply evens and odds. Don't memorize these! Just picture the dodgeball players!

#### Addition and Subtraction

When adding or subtracting, just picture whether or not a lone player is left over.

*   **The sum of two even numbers is always an even number.** And **the difference between two even numbers is always an even number.** 
    *(Everyone had a partner before, so if you combine the teams or take some pairs away, everyone still has a partner.)*
*   **The sum of two odd numbers is always an even number.** And **the difference between two odd numbers is always an even number.** 
    *(Think about it! Both odd teams had one lonely, leftover player. When you add the teams together, those two leftovers finally find each other and become a pair! No more leftovers. It becomes even.)*
*   **The sum of an even number and an odd number is always an odd number.** And **the difference between an even number and an odd number is always an odd number.** 
    *(The even team has no leftovers. The odd team has one leftover. Put them together, and you still have that one awkward leftover player. The result is odd.)*

Here is a simple table to show how adding or subtracting "like" types makes an even, and "opposite" types makes an odd:

| Operation | Like Types | Operation | Opposite Types |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Even + Even** | **Even** | **Even + Odd** | **Odd** |
| **Even - Even** | **Even** | **Even - Odd** | **Odd** |
| **Odd + Odd** | **Even** | **Odd + Even** | **Odd** |
| **Odd - Odd** | **Even** | **Odd - Even** | **Odd** |

#### Multiplication: The "Even" Infection

Multiplication works like an infection in a zombie video game. Think of an even number as a zombie carrying the "even virus" (the number 2). Because an even number is a multiple of 2, there is a 2 hiding inside of it. 

When you multiply numbers together, if even *one* of those numbers is even, it spreads the virus and guarantees the final answer is evenly divisible by 2. 

*   **The product of two even numbers is always an even number.** *(Tons of 2s spreading the virus).*
*   **The product of an even number and an odd number is always an even number.** *(The even number sneaks the virus into the mix!)*
*   **The product of two odd numbers is always an odd number.** *(No 2s in the first number, no 2s in the second number. It is totally safe from the virus, so the answer stays totally odd).*

## Part 4: Finding Common Ground: GCF and LCM

When we look at two different numbers, it's super helpful to compare them. What pieces do they share? Where do they match up? This brings us to the GCF and the LCM.

### Greatest Common Factor (GCF)

> **The Greatest Common Factor of two whole numbers is the largest positive integer that divides both whole numbers without leaving a remainder.**

Imagine you have a piece of candy rope that is 12 inches long and another that is 18 inches long. You want to cut both of them into identical, smaller pieces to share, without wasting any candy. What's the biggest piece you can cut?

Let's find out step-by-step:

1. List all the factors of 12 (the numbers that divide perfectly into it). They are: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, and 12.
2. List all the factors of 18. They are: 1, 2, 3, 6, 9, and 18.
3. Circle the ones they share. They both share 1, 2, 3, and 6.
4. Pick the biggest one! The largest number on both lists is 6.

So, the GCF of 12 and 18 is 6. 

### Least Common Multiple (LCM)

> **The Least Common Multiple of two whole numbers is the smallest positive integer that is a multiple of both whole numbers.**

While GCF is about looking at the smaller building blocks, the LCM is about looking into the future to find where two numbers will eventually match up. 

Imagine two runners on a track. Runner A completes a lap every 4 minutes. Runner B completes a lap every 6 minutes. When will they cross the start line at the exact same time?

Let's solve it step-by-step:

1. List the multiples of 4 (like counting by 4s). They are: 4, 8, 12, 16, 20, 24...
2. List the multiples of 6 (counting by 6s). They are: 6, 12, 18, 24, 30...
3. Find the numbers they share. They both hit 12 and 24!
4. Pick the smallest one. We want the *Least* Common Multiple (the very first time they cross). The smallest matching number is 12.

So, the LCM of 4 and 6 is 12.
