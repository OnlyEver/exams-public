## 1. The Magic of the Base-10 Number System

Our numbering system is an awesome tool known as the base-10 system. Why ten? Probably because we have ten fingers! But the true superpower of this system is that the exact same symbol—like a "7"—can mean seven video games, seventy video games, or seven-thousandths of a video game. It all depends entirely on where you park that number.

### The Whole Number Hierarchy
Let’s start with solid, whole integers. An integer is just a whole number with no broken pieces. 

> **Core Principle:** **In an integer, the rightmost digit occupies the ones place.**

This is your starting line. If your score in a game is 4,532, that 2 at the very right is in the ones place. From here, we walk to the left. 

As you take steps to the left, your score blows up super fast. **Moving to the left from the ones place, the sequence of place values is tens, hundreds, thousands, ten-thousands, and hundred-thousands.** 

But *why* does it get so big? Because of a strict rule in our math universe: 

> **The base-10 number system dictates that each place value is ten times the value of the place immediately to the right.**

Every step to the left acts like a 10x multiplier! A 1 sitting in the tens place gives you ten times more points than a 1 sitting in the ones place. 

### The Missing Hero: Zero
Imagine you want to write the number "five hundred and two." You put a 5 in the hundreds place, and a 2 in the ones place. But if you just shove them together, you get 52. The 5 lost its superpower; it slid over into the tens place!

This is where the greatest invention in math steps up: Zero. **Zero functions as a placeholder in the place value system to maintain the correct mathematical position of other non-zero digits.** Think of zero like a friend saving your seat at the lunch table. By writing 502, zero bravely sits in the tens place. It holds that physical spot so the 5 stays perfectly safe in the hundreds place. It means "nothing" in value, but it does "everything" to hold the number together.

### Calculating True Value
How do we know what a number is actually worth when we look at it? We just do a quick translation. 

**A specific digit's total mathematical value is determined by multiplying the digit by the specific place value the digit occupies.** 

Let's look at an 8 parked in the ten-thousands place. Here are the steps to find its true value:
1. Identify the digit: 8.
2. Identify the place value spot: 10,000.
3. Multiply them together: 8 x 10,000 = 80,000.
The digit tells you *how many* you have, and the position tells you *what kind* of thing you have!

### Crossing the Mirror: Decimals
What happens if we walk backward? If every step left multiplies your score by ten, then every step right has to divide your score by ten. 

> **Each place value is one-tenth the value of the place immediately to the left in the base-10 number system.**

You are in the thousands... step right to the hundreds... step right to the tens... step right to the ones. You have reached the very edge of the whole numbers. But what if we try to step right *again*? 

We smack into a wall: the decimal point. 

**The decimal point is a mathematical symbol that separates the whole number portion of a value from the fractional portion.** 

Imagine whole pizzas on the left, and tiny pizza crumbs on the right. When you cross to the right of the decimal point, you shrink down into the tiny world of fractions. 
* **The place value immediately to the right of the decimal point is the tenths place.** Exactly like it sounds, **the tenths place represents fractions with a denominator of ten** (which looks like 1/10).
* Keep walking to the right into the tiny crumb world. **Moving to the right from the tenths place, the sequence of decimal place values is hundredths, thousandths, and ten-thousandths.** 

| Base-10 Direction | Place Value Name | Mathematical Value |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Left | Ten-Thousands | 10,000 |
| Left | Thousands | 1,000 |
| Left | Hundreds | 100 |
| Left | Tens | 10 |
| **Anchor** | **Ones** | **1** |
| **Boundary** | **Decimal Point** | **.** |
| Right | Tenths | 1/10 |
| Right | Hundredths | 1/100 |
| Right | Thousandths | 1/1000 |
| Right | Ten-Thousandths | 1/10000 |

## 2. The Art of Translation: Naming Numbers

Knowing how the parts work is great, but you also have to be able to *speak* math out loud. Sometimes, the way people talk in real life breaks strict math rules!

Have you ever heard someone say, "My score is one hundred and fifty"? In math language, that is actually a mistake! In math, the word "and" is a special warning siren. 

> **To express a decimal number in words, the decimal point is read aloud using the word 'and'.**

The word "and" is your verbal warning that you are leaving whole numbers and crossing the wall into fractions. So, 150 is just read as "one hundred fifty." 

So, how do we read the tiny crumb pieces on the right side of the decimal? Let's take the number 0.345. 
**To express the fractional part of a decimal number in words, state the number as a whole number followed by the place value name of the rightmost digit.** 

Here are the steps to read 0.345 out loud:
1. Look at the chunk of digits after the decimal: 345. Read that chunk normally: "Three hundred forty-five."
2. Look at the very last digit on the right. The 5 is sitting in the *thousandths* place.
3. Mash them together: "Three hundred forty-five thousandths."

Let's try it with a bigger number: **42.017**. Here are the steps:
1. Read the whole number part on the left: "Forty-two."
2. See the decimal point? Say: "AND."
3. Read the fractional part on the right. The chunk is 017, which we just read as "seventeen."
4. Check the rightmost digit. The 7 is in the *thousandths* place. 
5. Put it all together: "Forty-two and seventeen thousandths."

## 3. The Grand Landscape: The Number Line

Now that we built our numbers and know their names, we need a map to see where they live. In math, our favorite map is the number line. 

**A horizontal number line is a visual representation where numerical values strictly increase from left to right.** 
Think of this like a side-scrolling video game level. If you walk your character to the right, you get more health, bigger scores, and more money. On the flip side, **numerical values strictly decrease from right to left on a horizontal number line.** Walking left means losing points.

### The Origin: Zero
Sitting in the exact dead-center of this giant map is our good friend, Zero. It acts as the border line between two worlds. 
**The number zero is classified as neither positive nor negative.** It is totally neutral—like a tied game with zero points.

### The Hemispheres: Positive and Negative
To the right of zero is the happy zone. This is where your allowance lives. **Positive numbers are always located to the right of zero on a horizontal number line.** 

To the left of zero is the upside-down world of owing your parents money (debt) and freezing cold weather. **Negative numbers are always located to the left of zero on a horizontal number line.**

Because of the supreme rule of the number line map (values *always* get bigger as you move right), we discover a solid truth:
> **Any positive number is strictly greater than any negative number.**

It doesn't matter how scary a negative number looks. The number -1,000,000 is parked way, way out on the left. A tiny positive decimal like 0.0001 is on the right side of zero. Therefore, having 0.0001 is mathematically greater than owing someone a million dollars!

## 4. The Great Equalizer: Comparing and Ordering Numbers

Math tests usually don't give you a nice, neat list of numbers. They love to throw a messy math soup at you: a jumbled mix of positive fractions, negative decimals, and whole numbers, and then they ask you to rank them from smallest to biggest. 

How do we fix this mess? We use the great equalizer.

### Strategy 1: The Universal Language
**To effectively compare a mixed set of fractions and decimals, convert all the values into a single common numerical format.** 

It is super hard to look at 3/8 and 0.41 and know which is bigger. It’s like trying to compare apples to oranges. You need them all to speak the same language! Usually, turning everything into decimals is the easiest trick.

How do you force a fraction to turn into a decimal? Just remember that the line in a fraction is actually a secret division sign!
**A fraction can be mathematically converted into a decimal by dividing the fraction's numerator by the fraction's denominator.** 

Let's convert 3/8 into a decimal using steps:
1. Take the numerator (the top number): 3.
2. Take the denominator (the bottom number): 8.
3. Divide the top by the bottom: 3 divided by 8 = 0.375.
Boom! Now it's a decimal, ready to battle the others.

### Strategy 2: Lining up Decimals
Let’s pretend you turned all your positive numbers into decimals. You are staring at 0.41 and 0.375. Which is bigger? 

Do *not* fall for the trap of thinking a longer number is bigger (375 looks bigger than 41, but don't be fooled!).

**When comparing two positive decimal numbers, align the decimal points vertically and sequentially compare the digits from left to right.** 

If the numbers look uneven and lopsided, you can fix them. 
**Appending trailing zeros to the extreme right of a decimal number's fractional part does not alter the decimal's overall mathematical value.** 
That means adding a zero to the end of 0.41 to make it 0.410 changes absolutely nothing about its actual size. It just makes it match the length of 0.375 so it looks neat. 

Let's compare them step-by-step:
1. Add your trailing zero to 0.41 to get 0.410.
2. Stack them up, keeping the decimal points perfectly lined up:
0.410
0.375
3. Start on the left side (the tenths place). The top number has a 4. The bottom number has a 3. 
4. Stop right there! Four-tenths is bigger than three-tenths. Therefore, 0.41 is greater than 0.375. 

### Strategy 3: Comparing Fractions Directly
Sometimes, you might not *want* to divide. If a problem gives you 5/12 and 7/12, you don't need to make them decimals. 

**To compare fractions without converting the fractions into decimals, rewrite the fractions so all the fractions share a common denominator.** 

When the denominators (bottom numbers) are exactly the same, it means your pizza is cut into the exact same size slices. The game just becomes: who has more slices? 
**When comparing positive fractions that share a common positive denominator, the fraction containing the larger numerator possesses the greater overall value.** 

Let's do the steps for 5/12 and 7/12:
1. Check the bottoms: They are both 12. The slice sizes match!
2. Look at the numerators (top numbers): 7 and 5.
3. Since 7 slices is more than 5 slices, 7/12 is greater than 5/12.

### Strategy 4: Surviving the Upside-Down (Negative Numbers)
This is where smart students make silly mistakes. The negative side of the number line plays tricks on your brain. 

To understand negative numbers, we have to talk about **absolute value**. Absolute value is just how many spaces a number is from zero, ignoring the minus sign completely. The absolute value of -99 is 99 spaces. 

If someone says you have a "larger absolute value" in the negative world, it means you have a *larger debt*. Is owing more money a good thing? Nope! It makes you poorer. 

> **For negative numbers, the number possessing the larger absolute value represents the smaller overall mathematical value.**

The number -99 has a larger absolute value than -2. But because it represents a much deeper hole you are stuck in, -99 is actually the *smaller* number mathematically. 

Think about our map. 
**A negative number with a larger absolute value is positioned further to the left on a horizontal number line than a negative number with a smaller absolute value.** And what is the supreme rule of the number line? Moving left *always* means you are getting smaller. 

This tricky rule works for fractions, too! 
**When comparing negative fractions that share a common positive denominator, the fraction containing the more negative numerator possesses the lesser overall value.** 

Let's compare -7/8 and -3/8 using steps:
1. Check the bottoms: They are both 8.
2. Look at the tops: -7 and -3. 
3. Think about debt. Owing 7 slices (-7) is a worse debt than owing 3 slices (-3).
4. Because -7 is further left on the map, -7/8 is *less* than -3/8.

## Synthesis: A Praxis Masterclass Application

Let’s use every single tool we just learned to beat a giant boss-level question. 

**Question:** Order the following numbers from smallest (least) to biggest (greatest): 
-0.6, 5/8, -3/4, 0.65

**Step 1: Separate the map.**
We already know that negative numbers are always smaller than positive ones. Let's split them into two mini-battles:
The Negatives: -0.6 and -3/4
The Positives: 5/8 and 0.65

**Step 2: Make them speak a common language.**
Let’s convert the fractions into decimals using division (top divided by bottom).
1. For -3/4: -3 divided by 4 equals -0.75.
2. For 5/8: 5 divided by 8 equals 0.625.

Now our updated list of numbers looks like this: 
-0.6, 0.625, -0.75, 0.65

**Step 3: Order the Negatives (The Upside-Down Rules)**
We need to compare -0.6 and -0.75. 
1. Add a trailing zero to make them match: -0.60 and -0.75. 
2. Ask: Which has the *larger absolute value* (which is the bigger debt)? 0.75 is a bigger debt than 0.60. 
3. Because they are negative, the bigger absolute value sits further left on the number line, meaning it is the *smaller overall mathematical value*. 
So, **-0.75 is less than -0.60**. 

**Step 4: Order the Positives (Lining them up)**
We need to compare 0.625 and 0.65. 
1. Add a trailing zero to match the length: 0.65 becomes 0.650.
2. Stack them up:
0.625
0.650
3. Compare from left to right: The tenths place is a tie (both are 6). Move right to the hundredths place: 2 versus 5. 
4. The 5 wins! 
So, **0.625 is less than 0.650**.

**Step 5: The Final Map**
Now we just paste our two halves together, walking from the far left (smallest) to the right (biggest):
-0.75, -0.60, 0.625, 0.650

Finally, change them back into their original disguises for your final answer:
**-3/4, -0.6, 5/8, 0.65**
