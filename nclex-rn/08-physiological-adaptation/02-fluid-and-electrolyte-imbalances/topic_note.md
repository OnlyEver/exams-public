# The Architecture of the Ocean Inside You: Fluid and Electrolytes Masterclass

Welcome to the beautifully balanced, endlessly fascinating world of fluid and electrolytes. Think about it: you are essentially a highly organized, walking sack of [saltwater](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saline_water). To keep you alive, your body constantly plays a brilliant, high-stakes game of [cellular physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biophysics), moving water and [ions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ion) across [microscopic membranes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_membrane). 

![A detailed cross-section of a cell membrane, illustrating the primary lipid bilayer barrier across which fluid and electrolytes must constantly shift to maintain homeostasis.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Cell_membrane_detailed_diagram_en.svg)

If you want to master the [NCLEX-RN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Council_Licensure_Examination), you can't just memorize numbers. You have to understand *why* the body behaves the way it does. You have to see the fluid shifting and feel the [electrical charges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_charge) firing. Let's break down the rules of the game.

---

## Part 1: The Water (Fluid Volume)

Before we talk about the particles in the soup, we need to understand the soup's broth: the fluid itself. The concentration of our internal fluid is strictly regulated. **The normal [serum osmolality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasma_osmolality) range is 275 to 295 [milliosmoles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osmole_%28unit%29) per [kilogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kilogram).** If you are below 275, your fluids are too watery; above 295, and you’re essentially a concentrated syrup. 

### Fluid Volume Deficit (Hypovolemia)
What happens when there simply isn’t enough fluid in the pipes? **[Fluid volume deficit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume_depletion) decreases [circulating blood volume](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_volume).** When the [circulatory system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circulatory_system) is half-empty, the body has to compensate brilliantly and desperately to keep the [brain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brain) and [organs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organ_%28biology%29) alive. 

Because there is less fluid to pump, the [heart](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heart) compensates by beating faster just to maintain [output](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardiac_output)—this means **decreased circulating blood volume leads to resting [tachycardia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tachycardia)**. Furthermore, without enough volume to fight [gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity), standing up becomes a problem. The blood pools in the [legs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_leg), and **decreased circulating blood volume leads to [orthostatic hypotension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthostatic_hypotension)**. 

Visually, the patient dries out. The body hoards water from the [tissues](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tissue_%28biology%29), which is why **fluid volume deficit presents with dry oral [mucous membranes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mucous_membrane)** and **poor [skin turgor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skin_turgor)** (the skin stays "tented" when pinched). The [kidneys](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kidney) notice the drought and concentrate the [urine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urine) as much as biologically possible, which is why **fluid volume deficit causes a high [urine specific gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urine_specific_gravity) greater than 1.030**.

![Severe dehydration leading to decreased skin turgor, physically demonstrating the body's lack of interstitial fluid as the skin remains tented when pinched.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Adult_cholera_patient.jpg)

> **How to Fix It:** We need to refill the tank, but we can't use plain water because it would disrupt the delicate osmolality. Instead, **[nurses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nurse) treat fluid volume deficit by administering [isotonic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tonicity) [intravenous fluids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intravenous_therapy).** Why isotonic? Because it matches the body’s natural concentration, staying inside the [blood vessels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_vessel) where we need it. **Isotonic intravenous fluids include 0.9 percent [sodium chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride)** ([Normal Saline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saline_%28medicine%29)) and **[Lactated Ringers solution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ringer%27s_lactate_solution).** 
> 
> ![Red blood cells suspended in an isotonic solution maintain their normal shape and volume, illustrating why isotonic IV fluids safely replenish blood volume without causing fluid shifts across the cell membrane.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Blausen_0685_OsmoticFlow_Isotonic.png)
> 
> *The Goal:* How do you know your plumbing repair worked? **A successful response to fluid volume deficit treatment includes a return to baseline [blood pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_pressure).**

### Fluid Volume Excess (Hypervolemia)
Now, imagine the opposite. The pipes are overfilled. **[Fluid volume excess](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypervolemia) increases [hydrostatic pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrostatics) in the [peripheral capillaries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capillary).** Hydrostatic pressure is just fluid pressure pushing against the vessel walls. 

When the pressure gets too high, the pipes leak. **Increased capillary hydrostatic pressure pushes fluid into the [interstitial space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstitial_fluid).** Since gravity pulls fluid downward, **excess fluid in the interstitial space manifests as [peripheral edema](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peripheral_edema)** (swollen [ankles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ankle) and legs). 

But the fluid doesn't just pool in the legs. It backs up in the [great veins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venae_cavae), meaning **fluid volume excess causes [jugular vein distention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jugular_venous_pressure) in an upright client**. The heart pumps forcefully against this massive volume, which is why **fluid volume excess causes a bounding peripheral [pulse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulse)**. Eventually, the fluid backs up into the [alveolar spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulmonary_alveolus) of the [lungs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lung). When air bubbles through this water, **fluid volume excess causes [pulmonary crackles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crackles) upon lung [auscultation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Auscultation)**.

![Marked jugular vein distention in a patient, a classic clinical sign of fluid volume excess where high pressures cause blood to back up into the venous system.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Elevated_JVP.JPG)

> **How to Fix It:** We have to drain the tub and turn off the faucet. **Management of fluid volume excess includes administering [loop diuretics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Loop_diuretic)** (to force the kidneys to excrete water), **restricting daily dietary [sodium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium) intake** (because where sodium goes, water follows), and **restricting daily oral fluid intake**. 
> 
> *The Goal:* **A successful response to fluid volume excess interventions is demonstrated by a decrease in daily [body weight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_body_weight).** Remember the golden rule of fluid mass: **One kilogram of acute body weight loss represents exactly one [liter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Litre) of fluid loss.**

## Part 2: Sodium (The Maestro of Osmolality)

**The normal serum sodium range is 135 to 145 milliequivalents per liter.** Sodium is the undisputed king of the outside of the cell. In fact, **sodium is the primary extracellular [cation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ion) responsible for maintaining [extracellular fluid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extracellular_fluid) [osmolality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osmotic_concentration).** It dictates where water travels. 

### Hyponatremia (Too Much Water, Not Enough Salt)
**[Hyponatremia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyponatremia) is defined as a serum sodium level below 135 milliequivalents per liter.** If the [bloodstream](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood) is dilute (low sodium), [osmosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osmosis) demands that water move to an area of higher concentration to achieve balance. 

So, where does the water go? It rushes *into* the [cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_%28biology%29). Pathologically, **hyponatremia causes an osmotic shift of water into [brain cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brain_cell).** The brain cells act like sponges. **Water shifting into brain cells leads to [cerebral edema](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cerebral_edema)** (brain swelling). Because the brain is trapped in a rigid, bony [skull](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_skull) with no room to expand, **cerebral edema presents as [altered mental status](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Altered_level_of_consciousness)** and violently **causes [generalized seizures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generalized_seizure)**. 

![In a hypotonic (hyponatremic) environment, osmosis forces water into the cell, causing it to swell and potentially rupture. This process in the brain causes life-threatening cerebral edema.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Blausen_0684_OsmoticFlow_Hypotonic.png)

> **The Fix:** If the patient is seizing, this is an extreme [emergency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medical_emergency). **Severe symptomatic hyponatremia is treated with [hypertonic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tonicity) [saline solutions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saline_%28medicine%29)**, which **include 3 percent sodium chloride**. This extremely salty fluid pulls the water back out of the swollen brain cells and into the bloodstream.

### Hypernatremia (Too Much Salt, Not Enough Water)
**[Hypernatremia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypernatremia) is defined as a serum sodium level above 145 milliequivalents per liter.** The blood is now a salty [brine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brine). Osmosis kicks in again, but in reverse. **Hypernatremia causes an osmotic shift of water out of [intracellular spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intracellular_fluid).** The cells shrivel like raisins. 

![In a hypertonic (hypernatremic) environment, water is pulled out of the cell to dilute the salty bloodstream, causing the cell to shrivel and disrupting normal cellular function.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Blausen_0683_OsmoticFlow_Hypertonic.png)

When the brain’s [thirst center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thirst) cells shrivel, **hypernatremia presents with extreme [thirst](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thirst)**. The [neurological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neurology) irritation from shriveled [neurons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neuron) means **hypernatremia presents with severe restlessness**. 

> **The Fix:** We must rehydrate the cells gently. **Hypernatremia is managed by administering [hypotonic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tonicity) intravenous fluids**, which **include 0.45 percent sodium chloride** (Half-Normal Saline). This watery fluid dilutes the salty blood and slowly rehydrates the tissues.

## Part 3: Potassium (The Intracellular Electrician)

If sodium rules the outside, [potassium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_in_biology) rules the inside. **The normal serum potassium range is 3.5 to 5.0 milliequivalents per liter.** It may be a narrow window, but it's critical because **potassium is the primary intracellular cation responsible for [resting membrane potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resting_potential).** Potassium allows the electrical systems of the heart and [muscles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muscle) to reset and fire properly. 

### Hypokalemia (Low Electricity)
**[Hypokalemia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypokalemia) is defined as a serum potassium level below 3.5 milliequivalents per liter.** The electrical circuits become sluggish. The muscles can't fire properly, which **presents with generalized [muscle weakness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muscle_weakness)** and **decreases [gastrointestinal motility](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gastrointestinal_motility)** (causing the [bowels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intestine) to freeze up). 

More dangerously, the heart's electrical [repolarization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Repolarization) gets delayed. On an [electrocardiogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrocardiography) (EKG), **hypokalemia causes prominent [U waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U_wave)** and **causes flattened T waves**.

![An electrocardiogram tracing showcasing a prominent U wave following the T wave, a classic electrical hallmark of delayed repolarization caused by hypokalemia.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/U_wave.svg)

> **The Fix:** **Hypokalemia is treated by administering oral [potassium supplements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_supplement).** However, **severe hypokalemia is treated by administering intravenous [potassium chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_chloride_%28medical_use%29).** 
> 
> 🚨 **FEYNMAN WARNING:** This is lethal stuff if mishandled. Concentrated potassium acts as a chemical [defibrillator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Defibrillation). Therefore, **intravenous potassium chloride must always be diluted in a compatible fluid before administration** and **must never be administered by rapid [intravenous push](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intravenous_therapy)**. If you push it fast, you will stop the patient's heart instantly.

### Hyperkalemia (Overloaded Electricity)
**[Hyperkalemia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperkalemia) is defined as a serum potassium level above 5.0 milliequivalents per liter.** The electrical circuits are dangerously overloaded. The heart repolarizes too aggressively and erratically. 

On an EKG, **hyperkalemia causes tall peaked T waves** and **widens the [QRS complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/QRS_complex)**. If left unchecked, this rapidly degrades into [cardiac arrest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardiac_arrest).

![An ECG of a patient with an elevated potassium level, clearly demonstrating the dangerously tall, peaked T waves that occur before the rhythm degrades into a fatal arrhythmia.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/HyperK2014.JPG)

> **The Fix:** This is an acute emergency. We must manage it in three distinct, brilliant steps:
> 1.  **Shift it:** **Hyperkalemia is acutely managed by administering intravenous [regular insulin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_insulin) alongside intravenous [dextrose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glucose).** Why insulin? Because **intravenous regular insulin drives extracellular potassium into the intracellular space**, hiding it away where it can't harm the heart. Why dextrose? Because **intravenous dextrose prevents [hypoglycemia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypoglycemia) during hyperkalemia insulin therapy.**
> 2.  **Protect the heart:** **Intravenous calcium gluconate is administered in hyperkalemia to protect the [myocardium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardiac_muscle) from fatal [arrhythmias](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arrhythmia).** It acts as a shield for the cardiac muscle while we fix the potassium levels.
> 3.  **Remove it:** Shifting potassium into cells is temporary. To get it out of the body, **[sodium polystyrene sulfonate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_polystyrene_sulfonate) is administered to remove excess body potassium through [fecal excretion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Defecation).**

## Part 4: The Calcium-Phosphorus Seesaw

Nature loves a seesaw, and [Calcium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calcium_in_biology) and [Phosphorus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphorus) are perfectly balanced on one. Let's start with Calcium. **The normal [serum calcium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_calcium) range is 9.0 to 10.5 [milligrams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milligram) per [deciliter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Litre).** The body regulates this pool of calcium beautifully: **endogenous [parathyroid hormone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parathyroid_hormone) increases serum calcium levels** (pulling it from [bones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bone) into the blood), while **endogenous [calcitonin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calcitonin) decreases serum calcium levels** (pushing it back into the bone).

![The negative feedback loop of parathyroid hormone (PTH), demonstrating how the body pulls calcium from bones, kidneys, and intestines to tightly regulate serum calcium levels.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Parathyroid_Hormone_Negative_Feedback.svg)

Calcium's main job is to act like a [sedative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sedative) at the [neuromuscular junction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neuromuscular_junction). It keeps your [nerves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nerve) calm. 

### Hypocalcemia (The Nerves are Too Excited)
**[Hypocalcemia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypocalcemia) is defined as a serum calcium level below 9.0 milligrams per deciliter.** Without calcium's calming effect, the nerves become incredibly twitchy and hyper-excitable. 

You can test this physical irritability! **Hypocalcemia presents with a positive [Chvostek sign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chvostek_sign)**, and **a positive Chvostek sign is facial muscle twitching when the [facial nerve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Facial_nerve) is tapped.** You will also see **a positive [Trousseau sign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trousseau_sign_of_latent_tetany)**, and **a positive Trousseau sign is [carpal spasm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carpal_spasm) induced by inflating a blood pressure cuff above the [systolic blood pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Systole).** 

But twitchy muscles aren't just a parlor trick; they are deadly. The muscles around the [airway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Respiratory_tract) can spasm. Thus, **hypocalcemia increases the risk of [laryngeal stridor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stridor)**, which can close off the patient's breathing. 

> **The Fix:** **Acute symptomatic hypocalcemia is managed by administering intravenous calcium gluconate.**

### Hypercalcemia (The Nerves are Too Sedated)
**[Hypercalcemia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercalcemia) is defined as a serum calcium level above 10.5 milligrams per deciliter.** The nerves are overly sedated. **Hypercalcemia presents with profound muscle [flaccidity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flaccid_paralysis)** and **severely decreases [deep tendon reflexes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tendon_reflex).** 

Furthermore, all that heavy calcium floating in the blood has to go somewhere. It filters through the kidneys and hardens, which means **hypercalcemia increases the risk of [renal calculi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kidney_stone_disease) formation** (kidney stones). 

![A kidney stone composed of calcium oxalate with sharp edges, illustrating a painful and common complication that arises when excess calcium crystallizes in the renal system.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Kidney_stone_composed_of_calcium_oxalate_showing_sharp_edges.jpg)

> **The Fix:** Wash it out! **Hypercalcemia is managed by administering large volumes of intravenous normal saline to promote [renal excretion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Excretion) of calcium.**

### The Other Side of the Seesaw: Phosphorus
**The normal serum phosphorus range is 3.0 to 4.5 milligrams per deciliter.** 

Here is the grand mechanism: **Serum calcium concentrations and serum phosphorus concentrations share an inverse [physiological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physiology) relationship.** When one goes up, the other goes down. 

- **[Hypophosphatemia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypophosphatemia) is defined as a serum phosphorus level below 3.0 milligrams per deciliter.** Phosphorus is required to make [ATP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate) (cellular energy). Without it, **hypophosphatemia presents with severe peripheral muscle weakness.**
- **[Hyperphosphatemia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperphosphatemia) is defined as a serum phosphorus level above 4.5 milligrams per deciliter.** Because of the seesaw relationship, high phosphorus forces calcium to drop. Therefore, **hyperphosphatemia clinical manifestations exactly mirror the physical symptoms of hypocalcemia** (think Chvostek, Trousseau, and stridor!). 

> **The Fix for High Phosphorus:** **Hyperphosphatemia is managed by administering oral [phosphate binders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphate_binder) with daily meals.** These act like sponges in the [gut](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gastrointestinal_tract), catching dietary phosphorus before it absorbs. **Common oral phosphate binders include the medication [sevelamer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sevelamer).**

## Part 5: Magnesium (The Deep Chill Pill)

Lastly, let's talk about the body's ultimate relaxation mineral: [Magnesium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnesium_in_biology). **The normal serum magnesium range is 1.3 to 2.1 milliequivalents per liter.** Like calcium, magnesium stabilizes the neuromuscular junction and [central nervous system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_nervous_system). 

### Hypomagnesemia (Loss of Chill)
**[Hypomagnesemia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypomagnesemia) is defined as a serum magnesium level below 1.3 milliequivalents per liter.** Without magnesium, the body's brakes fail. 

**Hypomagnesemia pathologically increases neuromuscular excitability.** Physically, this **presents with hyperactive deep tendon reflexes**. Cardiologically, the [ventricles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ventricle_%28heart%29) become incredibly irritable. **Hypomagnesemia causes a specific ventricular arrhythmia called [Torsades de pointes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torsades_de_pointes)**, a twisting, chaotic rhythm that is frequently fatal.

![An ECG strip displaying Torsades de pointes, a chaotic and life-threatening twisting ventricular arrhythmia frequently triggered by severe hypomagnesemia.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Torsades_converted_by_AICD_ECG_strip_Lead_II.JPG)

> **The Fix:** Replace the brakes. **Severe hypomagnesemia is managed by administering intravenous [magnesium sulfate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnesium_sulfate_%28medical_use%29).**

### Hypermagnesemia (Too Much Chill)
**[Hypermagnesemia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypermagnesemia) is defined as a serum magnesium level above 2.1 milliequivalents per liter.** The [nervous system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nervous_system) is utterly sedated. 

**Hypermagnesemia pathologically depresses the central nervous system.** The reflexes don't just slow down; **hypermagnesemia causes completely absent deep tendon reflexes.** And because the [diaphragm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thoracic_diaphragm) is a muscle controlled by nerves, **hypermagnesemia causes life-threatening [respiratory depression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypoventilation).** 

> **The Fix:** We need an antidote to fight this deep sedation. Brilliantly, we use a different ion to compete at the [receptors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Receptor_%28biochemistry%29). **Hypermagnesemia toxicity is treated by administering intravenous calcium gluconate as an [antidote](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antidote).**

### The Feynman Summary: Seeing the Whole Board

To pass the [NCLEX](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Council_Licensure_Examination) and, more importantly, to save lives on the floor, you cannot just look at a [lab value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reference_ranges_for_blood_tests) in a vacuum. You must see the fluid moving, feel the electrical charge of potassium shifting, and watch the seesaw of calcium and phosphorus tip back and forth. 

*   **Fluid Volume:** Follow the blood pressure, the [heart rate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heart_rate), and the weight. (1 kg = 1 L).
*   **Sodium:** Follow the water. Protect the brain.
*   **Potassium:** Follow the electricity. Protect the heart. 
*   **Calcium/Magnesium:** Follow the reflexes. Protect the airway.

Master the *why*, and the *what* will naturally follow. Now, go balance some bathtubs!
