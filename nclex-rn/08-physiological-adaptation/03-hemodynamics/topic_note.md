Here is a secret about the [cardiovascular system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circulatory_system): it is, at its core, just a magnificent plumbing and electrical network. When we study [hemodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hemodynamics), we aren't studying abstract medicine; we are studying [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics). We are studying [fluid dynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluid_dynamics), [resistance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vascular_resistance), [electrical circuits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrical_circuit), and the incredible mechanical [pump](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pump) that keeps the whole operation running. 

If you can understand how a pump moves fluid through pipes, you can understand hemodynamics. If you can understand the wiring that makes the pump squeeze, you can master [electrocardiograms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrocardiography). Let's pull back the curtain on this beautifully engineered system so you can ace the [NCLEX-RN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Council_Licensure_Examination) and, more importantly, save lives.

---

## Part I: The Physics of the Pump

Let’s start with the central equation of life. How much [blood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood) is actually moving through the body? 

> **[Cardiac output](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardiac_output) equals [heart rate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heart_rate) multiplied by [stroke volume](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stroke_volume).** 

It is wonderfully simple. **Cardiac output is the volume of blood pumped by the [heart](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heart) in one minute.** In a normal, healthy state, **normal cardiac output ranges from 4 to 8 [liters per minute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volumetric_flow_rate) in a resting adult.** To get that number, the heart relies on two things: how fast it beats (Heart Rate) and how much it pushes with each beat (**Stroke volume is the amount of blood ejected by the [left ventricle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Left_ventricle) during each [contraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Systole)**).

![Major factors influencing cardiac output, illustrating how heart rate and stroke volume (affected by preload, contractility, and afterload) determine total blood flow.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/2036_Summary_of_Factors_in_Cardiac_Output.jpg)

But what determines stroke volume? Three brilliant mechanical concepts: **[Preload](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Preload_%28cardiology%29), [Afterload](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Afterload), and [Contractility](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myocardial_contractility).**

### 1. Preload (The Stretch)
Imagine stretching a [rubber band](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rubber_band). The further you stretch it, the harder it snaps back. That is exactly what happens in the heart. **Preload is the degree of [myocardial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardiac_muscle) fiber stretch at the end of [ventricular diastole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diastole)** (right before it squeezes). 

Nature has a neat trick up her sleeve here: **increased preload increases stroke volume up to a physiological limit.** If you stretch the heart with more [venous return](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venous_return), it contracts harder. But stretch it *too* much, and the rubber band loses its [elasticity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elasticity_%28physics%29)—that's [heart failure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heart_failure).

### 2. Afterload (The Resistance)
Now imagine trying to blow water out of a tiny straw versus a wide garden hose. The resistance you feel? That’s afterload. **Afterload is the [resistance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vascular_resistance) the [ventricles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ventricle_%28heart%29) must overcome to eject blood.** 

### 3. Contractility (The Muscle)
Even with perfect preload and afterload, the muscle itself has to be strong. **Contractility is the inherent strength of the myocardial contraction.** 

### Pharmacological Fine-Tuning
When the physics gets out of balance, we use drugs to manipulate the pump and the pipes:
*   **[Vasodilator medications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vasodilation) decrease [systemic vascular resistance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Systemic_vascular_resistance)**, opening the pipes to make pumping easier.
*   **[Vasopressor medications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antihypotensive_agent) increase systemic vascular resistance**, clamping down the pipes to keep [blood pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_pressure) up.
*   **[Positive inotropic medications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inotrope) increase myocardial contractility**, making the heart muscle squeeze with more vigor.
*   **[Negative inotropic medications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inotrope) decrease myocardial contractility**, allowing the heart to rest and require less [oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen).

## Part II: Reading the Gauges (Hemodynamic Monitoring)

How do we measure all these forces inside a living patient? We drop sensors into the pipes. 

To measure the right side of the heart, we look at the venous return. **[Central venous pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_venous_pressure) measures [right ventricular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_ventricle) preload.** What’s a healthy stretch for the right side? **Normal central venous pressure ranges from 2 to 8 [millimeters of mercury](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Millimeter_of_mercury).** 

To measure the resistance the left ventricle fights, we use the [arteries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artery). **Systemic vascular resistance measures left ventricular afterload.** Conversely, the right ventricle has to push into the [lungs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lung), so **[pulmonary vascular resistance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vascular_resistance) measures right ventricular afterload.**

When we need to know exactly what is happening deep inside the heart, we thread in a special line. **A [pulmonary artery catheter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulmonary_artery_catheter) measures right-sided heart pressures.** But the real trick is the balloon at the end. When we inflate it, **[pulmonary artery wedge pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulmonary_wedge_pressure) provides an indirect measurement of [left atrial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Left_atrium) pressure.** 

![Diagram of a pulmonary artery catheter in position, used to measure right-sided heart pressures and indirectly assess left atrial pressure via the wedge technique.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Pulmonary_artery_catheter_english.JPG)

> **Normal pulmonary artery wedge pressure ranges from 6 to 12 millimeters of mercury.** 

If that number is high, fluid is backing up. **An elevated pulmonary artery wedge pressure indicates left ventricular failure**, or, more simply, **an elevated pulmonary artery wedge pressure can indicate [fluid volume overload](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypervolemia).** 

### The Phlebostatic Axis: The Crucial Zero Point
All these pressure readings are useless if the sensor isn’t perfectly leveled to the heart. We level our equipment to the **phlebostatic axis**, which **is located where the [fourth intercostal space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intercostal_space) meets the [midaxillary line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axillary_lines).** 

This is basic physics: **[hemodynamic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hemodynamics) pressure [transducers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transducer) must be leveled to the phlebostatic axis for accurate readings.** 
*   **Leveling a hemodynamic transducer above the phlebostatic axis produces falsely low pressure readings** ([gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity) pulls the fluid down away from the sensor).
*   **Leveling a hemodynamic transducer below the phlebostatic axis produces falsely high pressure readings** (gravity pushes extra fluid weight onto the sensor).

## Part III: When the Pump Fails (Decreased Cardiac Output)

When cardiac output drops, the body panics and tries to save the [brain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brain) and the heart. 

First, we look at overall systemic pressure. **[Mean arterial pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean_arterial_pressure) is the average pressure in the [systemic circulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Systemic_circulation) throughout the [cardiac cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardiac_cycle).** 

> **Mean arterial pressure equals the sum of the [systolic pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Systolic_pressure) plus twice the [diastolic pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diastolic_pressure) divided by three.**

To keep the organs alive, **a mean arterial pressure of at least 65 millimeters of mercury is required for adequate [organ perfusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perfusion).** 

If cardiac output begins to fail, the body triggers a cascade of alarms:
1.  **[Tachycardia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tachycardia) is a compensatory mechanism to maintain cardiac output.** (If the stroke volume is low, the heart beats faster to make up for it!)
2.  **A decreased [level of consciousness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Altered_level_of_consciousness) is an early clinical sign of decreased cardiac output.** (The brain is exquisitely sensitive to a lack of oxygen).
3.  **Cool extremities indicate [peripheral vasoconstriction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vasoconstriction) secondary to decreased cardiac output.** (The body [shunts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shunt_%28medical%29) blood away from the fingers and toes to save the core).
4.  **[Oliguria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oliguria) indicates poor [renal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kidney) perfusion associated with decreased cardiac output.** (The kidneys stop making [urine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urine) because they aren't receiving enough blood flow).
5.  **[Hypotension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypotension) is a late clinical sign of decreased cardiac output.** (By the time the blood pressure drops, the compensatory mechanisms have completely failed).

If this downward spiral isn't stopped, the patient enters **[cardiogenic shock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardiogenic_shock)**, which **is a severe state of decreased cardiac output despite adequate [intravascular volume](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_volume).** The tank is full, but the pump is fundamentally broken.

How broken? We check the "squeeze" percentage. **Normal left ventricular [ejection fraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ejection_fraction) ranges from 55 to 70 percent.** However, **an ejection fraction less than 40 percent indicates significant left ventricular dysfunction.** 

When the ventricles are failing and stretching out of shape, they secrete a distress [hormone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hormone). **[B-type natriuretic peptide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brain_natriuretic_peptide) levels elevate in response to ventricular stretching.** So, if you run lab work, **a B-type natriuretic peptide level greater than 100 [picograms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Picogram) per [milliliter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Millilitre) indicates heart failure.**

![A weakened, failing heart often exhibits over-stretched muscle fibers and ventricular dilation, leading to a drop in cardiac output and the release of B-type natriuretic peptide (BNP).](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Right_side_heart_failure.jpg)

## Part IV: The Electrical Wiring (Telemetry & Arrhythmias)

The heart’s mechanical pump is useless without its electrical [pacemaker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardiac_pacemaker). To monitor this, we use [telemetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telemetry). **Telemetry monitoring requires intact [skin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_skin) free of excess [hair](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hair) for optimal lead adherence.** 

You must place the leads perfectly to get a clear picture. Memorize this classic placement pattern:
*   **The white telemetry lead is placed on the right upper [chest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thorax).** (White on Right)
*   **The black telemetry lead is placed on the left upper chest.** (Smoke over Fire)
*   **The red telemetry lead is placed on the left lower chest.** (Fire)
*   **The green telemetry lead is placed on the right lower chest.** (Snow over Grass)
*   **The brown telemetry lead is placed near the center of the chest.** (Chocolate in the middle)

### The Normal Rhythms
A beautiful, [normal rhythm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_sinus_rhythm) has strict timing:
*   **A normal [PR interval](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PR_interval) ranges from 0.12 to 0.20 [seconds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second).**
*   **A normal [QRS complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/QRS_complex) duration ranges from 0.04 to 0.10 seconds.**
*   **A normal [QT interval](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/QT_interval) is typically less than 0.40 seconds.**

![Schematic representation of a normal sinus rhythm electrocardiogram, identifying the key intervals: PR interval, QRS complex, and QT interval.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/SinusRhythmLabels.svg)

The baseline itself tells a story about oxygen. **[ST-segment depression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ST_depression) on an electrocardiogram indicates [myocardial ischemia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myocardial_ischemia)** (the heart is starving for oxygen). Worse yet, **[ST-segment elevation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ST_elevation) on an electrocardiogram indicates acute [myocardial injury](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myocardial_infarction)** (the tissue is actively dying). 

![A 12-lead electrocardiogram demonstrating ST-segment elevation, an urgent clinical sign indicating acute myocardial injury.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/12_Lead_EKG_ST_Elevation_tracing_color_coded.jpg)

### The Electrical Glitches
When the wiring goes haywire, you must recognize it instantly.

**The Sinus Rhythms:**
*   **[Sinus bradycardia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sinus_bradycardia) is defined as a regular rhythm with a heart rate less than 60 [beats per minute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heart_rate).** If the patient is dizzy and [passing out](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syncope_%28medicine%29), **[Atropine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atropine) is the primary medication for treating symptomatic bradycardia.**
*   **[Sinus tachycardia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sinus_tachycardia) is defined as a regular rhythm with a heart rate greater than 100 beats per minute.**

**The Atrial Chaos:**
*   **[Atrial fibrillation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atrial_fibrillation) exhibits an absence of distinct [P waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/P_wave_%28electrocardiography%29) on an electrocardiogram** and **presents with an irregularly irregular ventricular rhythm.** Because the [atria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atrium_%28heart%29) are quivering, blood pools. Therefore, **atrial fibrillation increases the risk of systemic [thromboembolism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Embolism)** ([strokes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stroke)).

![Electrocardiogram recording comparing a normal sinus rhythm (top) with atrial fibrillation (bottom), showing the characteristic absence of P waves and an irregular ventricular response.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Atrial_Fibrillation.png)

*   **[Atrial flutter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atrial_flutter) presents with characteristic sawtooth waves on an electrocardiogram.** 
*   If the upper heart is racing stably ([SVT](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supraventricular_tachycardia)), **Adenosine is the first-line medication for stable supraventricular tachycardia.** Because it metabolizes in seconds, **Adenosine must be administered as a rapid [intravenous push](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intravenous_therapy).**

**The AV Blocks (The Delays):**
*   **[First-degree atrioventricular block](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First-degree_atrioventricular_block) exhibits a constant PR interval greater than 0.20 seconds.** (Just a steady delay).
*   **[Second-degree atrioventricular block type I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second-degree_atrioventricular_block) exhibits a progressively lengthening PR interval** until it **eventually drops a QRS complex.** (Long, longer, drop—that is a [Wenckebach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second-degree_atrioventricular_block)).
*   **[Second-degree atrioventricular block type II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second-degree_atrioventricular_block) exhibits a constant PR interval with intermittently dropped QRS complexes.** (Highly unpredictable and dangerous).
*   **[Third-degree atrioventricular block](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Third-degree_atrioventricular_block) is characterized by a complete lack of electrical conduction from the atria to the ventricles.** The atria and ventricles are beating entirely independently. This is an emergency! **Third-degree atrioventricular block requires immediate [pacing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artificial_pacemaker).**

![Electrocardiogram showing a third-degree (complete) atrioventricular block, where P waves are completely disconnected from the QRS complexes.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Rhythm_strip_showing_third_degree_heart_block.jpg)

**The Ventricular Nightmares (Life Threats):**
*   **[Premature ventricular contractions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Premature_ventricular_contraction) are early beats with wide QRS complexes.** A few are okay, but **frequent premature ventricular contractions can lead to [ventricular tachycardia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ventricular_tachycardia).**
*   **Ventricular tachycardia presents with wide QRS complexes at a rate greater than 100 beats per minute.** If they lose their [pulse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulse), **pulseless ventricular tachycardia requires immediate [defibrillation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Defibrillation).**

![An electrocardiogram showing ventricular tachycardia, characterized by wide, rapid QRS complexes that can critically impair cardiac output.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Electrocardiogram_of_Ventricular_Tachycardia.png)

*   **[Ventricular fibrillation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ventricular_fibrillation) exhibits a chaotic baseline without identifiable QRS complexes.** The heart is just quivering; there is zero cardiac output. **Ventricular fibrillation requires immediate [cardiopulmonary resuscitation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardiopulmonary_resuscitation)** and **requires immediate defibrillation.** 

![Ventricular fibrillation on an electrocardiogram presents as a chaotic, disorganized baseline with no identifiable QRS complexes, resulting in zero cardiac output.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Lead_II_rhythm_generated_ventricular_fibrilation_VF.JPG)

*   **[Asystole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asystole) presents as a flat line on an electrocardiogram indicating an absence of electrical activity.** *Do not shock a flatline!* **Defibrillation is not a valid treatment for asystole.** Instead, **Epinephrine is the primary pharmacological treatment for asystole.**

## Part V: Mechanical Fixes for Electrical Problems

When medications fail, we use electricity and hardware.

### Electricity: Defibrillation vs. Cardioversion
You must understand the difference between these two life-saving shocks. 
*   **Defibrillation delivers an unsynchronized electrical shock to the [myocardium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardiac_muscle).** It blasts all the cells at once, hoping the primary pacemaker resets. 
*   **[Synchronized cardioversion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardioversion) delivers a shock synchronized with the [R wave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/QRS_complex) on the electrocardiogram.** We do this for rhythms like unstable SVT or Atrial Fibrillation. Why synchronized? Because **delivering a shock on the T wave can precipitate ventricular fibrillation.** Crucially, **synchronized cardioversion requires the client to have a palpable pulse.**

### Hardware: Pacemakers and Devices
When the heart's natural pacemaker is broken, we install our own. **A pacemaker delivers electrical impulses to stimulate myocardial contraction.** 

On the [EKG](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrocardiography), you will see a sharp vertical line called a pacing spike.
*   **A pacing spike immediately preceding a P wave indicates atrial pacing.**
*   **A pacing spike immediately preceding a QRS complex indicates ventricular pacing.**

There are several ways to pace a heart:
1.  **[Transcutaneous pacing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transcutaneous_pacing) involves delivering electrical impulses through pads placed on the [chest wall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thoracic_wall).** This is painful, so **transcutaneous pacing is a temporary intervention for symptomatic bradycardia.**
2.  **[Transvenous pacing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artificial_pacemaker) involves threading a pacing wire through a [central vein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_venous_catheter) into the right ventricle.**
3.  **[Epicardial pacing wires](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artificial_pacemaker) are placed directly onto the heart muscle during [cardiac surgery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardiac_surgery).**

When using a transcutaneous pacemaker, how do you know it's working? **The energy setting for transcutaneous pacemakers is increased until mechanical capture is achieved.** You look at the monitor: **Electrical capture on an electrocardiogram is evidenced by a pacing spike followed by a QRS complex.** But EKG capture isn't enough; you need an actual pulse! **Assessing the [femoral pulse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Femoral_artery) is the preferred method to verify mechanical capture during transcutaneous pacing.**

![An electrocardiogram strip demonstrating true pacemaker capture, where each electrical pacing spike is successfully followed by a ventricular contraction.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/True-capture.png)

Sometimes, pacemakers malfunction. 
*   **Pacemaker failure to capture occurs when a pacing spike is not followed by a myocardial contraction.** (The wire fired, but the heart ignored it).
*   **Pacemaker failure to sense occurs when the device fires electrical impulses at inappropriate times.** (It is ignoring the heart's native rhythm).

*Patient Safety Note:* **Clients with a newly implanted pacemaker must restrict arm movement on the operative side** to prevent dislodging the fresh wires. Also, **[magnetic resonance imaging](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_resonance_imaging) is generally contraindicated for clients with traditional pacing devices.**

For patients with lethal arrhythmias, we implant a bigger safety net. **An [implantable cardioverter-defibrillator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Implantable_cardioverter-defibrillator) detects life-threatening ventricular arrhythmias** and automatically **delivers a shock to restore normal sinus rhythm.**

![Illustration of an implantable cardioverter-defibrillator (ICD) with its lead placed in the right ventricle to detect and shock life-threatening arrhythmias.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Blausen_0543_ImplantableCardioverterDefibrillator.svg)

### The Ultimate Plumber's Tool: The IABP
If the heart is failing fundamentally (like in cardiogenic shock), we use a balloon to help pump the blood. **The [intra-aortic balloon pump](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intra-aortic_balloon_pump) is a mechanical circulatory support device.** 
It sits in the [aorta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aorta) and works in total synchronization with the heart:
*   **An intra-aortic balloon pump inflates during diastole to increase [coronary artery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coronary_circulation) perfusion.** (Pushing blood backwards into the heart muscle).
*   **An intra-aortic balloon pump deflates right before systole to decrease afterload.** (Creating a [vacuum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vacuum) effect that makes it incredibly easy for the weak left ventricle to eject blood).

![The intra-aortic balloon pump sits in the descending aorta, inflating during diastole to improve coronary perfusion and deflating before systole to reduce afterload.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Intra-aortic_balloon_pump.jpg)

## Part VI: When the Kidneys Need a Lifeboat (Renal Replacement)

If hemodynamics fail, the kidneys fail. When this happens, we have to artificially replace [renal function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Assessment_of_kidney_function). 

**[Hemodialysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hemodialysis) relies on [diffusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffusion) to remove [waste products](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metabolic_waste) from the blood**, and it **relies on ultrafiltration to remove excess fluid from the blood.** 

![Schematic of a hemodialysis circuit, showing how blood is drawn, passed through a semipermeable dialyzer to remove waste and fluid via diffusion and ultrafiltration, and returned to the patient.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Hemodialysis-en.svg)

### The AV Fistula
To move massive amounts of blood, we surgically sew an artery and a [vein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vein) together. **An [arteriovenous fistula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arteriovenous_fistula) requires weeks to mature before hemodialysis use.** 

You must protect this lifeline. To check if it is working:
*   **A palpable thrill over an arteriovenous fistula indicates functional patency.** (It feels like a purring cat under the skin).
*   **An audible [bruit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bruit) over an arteriovenous fistula indicates functional patency.** (It sounds like a whooshing river through your [stethoscope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stethoscope)).

To protect it: **Blood pressure measurements must not be taken on an arm with an arteriovenous fistula**, and **[venipuncture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venipuncture) must not be performed on an arm with an arteriovenous fistula.**

### Dialysis Complications
Removing 3 liters of fluid in 4 hours takes a toll. **Hypotension is the most common complication during hemodialysis treatments.** 

If we remove waste products too quickly, the brain's [osmolarity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osmotic_concentration) gets entirely out of balance, pulling fluid into the [skull](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_skull). **[Dialysis disequilibrium syndrome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dialysis_disequilibrium_syndrome) can present with severe [headaches](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Headache)** and **can manifest as neurological [confusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confusion).**

### Continuous Renal Replacement Therapy (CRRT)
What if a patient is in cardiogenic shock and their mean arterial pressure is 60? Standard dialysis will bottom out their pressure and kill them. 

Enter CRRT. **[Continuous renal replacement therapy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hemofiltration) is indicated for hemodynamically unstable clients** because **continuous renal replacement therapy provides slow fluid removal to minimize hemodynamic compromise.** 

Instead of doing it in 4 hours, it runs 24/7. **[Continuous venovenous hemofiltration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hemofiltration) removes large volumes of fluid continuously over 24 hours.** 

Because blood is sitting in a machine all day, it wants to clot. **Regional anticoagulation with citrate is often used to prevent filter [clotting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coagulation) during continuous renal replacement therapy.** But there's a catch: [citrate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Citrate) binds to [calcium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calcium_in_biology) to stop the clotting cascade. Therefore, **[calcium levels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_calcium) must be closely monitored during continuous renal replacement therapy using citrate [anticoagulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anticoagulant)** to ensure the patient doesn't develop severe [hypocalcemia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypocalcemia).

### Final Thoughts for the Exam
When you sit down for the NCLEX, remember that hemodynamics is logical. Tracing the path of the blood, calculating the stretch and resistance, watching the electrical delays on the EKG, and protecting the fragile balance of [fluid shifts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluid_compartment)—it all follows the beautiful, predictable laws of physics. Understand the pump, protect the pipes, and you will do exceptionally well.
