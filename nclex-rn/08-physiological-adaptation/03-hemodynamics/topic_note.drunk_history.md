Listen to me, buddy. Slide your glass over, let me draw on this napkin, because you’re looking at hemodynamics all wrong. The cardiovascular system? It isn’t just biology. It’s a turf war. The heart is a mob boss running an intricate underground racket, and that racket’s primary export is Cardiac Output. It's the most important metric in the city: cardiac output is the volume of blood pumped by the heart in one minute. In a healthy operation, normal cardiac output ranges from 4 to 8 liters per minute in a resting adult. How does the boss calculate his daily take? Simple math. Cardiac output equals heart rate multiplied by stroke volume. The heart rate is how fast the boss barks the orders, and stroke volume is the amount of blood ejected by the left ventricle during each contraction—the actual muscle moving the goods out the door.

But to get that stroke volume, you need the right conditions. You need the muscle stretched and ready to snap. Preload is the degree of myocardial fiber stretch at the end of ventricular diastole, right before the big squeeze. It’s like pulling back a slingshot; increased preload increases stroke volume up to a physiological limit. Pull it too far, and the elastic snaps, but pull it just right, and it flies. Once the blood flies, though, it hits the rival gang’s barricades. Afterload is the resistance the ventricles must overcome to eject blood. Even with the perfect stretch and low resistance, the mob enforcer has to have raw grit. Contractility is the inherent strength of the myocardial contraction. 

We can monitor this whole turf war if we know where to drop our wiretaps. For the right side of the operation, central venous pressure measures right ventricular preload. A healthy stretch over there? Normal central venous pressure ranges from 2 to 8 millimeters of mercury. For the left side's struggle against the city streets, systemic vascular resistance measures left ventricular afterload. Meanwhile, the right side pushes against the lungs, so pulmonary vascular resistance measures right ventricular afterload. And when the boss needs chemical help to manipulate this racket? Vasodilator medications decrease systemic vascular resistance, bribing the cops to open the roads and lower the resistance. Vasopressor medications increase systemic vascular resistance, throwing up barricades and squeezing the pipes tight. When the muscle needs a pep talk, positive inotropic medications increase myocardial contractility. When they need to lay low and rest, negative inotropic medications decrease myocardial contractility.

But what happens when the empire starts to crumble? When the money stops flowing, the body panics. The first thing you'll see is the heart frantically trying to make up the difference: tachycardia is a compensatory mechanism to maintain cardiac output. It’s working faster, not smarter. Then, the boss’s brain gets starved of resources. A decreased level of consciousness is an early clinical sign of decreased cardiac output. As the panic spreads, the city shunts resources away from the suburbs to protect the downtown core; cool extremities indicate peripheral vasoconstriction secondary to decreased cardiac output. Down in the sanitation department, the workers just go on strike. Oliguria indicates poor renal perfusion associated with decreased cardiac output. And the absolute final death knell of the empire? Hypotension is a late clinical sign of decreased cardiac output. If the vault is full of fluid but the heart just cannot move the cash, cardiogenic shock is a severe state of decreased cardiac output despite adequate intravascular volume. 

To know how badly the left ventricle is failing, we look at the exact percentage of goods it can push out the door. Normal left ventricular ejection fraction ranges from 55 to 70 percent. If the boss is truly washed up, an ejection fraction less than 40 percent indicates significant left ventricular dysfunction. As the ventricles stretch and drown in their own incompetence, they scream for help by releasing a specific chemical. B-type natriuretic peptide levels elevate in response to ventricular stretching, so if you pull lab work and see a B-type natriuretic peptide level greater than 100 picograms per milliliter, that indicates heart failure. You have to keep the city’s overall pressure up. Mean arterial pressure is the average pressure in the systemic circulation throughout the cardiac cycle. To calculate this mob tax, mean arterial pressure equals the sum of the systolic pressure plus twice the diastolic pressure divided by three. If it drops too low, organs die. A mean arterial pressure of at least 65 millimeters of mercury is required for adequate organ perfusion.

We need cameras on this disaster, and that means reality TV-level monitoring. Telemetry! But you can't tape a camera to a fuzzy wall; telemetry monitoring requires intact skin free of excess hair for optimal lead adherence. You slap the cameras on in a very specific pattern. The white telemetry lead is placed on the right upper chest, and the black telemetry lead is placed on the left upper chest. The red telemetry lead is placed on the left lower chest, the green telemetry lead is placed on the right lower chest, and finally, the brown telemetry lead is placed near the center of the chest. For the heavy-duty, deep-cover wiretaps, a pulmonary artery catheter measures right-sided heart pressures. We inflate a tiny balloon on the end to peek at the left side of the heart across the tracks, meaning pulmonary artery wedge pressure provides an indirect measurement of left atrial pressure. A normal pulmonary artery wedge pressure ranges from 6 to 12 millimeters of mercury. If that number gets too high? An elevated pulmonary artery wedge pressure indicates left ventricular failure, or, to put it simply, an elevated pulmonary artery wedge pressure can indicate fluid volume overload. But these fancy pressure readings are entirely garbage if your equipment isn't leveled perfectly. The absolute center of the universe for leveling is a spot on the chest: the phlebostatic axis is located where the fourth intercostal space meets the midaxillary line. It is a law of physics that hemodynamic pressure transducers must be leveled to the phlebostatic axis for accurate readings. If your tripod slips, you're doomed. Leveling a hemodynamic transducer above the phlebostatic axis produces falsely low pressure readings, and leveling a hemodynamic transducer below the phlebostatic axis produces falsely high pressure readings. 

Now, let's talk about the heart's electrical system. Buddy, it is a series of messy, toxic breakups. A healthy relationship has perfect timing. A normal PR interval ranges from 0.12 to 0.20 seconds, a normal QRS complex duration ranges from 0.04 to 0.10 seconds, and a normal QT interval is typically less than 0.40 seconds. When the rhythm gets sluggish and depressed, sinus bradycardia is defined as a regular rhythm with a heart rate less than 60 beats per minute. If the patient is passing out from it, atropine is the primary medication for treating symptomatic bradycardia—it’s the chemical equivalent of a slap in the face. On the flip side, sinus tachycardia is defined as a regular rhythm with a heart rate greater than 100 beats per minute.

Then there are the atria. Oh, the atria are the dramatic upstairs neighbors. When they just start screaming over each other, atrial fibrillation exhibits an absence of distinct P waves on an electrocardiogram. Because they are just quivering and fighting, it presents with an irregularly irregular ventricular rhythm. The blood just sits up there, pooling and clotting while they argue, which is why atrial fibrillation increases the risk of systemic thromboembolism. Sometimes, they just repeat the same passive-aggressive remark on a loop; atrial flutter presents with characteristic sawtooth waves on an electrocardiogram. If the whole top half of the heart is racing out of control stably, we hit the reset button. Adenosine is the first-line medication for stable supraventricular tachycardia. Because its half-life is basically shorter than a bad blind date, adenosine must be administered as a rapid intravenous push.

What about the communication between the top and bottom of the heart? The AV blocks are pure, unadulterated ghosting. First-degree atrioventricular block exhibits a constant PR interval greater than 0.20 seconds—they always text back, just obnoxiously late. Second-degree atrioventricular block type I exhibits a progressively lengthening PR interval until it eventually drops a QRS complex. They take longer and longer to reply until they just stand you up. Second-degree atrioventricular block type II exhibits a constant PR interval with intermittently dropped QRS complexes. Unpredictable, random ghosting. Toxic! Finally, the ultimate breakup: third-degree atrioventricular block is characterized by a complete lack of electrical conduction from the atria to the ventricles. They are living in the same house but functioning totally independently. It's a disaster, meaning third-degree atrioventricular block requires immediate pacing. 

Down in the ventricles, things get lethal fast. Premature ventricular contractions are early beats with wide QRS complexes—someone jumping to wildly aggressive conclusions. A few are fine, but frequent premature ventricular contractions can lead to ventricular tachycardia. Ventricular tachycardia presents with wide QRS complexes at a rate greater than 100 beats per minute. If there's no pulse with that terrifying rhythm, pulseless ventricular tachycardia requires immediate defibrillation. Worse yet is absolute anarchy. Ventricular fibrillation exhibits a chaotic baseline without identifiable QRS complexes. The ventricles are just shaking like a leaf. There is zero output. Ventricular fibrillation requires immediate cardiopulmonary resuscitation, and of course, ventricular fibrillation requires immediate defibrillation. But if the monitor goes entirely flat? The relationship is dead. Asystole presents as a flat line on an electrocardiogram indicating an absence of electrical activity. Do you shock a corpse? No! Defibrillation is not a valid treatment for asystole. You need chemical adrenaline; epinephrine is the primary pharmacological treatment for asystole. 

You also have to watch the baseline for signs of tissue starvation. It’s like watching a plant wilt. ST-segment depression on an electrocardiogram indicates myocardial ischemia—it's begging for oxygen. But if the house is actively on fire? ST-segment elevation on an electrocardiogram indicates acute myocardial injury. 

When the toxic relationships get out of hand, we bring in the bouncers: electricity and hardware. You have to know what kind of shock to throw. Defibrillation delivers an unsynchronized electrical shock to the myocardium. It’s a barroom brawl, clearing the whole room at once. But if the patient is unstable but still has a rhythm like SVT, we use finesse. Synchronized cardioversion delivers a shock synchronized with the R wave on the electrocardiogram. Why so careful? Because delivering a shock on the T wave can precipitate ventricular fibrillation. Oh, and critically, synchronized cardioversion requires the client to have a palpable pulse. 

If the heart's natural pacemaker is garbage, we install our own. A pacemaker delivers electrical impulses to stimulate myocardial contraction. You'll see its fingerprints on the EKG: a pacing spike immediately preceding a P wave indicates atrial pacing, while a pacing spike immediately preceding a QRS complex indicates ventricular pacing. But sometimes, the hardware is just as incompetent as the heart. Pacemaker failure to capture occurs when a pacing spike is not followed by a myocardial contraction—it fired, but the heart ignored it. Pacemaker failure to sense occurs when the device fires electrical impulses at inappropriate times, completely oblivious to the heart's natural rhythm. 

If you're in an emergency, you slap on the sticky pads. Transcutaneous pacing involves delivering electrical impulses through pads placed on the chest wall. It feels like getting kicked by a horse, so transcutaneous pacing is a temporary intervention for symptomatic bradycardia. You crank the dial; the energy setting for transcutaneous pacemakers is increased until mechanical capture is achieved. You look at the monitor, where electrical capture on an electrocardiogram is evidenced by a pacing spike followed by a QRS complex. But looking at the screen isn't enough—you have to check the plumbing! Assessing the femoral pulse is the preferred method to verify mechanical capture during transcutaneous pacing. There are internal options, too. Transvenous pacing involves threading a pacing wire through a central vein into the right ventricle. Or, if the chest is wide open on an operating table, epicardial pacing wires are placed directly onto the heart muscle during cardiac surgery. If your patient just got a fresh internal device, strict rules apply. Clients with a newly implanted pacemaker must restrict arm movement on the operative side so they don't rip the wires right out of the heart muscle. And keep them away from giant magnets: magnetic resonance imaging is generally contraindicated for clients with traditional pacing devices. 

For the patients prone to sudden, lethal anarchy, we install a sleeping bodyguard. An implantable cardioverter-defibrillator detects life-threatening ventricular arrhythmias, and when things go south, an implantable cardioverter-defibrillator delivers a shock to restore normal sinus rhythm. If the heart muscle is fundamentally exhausted, we drop a literal balloon into the aorta to act as an assistant pump. The intra-aortic balloon pump is a mechanical circulatory support device, and it dances in perfect opposite-rhythm to the heart. An intra-aortic balloon pump inflates during diastole to increase coronary artery perfusion, forcefully shoving blood into the heart's own vessels to feed it. Then, an intra-aortic balloon pump deflates right before systole to decrease afterload, creating a sudden vacuum so the failing left ventricle can easily eject its blood. 

Finally, buddy, when the city's hemodynamics collapse, the sanitation department—the kidneys—shut down completely. We have to outsource the trash collection to a machine. Standard hemodialysis relies on diffusion to remove waste products from the blood, and hemodialysis relies on ultrafiltration to remove excess fluid from the blood. But to move that kind of extreme volume, you can't use a tiny vein; you have to build a super-pipe. An arteriovenous fistula requires weeks to mature before hemodialysis use. You have to protect this pipe with your life! Blood pressure measurements must not be taken on an arm with an arteriovenous fistula, and absolutely, venipuncture must not be performed on an arm with an arteriovenous fistula. How do you know the super-pipe is working? You feel for the purr and listen for the river. A palpable thrill over an arteriovenous fistula indicates functional patency, and an audible bruit over an arteriovenous fistula indicates functional patency. 

Dialysis is a kitchen disaster waiting to happen. If you drain the soup out of the pot too quickly, the whole cardiovascular system tips over! Hypotension is the most common complication during hemodialysis treatments. And if you yank the toxins out of the blood too fast, the brain's osmolarity gets totally confused and swells with fluid. This is bad news: dialysis disequilibrium syndrome can present with severe headaches, and dialysis disequilibrium syndrome can manifest as neurological confusion. 

But what if the mob boss is already in cardiogenic shock? You can't hook them up to a 4-hour massive dialysis machine; they'd bottom out and die instantly. We use the slow-cooker method. Continuous renal replacement therapy is indicated for hemodynamically unstable clients. It's gentle, because continuous renal replacement therapy provides slow fluid removal to minimize hemodynamic compromise. One specific type, continuous venovenous hemofiltration removes large volumes of fluid continuously over 24 hours. But blood hates sitting in a plastic machine all day; it wants to clot up and ruin everything. To stop this, regional anticoagulation with citrate is often used to prevent filter clotting during continuous renal replacement therapy. But there's a catch: citrate works by gobbling up calcium. If you aren't paying attention, you'll drain the patient's calcium entirely, which means calcium levels must be closely monitored during continuous renal replacement therapy using citrate anticoagulation.
