# The Physics of Survival: Mastering Medical Emergencies

Welcome to the wild, fast-paced world of medical emergencies! Here is the coolest thing about the human body: when it breaks down, it is kind of like fixing a broken video game console, a jammed pump, or leaky plumbing. If you understand how the body's pump, pipes, and electrical wires work, you can totally save a life. We are going to learn *why* nurses and doctors do what they do, in plain English. 

Let's dive in and learn how to keep the human machine running!

## Part I: The Pump and the Electrical Grid (Cardiac Resuscitation)

When a patient's heart stops beating, you get to be their temporary heart and lungs. You become the pump!

### The Mechanics of Basic Life Support (BLS)
Having air in your lungs doesn't help if there is no pressure to pump that oxygen to your brain. Because of this, **the sequence for Basic Life Support is Compressions, Airway, Breathing.** 

To be an awesome human pump, you need to follow these rules:
*   **Speed:** **Cardiopulmonary resuscitation compressions for an adult must be performed at a rate of 100 to 120 compressions per minute.** If you go slower, you don't build up enough pressure. If you go faster, you are just wiggling the chest and not actually pumping.
*   **Depth:** **Cardiopulmonary resuscitation compressions for an adult must be at least 2 inches deep.** You are literally squeezing the heart muscle to squirt the blood out!
*   **Recoil:** Imagine squeezing a rubber water balloon; if you don't let it pop back into shape, it can't fill back up with water. **Chest recoil during cardiopulmonary resuscitation allows the heart to refill with blood before the next compression.** So, don't lean your weight on their chest!

If you don't have special breathing tubes set up yet, **the correct compression-to-ventilation ratio for adult cardiopulmonary resuscitation without an advanced airway is 30 compressions to 2 breaths.** 

Let's look at how that math works out in real life if your goal is 120 compressions in one minute:
Step 1: You need 120 compressions in 60 seconds (1 minute).
Step 2: Each cycle has 30 compressions.
Step 3: Divide the total compressions by the compressions per cycle: 120 / 30 = 4.
Step 4: This means you will complete exactly 4 cycles of 30 compressions and 2 breaths in one minute!

When giving those breaths, don't blow super hard like you are blowing out candles on a birthday cake. **Rescue breaths during cardiopulmonary resuscitation should be delivered over 1 second**, and you know you did it perfectly when the **rescue breaths during cardiopulmonary resuscitation should produce visible chest rise.**

### Rebooting the Grid: Advanced Cardiac Rhythms
The heart's electrical wires can glitch out. 

Sometimes, the heart forgets how to squeeze together and just quivers like a bowl of Jell-O. This glitch is called ventricular fibrillation or pulseless ventricular tachycardia. Because the heart is still buzzing with messy electricity, **the initial intervention for ventricular fibrillation is immediate defibrillation.** The exact same rule applies to the other quivering rhythm: **the initial intervention for pulseless ventricular tachycardia is immediate defibrillation.** Defibrillation is when we use a machine to shock the chest.

Why do we shock them? We aren't actually "jump-starting" the heart! We are doing a hard reset, just like holding down the power button on a frozen computer. In fancy medical words, **defibrillation completely depolarizes the myocardium to allow the sinoatrial node to resume electrical pacemaking.** That just means the shock stops the messy electricity so the heart's main "boss chip" (the sinoatrial node) can take control again.

> **Warning: The Un-Shockable Rhythms**
> You cannot shock a heart that has zero electricity, like a TV that is completely unplugged. That means **asystole is a non-shockable cardiac rhythm.** Also, sometimes the screen is on, but the buttons don't work; **pulseless electrical activity is a non-shockable cardiac rhythm.** For these, you just keep doing compressions and give medicine.

### The Chemical Kick
While you do compressions, we need to give medicine that squeezes the blood vessels to force blood up to the brain. 
*   **Epinephrine is the primary medication administered during a cardiac arrest for any rhythm.** It is the ultimate super-booster!
*   **The standard cardiac arrest dosage of intravenous epinephrine is 1 milligram every 3 to 5 minutes.**

If you *did* shock the patient, but the heart stubbornly refuses to fix its glitch, we bring in a backup medicine. **Amiodarone is an antiarrhythmic medication used for ventricular fibrillation unresponsive to shock delivery.**

### The Aftermath: ROSC
How do you know if you won the game? **Return of spontaneous circulation is indicated by the restoration of a palpable pulse.** That means you can feel their heartbeat again!

But their brain has just been through a rough time without oxygen. To protect it from getting swollen, we cool their body down like putting an overheated phone in the shade. **Targeted temperature management is initiated after return of spontaneous circulation in comatose adult clients to optimize neurological recovery.**

## Part II: Opening the Valves (Airway & Overdose)

Airways are just fleshy tubes. When they get blocked, air can't get to the lungs. 

If someone is awake and chokes on a giant piece of pizza, **the abdominal thrust maneuver is the primary intervention for a conscious adult with a complete upper airway obstruction.** We squeeze their belly to use their own lung air to pop the pizza out!

If someone is passed out, their own tongue gets lazy and falls backward, blocking the tube. **The head-tilt chin-lift maneuver opens the airway by displacing the tongue anteriorly away from the posterior pharynx.** (That is a fancy way of saying tilting the head back pulls the tongue forward and out of the way!).
But wait! If they were in a car crash, tilting their head could hurt their neck bones. Instead, **a client with a suspected cervical spine injury requires the jaw-thrust maneuver to open the airway.** You just lift their jaw up without bending their neck.

What if the tube is perfectly open, but a drug makes the brain forget to breathe? This happens with opioid overdoses. **Naloxone is the specific antidote administered for opioid-induced respiratory depression.** It kicks the bad drug off the brain's buttons and turns breathing back on!

## Part III: Recognizing the Cliff Edge (The Rapid Response Team)

The best way to fix an emergency is to stop it before it happens. Patients usually show warning signs in their vital numbers before they crash. You want to call the coach for a timeout *before* you lose the game!

| Vital Sign Parameter | Trigger for Rapid Response Team (RRT) |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Heart Rate** | **A rapid response team should be called for acute changes in a client's heart rate below 40 beats per minute.** |
| **Heart Rate** | **A rapid response team should be called for acute changes in a client's heart rate above 130 beats per minute.** |
| **Blood Pressure** | **A rapid response team should be called for an acute change in a client's systolic blood pressure to below 90 millimeters of mercury.** |
| **Oxygenation** | **A rapid response team should be called for an acute drop in a client's oxygen saturation to below 90 percent despite oxygen supplementation.** |

## Part IV: When the Pipes Fail (Shock & Trauma)

"Shock" in medicine just means the body's delivery system failed, like a pizza delivery guy who gets a flat tire and never brings the food. The body's cells are starving for oxygen. Let's look at how the pipes can break.

### 1. The Pipes are Leaking (Hemorrhage & Hypovolemia)
If blood is leaking out, your first job is to plug the hole! **Direct pressure is the first-line intervention for controlling external hemorrhage.** Just push hard on the cut!
If pushing doesn't work for an arm or a leg, you need to block the pipe completely. **A tourniquet should be applied proximal to a bleeding site if direct pressure fails to control severe extremity hemorrhage.**

Once you stop the leak, you have to refill the water balloon. **Hypovolemic shock requires rapid administration of isotonic crystalloid intravenous fluids.** (This just means giving them IV fluids that act exactly like the water already in our blood).

### 2. The Pipes are Sabotaged (Anaphylactic Shock)
Sometimes the body's immune system freaks out over an allergy, like a bee sting. This causes two huge plumbing problems. 
First, the pipes get way too wide: **anaphylactic shock is characterized by massive systemic vasodilation.** 
Second, the pipes get tiny holes and leak water everywhere: **anaphylactic shock increases systemic capillary permeability.**

To fix this super fast, we use our super-booster medicine to squeeze the pipes back to normal.
*   **Intramuscular epinephrine is the first-line pharmacological treatment for anaphylaxis.** 
*   It goes right into a big muscle so it works fast: **Intramuscular epinephrine for anaphylaxis should be administered in the mid-outer thigh.** 
*   **The standard adult dose of intramuscular epinephrine for anaphylaxis is 0.3 to 0.5 milligrams.**

Let's do some quick math to see how much liquid that is, if you have a bottle that has 1 milligram in every 1 milliliter (mL) of water:
Step 1: Your goal dose is 0.5 milligrams.
Step 2: Your bottle has 1 milligram in 1 mL.
Step 3: Divide what you want by what you have: 0.5 / 1 = 0.5.
Step 4: You will put exactly 0.5 mL of liquid into your syringe!

### 3. Squeezing the Pump (Obstructive Shock)
Sometimes the pump (the heart) is totally healthy, but something heavy is crushing it from the outside so it can't beat. 

*   **Tension Pneumothorax:** Air gets trapped in the chest space like a balloon blowing up inside you. It pushes so hard it squishes the heart and pushes your windpipe sideways! **Tension pneumothorax is a life-threatening emergency characterized by tracheal deviation to the unaffected side of the chest.** How do we fix it? Pop the trapped air balloon! **Needle decompression is the immediate emergency treatment for a tension pneumothorax.**
*   **Cardiac Tamponade:** The protective sack around the heart fills up with fluid, squeezing the heart so it can't expand. Look for these three clues:
    1.  **Cardiac tamponade is characterized by muffled heart sounds upon auscultation** (when you listen with a stethoscope, the heart sounds quiet because you are listening through a wall of water).
    2.  **Cardiac tamponade is characterized by jugular venous distention** (the neck veins bulge out because blood backs up like a traffic jam).
    3.  **Cardiac tamponade is characterized by severe hypotension** (blood pressure drops super low because the squished heart can't pump).

## Part V: Electrical Storms in the Brain (Status Epilepticus)

If cardiac arrest is a glitch in the heart, a seizure is a wild lightning storm in the brain! Usually, seizures stop on their own. But if they don't, it is a huge emergency because the brain is burning up too much energy.

**Status epilepticus is a medical emergency characterized by a continuous seizure lasting more than 5 minutes**, OR it can happen if they keep having back-to-back seizures without waking up. **Status epilepticus is a medical emergency characterized by two or more seizures without complete recovery of consciousness between episodes.** 

To calm the lightning storm down, we give fast-acting chill-out medicines through an IV:
*   **Intravenous lorazepam is a first-line pharmacological intervention to halt status epilepticus.**
*   Or, as another great option: **intravenous diazepam is a first-line pharmacological intervention to halt status epilepticus.**

While the seizure is happening, you must protect their breathing tube! **A client experiencing a seizure must be placed in a side-lying position to maintain a patent airway.** This lets spit drain out so they don't choke. Also, **nothing should be inserted into the mouth of a client actively experiencing a seizure.** Never put a spoon or your fingers in there; you will just break their teeth!

## Part VI: The Black Box (Communication & Documentation)

In an emergency, talking clearly is just as important as the medicine! 

If something scary and sudden happens to your patient, you have to tell the doctor right away. **An unexpected client response during emergency treatment requires immediate notification of the primary health care provider using the Situation-Background-Assessment-Recommendation communication tool.** 
Why do we use that tool? **The Situation-Background-Assessment-Recommendation framework standardizes communication among health care team members during critical events.** It means everybody talks the exact same way, just like a pilot talking to the control tower, so no important details are missed!

Finally, if you didn't write it down, it never happened. After you save the day, you have to log everything perfectly. 
*   **Medical emergency documentation must include the exact time the emergency event was discovered.**
*   **Medical emergency documentation must include the exact time cardiopulmonary resuscitation was initiated.**
*   **Medical emergency documentation must include the exact names of all administered medications**, and **medical emergency documentation must include the exact dosages of administered medications.**
*   You also have to write down exactly how the body reacted to what you did! **Medical emergency documentation must record the client's physiological responses to each specific intervention.**

When you look at emergencies as just pumps, pipes, electricity, and good teamwork, it isn't scary anymore. You just find the broken part and fix it! Now get out there and keep studying!
