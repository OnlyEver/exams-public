# The Architecture of [Recovery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Healing): [Illness Management](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disease_management) & [Respiratory Mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Respiratory_mechanics)
**An [NCLEX-RN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Council_Licensure_Examination) Masterclass**

## Introduction: The [Physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics) of [Healing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Healing)

Listen, when we talk about [illness management](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disease_management), we aren’t just talking about handing out [pills](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tablet_%28pharmacy%29) and filing [paperwork](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medical_record). We are acting as [engineers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineer) of human recovery. We are managing beautifully complex, dynamic [systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/System)—[behavioral systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Behavioral_sciences), [mechanical systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanical_system), and chemical systems. Whether we are teaching a [patient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patient) how to survive a [failing heart](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heart_failure) or manipulating the [pressure gradients](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pressure_gradient) in a collapsing [lung](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lung), we are applying the fundamental rules of [nature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nature).

Let's break down exactly how we teach, how we evaluate, and how we keep the human [respiratory engine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Respiratory_system) running. Don't just [memorize](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Memorization) the rules—understand the *[mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanics)* behind them.

## Part I: The Human Element – [Education](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patient_education) and [Continuity of Care](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuity_of_care)

You can have the most elegant, scientifically perfect treatment plan in the world, but if the patient doesn't understand it or won’t do it, your plan is useless. It’s like transmitting a brilliant [radio broadcast](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radio_broadcasting) to a broken [receiver](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radio_receiver). 

### The Mechanics of [Communication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communication)
Before you even open your mouth to explain a [diagnosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medical_diagnosis), you have to [calibrate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calibration) your transmission. **The [nurse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nurse) must [assess](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nursing_assessment) the [health literacy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Health_literacy) level of the [client](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patient) before initiating education regarding an [acute condition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acute_care).** If you speak in high-level medical jargon to a patient who doesn't understand basic [anatomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anatomy), that [data](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data) is lost into the void. 

Once you deliver the [information](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information), how do you know it actually landed? You don't just ask, "Do you understand?" because everyone politely nods "yes" to that! Instead, we use a [closed feedback loop](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Negative_feedback). **The [teach-back method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teach-back_method) is a communication confirmation technique used to verify client [comprehension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reading_comprehension) of [health education](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Health_education).** You ask the patient to explain the [concept](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concept) back to you in their own words. It's the only way to prove the data transferred intact.

### The "Numbers Game" of [Chronic Illness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chronic_condition)
When we send patients home, we have to give them specific, measurable [parameters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parameter). **Successful [chronic illness management](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chronic_care_management) requires active client participation in creating the [treatment plan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nursing_care_plan).** You aren't dictating; you are collaborating to build a [system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/System) they can actually live with. 

Here are the non-negotiable rules you must teach for specific chronic conditions:

| The Condition | The Mechanical Problem | The Educational Rule |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **[Heart Failure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heart_failure)** | The [pump](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pump) is weak, so [fluid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluid) backs up into the [lungs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lung) and [tissue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tissue_%28biology%29). We measure this via [gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity) (a [scale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weighing_scale)). | **A client with [heart failure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heart_failure) must be taught to report a [weight gain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weight_gain) of more than two [pounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pound_%28mass%29) in one day.** Furthermore, **a client with heart failure must be taught to report a weight gain of more than five pounds in one week.** That weight isn't [fat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adipose_tissue); it's [water weight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water_retention_%28medicine%29). |
| **[Diabetes Mellitus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diabetes)** | [Neuropathy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peripheral_neuropathy) dulls [sensation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somatosensory_system) and poor [circulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circulatory_system) prevents [healing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wound_healing). A tiny pebble in a [shoe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shoe) can cause an [amputation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amputation). | **Education for a client with [diabetes mellitus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diabetes) includes instructions to inspect the [feet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foot) daily for [skin breakdown](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diabetic_foot_ulcer).** |
| **[Chronic Kidney Disease](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chronic_kidney_disease)** | The [kidneys](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kidney) have lost their ability to [filter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Filtration) and balance [electrolytes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrolyte). | **A client with [chronic kidney disease](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chronic_kidney_disease) requires education on restricting dietary sodium intake**, and equally critically, **a client with chronic kidney disease requires education on restricting dietary potassium intake.** |

![Daily weight monitoring on a scale is the most reliable non-invasive mechanical assessment for detecting fluid volume overload in heart failure clients.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Feet_on_scale.jpg)

### Engineering the Seamless Transition
[Discharge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hospital_discharge) isn't a sudden event; it's a meticulously planned [trajectory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trajectory). In fact, **[discharge planning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discharge_planning) for a client begins on the day of [admission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hospital_admission) to the [healthcare facility](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Health_facility).** [Nature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nature) abhors a [vacuum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vacuum), and [healthcare systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Health_system) abhor a gap in care. **[Continuity of care](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuity_of_care) involves seamless transitions of client care between different healthcare settings.** 

To prevent these dangerous gaps, **the nurse initiates [referrals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Referral_%28medicine%29) to [home health agencies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home_care) to promote continuity of care after hospital discharge.** Because managing chronic [disease](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disease) is incredibly complex, **[interdisciplinary collaboration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interdisciplinarity) ensures comprehensive management of a chronic illness during care transitions.** You need the [pharmacist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pharmacist), the [physical therapist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_therapy), and the [social worker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_work) all pulling the same rope. 

Finally, human [memory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Memory) is terrible, especially under [stress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stress_%28biology%29). Therefore, **the nurse provides written instructions regarding [medication schedules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medical_prescription) upon client discharge.** Memory fades; [paper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paper) doesn't.

## Part II: The [Feedback Loop](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feedback_loop) – Evaluating [Interventions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medical_procedure)

In [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics), you run an [experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Experiment), gather the data, and compare it to your [baseline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baseline_%28medicine%29). In [nursing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nursing), we do the exact same thing. 

When you give a [drug](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drug), your job has just begun. **The nurse evaluates the effectiveness of a [pharmacological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pharmacology) intervention by assessing the client after [medication administration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Route_of_administration).** 

How do we measure [success](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Success)? We look at the [delta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Delta_%28letter%29)—the change. **Evaluating [treatment effectiveness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_needed_to_treat) includes comparing current [vital signs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vital_signs) to the baseline vital signs of the client.** If you gave a [beta-blocker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beta_blocker), did the [heart rate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heart_rate) actually drop? [Subjective data](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subjectivity) is still valid data: **a decrease in a numerical [pain score](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pain_scale) indicates a positive evaluation of an [analgesic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analgesic) treatment plan.**

![A standardized numeric and facial pain scale provides an objective data point to evaluate the success of pharmacological interventions.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/%E9%9D%A2%E9%83%A8%E8%A1%A8%E6%83%85%E7%96%BC%E7%97%9B%E8%AF%84%E5%88%86%E9%87%8F%E8%A1%A8.png)

Every single [action](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_%28philosophy%29) and [reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_reaction) must be captured for the next [clinician](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clinician). **The nurse documents the client response to all nursing interventions in the [electronic health record](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_health_record).** 

> **The [Iteration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iteration) Rule:** What if the intervention *didn't* work? You don't just stare at the patient. **The [nursing process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nursing_process) requires modifying the [care plan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nursing_care_plan) upon failing to achieve expected client outcomes.** We iterate. We [adapt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adaptation).

## Part III: The [Physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics) of [Breathing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Breathing) – [Ventilation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ventilation_%28physiology%29) vs. [Oxygenation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygenation_%28medical%29)

Now, let's look at the most urgent system in the body: the [respiratory system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Respiratory_system). People often confuse ventilation and oxygenation, but they are two entirely different mechanical [processes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Process_%28science%29). Think of a [train station](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Train_station). 

### Ventilation: Moving the Trains
Ventilation is just the mechanical movement of [air](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth). It is the [bellows](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bellows) expanding and contracting. 

![Ventilation operates on the physical principle of a bellows: creating negative pressure to mechanically draw air into the lungs.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Bellows_(PSF).svg)

*   **Impaired ventilation involves a disruption in the mechanical movement of air into the lungs** (like an [obstruction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Airway_obstruction) in the [windpipe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trachea) or a paralyzed [diaphragm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thoracic_diaphragm)).
*   Equally, **impaired ventilation involves a disruption in the mechanical movement of air out of the lungs** (like the [air-trapping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air_trapping) we see in [asthma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asthma) or [COPD](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chronic_obstructive_pulmonary_disease), where the lungs hyper-inflate).

We measure ventilation by looking at [carbon dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_dioxide) ($PaCO_2$), the exhaust [gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas) of the human engine. 
*   **Normal [partial pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_pressure) of [arterial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arterial_blood) carbon dioxide is between 35 and 45 [millimeters of mercury](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Millimeter_of_mercury).**
*   If you stop ventilating effectively, the exhaust builds up in the [blood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood), turning it [acidic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acid). **A partial pressure of arterial carbon dioxide greater than 45 millimeters of mercury indicates [respiratory acidosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Respiratory_acidosis).**

### Oxygenation: Loading the Passengers
Oxygenation is what happens at the [microscopic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microscopic_scale) level. It's the passengers ([oxygen molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen)) getting onto the train ([hemoglobin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hemoglobin)). **Impaired oxygenation occurs upon inadequate transfer of oxygen from the [alveoli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulmonary_alveolus) into the [pulmonary capillaries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capillary).** You can have perfect ventilation ([breathing fast](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperventilation) and deep) but terrible oxygenation (a [pulmonary embolism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulmonary_embolism) blocking the [blood flow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hemodynamics)). 

![Oxygenation occurs strictly at the microscopic level, where oxygen molecules diffuse across the alveolar-capillary membrane to load onto hemoglobin.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Cross_section_of_an_alveolus_and_capillaries_showing_diffusion_of_gases.svg)

We measure oxygenation using [arterial oxygen saturation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen_saturation_%28medicine%29) ($SaO_2$). 
*   **Normal arterial oxygen saturation ranges from 95 [percent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Percentage) to 100 percent.**
*   If it drops, we enter the danger zone: **an arterial oxygen saturation below 90 percent is a clinical indicator of [hypoxemia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypoxemia).**

### The Clinical Signs of [Hypoxia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypoxia_%28medical%29)
When the [brain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brain) runs out of oxygen, it doesn't quietly go to [sleep](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sleep). It panics. 
*   **[Restlessness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Psychomotor_agitation) is an early [clinical manifestation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medical_sign) of hypoxia.** If a perfectly calm patient suddenly can't sit still and is tearing off their [sheets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bed_sheet), check their oxygen!
*   In older [populations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Population), the brain's warning system alters slightly: **[confusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confusion) is an early clinical manifestation of hypoxia in [older adult](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old_age) clients.**
*   If you wait too long, you see the late, catastrophic signs. **[Cyanosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyanosis) of the [mucous membranes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mucous_membrane) is an observable sign of severe inadequate [tissue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tissue_%28biology%29) oxygenation.** 
*   If the body suffers from low oxygen for years (like in severe COPD), the anatomy physically changes to compensate: **[clubbing of the fingers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nail_clubbing) is a late clinical sign of [chronic hypoxia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypoxia_%28medical%29).**

![Nail clubbing is a structural anatomical adaptation resulting from years of chronic tissue hypoxia, frequently observed in severe COPD.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Clubbing.svg)

## Part IV: Manipulating the Mechanics – [Respiratory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Respiratory_system) Interventions

If the system is failing, how do we fix it? We use positioning, pressure, and fluid dynamics.

### [Gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity) and [Anatomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anatomy)
*   **The nurse places a client with impaired ventilation in a [high Fowler position](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fowler%27s_position) to facilitate maximum chest expansion.** By sitting them straight up, gravity pulls the heavy [abdominal organs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abdomen) down, giving the diaphragm room to drop. 
*   What about the sickest of the sick? In [Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acute_respiratory_distress_syndrome) (ARDS), the lungs are filled with [fluid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluid), squashing the healthy alveoli at the back of the lungs. The solution? Flip them over! **[Prone positioning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prone_position) improves oxygenation in clients with acute respiratory distress syndrome by recruiting collapsed alveoli.**

![Prone positioning alters the physical pressure gradients in the chest cavity, actively recruiting collapsed posterior alveoli in patients with ARDS.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Supine_and_prone_diagrams-en.svg)

### Breathing Mechanics
We can teach patients to manually override their broken lung mechanics.
*   **[Diaphragmatic breathing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diaphragmatic_breathing) decreases the [work of breathing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Work_of_breathing) for clients with [chronic respiratory disease](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Respiratory_disease)** by forcing them to use their primary [breathing muscle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muscles_of_respiration) efficiently rather than exhausting their secondary [chest muscles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thoracic_wall).
*   In COPD, the [airways](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Respiratory_tract) are floppy and collapse when the patient tries to [exhale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exhalation). We fix this by creating a localized [back-pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_pressure): **[pursed-lip breathing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pursed_lip_breathing) helps prevent [airway collapse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Airway_obstruction) in clients with [chronic obstructive pulmonary disease](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chronic_obstructive_pulmonary_disease).** 
*   After [surgery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surgery), patients take shallow [breaths](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Breathing) because it hurts, leading to collapsed lung bases ([atelectasis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atelectasis)). We give them a [device](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medical_device) that requires physics to work: **[Incentive spirometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Incentive_spirometer) promotes [deep breathing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diaphragmatic_breathing) in [postoperative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perioperative) clients**, and ultimately, **incentive spirometry prevents [alveolar collapse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atelectasis) in postoperative clients.**

![An incentive spirometer utilizes visual mechanical feedback to encourage deep, sustained diaphragmatic breathing, effectively preventing postoperative alveolar collapse.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Incentive_Spirometer.png)

### [Secretions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Secretion) and [Pharmacology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pharmacology)
If the [pipes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pipe_%28fluid_conveyance%29) are gunked up with thick [mucus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phlegm), we don't just need drugs; we need a [solvent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solvent). **The nurse encourages a [fluid intake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drinking) of at least two to three [liters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Litre) per day to thin [respiratory secretions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phlegm) in clients with [pneumonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pneumonia).**

When we do use drugs, the [order of operations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Order_of_operations) matters immensely. 
*   **[Albuterol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salbutamol) is a [short-acting bronchodilator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Short-acting_beta-agonist) used for [acute asthma exacerbations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acute_severe_asthma).** It kicks open the clamped airways.
*   Because you need the door open for other drugs to get deep into the lungs, **the nurse administers a short-acting [bronchodilator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bronchodilator) before administering an [inhaled corticosteroid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inhaled_corticosteroid).**

How do we know these respiratory treatments worked? We listen to the [airflow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Airflow). **The nurse [auscultates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Auscultation) the [lung fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lung) to evaluate the effectiveness of respiratory treatments.**

## Part V: [Supplemental Oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen_therapy) & [Artificial Pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positive_airway_pressure)

### [Oxygen Delivery Devices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen_mask)
When [room air](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Breathing_gas) (21% oxygen) isn't enough, we supplement.
*   The basic tool is the [nasal cannula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nasal_cannula). **A nasal cannula delivers supplemental [oxygen concentrations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fraction_of_inspired_oxygen) ranging from 24 percent to 44 percent.**
*   If they need maximum support without being [intubated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tracheal_intubation), we use the [bag mask](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bag_valve_mask). **A [non-rebreather mask](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-rebreather_mask) delivers the highest concentration of supplemental oxygen among [low-flow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen_therapy) oxygen delivery systems.** 
*   When a [blood clot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thrombus) hits the lung, [gas exchange](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas_exchange) stops instantly. Time is tissue. **A client with a [pulmonary embolism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulmonary_embolism) requires immediate administration of supplemental oxygen.**

![A non-rebreather mask utilizes a continuous flow reservoir bag and one-way valves to deliver the highest possible concentration of supplemental oxygen in acute hypoxemia.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/NRBer.JPG)

> **The [Toxicity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toxicity) Warning:** [Oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen) is a [drug](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drug), and like any drug, it has a [toxicity profile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toxicity). **[Oxygen toxicity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen_toxicity) is a [complication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complication_%28medicine%29) of administering high concentrations of supplemental oxygen over a [prolonged period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time).** It generates [free radicals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radical_%28chemistry%29) that destroy the very lung tissue we are trying to save.

### [Positive Airway Pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positive_airway_pressure) (PAP)
Sometimes, the problem isn't the percentage of oxygen; it's that the airways are physically collapsing. We use [machines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Machine) to blow air like a [splint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Splint_%28medicine%29) to keep the airways open.
*   **[Continuous positive airway pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_positive_airway_pressure) (CPAP) maintains [positive pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positive_pressure) in the airways throughout the entire respiratory cycle.** It’s one constant, unyielding pressure pushing in.
*   **[Bilevel positive airway pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bilevel_positive_airway_pressure) (BiPAP) provides two different levels of pressure during [inspiration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inhalation) and [expiration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exhalation).** It gives a high pressure when the patient breathes in, and a lower pressure when they breathe out, making it easier to exhale against the machine while still keeping the airways from collapsing.

## Part VI: [Invasive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invasiveness_of_surgical_procedures) Plumbing – [Suctions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suction_%28medicine%29) and [Chest Tubes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chest_tube)

When the body's natural [clearing mechanisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mucociliary_clearance) fail, or the [vacuum seal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vacuum) of the chest is broken, we have to intervene invasively.

### [Endotracheal Suctioning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suction_%28medicine%29)
If a patient has an [endotracheal tube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tracheal_tube), they can't [cough](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cough) up secretions. We have to [vacuum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vacuum) them out. But remember: when you suction a tube, you aren't just sucking out mucus; you are sucking out their oxygen!
*   To prepare the patient for this temporary [suffocation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asphyxia), **the nurse hyperoxygenates the client with 100 percent oxygen before initiating [endotracheal suctioning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suction_%28medicine%29).**
*   **Endotracheal suctioning must be limited to 10 [seconds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second) per suction pass to prevent severe [hypoxemia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypoxemia).**

### [Chest Tubes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chest_tube) and the [Pleural Space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pleural_cavity)
The lungs expand because the pleural space surrounding them operates on a vacuum ([negative pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Negative_pressure)). If air or fluid gets into that space, the vacuum is lost, and the lung [collapses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pneumothorax). We [insert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medical_procedure) a [chest tube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chest_tube) to [drain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drain_%28surgery%29) the air/fluid and restore the vacuum using a closed [water-seal system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chest_tube). 

You must understand the two critical chambers of a [chest drainage system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chest_tube):
1.  **The [Suction Control Chamber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chest_tube):** This controls the amount of vacuum pulling on the patient. **The nurse must verify the continuous bubbling in the suction control chamber of a [water-seal chest drainage system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chest_tube).** Continuous bubbling here is *good*—it proves the [mechanical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanics) [wall suction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suction_%28medicine%29) is actively working.
2.  **The [Water-Seal Chamber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chest_tube):** This acts as a [one-way valve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Check_valve), letting air escape the patient's chest but preventing room air from going back in. If there is a [hole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hole) in the patient's lung leaking air into the system, you will see it here. **Intermittent bubbling in the water-seal chamber of a chest tube drainage system indicates an active air leak.** (Every time the patient [exhales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exhalation), air is pushed out of the pleural space, causing a [bubble](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bubble). Continuous bubbling in the water-seal, however, would mean a massive leak or a [disconnected](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disconnection) tube!)

![In a closed water-seal drainage system, continuous bubbling in the suction chamber confirms mechanical vacuum, while intermittent bubbling in the water-seal chamber reveals an active patient air leak.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Labelled_chest_tube_drainage_system.png)

### The Ultimate [Respiratory Emergency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medical_emergency): [Tension Pneumothorax](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tension_pneumothorax)
If air leaks into the pleural space but cannot [escape](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluid_dynamics), [pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pressure) builds continuously with every single breath. It crushes the lung, then physically pushes the [heart](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heart) and [trachea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trachea) to the opposite side of the [chest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thorax). 
*   **[Tracheal deviation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tracheal_deviation) is a classic clinical sign of a [tension pneumothorax](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tension_pneumothorax).** 
*   You don't have time to wait for an [x-ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiography). This is a purely mechanical [blockage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obstructive_shock) of [cardiac output](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardiac_output) and ventilation. **A tension pneumothorax requires immediate [medical intervention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medical_intervention) with [needle decompression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Needle_decompression)** to vent the trapped air and save the patient's [life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life).

![A chest radiograph revealing a severe tension pneumothorax. The massive accumulation of trapped air (dark space) creates extreme positive pressure, mechanically displacing the trachea and heart to the opposite side.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Pneumothorax_CXR.jpg)
