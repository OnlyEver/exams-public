Welcome to the fascinating world of human telemetry. When we take a patient’s [vital signs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vital_signs), we aren't just writing numbers on a chart; we are lifting the hood of a highly complex, beautifully engineered machine to see how the engine is running. Every temperature shift, every pulse wave, every breath, and every millimeter of mercury tells a story of adaptation, compensation, or failure. 

To master [vital signs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vital_signs) for the [NCLEX-RN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Council_Licensure_Examination), you can't just memorize isolated numbers. You have to understand *why* the body is doing what it's doing. Let's break down the mechanics of life.

---

## 1. The Cardinal Rules of Vital Signs

Before we touch a [thermometer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermometer) or a blood pressure cuff, we must understand the overarching principles of physiological monitoring. 

First, context is everything. **Changes in [vital signs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vital_signs) must be evaluated in the context of the client's individual [baseline measurements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baseline_%28medicine%29).** A [blood pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_pressure) of 90/60 might be a catastrophic drop for a [hypertensive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypertension) patient, but perfectly normal for a 20-year-old marathon runner. If you ever notice a sudden, unexplained change in a client's [vital signs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vital_signs), do not wait for the next scheduled rounds. **A sudden, unexplained change requires immediate nursing reassessment.** 

We also have to trust our clinical skills over our electronics. Machines get confused. Therefore, **the nurse must verify abnormal automated vital sign readings with manual measurements.**

Finally, we have to account for the environment and the patient's recent actions:
*   **Acute pain** stimulates the sympathetic nervous system (your "fight or flight" response) to **increase the [heart rate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heart_rate)** and **increase [blood pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_pressure)**.
*   **Smoking** causes transient increases in [blood pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_pressure) and [heart rate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heart_rate) due to systemic vasoconstriction. Because of this, **the nurse should wait 15 to 30 minutes to measure [vital signs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vital_signs) if the client has recently smoked.**

## 2. Temperature: The Engine's Heat

Your body is a furnace. It generates heat through [metabolism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metabolism) and loses heat to the environment. The **normal adult [oral temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_body_temperature) range is 36.5 to 37.5 degrees [Celsius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celsius)**. But *where* we measure this heat matters immensely. 

If we want accuracy, we have to respect the [anatomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anatomy). If a client has recently consumed hot or cold liquids, the [oral cavity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mouth) is temporarily altered. **The nurse should wait 15 to 30 minutes to measure an [oral temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_body_temperature)** in this case.

### Routes of Measurement
We often compare other routes to the oral baseline:
*   **Rectal temperatures** are generally **0.5 degrees [Celsius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celsius) higher** than oral temperatures because they are closer to the heavily insulated core.
*   **[Axillary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axilla) temperatures** are generally **0.5 degrees [Celsius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celsius) lower** than oral temperatures because the armpit is exposed to the periphery.
*   **Tympanic thermometers assess core body temperature via the [tympanic membrane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eardrum)** (the eardrum), which shares an incredibly rich blood supply with the [hypothalamus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypothalamus)—the body's thermostat. 
    *   *The Anatomy Trick:* The ear canal isn't straight. **Measuring a tympanic temperature on an adult requires pulling the [pinna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Auricle_%28anatomy%29) up and back** to straighten the canal. However, **measuring a tympanic temperature on a child under three years old requires pulling the [pinna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Auricle_%28anatomy%29) down and back.**

### The Pathophysiology of Temperature Changes
What happens when the engine runs too hot or too cold? 

> **[Hyperthermia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperthermia)** is an elevation in body temperature above the normal physiological range. 

When body temperature is elevated, the body's **[basal metabolic rate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basal_metabolic_rate) increases**. Think of it as a fire burning faster; it needs more oxygen and expels more waste. Therefore, **an increased [basal metabolic rate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basal_metabolic_rate) leads to a compensatory increase in the [heart rate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heart_rate)** (to deliver the blood) and a **compensatory increase in the [respiratory rate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Respiratory_rate)** (to bring in more oxygen).

> **[Hypothermia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypothermia)** is defined as a core body temperature below 35 degrees [Celsius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celsius). 

Conversely, freezing the engine slows everything down. **Severe [hypothermia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypothermia) depresses the myocardium and decreases the [heart rate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heart_rate)**, leading to dangerous [bradycardia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bradycardia).

## 3. Heart Rate: The Pump and the Pipes

The heart is a pump pushing fluid through elastic pipes. The **normal resting heart rate for an adult is 60 to 100 beats per minute**. 
*   **[Tachycardia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tachycardia)** is defined as a resting adult [heart rate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heart_rate) **greater than 100** beats per minute.
*   **[Bradycardia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bradycardia)** is defined as a resting adult [heart rate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heart_rate) **less than 60** beats per minute.

### Assessing the Rhythm
We assess the heart centrally (the apical pulse) and peripherally (like the radial pulse). **The apical pulse is assessed at the fifth intercostal space at the midclavicular line**—right over the apex of the heart. If the client has an irregular rhythm, a quick 15-second check won't cut it. **An apical pulse should be counted for one full minute to ensure accuracy in clients with irregular rhythms.**

Sometimes, the pump is struggling. A **pulse deficit** occurs when the apical pulse rate exceeds the radial pulse rate. Why does this happen? **A pulse deficit indicates that some cardiac contractions are too weak to produce a [palpable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palpation) peripheral pulse.** The heart is beating, but the shockwave never reaches the wrist!

If you cannot feel a peripheral pulse at all, do not immediately panic—use technology. **A Doppler ultrasound device is used to assess non-palpable peripheral pulses** by bouncing sound waves off moving [red blood cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_blood_cell).

### Pulse Volume and Compensation
The *feel* of the pulse tells a hemodynamic story:
*   A **bounding peripheral pulse** feels full and powerful. It indicates **increased stroke volume or increased [pulse pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulse_pressure)**.
*   A **thready peripheral pulse** feels weak and easily obliterated. It indicates **decreased stroke volume**.

Why does the heart speed up or slow down? 
*   If the tank is leaking (loss of blood or fluids), **hypovolemia causes a compensatory increase in the [heart rate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heart_rate) to maintain [cardiac output](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardiac_output)**. 
*   On the flip side, bearing down or certain throat stimulations trigger the [vagus nerve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vagus_nerve). **Vagal nerve stimulation causes a decrease in the [heart rate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heart_rate).**

### The Shock Profiles
When a patient is crashing, their [vital signs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vital_signs) tell you *how* they are crashing:
*   **New onset [tachycardia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tachycardia) accompanied by [hypotension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypotension) suggests acute [hemorrhage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bleeding) or distributive shock.** The tank is empty or inappropriately dilated, so the heart pumps furiously to compensate.
*   **Neurogenic shock presents uniquely with [bradycardia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bradycardia) and [hypotension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypotension).** Why? Because the sympathetic nervous system has been severed (usually via spinal cord injury), leaving the [parasympathetic nervous system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parasympathetic_nervous_system) entirely unopposed.

## 4. Respirations: The Bellows

The **normal resting [respiratory rate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Respiratory_rate) for an adult is 12 to 20 breaths per minute.** 
*   **Tachypnea** is a [respiratory rate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Respiratory_rate) greater than 20.
*   **Bradypnea** is a [respiratory rate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Respiratory_rate) less than 12.

*Psychomotor tip:* If someone knows you are watching them breathe, they will consciously change their breathing pattern. Therefore, **respiratory assessment should occur without the client realizing their breathing is being monitored** (often done while pretending to still take their radial pulse).

### What Drives the Breath?
*   **[Hypoxia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypoxia_%28medical%29)** (low oxygen) **stimulates the peripheral chemoreceptors to increase the [respiratory rate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Respiratory_rate).** The body is gasping for air.
*   Conversely, **[opioid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Opioid) medications depress the [central nervous system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_nervous_system) and cause a decrease in the [respiratory rate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Respiratory_rate).** This is why we carefully monitor respiratory rates in post-operative patients on [morphine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morphine).

### Abnormal Breathing Patterns
Nature provides brilliant, yet alarming, visual cues when acid-base or neurological balances fail.

| Pattern | Description | Pathophysiological Meaning |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **[Cheyne-Stokes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cheyne%E2%80%93Stokes_respiration)** | Alternating periods of deep breathing and [apnea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apnea). | Often seen in dying patients or severe brain injury (the [brainstem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brainstem)'s respiratory center is failing). |
| **[Kussmaul](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kussmaul_breathing)** | Abnormally deep and rapid respirations. | **A compensatory mechanism for [metabolic acidosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metabolic_acidosis).** (e.g., [Diabetic Ketoacidosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diabetic_ketoacidosis)). The body is desperately trying to blow off acidic [carbon dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_dioxide) (CO2). |

## 5. Blood Pressure: The Physics of Perfusion

[Blood pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_pressure) is the tension of the fluid against the pipes. **Normal adult [systolic blood pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Systole) is less than 120 millimeter of mercury**, and **normal adult diastolic blood pressure is less than 80 millimeter of mercury.**

The difference between these two numbers is vital. **[Pulse pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulse_pressure) is the numerical difference between the systolic and diastolic blood pressure values.** A wide [pulse pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulse_pressure) can indicate increased [intracranial pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intracranial_pressure), while a narrow one can indicate [heart failure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heart_failure) or hypovolemia.

### The Mechanics of the Hemodynamics
The pipes and the [blood volume](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_volume) strictly control the pressure:
*   **Decreased [blood volume](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_volume)** causes a **decrease in [blood pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_pressure)**.
*   **Systemic vasodilation** (opening the pipes) causes a **decrease in [blood pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_pressure)**.
*   **Systemic vasoconstriction** (clamping the pipes) causes an **increase in [blood pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_pressure)**.

### The Psychomotor Physics of the BP Cuff
If you measure BP incorrectly, you are treating a phantom problem. The cuff must fit the arm perfectly based on pure physics:
*   **The width of the blood pressure cuff bladder should cover forty percent (40%) of the client's arm circumference.**
*   **The length of the blood pressure cuff bladder should encircle eighty percent (80%) of the client's arm circumference.**

What happens if you use the wrong size?
*   **A blood pressure cuff that is too narrow will result in an artificially HIGH [blood pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_pressure) reading.** (It takes too much pressure to force a tiny cuff to occlude the [artery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artery)).
*   **A blood pressure cuff that is too wide will result in an artificially LOW [blood pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_pressure) reading.** (The wide cuff occludes the [artery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artery) too easily).

Positioning and deflation speed are equally critical:
*   **The client's arm should be positioned at the level of the heart.**
*   **Positioning the arm ABOVE the heart level causes a falsely LOW reading** (fighting [gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity)).
*   **Positioning the arm BELOW the heart level causes a falsely HIGH reading** ([gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity) adds pressure).
*   **Crossing the legs during measurement causes a falsely elevated systolic reading** due to compressed venous return in the lower body translocating blood upward.
*   **Deflating the cuff too slowly causes venous congestion in the arm**, skewing the numbers.
*   **Deflating the cuff too quickly results in a falsely LOW systolic reading and a falsely HIGH diastolic reading.** (You miss the first sound, and you mistake the muffled sounds for the complete cessation of sound).

### Pathological Blood Pressure Phenomena

#### Orthostatic Hypotension
When you stand up, [gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity) pulls your blood into your legs. A healthy body instantly vasoconstricts to push it back up. If this reflex fails:
> **Orthostatic hypotension** involves a **drop in [systolic blood pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Systole) of at least 20 mmHg** OR a **drop in diastolic blood pressure of at least 10 mmHg** when moving from [supine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supine_position) to standing. 

To prove this, **measuring orthostatic [blood pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_pressure) requires assessing [blood pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_pressure) and [heart rate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heart_rate) in the [supine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supine_position), sitting, and standing positions.**

#### Paradoxical Blood Pressure (Pulsus Paradoxus)
> **[Paradoxical blood pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulsus_paradoxus) is a decrease in [systolic blood pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Systole) of more than 10 millimeter of mercury during [inspiration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inhalation).** 

Why? When you breathe in, negative pressure pulls blood into the right side of the heart. If fluid is trapped in the sac around the heart (cardiac tamponade), the bulging right ventricle crushes the left ventricle, causing a massive drop in systemic output. This makes [paradoxical blood pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulsus_paradoxus) a **classic clinical sign of cardiac tamponade.**

#### Cushing's Triad
If the pressure inside the skull ([intracranial pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intracranial_pressure)) becomes massive, the brain desperately tries to keep blood flowing in against the squeeze. **Severely increased [intracranial pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intracranial_pressure) causes an increase in [systolic blood pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Systole).** 

If you see the following three signs, the brainstem is herniating. **Cushing's triad is a late sign of severely increased [intracranial pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intracranial_pressure):**
1.  **Systolic hypertension with a widening [pulse pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulse_pressure)**
2.  **Pathological [bradycardia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bradycardia)**
3.  **Irregular respirations**

## 6. Pulse Oximetry: The Oxygen Freight Train

[Pulse oximetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulse_oximetry) measures the [red blood cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_blood_cell), which act as boxcars carrying oxygen. Specifically, **[pulse oximetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulse_oximetry) measures the percentage of [hemoglobin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hemoglobin) binding sites occupied by oxygen.** 

**A normal [pulse oximetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulse_oximetry) reading in a healthy adult is 95 percent to 100 percent.**

### Pitfalls of the Pulse Ox
The pulse oximeter relies on light absorption. Therefore, things that block or alter light reception will trick the machine:
*   **Dark nail polish can interfere with accurate [pulse oximetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulse_oximetry) readings on the fingers.** (Remove it!).
*   **Poor peripheral perfusion causes inaccurate [pulse oximetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulse_oximetry) readings.** If the fingers are freezing cold or the patient is in [shock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shock_%28circulatory%29), blood isn't reaching the sensor.

**The Carbon Monoxide Trap:** Carbon monoxide (CO) has an affinity for [hemoglobin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hemoglobin) that is 200 times stronger than oxygen. It will bully oxygen off the binding sites. The pulse oximeter only sees that the "boxcars" are full, but it cannot tell they are full of poison. Therefore, **carbon monoxide poisoning produces falsely elevated [pulse oximetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulse_oximetry) readings.**

Finally, if the machine isn't lying, you must act fast. **An acute decrease in [oxygen saturation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen_saturation_%28medicine%29) requires immediately assessing the client's airway patency.** Always look at your patient first. Is their airway open? Are they choking? Secure the plumbing before you stare at the monitor.

### Conclusion

[Vital signs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vital_signs) are the ultimate physiological report card. When you see a high [heart rate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heart_rate), ask yourself: *Is this pain? Is it hypovolemia? Is it compensation for a [fever](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fever)?* When you see a low [blood pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_pressure), ask yourself: *Is my cuff too wide, or is this patient going into [shock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shock_%28circulatory%29)?* 

Apply the physics, trust your manual assessments, and always, *always* trace the vital sign back to the underlying physiology. That is how you think like an elite nurse, and that is how you conquer the [NCLEX-RN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Council_Licensure_Examination).
