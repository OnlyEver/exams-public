Listen to me, buddy. Slide that coaster over here and focus, because I need to explain what surgery actually is. People think surgery is a neat, sterile little fix. It’s not. Surgery is a chaotic, massive reality TV home renovation on a highly dysfunctional nightclub called The Human Body. The surgeons are the sledgehammer-swinging contractors who tear down the walls to "fix" a problem, and the nurses? We are the frantic nightclub managers left behind to keep the building from collapsing, flooding, or catching fire before the grand re-opening. 

Let's start in the VIP lounge—the lungs. Your alveoli are millions of tiny, wet party balloons that need to stay inflated to keep the oxygen flowing. But the contractors just sliced open the club's walls, and now it hurts to move. Alveolar collapse, which we call atelectasis, occurs postoperatively due to shallow breathing from incision pain. The guests are too sore to take a deep breath, so the balloons just deflate. And this drama happens fast; atelectasis typically manifests 24 to 48 hours after surgery. To fix it, we hand them a plastic party horn. We literally make them do the work. An incentive spirometer promotes sustained maximum inspiration to prevent atelectasis, forcing them to pull a long, deep vacuum that violently pops those collapsed balloons back open. 

But it gets worse, because the club’s cleaning crew is completely knocked out. General anesthesia is a terrible promoter; it severely depresses ciliary action in the respiratory tract. Those millions of tiny microscopic brooms just drop their dustpans and go on strike. Naturally, depressed ciliary action increases the risk of retained respiratory secretions. The trash piles up. And what happens to warm, wet trash left in a dark club? It breeds monsters. Retained respiratory secretions create an environment for bacterial growth leading to pneumonia. 

Right next door to the VIP lounge is the filthy kitchen—the stomach. Aspiration occurs when gastric contents or secretions crash the party and enter the tracheobronchial tree. It’s acid in the air ducts! This is why we lock the kitchen down before the surgeons even arrive. Preoperative fasting reduces the volume and acidity of gastric contents, so if the garbage spills, there’s less of it to burn the lungs. But after surgery, the bouncer at the throat is passed out. An absent gag reflex indicates a high risk for aspiration. If our VIP patient is knocked out cold, gravity is our only weapon. Placing an unconscious postoperative client in a lateral position facilitates the drainage of oral secretions—literally pouring the drool out the side door. At the same time, placing an unconscious postoperative client in a lateral position prevents the lazy bartender, the tongue, from falling backward and obstructing the airway. When they finally wake up a bit, we prop them up on pillows. Elevating the head of the bed decreases the gravitational flow of gastric contents into the esophagus. 

If the kitchen garbage *does* spill into the lungs, the alarms trip instantly. A sudden drop in oxygen saturation after an episode of emesis strongly suggests an aspiration event just went down. The trash takes the path of least resistance, which happens to be the widest, steepest laundry chute on the right side. That’s why decreased breath sounds in the right lower lung lobe indicate potential aspiration of gastric contents. To make sure our clean-up efforts are working, pulse oximetry readings evaluate systemic oxygenation status following interventions to prevent aspiration. 

Now, let's talk about the plumbing and the money—the blood. The contractors spilled a ton of it in the OR. Surgical blood loss decreases circulating intravascular volume leading to decreased cardiac output. The club is running out of liquid cash! The autonomic manager completely panics. Hypovolemic shock triggers the sympathetic nervous system to aggressively increase the heart rate to keep the music playing. This is why tachycardia is an early clinical sign of hypovolemic shock! It’s the manager sprinting around the club in a frenzy. Meanwhile, the club ruthlessly boards up the cheap seats to save the VIPs. Hypovolemia triggers peripheral vasoconstriction to shunt blood to vital organs like the brain and heart. Do not wait for the building to collapse to intervene! Hypotension is a late clinical sign of hypovolemic shock; if the blood pressure drops, the manager has already lost control. Want to know if the VIPs are getting paid? Check the kidneys' tip jar. Urine output less than 30 milliliters per hour indicates decreased renal perfusion, proving the cash flow has completely stalled.

While we're worrying about volume, we also have to worry about stagnation in the basement. Blood is a frantic river, and if it stops, it turns into concrete. Venous stasis from postoperative immobility increases the risk of deep vein thrombosis. To stop the concrete from setting, we have to keep the river flowing. Taking a deep breath is a double whammy—it doesn't just help the lungs, deep breathing creates negative intrathoracic pressure to suck and draw venous blood toward the heart like a giant vacuum! We also kick the guests out of bed. Early ambulation stimulates the calf muscle pump to enhance venous return. If they can’t walk, we suit them up in tight gear. Anti-embolism stockings provide graduated compression to reduce venous pooling in the legs. But don't you dare roll them down like a 1980s sweatband! Rolling the tops of anti-embolism stockings creates a tourniquet effect, and a tourniquet effect on the lower extremities impairs venous return and massively increases thrombosis risk. We also wrap their legs in robot massagers. Sequential compression devices promote venous return by applying intermittent pressure to the lower extremities. But they aren't permanent! Sequential compression devices require periodic removal to assess underlying skin integrity so we don't accidentally rot their skin off. And watch where you put pillows! Placing a pillow directly under the knees creates pressure on the popliteal vein, and that localized pressure on the popliteal vein from pillows or bed adjustments impedes venous return, trapping the blood. We obsessively monitor the pipes. Measurement of calf circumference evaluates the presence of lower extremity edema related to venous stasis. If you see a glowing red alarm—specifically, redness and unilateral warmth in the lower extremity—that indicates a high probability of deep vein thrombosis. We also send in a chemical hitman. Prophylactic subcutaneous administration of enoxaparin prevents clot formation by targeting and inhibiting coagulation Factor Xa, the mob boss of the clotting cascade. If we fail, the concrete block breaks loose. A pulmonary embolism occurs when a thrombus dislodges and occludes the pulmonary artery, jamming the main air vent! This is why a pulmonary embolism is a potentially fatal complication of deep vein thrombosis.

Upstairs, the kitchen and bathrooms are completely out of order. The surgeons rummaged through the kitchen equipment, and surgical manipulation of the bowel causes temporary cessation of intestinal peristalsis. The cooks are terrified and freeze. This paralytic ileus presents with an absolute absence of bowel sounds. Dead silence. To get the cooks cooking again, you walk the patient down the hall! Early ambulation stimulates gastrointestinal motility to prevent paralytic ileus. The glorious moment you know the kitchen is open? The return of flatus indicates the resolution of paralytic ileus! Down the hall, the plumbers are knocked out. Anesthesia suppresses the micturition reflex—the brain’s text message to pee—and anesthesia decreases bladder tone, so the muscle can't even squeeze. The water backs up. Postoperative urinary retention is defined as the inability to void 6 to 8 hours after surgery. If you push on their pelvis, you'll feel a massive water balloon, because bladder distention presents as a palpable mass above the symphysis pubis.

Finally, let’s talk about the structural walls of this club—the incisions. Sometimes the glue doesn’t hold. Wound dehiscence is the separation of surgical incision layers. It happens right when you get complacent, as wound dehiscence typically occurs 5 to 10 days after surgery. Why? Because the guest coughed! Increased intra-abdominal pressure from coughing increases the risk of wound dehiscence. So we give them a shock absorber. Splinting an abdominal incision with a pillow during coughing reduces mechanical stress on the wound. But if the wall completely fails? Absolute horror show. Wound evisceration is the protrusion of internal organs through an open surgical incision. The plumbing literally falls out of the building! This isn’t a "put a band-aid on it" moment. Wound evisceration is a medical emergency requiring immediate surgical intervention. Don't shove the organs back in! Throw a wet tarp over it! Covering an eviscerated wound with sterile saline-soaked dressings prevents tissue necrosis by keeping the organs wet and alive. And lastly, watch for the slow creep of mold in the walls, because surgical site infections typically manifest 3 to 5 days postoperatively.
