Welcome to the amazing machine that is the human body! Being a nurse is like being a master mechanic or a detective. You aren't just looking at a person; you are looking at a giant video game console with wires, pumps, pipes, and cooling systems. 

Our job is to catch tiny glitches before the whole system crashes. If you notice things going wrong early, you have to tell someone. **Early identification of patient deterioration requires prompt notification of the primary healthcare provider.** Before you can call for backup, you need to know exactly what to look for. 

Let's break down how we check out the body's different systems!

---

## 1. The Control Center: Neurological Assessments

The brain is the body’s main computer, locked safely inside the skull. Since we can't see the computer itself, we have to look at what it controls: movement, talking, and reflexes.

### The Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS)
When we want to see how awake and alert a patient is, we use a scoring system called the **Glasgow Coma Scale**. This scale checks three main things:
* **The Glasgow Coma Scale evaluates a patient's eye opening response.**
* **The Glasgow Coma Scale evaluates a patient's verbal response** (how they talk).
* **The Glasgow Coma Scale evaluates a patient's motor response** (how they move).

Let's look at how the math works for getting a perfect score:
1. Check the eye opening response and give a score (a perfect score is 4).
2. Check the verbal response and give a score (a perfect score is 5).
3. Check the motor response and give a score (a perfect score is 6).
4. Add the scores together: 4 + 5 + 6 = 15.

**A Glasgow Coma Scale score of 15 indicates a fully alert and oriented patient.** Their computer is running perfectly!
But, if the score is very low, there is a big problem. **A Glasgow Coma Scale score of 8 or less indicates severe brain injury or coma.**

### Windows to the Brain: Pupils
The eyes are like webcams connected right to the brain. When we shine a flashlight into them, we are testing the wires. **Pupil reactivity to light indicates the functional intactness of the oculomotor nerve** (the wire that controls eye movement). 

Nurses use a memory trick for perfect eyes. **The acronym PERRLA stands for Pupils Equal, Round, Reactive to Light, and Accommodation.**

What if one pupil is huge and the other is tiny? That means the brain might be swelling up and squishing that wire. Because of this, **asymmetrical pupils can indicate a severe brain injury or herniation**. This is a massive emergency!

### Recognizing Neurological Crises
Sometimes, just one part of the brain's hardware gets damaged. **Unilateral weakness during a motor assessment strongly suggests a focal neurological lesion like a stroke.** (Unilateral just means one side of the body is weak, like the left arm but not the right). To check this fast, we use a test called the **Cincinnati Prehospital Stroke Scale**, which checks three things:
1. **The Cincinnati Prehospital Stroke Scale assesses for the presence of facial droop** (is one side of the face sagging?).
2. **The Cincinnati Prehospital Stroke Scale assesses for the presence of arm drift** (when holding both arms out, does one fall down?).
3. **The Cincinnati Prehospital Stroke Scale assesses for abnormal speech patterns** (are their words slurred?).

If the whole brain is swelling up, it fights for blood. This creates a really bad trio of signs called **Cushing's triad**, which **consists of severe bradycardia, irregular respirations, and a widening pulse pressure.** (Bradycardia means a very slow heart rate). 

Warning: **Cushing's triad is a late and life-threatening sign of increased intracranial pressure.** This means the brain is totally out of room inside the skull!

### Behavioral and Sedation Scales
Sometimes the brain has a software glitch from chemicals. 
* We use a test called the CIWA-Ar because **the CIWA-Ar clinical scale evaluates the severity of alcohol withdrawal symptoms** (when someone's body is reacting badly after stopping alcohol).
* In the intensive care unit (ICU), we use another tool. **The Richmond Agitation-Sedation Scale assesses the level of sedation and agitation in intensive care patients** (checking if they are too sleepy or too restless).

## 2. The Pump and the Pipes: Cardiovascular & Hemodynamics

Think of the heart as a water pump and the blood vessels as plumbing pipes. If the pump breaks, or the pipes leak, the whole body gets sick.

### Assessing the Pump
First, we listen to the pump itself. We check the main heartbeat, called the apical pulse. **An apical pulse is assessed by placing the stethoscope at the fifth intercostal space at the left midclavicular line.** 

Because some strong heart medicines (like digitalis) change the heart's timing, **the apical pulse must be auscultated for one full minute prior to administering digitalis medications.** (Auscultated just means listened to with a stethoscope).

What if the pump is getting weak? If we hear an extra sound, like a sloshing heartbeat, that is a clue. **The presence of an S3 heart sound in an older adult is an early auscultatory sign of heart failure.**

### Assessing the Pipes (Perfusion)
We need to make sure blood is reaching the very tips of the fingers and toes. We test this by pressing on a fingernail until it turns white, then letting go to see how fast the pink color comes back. **The normal range for capillary refill time is less than 3 seconds.**

If it takes a long time for the color to return, **a capillary refill time greater than 3 seconds indicates poor peripheral tissue perfusion** (blood isn't reaching the edges of the body well).

We also feel the pulse in the wrists or feet to feel how strong the blood is flowing.
* **Peripheral pulses are graded on a scale where 0 indicates an absent pulse.** (No heartbeat felt at all).
* **Peripheral pulses are graded on a scale where 2+ indicates a normal and palpable pulse.**
* **Peripheral pulses are graded on a scale where 3+ or 4+ indicates a bounding pulse** (beating super hard!).

### Fluid Overload and Heart Failure
If the right side of the heart is failing, blood gets backed up like a traffic jam. We can see this in the neck veins. **Jugular venous distention is properly assessed with the patient positioned supine at a 30 to 45-degree angle.** (Supine means lying on their back). If the neck veins bulge out at this angle, **the presence of jugular venous distention indicates right-sided heart failure or systemic fluid volume overload** (way too much fluid in the body).

Gravity pulls that extra fluid down, causing swelling called edema. If you poke the swollen area and it leaves a dent, that's pitting edema. 
* **A 1+ pitting edema describes a 2-millimeter skin depression that disappears rapidly.**
* **A 4+ pitting edema describes an 8-millimeter skin depression that persists for a long time.**

If a patient stays in bed all day, gravity pulls fluid to their backside instead of their feet. So, **pitting edema over the sacrum in a bedridden patient indicates systemic fluid volume overload.** (The sacrum is the bone at the very bottom of the spine).

### Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT)
Sometimes a pipe gets a dangerous clog, like a blood clot in the leg. A **deep vein thrombosis is assessed by examining the lower extremities for unilateral swelling, redness, and warmth.** 

Nurses used to bend the patient's foot back to see if it hurt, but we don't do that anymore. **Homan's sign is no longer recommended for deep vein thrombosis assessment due to low diagnostic reliability** (it's not a trustworthy test and might knock the clot loose!).

### Shock and Kidney Perfusion
If the body is losing blood or water, the heart panics and beats way faster to make up for it. Because of this, **tachycardia is often the first physiological sign of clinical deterioration or shock.** (Tachycardia means a fast heartbeat). 

When the pump is weak or fluid is low, the kidneys stop making pee to save water. Therefore, **a sudden decrease in urine output below 30 milliliters per hour indicates potential acute kidney impairment.** It also means the heart isn't pumping enough blood, so **a sudden decrease in urine output below 30 milliliters per hour indicates potential decreased cardiac output.**

## 3. The Bellows: Respiratory Assessments

Oxygen is the fuel that keeps our body's engine running. Without it, the game is over fast. 

### The Earliest Sign of Hypoxia
Long before a person turns blue from lack of oxygen (hypoxia), their brain starts to panic. Because of this, **restlessness or altered mental status is often the earliest behavioral sign of hypoxia.** 

### Respiratory Mechanics
Let's look at how fast someone is breathing.
* **The normal adult resting respiratory rate is 12 to 20 breaths per minute.**
* **Bradypnea is defined as a resting respiratory rate of less than 12 breaths per minute in an adult.** (Too slow!)
* **Tachypnea is defined as a resting respiratory rate of greater than 20 breaths per minute in an adult.** (Too fast!)

If a patient is struggling to breathe, sit them up! **Elevating the head of the bed is a primary immediate intervention for acute respiratory distress.** Sitting up helps the lungs expand easily.

### Auscultating Lung Sounds
When we listen to the lungs with a stethoscope, weird noises tell us exactly what is wrong inside:
* **Crackles auscultated in the lungs indicate the presence of fluid in the alveoli or small airways.** It sounds like blowing bubbles in a glass of milk!
* **Wheezing auscultated in the lungs indicates narrowing or obstruction of the lower airways.** It sounds like a musical whistle, like air squeezing out of a tight balloon.
* **Stridor is a high-pitched respiratory sound indicating dangerous upper airway obstruction.** This means the main windpipe is closing up, which is a huge emergency!

### Oxygen Saturation & Chronic Hypoxia
We check oxygen levels using a glowing finger clip. **Pulse oximetry measures the percentage of arterial hemoglobin saturated with oxygen** (how full the blood's delivery trucks are). But it only works if blood is flowing to the fingers!

If a person is super cold, their blood vessels shrink up to keep the warm blood in their chest. **Clinical hypothermia causes severe peripheral vasoconstriction** (shrinking of blood vessels in the arms and legs). This shrinking, or **peripheral vasoconstriction can lead to falsely low pulse oximetry readings on the extremities.** The machine will give you a bad score just because their hands are cold!

If someone goes without enough oxygen for many years, their fingers actually change shape. **Clubbing of the fingers is an assessment finding characterized by an increased angle between the nail base and the skin.** Seeing this **finger clubbing indicates the presence of chronic systemic tissue hypoxia** (a long-term lack of oxygen in the body).

### Preventing Pulmonary Collapse
After surgery, it hurts to take deep breaths, so people take tiny, shallow breaths. But **inadequate lung expansion significantly increases the risk of developing postoperative atelectasis** (which is when the tiny air sacs in the lungs collapse like deflated balloons). To fix this, we use a special plastic breathing toy. With this tool, **incentive spirometry volume measurements evaluate a patient's lung expansion capacity**, making sure they breathe deep enough to pop those balloons open!

## 4. The Armor and the Engine: Skin, Wounds, and Nutrition

Our skin is like the armor on a video game character. To fix a cut in the armor, the body needs good blood flow and building materials. 

### Risk Assessments for Pressure Injuries
If someone lays in bed too long, their skin can break down and cause pressure sores. We use a checklist called **The Braden Scale** to see who is in danger. **A lower numerical score on the Braden Scale indicates a higher risk of developing pressure injuries.** The scale gives points by checking six things:
1. **The Braden Scale assesses sensory perception for pressure injury risk** (can they feel if they are uncomfortable?).
2. **The Braden Scale assesses skin moisture for pressure injury risk** (is the skin wet from sweat?).
3. **The Braden Scale assesses patient activity for pressure injury risk** (are they getting out of bed?).
4. **The Braden Scale assesses patient mobility for pressure injury risk** (can they roll over?).
5. **The Braden Scale assesses patient nutrition for pressure injury risk** (are they eating enough food to stay strong?).
6. **The Braden Scale assesses friction and shear for pressure injury risk** (is their skin rubbing hard against the bedsheets?).

### Assessing Healing Wounds (REEDA)
When looking at a healing cut or surgery spot, nurses use a memory trick. **The REEDA acronym is used to assess surgical incisions or localized wounds.**
* **The R in the REEDA acronym stands for redness.**
* **The first E in the REEDA acronym stands for edema** (swelling).
* **The second E in the REEDA acronym stands for ecchymosis** (bruising).
* **The D in the REEDA acronym stands for discharge or drainage** (is anything leaking out?).
* **The A in the REEDA acronym stands for the approximation of wound edges** (are the edges neatly glued together?).

### The Building Blocks: Albumin
To fix a wound, the body needs protein, kind of like how a builder needs bricks to fix a wall. **Serum albumin levels are used to assess long-term nutritional status related to wound healing.** 
* **The normal range for serum albumin is 3.5 to 5.0 grams per deciliter.**
* If a patient doesn't eat well, their protein drops. **A serum albumin level below 3.5 grams per deciliter increases the risk of delayed wound healing.** No bricks means the wall can't be fixed!

### The Great Saboteur: Diabetes Mellitus
Too much sugar in the blood is really bad for tiny blood vessels. **Diabetes mellitus impairs peripheral blood flow**, which means blood gets stuck. This makes it a huge problem, so **diabetes mellitus is a major systemic risk factor for delayed wound healing.**

To see if a patient's sugar has been too high over time, we check a special blood test. **Hemoglobin A1c blood tests measure the average blood glucose level over the past two to three months.** 
* **Elevated Hemoglobin A1c levels indicate poorly controlled diabetes mellitus.**
* Having this **poorly controlled diabetes mellitus increases the risk of microvascular complications and delayed tissue healing** (meaning small blood vessels get ruined and cuts won't heal).

### Pigmentation Considerations
We have to check skin carefully depending on the patient's skin tone. 
* If we are looking for a blue color from low oxygen, **cyanosis in dark-skinned individuals is best assessed by examining the oral mucosa or conjunctiva** (the inside of the mouth or the inside of the eyelids).
* If we are looking for a yellow color from a sick liver, **jaundice in dark-skinned individuals is best assessed by examining the sclera or the hard palate** (the white part of the eyes or the roof of the mouth).

## 5. Navigation, Gravity, and Senses: Fall Risks

If the nervous system, muscles, or senses glitch out, the patient might fall. Falls are super dangerous! We use a math test to see if someone might fall. **A higher numerical score on the Morse Fall Scale indicates a higher risk for falls.** This scale assigns risk points based on six things:
1. **The Morse Fall Scale assigns risk points for a patient's history of falling.** (Have they fallen before?)
2. **The Morse Fall Scale assigns risk points for the presence of secondary medical diagnoses.** (Do they have more than one sickness?)
3. **The Morse Fall Scale assigns risk points for the use of ambulatory aids like crutches or walkers.**
4. **The Morse Fall Scale assigns risk points for the presence of an intravenous line or saline lock.** (An IV pole is easy to trip over!)
5. **The Morse Fall Scale assigns risk points for impaired patient gait.** (Are they wobbly when they walk?)
6. **The Morse Fall Scale assigns risk points for impaired or overestimated mental status.** (Do they *think* they can walk, but actually can't?)

### Assessing Senses and Muscle Strength
We have to check how the patient senses the world around them!
* If you can't see a toy on the floor, you will trip on it. **Visual impairment significantly increases a patient's risk for accidental falls.** To check their eyesight from far away, **the Snellen chart evaluates distance visual acuity** (that's the chart with the big E at the top).
* For hearing, **the whisper test screens for high-frequency hearing loss in clinical settings.**
* If you can't feel your feet, you might step on something sharp and not know it! **Decreased tactile sensation increases the risk of unrecognized thermal or mechanical injuries** (like burns or cuts). To test this, nurses poke the foot gently with a little plastic wire. **Monofilament testing evaluates the loss of protective tactile sensation in the feet.**

Next, we test their muscles. **Muscle strength is clinically graded on a scale ranging from 0 to 5:**
* **A muscle strength grade of 5 indicates normal strength against full applied resistance.** (They are super strong!)
* **A muscle strength grade of 0 indicates no visible or palpable muscle contraction.** (The muscle isn't moving at all).

### The Postural Threat: Orthostatic Hypotension
Have you ever stood up too fast and felt dizzy? That happens because gravity pulls your blood down, and your body has to work hard to pump it back to your brain. 
* To test if the body is failing to do this, we do a special check. **Assessing for orthostatic hypotension involves measuring blood pressure and heart rate in supine, sitting, and standing positions.**
* Let's do the math to see if they fail the test:
  1. Measure the top blood pressure number while they are lying down (example: 120).
  2. Measure the top blood pressure number while they are standing up (example: 100).
  3. Subtract the standing number from the lying down number: 120 - 100 = 20.
* Because the number dropped by 20, they have the condition! **A drop in systolic blood pressure of at least 20 mmHg upon standing defines orthostatic hypotension.**
* Because it makes the patient dizzy, **orthostatic hypotension significantly increases a patient's fall risk upon ambulation** (ambulation just means walking around).

## 6. The Intake and Output: Gastrointestinal Assessments

Finally, we check the stomach and intestines. 

### Swallowing and Aspiration
Can the patient swallow their food properly, or will it accidentally go down into their lungs? 
* **Dysphagia is the medical term for difficulty swallowing.**
* It is very dangerous because **dysphagia significantly increases a patient's risk of dangerous pulmonary aspiration** (which means food or water gets down into the lungs where it doesn't belong). 
* How do we check this? **Aspiration risk is clinically assessed by evaluating the patient's gag reflex and ability to swallow water safely.**

### Auscultating the Abdomen
The intestines are like a long, squishy tube that squeezes your food. 
* When you listen with a stethoscope, you should hear gurgles. **Normal bowel sounds occur at a rate of 5 to 30 times per minute.**
* If you don't hear anything, the tube might be stuck. But you have to be totally sure before you say it is completely quiet! **Absent bowel sounds must be confirmed by listening continuously for five minutes in each abdominal quadrant.** (The belly is split into four squares, or quadrants, so you have to listen to each one for a long time!).

### Final Thoughts
When you check on a patient, you are like a really smart detective. You notice all the little clues. You know that if their blood sugar is too high, their cuts won't heal. You know that if their oxygen is getting low, they might act restless before the glowing finger clip even shows a problem. If you learn how all these body systems work together, you won't just pass your nursing test—you will be an awesome nurse who saves lives!
