Slide into the booth, grab a coaster, and listen to me, because we need to talk about the most dramatic reality TV show in the universe: the human brain. When the brain gets hijacked by a substance, it’s not a lack of willpower; it’s a hostile corporate takeover. The original directors are fired, and the substance is now sitting in the big leather chair. But before we even knock on the door of this chaotic production, you and I, as nurses, have to do something critical. Nurses must assess their own personal attitudes and biases regarding substance use disorders before providing client care. If you walk into that room radiating judgment, you’ve already lost. Why? Because a nonjudgmental, empathetic nursing approach directly encourages honest client disclosure about their complete substance use history. If they think you're judging them, they will lie to you like a reality star at a reunion special, and in this field, those lies can be fatal. 

So, what is the actual drama? Substance use disorder involves a problematic pattern of substance use leading to clinically significant impairment or distress. It’s the ultimate toxic relationship. And just like any toxic relationship, eventually, the grand romantic gestures stop working. Tolerance occurs when a client requires increasingly larger doses of a substance to achieve the desired effect. You need a bigger bouquet of red flags just to feel a spark. But if that toxic partner suddenly packs their bags and leaves? Total system meltdown. Substance withdrawal involves physiological and psychological symptoms occurring when a client abruptly stops or reduces prolonged substance use. The house is quite literally screaming. To survive this cognitive dissonance, the brain hires a PR team. Denial is a primary defense mechanism used by clients to minimize or ignore the severity of a substance use disorder. They’ll sit in a burning kitchen and tell you they just like it warm.

Now, here’s a wild plot twist: the intruder doesn't even have to be a chemical. Non-substance dependencies activate the exact same brain reward systems as chemical substance addictions. The brain doesn’t care if it’s a bottle of vodka or a slot machine; the dopamine fireworks are identical. That’s why gambling disorder is classified by the DSM-5 as a non-substance-related addictive disorder. The casino is the drug, and treatment for gambling disorder primarily involves cognitive behavioral therapy and participation in Gamblers Anonymous to rewrite the brain's broken script. We’re also seeing this with screens. Internet gaming disorder involves excessive and prolonged internet gaming that results in significant daily functioning impairment. When raiding a virtual dungeon costs you your real-life job and hygiene, the screen has become the needle. 

But let’s talk about the heavyweight champion of toxic relationships: Alcohol. Alcohol is the mob boss of the central nervous system. To figure out if a client is secretly working for the mob, the CAGE questionnaire assesses clients for potential alcohol use disorder using four specific screening questions: have you tried to Cut down, get Annoyed by criticism, feel Guilty, or need an Eye-opener? If we need hard proof of what's happening right now, blood alcohol concentration levels accurately measure the exact amount of alcohol actively present in a client's bloodstream. But if we want to look at the footprints left in the mud over the last few days, urine toxicology screening provides objective clinical data regarding the recent use of various illicit substances. 

When the alcohol mob boss abruptly leaves town, the underlings go absolutely feral. Alcohol withdrawal symptoms typically begin between four and twelve hours after the last drink. To track the severity of this riot, the Clinical Institute Withdrawal Assessment for Alcohol, Revised scale measures the severity of alcohol withdrawal symptoms. But days two and three are when the city catches fire. Delirium tremens is a severe, life-threatening manifestation of alcohol withdrawal, and delirium tremens typically occurs forty-eight to seventy-two hours after alcohol cessation. It’s a full-scale purge. Symptoms of delirium tremens include severe agitation, visual hallucinations, tachycardia, and severe hypertension. Their heart is racing, their blood pressure is blowing the roof off, and they are seeing imaginary hitmen. 

Because their brain is electrically overheating, seizure precautions are a mandatory safety measure for clients experiencing severe alcohol withdrawal. You have to earthquake-proof the room! Seizure precautions include padding the bed rails, keeping the bed in the lowest position, and maintaining airway equipment at the bedside. To quell the riot, you send in the SWAT team. Benzodiazepines are the primary pharmacological treatment for managing alcohol withdrawal symptoms. Lorazepam is a commonly prescribed, fast-acting benzodiazepine used to prevent seizures during acute alcohol withdrawal. Meanwhile, chlordiazepoxide is an extended half-life benzodiazepine used to manage alcohol withdrawal over a smoother, longer taper. 

But the mob boss didn't just cause a riot; he starved the city. Alcoholics are chronically deficient in Vitamin B1, so intravenous thiamine is administered to clients with alcohol use disorder to prevent Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome. Without it, the brain physically deteriorates. Wernicke encephalopathy is the acute phase, and it presents with a clinical triad of confusion, ataxia, and abnormal eye movements. They are stumbling around with dizzy eyes, totally bewildered. If you don't fix it, it becomes Korsakoff syndrome, which is a chronic complication of thiamine deficiency characterized by severe short-term memory loss and confabulation. And what is confabulation? It is the unconscious filling of memory gaps with fabricated information to conceal memory loss. They aren't lying to you; their broken hard drive is just auto-generating fake lore to cover up corrupted files.

Once the client kicks the mob boss out, we can set booby traps to keep him out. Disulfiram is an abstinence maintenance medication that causes severe adverse reactions when combined with alcohol. If they cheat, the trap snaps: consuming alcohol while taking disulfiram causes flushing, throbbing headache, tachycardia, and severe nausea. Because this trap is so sensitive, clients taking disulfiram must avoid all products containing hidden alcohol. I mean zero loopholes. Hidden sources of alcohol include mouthwash, liquid cough syrups, aftershave, and vanilla extract. One splash of aftershave and they’ll be puking with a pounding headache. We can also use naltrexone, which is an opioid antagonist that effectively reduces alcohol cravings in clients with alcohol use disorder by duct-taping over the pleasure button, or acamprosate, which is a medication used to maintain abstinence in clients with alcohol use disorder by decreasing emotional distress. 

Now, if alcohol is a loud mob boss, opioids are a heavy, suffocating blanket. An opioid overdose manifests with a classic triad of respiratory depression, pinpoint pupils, and a decreased level of consciousness. They are knocked out, their eyes look like tiny dots, and they aren't breathing. You hit the eject button! Naloxone is the specific opioid antagonist used to reverse opioid-induced respiratory depression. But here is the terrifying catch: naloxone has a significantly shorter half-life than most opioids. The eject button wears off fast. Because of this, clients receiving naloxone require continuous monitoring for recurrent respiratory depression as the antidote wears off. The intruder is still in the house, just temporarily stunned; if you stop watching, the client will stop breathing again. 

When they withdraw from opioids, they won't die, but they will wish they were dead. The Clinical Opiate Withdrawal Scale quantifies the severity of opioid withdrawal symptoms to guide treatment interventions. It’s like the flu from hell: opioid withdrawal symptoms include rhinorrhea, yawning, piloerection, diarrhea, and severe muscle aches. Goosebumps, explosive bowels, runny nose—pure misery. To manage this safely, methadone is a long-acting synthetic opioid used for opioid substitution therapy and safe withdrawal management. It's the highly regulated, boring replacement roommate. Buprenorphine is a partial opioid agonist used to decrease opioid cravings and safely manage withdrawal symptoms. And to turn down the internal thermostat, clonidine is an alpha-2 adrenergic agonist used to relieve autonomic hyperactivity and lower blood pressure during opioid withdrawal.

What about the uppers? Stimulants like cocaine floor the gas pedal until the engine melts. Stimulant intoxication causes tachycardia, hypertension, pupillary dilation, and dangerous hyperthermia. You are roasting alive with saucer eyes. It gets worse: cocaine intoxication places clients at an extremely high risk for life-threatening coronary artery vasospasm. The heart tubes cramp shut, causing heart attacks in twenty-year-olds! Severe stimulant toxicity often leads to cardiac arrhythmias, myocardial infarction, and seizures. But what goes up, must crash. Stimulant withdrawal produces profound depression, fatigue, increased appetite, and vivid nightmares. They sleep for days, eat everything, and dream of demons because their dopamine bank account is massively overdrawn. 

On the flip side, benzodiazepines are the brain's brakes. If someone overdoses on them, flumazenil is the specific competitive antagonist used to reverse severe benzodiazepine toxicity. But you cannot just rip the brakes out of a moving car! Abrupt discontinuation of benzodiazepines precipitates life-threatening withdrawal seizures. The brain spirals out of control. Therefore, safe benzodiazepine withdrawal requires a gradual, medically supervised tapering schedule. 

Let's do a lightning round of the wildcards. Cannabis intoxication is characterized by conjunctival injection, increased appetite, dry mouth, and tachycardia. Red eyes, munchies, cottonmouth. Hallucinogens? Total sensory anarchy. Hallucinogen intoxication causes synesthesia, panic attacks, paranoia, and highly unpredictable behavior. Seeing sounds and tasting colors. Because their reality is shattering, nursing care for hallucinogen toxicity involves placing the client in a quiet, low-stimulus environment to prevent severe agitation. Keep it dark and calm so their brain doesn't explode. Then there are the inhalants—huffing paint or glue. Inhalant toxicity poses a high risk for sudden cardiac death due to ventricular fibrillation. The heart literally turns into a vibrating bag of worms. And for the chain-smokers? Varenicline is a prescription medication utilized to significantly reduce nicotine cravings and manage nicotine withdrawal symptoms. But watch out! Varenicline therapy carries a documented risk for severe neuropsychiatric adverse effects including new-onset suicidal ideation. The anti-smoking pill might make them want to end it all—you have to monitor their mood!

Finally, how do we clean up the wreckage of the reality show? We use motivational interviewing techniques to help clients resolve ambivalence about changing highly ingrained substance use behaviors. We Jedi-mind-trick them into finding their own reasons to quit. Often, there’s a massive leak in the roof while the kitchen is on fire. A dual diagnosis indicates the co-occurrence of a substance use disorder and a psychiatric mental health disorder. You cannot fix one and ignore the other. Effective treatment of a dual diagnosis requires addressing both the psychiatric disorder and the substance use disorder simultaneously. 

If they trip and fall on the stairs, we don't throw them back into the basement. Relapse is viewed as a common, expected part of the recovery process rather than a complete failure of treatment. To prevent the next fall, identifying personal triggers for substance use is a crucial, foundational component of relapse prevention planning. You have to know exactly which text message or song will make you call the toxic ex. 

Sometimes, we can't force them into rehab today, but we can keep them alive. Harm reduction strategies focus on minimizing the negative health consequences associated with drug use without demanding immediate abstinence. We know that chronic intravenous drug use significantly increases the risk for transmitting bloodborne pathogens like HIV and Hepatitis C. So, providing clean syringe exchange programs is an evidence-based harm reduction strategy for individuals engaging in intravenous drug use. We hand out clean needles to keep them HIV-free until they are ready to quit. 

When they are ready, they need an army. Alcoholics Anonymous is a widely utilized 12-step self-help group for individuals seeking recovery from alcohol use disorder, while Narcotics Anonymous is a support group based on the 12-step model specifically tailored for individuals recovering from drug addiction. The absolute foundational principle of 12-step programs is acknowledging personal powerlessness over the addiction. Step one: admit the mob boss won. Furthermore, 12-step recovery programs advocate for complete lifelong abstinence from the addictive substance. Zero contact, forever. And because the blast radius hits the whole family, Al-Anon is a dedicated support group designed specifically for family members and friends of individuals with alcohol use disorder. Families need this to unlearn codependency, which involves dysfunctional helping behaviors that unintentionally enable another person's continued substance use. Paying the hitman's parking tickets isn't love; it's just keeping the mob in business. 

So drink your water, wipe down the bar, and remember: addiction is neurobiology, withdrawal is a battlefield, and recovery is rebuilding a city from the ashes. Know your meds, protect their airway, and keep your empathy dialed to the max.
