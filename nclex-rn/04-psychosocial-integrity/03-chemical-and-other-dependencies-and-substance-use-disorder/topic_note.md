Welcome! Let’s talk about the [brain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brain). More specifically, let’s talk about what happens when the most powerful, beautifully complex computer in the universe—the [human brain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_brain)—gets its fundamental [reward circuitry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reward_system) hijacked. 

When we talk about [substance use](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Substance_use) and dependency, we are not talking about a lack of willpower. We are talking about [neurobiology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neuroscience). If you want to be an extraordinary [nurse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nursing), you have to understand the mechanisms of [addiction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Addiction), the lethal dangers of [withdrawal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drug_withdrawal), and the profound [psychology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Psychology) of recovery.

Let's dive right in.

## 1. The Foundation: Looking in the Mirror First

Before you ever walk into a client's room, there is a mandatory first step: **[nurses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nursing) must assess their own personal attitudes and biases regarding [substance use disorders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Substance_use_disorder) before providing client care.** Why? Because judgment shuts down communication. A **nonjudgmental, empathetic nursing approach directly encourages honest client disclosure about their complete substance use history.** If the client senses you are judging them, they will lie to you—and in this field of [medicine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medicine), those lies can be fatal.

### The Mechanics of the Hijacked Brain
> **[Substance Use Disorder (SUD)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Substance_use_disorder)** involves a problematic pattern of substance use leading to clinically significant impairment or distress.

When a client uses a substance repeatedly, their brain adapts. This leads to **[tolerance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drug_tolerance)**, which occurs when a client requires increasingly larger doses of a substance to achieve the desired effect. The brain’s [receptors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Receptor_%28biochemistry%29) [down-regulate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Downregulation_and_upregulation). 

![The brain's dopaminergic reward structures, including the ventral tegmental area and nucleus accumbens, are fundamentally altered and hijacked during the development of substance use disorders.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Overview_of_reward_structures_in_the_human_brain.jpg)

If you take that substance away, the brain is left deeply unbalanced. This is **[substance withdrawal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drug_withdrawal)**—the [physiological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physiology) and psychological symptoms occurring when a client abruptly stops or reduces prolonged substance use.

Because the brain's survival wiring is now tied to the substance, the [ego](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Id%2C_ego_and_superego) protects the behavior. **[Denial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Denial)** is a primary [defense mechanism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Defence_mechanism) used by clients to minimize or ignore the severity of a substance use disorder. 

### The Illusion of "Chemicals Only"
You might think addiction requires a powder, a pill, or a bottle. It doesn't. **Non-substance dependencies activate the exact same brain reward systems as chemical substance addictions.** 
*   **[Gambling disorder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Problem_gambling)** is officially classified by the [DSM-5](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DSM-5) as a non-substance-related addictive disorder. Treatment primarily involves [cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cognitive_behavioral_therapy) and participation in [Gamblers Anonymous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamblers_Anonymous).
*   **[Internet gaming disorder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Video_game_addiction)** involves excessive and prolonged internet gaming that results in significant daily functioning impairment. The screen becomes the drug.

## 2. Alcohol Use Disorder: The Heavyweight

[Alcohol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alcohol_%28drug%29) is legal, it’s everywhere, and its withdrawal syndrome is one of the few that can outright kill you. 

### Screening and Diagnostics
To screen clients for potential [alcohol use disorder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alcohol_use_disorder), we use the **[CAGE questionnaire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CAGE_questionnaire)**, which relies on four specific screening questions (Cut down, Annoyed, Guilty, Eye-opener). 

When we need objective data about what is in the client's system:
*   **[Blood alcohol concentration (BAC)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_alcohol_content)** levels accurately measure the exact amount of alcohol actively present in a client's bloodstream right now.
*   **[Urine toxicology screening](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drug_test)** provides objective clinical data regarding the recent use of various [illicit substances](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Illegal_drug_trade), acting as a historical snapshot of the past few days.

![Symptoms associated with varying blood alcohol concentration (BAC) levels, providing objective clinical data regarding intoxication severity.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Symptoms_of_BAC%2C_0.02%25_to_0.50%25_concentration.svg)

### The Lethal Timeline of Alcohol Withdrawal
Alcohol acts as a heavy chemical blanket on the [central nervous system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_nervous_system). The brain compensates by turning up its excitatory pathways. When a client suddenly stops drinking, that heavy blanket is ripped away, but the brain is still running in overdrive.

*   **4 to 12 Hours:** **[Alcohol withdrawal symptoms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alcohol_withdrawal_syndrome) typically begin between four and twelve hours after the last drink.** 
*   **48 to 72 Hours:** This is the danger zone. **[Delirium tremens (DTs)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Delirium_tremens)** typically occurs forty-eight to seventy-two hours after alcohol cessation. **Delirium tremens is a severe, life-threatening manifestation of alcohol withdrawal.** 
*   **The Symptoms of DTs:** Severe agitation, visual [hallucinations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hallucination), [tachycardia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tachycardia), and severe [hypertension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypertension). The brain is electrically overheating.

![An 1800s medical illustration depicting a patient experiencing the severe hallucinations and acute confusion characteristic of delirium tremens, a life-threatening complication of alcohol withdrawal.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/An_alcoholic_man_with_delirium_Wellcome_L0060780_(level_correction).jpg)

To quantify and track this chaos, we use the **[Clinical Institute Withdrawal Assessment for Alcohol, Revised (CIWA-Ar)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clinical_Institute_Withdrawal_Assessment_for_Alcohol)** scale, which measures the severity of alcohol withdrawal symptoms and tells us exactly how much medication to give.

### Treating the Alcohol Withdrawal Storm
Because the brain is hyper-excitable, **[seizure precautions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seizure) are a mandatory safety measure for clients experiencing severe alcohol withdrawal.** What does this mean practically? **Seizure precautions include padding the bed rails, keeping the bed in the lowest position, and maintaining airway equipment at the bedside.**

To chemically calm the brain down, **[benzodiazepines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzodiazepine) are the primary pharmacological treatment for managing alcohol withdrawal symptoms.**
*   **[Lorazepam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorazepam)** is a commonly prescribed, fast-acting benzodiazepine used to quickly prevent seizures during acute alcohol withdrawal.
*   **[Chlordiazepoxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chlordiazepoxide)** is an extended half-life benzodiazepine used to smoothly manage and taper alcohol withdrawal over a longer period.

### Brain Damage: The Thiamine Crisis
[Alcoholics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alcoholism) are frequently malnourished, specifically lacking [Vitamin B1 (Thiamine)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thiamine). Without it, the brain physically deteriorates.
*   We immediately administer **[intravenous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intravenous_therapy) thiamine** to clients with alcohol use disorder to prevent **[Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wernicke%E2%80%93Korsakoff_syndrome)**.
*   **[Wernicke encephalopathy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wernicke_encephalopathy)** is the acute phase, presenting with a classic clinical triad: **confusion, [ataxia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ataxia) (unsteady gait), and abnormal eye movements ([nystagmus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nystagmus)).**
*   **[Korsakoff syndrome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korsakoff_syndrome)** is the chronic, largely irreversible complication of thiamine deficiency characterized by severe [short-term memory loss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Short-term_memory) and [confabulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confabulation). 
*   What is **confabulation**? It is the unconscious filling of memory gaps with fabricated information to conceal memory loss. They aren't lying to you maliciously; their broken brain is desperately trying to patch the missing data files.

![Horizontal nystagmus, characterized by rapid, involuntary eye movements, is a hallmark clinical sign of the acute phase of Wernicke encephalopathy caused by severe thiamine deficiency.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Horizontal-nystagmus.gif)

### Maintaining Abstinence: The Medications
Once the client is sober, we use [pharmacology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pharmacology) to help keep them that way:
1.  **[Disulfiram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disulfiram):** This is an abstinence maintenance medication that causes severe adverse reactions when combined with alcohol. It operates on pure punishment. **Consuming alcohol while taking disulfiram causes [flushing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flushing_%28physiology%29), throbbing headache, tachycardia, and severe [nausea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nausea).** Because of this, **clients taking disulfiram must avoid all products containing hidden alcohol,** including [mouthwash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mouthwash), liquid [cough syrups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cough_medicine), [aftershave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aftershave), and [vanilla extract](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vanilla_extract).
2.  **[Naltrexone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naltrexone):** This is an [opioid antagonist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Opioid_antagonist) that effectively reduces alcohol cravings in clients with alcohol use disorder by blocking the pleasure pathways.
3.  **[Acamprosate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acamprosate):** A medication used to maintain abstinence by decreasing the emotional distress and baseline anxiety of living without alcohol.

## 3. The Opioid Crisis: Chasing the Dragon

[Opioids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Opioid) bind to the brain's [mu-receptors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%CE%BC-opioid_receptor), wiping out pain and causing massive [euphoria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euphoria). But they also tell the [brain stem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brainstem) to stop breathing.

### Opioid Overdose
An **[opioid overdose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Opioid_overdose) manifests with a classic triad: [respiratory depression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypoventilation), [pinpoint pupils](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Miosis), and a decreased [level of consciousness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Altered_level_of_consciousness).** 

![Pinpoint pupils (miosis) are a classic, defining clinical sign of opioid intoxication and potential overdose.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Myosis_due_to_opiate_use.jpg)

The absolute priority is [oxygenation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen_saturation_%28medicine%29). **[Naloxone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naloxone) is the specific opioid antagonist used to reverse opioid-induced respiratory depression.** It kicks the opioid off the receptor instantly. But here is the critical catch: **Naloxone has a significantly shorter [half-life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biological_half-life) than most opioids.** Therefore, **clients receiving naloxone require continuous monitoring for recurrent respiratory depression as the [antidote](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antidote) wears off.** If the Naloxone wears off before the [Heroin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heroin) or [Fentanyl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fentanyl) does, the client will stop breathing again.

![An emergency naloxone kit. Naloxone rapidly reverses opioid-induced respiratory depression, though its short half-life requires clients to be monitored closely for recurrent symptoms as the antagonist wears off.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/NaloxoneKit.jpg)

### Opioid Withdrawal
While alcohol withdrawal can kill you, [opioid withdrawal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Opioid_withdrawal) just makes you *wish* you were dead. It is intensely uncomfortable but rarely fatal.
*   We use the **[Clinical Opiate Withdrawal Scale (COWS)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clinical_Opiate_Withdrawal_Scale)** to quantify the severity of opioid withdrawal symptoms to guide treatment interventions.
*   **Opioid withdrawal symptoms include [rhinorrhea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhinorrhea) (runny nose), [yawning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yawn), [piloerection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goose_bumps) (goosebumps—hence the term "[quitting cold turkey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_turkey)"), [diarrhea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diarrhea), and severe [muscle aches](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myalgia).**

![Piloerection, commonly known as goosebumps, is a pronounced autonomic symptom experienced during acute opioid withdrawal, which gave rise to the phrase "quitting cold turkey."](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Chaire_de_poule.jpg)

### Opioid Maintenance and Management Medications
*   **[Methadone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methadone):** A long-acting synthetic opioid used for [opioid substitution therapy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Opioid_replacement_therapy) and safe withdrawal management. It prevents withdrawal without providing the massive "high."
*   **[Buprenorphine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buprenorphine):** A [partial opioid agonist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_agonist) used to decrease opioid cravings and safely manage withdrawal symptoms. 
*   **[Clonidine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clonidine):** This isn't an opioid at all; it's an [alpha-2 adrenergic agonist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha-2_adrenergic_receptor) used to relieve autonomic hyperactivity and lower [blood pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_pressure) during opioid withdrawal.

## 4. Uppers and Downers: Stimulants and Sedatives

### Stimulants (Cocaine, Amphetamines)
[Stimulants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stimulant) floor the brain's gas pedal. 
*   **Stimulant intoxication causes tachycardia, [hypertension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypertension), [pupillary dilation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mydriasis), and dangerous [hyperthermia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperthermia).** 
*   **Severe stimulant toxicity often leads to [cardiac arrhythmias](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arrhythmia), [myocardial infarction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myocardial_infarction), and seizures.** 
*   A highly testable danger: **[Cocaine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cocaine) intoxication places clients at an extremely high risk for life-threatening [coronary artery vasospasm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coronary_artery_spasm)**, causing "cocaine-induced heart attacks" even in healthy young people.

What goes up must come down. **Stimulant withdrawal produces profound [depression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Major_depressive_disorder), fatigue, increased appetite, and vivid nightmares.** Their [dopamine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dopamine) stores are entirely depleted.

### Benzodiazepines (The Brakes)
Benzodiazepines (like [Diazepam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diazepam), [Alprazolam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alprazolam)) enhance [GABA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamma-Aminobutyric_acid), slowing the brain down.
*   **Abrupt discontinuation of benzodiazepines precipitates life-threatening withdrawal seizures.** The brain, suddenly un-braked, spirals out of control. 
*   Therefore, **safe benzodiazepine withdrawal requires a gradual, medically supervised tapering schedule.**
*   If a client overdoses on benzos, **[Flumazenil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flumazenil) is the specific [competitive antagonist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Competitive_antagonist) used to reverse severe benzodiazepine toxicity.** 

![Diagram of the GABA-A receptor complex, illustrating the specific allosteric binding site where benzodiazepines act to enhance inhibitory neurotransmission and slow the central nervous system.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/GABAA-receptor-protein-example-en.svg)

## 5. The Psychotropics and Others: Cannabis, Hallucinogens, Inhalants, & Nicotine

*   **[Cannabis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cannabis_%28drug%29):** Intoxication is characterized by **[conjunctival injection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_eye_%28medicine%29) (bloodshot eyes), increased appetite, [dry mouth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xerostomia), and tachycardia.** 
*   **[Hallucinogens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hallucinogen) ([LSD](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LSD), [PCP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phencyclidine)):** Intoxication causes **[synesthesia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synesthesia) (seeing sounds or hearing colors), [panic attacks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Panic_attack), [paranoia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paranoia), and highly unpredictable behavior.** Because their sensory processing is wildly distorted, **nursing care for hallucinogen toxicity involves placing the client in a quiet, low-stimulus environment to prevent severe agitation.**

![A visual representation of synesthesia, a sensory distortion often induced by hallucinogens where stimulation of one sensory pathway leads to involuntary experiences in a second sensory pathway.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Synesthesia.svg)

*   **[Inhalants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inhalant) (Huffing paint, glue):** These are uniquely deadly. **Inhalant toxicity poses a high risk for [sudden cardiac death](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sudden_cardiac_death) due to [ventricular fibrillation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ventricular_fibrillation).** The [solvents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solvent) highly sensitize the [myocardium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardiac_muscle) to [epinephrine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adrenaline).
*   **[Nicotine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicotine):** To help clients quit smoking, **[Varenicline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Varenicline) is a prescription medication utilized to significantly reduce nicotine cravings and manage nicotine withdrawal symptoms.** However, you must warn the client: **Varenicline therapy carries a documented risk for severe neuropsychiatric adverse effects including new-onset [suicidal ideation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suicidal_ideation).**

## 6. The Long Road Home: Treatment, Psychology, and Support

[Detox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drug_detoxification) is just the biological beginning. Recovery is a massive psychological undertaking.

### The Psychology of Change
Clients are often deeply conflicted about quitting. Nurses use **[motivational interviewing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Motivational_interviewing) techniques to help clients resolve [ambivalence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ambivalence) about changing highly ingrained substance use behaviors.** We don't tell them what to do; we guide them to find their own internal reasons for change.

Often, addiction does not occur in a vacuum. **A [dual diagnosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_diagnosis) indicates the co-occurrence of a substance use disorder and a [psychiatric mental health disorder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mental_disorder)** (e.g., Alcohol Use Disorder + [Major Depressive Disorder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Major_depressive_disorder)). Here is the golden rule: **effective treatment of a dual diagnosis requires addressing both the psychiatric disorder and the substance use disorder simultaneously.** You cannot fix one while ignoring the other.

When a client slips up, how do we frame it? **[Relapse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relapse) is viewed as a common, expected part of the recovery process rather than a complete failure of treatment.** To prevent the next slip, **identifying personal triggers for substance use is a crucial, foundational component of relapse prevention planning.**

### Harm Reduction
We cannot always force immediate sobriety, but we can save lives today. **[Harm reduction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harm_reduction) strategies focus on minimizing the negative health consequences associated with drug use without demanding immediate abstinence.** 
For example, **chronic [intravenous drug use](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drug_injection) significantly increases the risk for transmitting [bloodborne pathogens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood-borne_disease) like [HIV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HIV) and [Hepatitis C](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hepatitis_C).** Therefore, **providing clean [syringe exchange programs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Needle_and_syringe_programmes) is an evidence-based harm reduction strategy for individuals engaging in intravenous drug use.** 

![A sterile injection kit obtained from a needle exchange program. Providing clean equipment is an evidence-based harm reduction strategy that significantly lowers the transmission rates of bloodborne pathogens.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Who_needs_a_virtual_world%3F.jpg)

### The 12-Step Philosophy
Community support is often the anchor of long-term recovery.
*   **[Alcoholics Anonymous (AA)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alcoholics_Anonymous)** is a widely utilized [12-step](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Twelve-step_program) self-help group for individuals seeking recovery from alcohol use disorder.
*   **[Narcotics Anonymous (NA)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Narcotics_Anonymous)** is a support group based on the 12-step model specifically tailored for individuals recovering from drug addiction.
*   The **foundational principle of 12-step programs is acknowledging personal powerlessness over the addiction.**
*   Furthermore, **12-step recovery programs advocate for complete lifelong abstinence from the addictive substance.** There is no "casual use" in this model.

![A sobriety token, or "chip," given to members of Alcoholics Anonymous to mark specific lengths of continuous abstinence, reflecting the 12-step program's core commitment to lifelong recovery.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/AA_-_Medalj.jpg)

Finally, addiction is a family disease. **[Al-Anon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Anon/Alateen) is a dedicated support group designed specifically for family members and friends of individuals with alcohol use disorder.** Often, families need help recognizing **[codependency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Codependency)**, which involves **dysfunctional helping behaviors that unintentionally enable another person's continued substance use.** 

***

**Professor's Closing Note:** Remember, behind every grim [toxicology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toxicology) screen, behind every elevated CIWA score, and behind every defensive denial is a human being whose brain chemistry has been fundamentally altered. Treat the biology, secure the safety, but most importantly, maintain your [empathy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empathy). That is the art and science of elite nursing.
