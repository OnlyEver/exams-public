Let’s start with the paperwork, because before a mob boss goes down, he needs a succession plan. That’s what advance directives are—they are legal documents specifying a client's healthcare preferences in the event of incapacitation. It's the ultimate preemptive strike against chaos. Specifically, a living will is the boss's strict orders, outlining specific medical interventions a client accepts or refuses at the end of life. It says, "No ventilators, but keep the cannolis coming." Meanwhile, a durable power of attorney for healthcare designates a surrogate decision-maker for a client's medical care. That’s the Underboss stepping up to the plate. But listen to me, if the Underboss just walks into the hospital and says, "I'm in charge," it means nothing! The nurse must verify that a copy of the advance directive is placed in the client's medical record. If the papers aren't in the chart, medically, they are a ghost. 

Now, how do we treat this failing empire? You have to know the difference between palliative care and hospice care, or you’re going to look like a fool in the kitchen. Palliative care aims to improve the quality of life for clients with serious illnesses, and the beauty of it is that palliative care can be provided concurrently with curative disease treatments. You are fixing the oven while still actively trying to bake the wedding cake. But hospice care? That’s when the health inspector walks in and says the building is condemned. Hospice care is indicated when a client has a physician-certified life expectancy of six months or less. At this point, you stop trying to fix the oven. Hospice care strictly excludes curative disease treatments. You aren't trying to cure anything; hospice care focuses exclusively on symptom management and comfort. You pour the wine, dim the lights, and ride out the fire.

While the patient is shutting down, the family is losing their minds. It's like the finale of a messy reality TV show. Anticipatory grief is the emotional response experienced before an expected loss occurs. The family is literally mourning the breakup while the partner is still packing boxes in the living room! Kubler-Ross identified five stages of grief, and they are the exact blueprint of a reality TV meltdown: the five stages of grief include denial, anger, bargaining, depression, and acceptance. But here is the secret—individuals do not progress through the stages of grief in a linear or predictable order. A husband might find acceptance on Tuesday, only to throw a chair in anger on Wednesday. That’s normal! However, if season three is over and someone is still pretending they are married, you have a problem. Prolonged denial by a family member regarding a client's terminal prognosis requires additional psychosocial assessment. Bring in the producers, bring in psych! 

And what about the person holding this mess together? Caregiver role strain is the physical, emotional, and financial exhaustion resulting from caring for a dependent family member. They are drowning! You have to throw them a life raft, which means offering respite care, an intervention that provides temporary relief to primary caregivers of terminally ill clients. Send them to a hotel! Give them a nap! When you are talking to these exhausted families, open-ended questions encourage families to express feelings regarding a client's impending death. Ask them, "What is going through your mind?" But whatever you do, nurses must avoid using cliches like "Everything happens for a reason" when communicating with grieving families. That is the quickest way to get a drink thrown in your face. Sometimes, the best thing to say is absolutely nothing. Therapeutic presence involves the nurse being physically and emotionally attentive to the grieving family without necessarily speaking. Just stand there and anchor the room. You can also get them involved, because facilitating family participation in physical care can help family members cope with the client's impending death. Let the daughter wipe the mascara off her mother's face. Above all, providing education to the family regarding expected physical changes during the dying process drastically reduces caregiver anxiety. If you tell them the engine is going to smoke before it stalls, they won't scream when they see the smoke.

You also have to know exactly who you are dealing with, culturally and spiritually. You wouldn't serve a steak at a vegan wedding. Spiritual distress in dying clients can manifest as anger toward a higher power. They are screaming at the universe! When this happens, chaplains or spiritual care providers should be offered to clients expressing spiritual distress at the end of life. Bring in the spiritual Consigliere. And respect the traditions! Islamic end-of-life practices often require the client's face to be turned toward Mecca. Orthodox Jewish traditions may require the body of the deceased to remain untouched for a specific period after death, waiting for their holy society to arrive. Catholic end-of-life practices often involve the administration of the Sacrament of the Sick by a priest to clear the slate. And Buddhist traditions often emphasize an unclouded mind at the time of death, which means Buddhist clients may refuse consciousness-altering pain medications near the time of death to maintain mental clarity. They want a clear head when they meet whatever comes next.

Now, let's talk about the physical crash. The restaurant is closing for good, and the systems are shutting down methodically. Families freak out when the patient stops eating, but a dying client may experience anorexia and decreased thirst as natural physiological processes. The kitchen is closed! Stop ordering food! Forcing fluids or artificial nutrition on an actively dying client can increase respiratory secretions and discomfort. You are shoving food into a closed kitchen, creating a massive garbage pile. The dishwashers are quitting, too. Oliguria and anuria are physiological signs of failing kidney function during the dying process. Because the blood volume is dropping, the body hoards it all in the core, meaning extremities often feel cool to the touch as peripheral circulation diminishes. This decreased peripheral circulation is exactly what causes mottling of the skin before death—that terrifying, marbled, purplish pattern creeping up their legs. The structure of the building is giving way, too. You'll see Kennedy terminal ulcers, which are rapid skin breakdowns that occur rapidly as part of the dying process. The hinges are falling off the doors, because decreased muscle tone leads to jaw sagging in an actively dying client. And the floodgates? They pop completely open. Sphincter relaxation near the time of death often results in urinary and fecal incontinence.

Then comes the breathing. The engine starts misfiring. Cheyne-Stokes respirations involve periods of deep breathing alternating with periods of apnea. It revs wildly, then goes dead silent, over and over. Cheyne-Stokes respirations are a physiological sign of impending death. Then you hear it—the death rattle, which refers to noisy breathing caused by the accumulation of respiratory secretions in the airway of a dying client. The pipes are gurgling! How do we fix this? Anticholinergic medications are administered to decrease respiratory secretions in a dying client. Dry those pipes out! Also, elevating the head of the bed can alleviate dyspnea in a dying client. Pop the roof hatch so they can get some air! Our ultimate weapon here is the heavy stuff: opioids are commonly administered to manage both pain and dyspnea at the end of life. Morphine kills the pain and tells the brain to stop panicking for oxygen. 

But what if you give a massive dose of morphine to stop their agony, and their breathing stops completely? Did you just commit a crime? Enter the ultimate ethical loophole: the principle of double effect ethically justifies administering pain medication that may unintentionally hasten death. The catch? The primary intent of medication administration under the principle of double effect must be symptom relief. If your intent was to wipe out the pain, and the collateral damage was the heart stopping a few hours early, you are ethically and legally clear. Keep them comfortable! Routine vital sign measurement is often discontinued during active dying to prevent unnecessary disturbance of the client. Stop auditing a burning building! Keep the bar wiped down—oral care with moistened swabs prevents mucosal dryness and promotes comfort in a dying client. And remember this golden rule: hearing is widely considered the last physical sense to decline before death. The microphone is always hot. Nurses should speak clearly and respectfully to unresponsive dying clients because auditory function often remains intact.

Finally, the end arrives. The legal time of death must be documented in the medical record by an authorized healthcare provider. The health inspector stamps the closed sign. Gravity takes over, pulling the remaining blood down to the floorboards. Livor mortis is a purple or red skin discoloration caused by the pooling of blood in dependent areas of the body. Now, your job shifts to staging the scene. Postmortem care involves preparing the client's body for viewing by the family. But before you touch anything, you have to know if it's a crime scene. Nurses must leave all medical tubes in place if an autopsy is required! Do not touch the evidence! However, nurses must remove all medical tubes during postmortem care if no autopsy is required. Once you are cleared to clean up, you perform standard postmortem care, which includes closing the client's eyes. You also know that standard postmortem care includes positioning the body supine with a pillow under the head. Why the pillow? So the blood doesn't pool in their face and turn it purple right where the family is looking! And finally, the ultimate cosmetic fix: dentures should be placed in the client's mouth during postmortem care to maintain facial shape.
