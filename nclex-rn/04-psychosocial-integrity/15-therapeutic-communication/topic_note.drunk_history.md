Now, sometimes the patient’s neurological Wi-Fi router is completely fried. I’m talking about aphasia, which is a neurological communication disorder affecting the ability to speak or understand language. It is a reality TV nightmare inside their head. Clients with receptive aphasia benefit from the use of simple language during interactions. Even better, you give them the picture book! Alright, time to break out the gadgets. The absolute foundation is active listening, which is a therapeutic technique involving complete focus on the client's verbal and nonverbal messages. You are laser-locked onto defusing the bomb, not thinking about what you’re having for lunch. This builds empathy, and empathy is the ability to understand and share the feelings of another person. It means you climb down into the muddy trench with them. And when you look at them and say, "Man, this trench is incredibly muddy and miserable," you are validating them. Validating a client's feelings demonstrates nursing empathy. 

Let’s look at your openers. Using broad openings is a therapeutic communication technique allowing the client to select the topic of conversation. You literally hand them the TV remote and let them pick the channel! We also use open-ended questions. Open-ended questions encourage the client to verbalize feelings, and they encourage the client to provide detailed verbal responses. We want the full director's cut of their emotional state, not just the movie trailer! 

Then you have your mirrors. Restating is a therapeutic communication technique where the nurse repeats the main idea of the client's message. They throw the ball, you bounce the exact same ball back to prove you caught it. Paraphrasing is a therapeutic technique involving restating the client's message in the nurse's own words. You take their raw ingredients and bake it into a tiny summary cupcake! Reflecting is a therapeutic technique directing client questions and feelings back to the client. You hold up a mirror! They ask, "Should I take this pill?" You reflect: "What do *you* think about taking it?"

Next, grab your shovels, we’re digging! Clarifying is a therapeutic communication technique used to understand a vague or ambiguous client statement. They say something weird, you wipe the fog off the windshield to see what they actually meant. Exploring is a therapeutic communication technique delving further into a subject or idea mentioned by the client. You hit a plot hole in their story, and you dig deeper! Focusing is a therapeutic technique directing the client's attention to a single topic or specific point. You shine a massive theater spotlight on one single actor on the stage so they don't get distracted.

Don't forget your grounders. Silence is a therapeutic technique giving the client time to organize thoughts and process emotions. You gotta let the steak rest before you slice it so the juices settle! Offering self is a therapeutic technique where the nurse makes themselves available to the client unconditionally. "I'm just going to sit on this couch with you until the storm passes, no strings attached." Acknowledging is a therapeutic technique involving recognition of a client's effort or change. You aren't throwing a parade, you're just giving them a solid nod of respect because they managed to brush their hair today.
