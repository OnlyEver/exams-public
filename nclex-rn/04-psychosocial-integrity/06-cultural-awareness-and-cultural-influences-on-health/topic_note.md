# The Cultural Blueprint: How Human Beliefs Shape Healing

Welcome! Pull up a chair. Today we are going to talk about something infinitely fascinating—the [human mind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mind) and the "[operating systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operating_system)" that run it. 

When you look at a patient in a [hospital bed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hospital_bed), it is easy to see them simply as a [biological machine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biological_machine). A failing [kidney](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kidney) here, a fractured [femur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Femur) there. But patients are not just collections of [organs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organ_%28biology%29); they are breathing repositories of [history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History), [belief](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belief), and [culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Culture). If you try to fix the "[hardware](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_hardware)" of the body without understanding the "[software](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Software)" of the client's culture, you are going to encounter a lot of system errors.

To be a truly elite [nurse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nurse), you must be a cultural detective. Let’s dive into how we seamlessly integrate [cultural awareness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cultural_sensitivity) into the [plan of care](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nursing_care_plan).

## 1. The Starting Line: Assessment and the Traps of Assumption

Imagine you are handed a piece of a [puzzle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Puzzle), but you don't know what the final picture looks like. How do you find out? You ask! 

**Cultural assessment must begin with the client's self-reported [cultural identity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cultural_identity).** You cannot look at someone’s [skin color](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_skin_color), [facial features](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Face), or [surname](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surname) and deduce their culture. **Relying on a client's [physical appearance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_physical_appearance) to determine cultural identity is inaccurate.** Nature provides the [biology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology), but only the individual can report their culture.

As you step into the patient's room, you must immediately disarm two major [psychological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Psychology) traps: **[Ethnocentrism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethnocentrism)** and **[Stereotyping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stereotype)**.

> **[Ethnocentrism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethnocentrism)** is the belief that one's own cultural practices are superior to the cultural practices of others. 

If you grew up believing that [Western](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_world), [allopathic medicine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allopathic_medicine) is the *only* valid way to view [health](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Health), you are wearing ethnocentric blinders. **Nurses must suspend personal ethnocentrism to provide [culturally competent care](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cultural_competence_in_healthcare).** You don't have to adopt their beliefs, but you *must* respect them.

> **[Stereotyping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stereotype)** assumes all members of a specific cultural group share the exact same beliefs. 

It is a shortcut our [brains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brain) take to save energy, and it is terribly dangerous in [medicine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medicine). While [cultural norms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norm_%28social%29) exist, **individual client preferences can differ significantly from generalized cultural norms.** Always verify the individual's specific stance.

### The Hidden Dimensions: Time and Literacy
A comprehensive cultural assessment doesn't stop at [ethnicity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethnicity). It includes two subtle but profound factors:
1. **[Health Literacy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Health_literacy):** A comprehensive cultural assessment includes exploring the client's health literacy level. How do they conceptualize [disease](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disease)? Can they navigate our complex [healthcare](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Health_care) [jargon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medical_terminology)?
2. **[Time Orientation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_perspective):** A cultural assessment evaluates the client's time orientation. Did you know the human perception of time is cultural? **Time orientation categorizes cultures as past-oriented, present-oriented, or future-oriented.** 
   * *Future-oriented* clients will happily take [preventative medications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Preventive_healthcare) to avoid a [heart attack](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myocardial_infarction) ten years from now. 
   * *Present-oriented* clients might struggle with preventative care, prioritizing immediate comfort or pressing daily survival needs over abstract future benefits. Adjust your teaching accordingly!

## 2. Fueling the Body: Cultural Diets

[Food](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food) is incredibly personal. It is comfort, [tradition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tradition), and [religion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Religion) plated up together. **The plan of care must incorporate the client's cultural [dietary preferences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diet_%28nutrition%29).** 

However, your [license](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Occupational_licensing) is on the line, which means [safety](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Safety) comes first. **The nurse must verify that cultural dietary preferences are medically safe for the client.** (For instance, high-[sodium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_in_biology) cultural dishes might be [contraindicated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contraindication) for a [heart failure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heart_failure) patient, requiring a collaborative compromise).

Here is a quick breakdown of major cultural dietary rules you will see on the [NCLEX](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Council_Licensure_Examination):

| Cultural/Religious Group | Key Dietary Practices to Remember |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Practicing [Muslims](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muslims)** | May request a **[Halal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halal)** diet. Furthermore, practicing Muslims may [fast](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fasting) from [dawn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dawn) to [sunset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunset) during the month of **[Ramadan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ramadan)**. (Adjust medication administration schedules around fasting if medically safe!) |
| **[Orthodox Jews](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthodox_Judaism)** | Require **[Kosher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kashrut)** meals. ## 3. Clashing Paradigms: Blood, Botanicals, and Biology

Sometimes, cultural and religious beliefs will directly intersect—and sometimes conflict—with standard [medical interventions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medical_procedure).

### The Jehovah’s Witness and the Blood Barrier
This is an absolute classic NCLEX concept. **[Jehovah's Witnesses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jehovah%27s_Witnesses) generally refuse [whole blood transfusions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_transfusion).** Furthermore, they **generally refuse [red blood cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_blood_cell) transfusions** (as well as [white blood cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_blood_cell), [platelets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platelet), and [plasma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_plasma)). 

If your patient is [hemorrhaging](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bleeding), what *can* you do? [Physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics) tells us we need to maintain the body's [fluid volume](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_volume) to keep [blood pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_pressure) up. Thankfully, **some Jehovah's Witnesses accept [blood volume expanders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume_expander).** A prime example: **[Dextran](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dextran) is an example of a blood volume expander acceptable to some Jehovah's Witnesses**, alongside [normal saline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saline_%28medicine%29) and [lactated Ringer's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ringer%27s_lactate_solution). 

![Lactated Ringer's solution is an isotonic crystalloid fluid commonly used as a blood volume expander for clients who refuse blood transfusions.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/LactateRingers.jpg)

### Complementary and Alternative Medicine (CAM)
Patients often try to fix themselves before they come to us. **The nurse must identify the client's use of [complementary and alternative medicine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alternative_medicine).** They might be drinking [herbal teas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herbal_tea), using [acupuncture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acupuncture), or taking potent [botanicals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herbal_medicine). 

Why do we care? Because [chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemistry) is chemistry! **The nurse must evaluate complementary and alternative medicine practices for contraindications with prescribed [medical therapies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Therapy).** (e.g., [St. John's Wort](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypericum_perforatum) interfering with [antidepressants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antidepressant), or [Gingko Biloba](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ginkgo_biloba) increasing bleeding risks with [anticoagulants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anticoagulant)).

![St. John's Wort is a popular botanical supplement that can cause dangerous contraindications and drug interactions, particularly with prescribed antidepressants.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Saint_John's_wort_flowers.jpg)

## 4. The Spectrum of Suffering and the Final Transition

### The Perception of Pain
[Pain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pain) is a [neurological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neurology) signal, but *[suffering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suffering)* is a cultural experience. **Cultural beliefs heavily influence a client's perception of pain.** 
* **Some cultures encourage loud vocalization of pain**, viewing it as a healthy release or a necessary call for [family](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Family) support.
* In stark contrast, **some cultures value [stoicism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stoicism) in response to pain**, viewing silent endurance as a sign of strength or spiritual fortitude. Do not assume a stoic patient is pain-free! You must look for [non-verbal cues](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonverbal_communication) and assess diligently.

![While pain originates as a biological signal processed by the cerebral cortex, a client's expression of that suffering is largely dictated by their cultural background.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Schematic_of_cortical_areas_involved_with_pain_processing_and_fMRI.jpg)

### End-of-Life Rituals
When the biological machine finally fails, culture dictates how we honor the departure. **The nurse must accommodate cultural [end-of-life rituals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/End-of-life_care) when formulating the care plan.** 

For example, consider the beautiful and specific traditions in [Islam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islam). **Some Islamic traditions require the deceased body to be washed only by a person of the same [gender](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gender).** Additionally, **some Islamic traditions require the deceased body to be positioned facing [Mecca](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mecca).** If you know this, you can proactively adjust the [bed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bed) and make the appropriate arrangements to honor the family's [faith](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Faith).

![The Kaaba in Mecca, Saudi Arabia. In Islamic tradition, positioning a deceased client's body to face Mecca is a profound sign of spiritual respect.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Kaaba%2C_Makkah6.jpg)

## 5. The Unspoken Rules: Touch, Eyes, and Power Dynamics

The space between you and your patient is governed by invisible cultural tripwires. Walk carefully.

### The Eyes and the Hands
In [America](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States), we are taught to look people in the eye and give a firm [handshake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Handshake). But **cultural norms dictate the acceptable use of direct [eye contact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eye_contact).** While [Westerners](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_world) might view avoiding eye contact as shifty or unconfident, **some cultures consider direct eye contact a sign of disrespect**, especially toward [authority figures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Authority) (like a nurse or [doctor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physician)!).

Likewise, **cultural norms dictate the acceptable use of [physical touch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haptic_communication).** Never assume your touch is welcome. Let’s talk about the head. **In some cultures, the [head](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_head) is considered the most sacred part of the body**, believed to be the resting place of the [soul](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soul). Therefore, **the nurse must ask permission before touching a client's head.** 

### Family Structure and the Seat of Power
When a tough medical choice must be made, who makes the call? You must map out the family's [hierarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hierarchy). **The nurse must identify the primary [decision-maker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decision-making) within the client's family structure.**

* **[Patriarchal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patriarchy) family structures designate the oldest male as the primary healthcare decision-maker.**
* **[Matriarchal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matriarchy) family structures designate the oldest female as the primary healthcare decision-maker.**

If you spend twenty minutes explaining an [informed consent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Informed_consent) form to a patriarchal patient’s youngest daughter, you have wasted your time and potentially offended the family. Find the decision-maker!

## 6. Bridging the Gap: The Mechanics of Medical Translation

Imagine being trapped in a foreign country, terrified, in pain, and a [scientist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientist) in a [white coat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_coat) is babbling at you in a [language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Language) you don't understand. Terrifying, right? 

**The nurse must identify the client's preferred language for medical [communication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communication).** Once identified, if there is a barrier, you must act with precision. **The nurse must use a certified [medical interpreter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Language_interpretation) for clients with [limited English proficiency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limited_English_proficiency) (LEP).**

### The "Never" Rules of Interpretation
Do not try to take shortcuts here. The NCLEX will test your commitment to these boundaries:
1. **Using family members as medical interpreters violates client [confidentiality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confidentiality).** (Does a teenage son need to translate his mother's [gynecological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gynaecology) [symptoms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symptom)? Absolutely not.)
2. **Using family members as medical interpreters increases the risk of [medical translation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medical_translation) [errors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medical_error).** They are not trained in [clinical terminology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medical_terminology). They might downplay symptoms to avoid worrying the patient. 
3. **[Minor children](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minor_%28law%29) must never be used as medical interpreters.** It places unimaginable psychological burdens on a child.
4. **The nurse must use a certified medical interpreter instead of [bilingual](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bilingualism) [unlicensed assistive personnel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unlicensed_assistive_personnel) (UAP) for medical explanations.** A bilingual nursing assistant is fantastic for asking if the patient wants water, but they are *unlicensed* and legally unqualified to translate complex [discharge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hospital_discharge) instructions or consent forms.

### How to Work with the Interpreter
When the certified interpreter arrives (in person, on [video](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Videotelephony), or by [phone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telephone)), *you* are still the nurse communicating with *your* patient. 

* **The nurse must speak directly to the client during an interpreted conversation.** 
* **The nurse must look directly at the client during an interpreted conversation.** (Do not look at the [iPad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IPad) or the interpreter and say, "Tell him that he needs to...") Look into your patient's eyes and say, "You need to..."
* **The nurse must speak in short [sentences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sentence_%28linguistics%29) when communicating through a medical interpreter.** Give them bite-sized pieces of [data](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data) to translate accurately.
* **The nurse must avoid using [medical jargon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medical_terminology) when communicating through a medical interpreter.** (Say "[high blood pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypertension)," not "[idiopathic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Idiopathic) [hypertension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypertension).")
* **The nurse must avoid using [idiomatic expressions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Idiom) when communicating through a medical interpreter.** If you say, "You're going to [kick the bucket](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kick_the_bucket) if you don't take this," the [literal translation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Literal_translation) might confuse or terrify the patient! Keep it literal.

![When working with an interpreter, the healthcare provider must maintain direct eye contact and speak directly to the client, not the translator.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/US_Navy_091010-F-3177P-029_Maj._Scott_Hughes_gathers_medical_information_from_a_patient_with_the_help_of_Hasbi%2C_a_local_interpreter.jpg)

### The Final Step: Documentation and Evaluation
If it isn't documented, it didn't happen. The [chart](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medical_record) must reflect your meticulous care.
* **The client's [medical record](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medical_record) must explicitly document the client's preferred language.**
* **The medical record must document the use of a medical interpreter during healthcare encounters.**
* Crucially, **the medical record must document the specific name or identification number of the medical interpreter used.** This is for legal traceability.

Finally, how do you know the "software" successfully processed the new data? You test the output. **The nurse must use the [teach-back method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teach-back_method) to evaluate the client's [comprehension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Understanding) of translated medical information.** Ask the patient to explain the instructions back to you in their own words via the interpreter. Only then can you be sure true [healing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Healing) communication has occurred. 

Now, go out there, suspend your assumptions, honor the beautiful complexity of your clients' cultures, and practice brilliant [nursing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nursing)!
