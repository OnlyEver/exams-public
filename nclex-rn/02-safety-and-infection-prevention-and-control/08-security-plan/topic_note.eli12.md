## Part I: The Mathematics of Triage

Let’s start with a puzzle. How do you decide who gets help first when an emergency happens? 

We have to understand the huge difference between a normal everyday emergency and a massive disaster. They have completely different rules, kind of like how playing a regular soccer game is different from playing a survival video game.

### The Everyday Emergency Room 
In normal, day-to-day life, **emergency department triage priorities focus on treating the most critically ill patients first regardless of resource consumption.** If someone comes in with a really bad heart problem, the hospital throws everything at them. Six nurses, three doctors, tons of medicine—the hospital uses up tons of supplies to save that one person, because the hospital has plenty of supplies in the supply closet. 

### Disaster Triage and the START Method
Now, imagine a huge tornado hits the town. You suddenly have fifty hurt people all at once, and only three nurses. 

Let's do some quick math to show how we solve this:
Step 1: Imagine you have 5 special bandages. You have 6 patients. 
Step 2: One very hurt patient needs all 5 bandages to survive. The other 5 patients each need exactly 1 bandage to survive.
Step 3: If you give all 5 bandages to the one very hurt patient, you save 1 life. (5 - 5 = 0 bandages left). But the other 5 patients don't make it.
Step 4: If you give 1 bandage to each of the 5 less-hurt patients, you save 5 lives! (5 / 1 = 5 people helped).
Step 5: Since saving 5 lives is greater than saving 1 life (5 > 1), you choose to help the group.

In a disaster, the rules flip. **Disaster triage principles prioritize doing the greatest good for the greatest number of people using available resources.** We stop focusing on just one person and start thinking about the whole group.

To do this, we use the **Simple Triage and Rapid Treatment method**. This system **categorizes disaster patients based on the severity of injuries and the likelihood of survival.** It helps us make smart, mathematical choices instead of letting our feelings take over. 

We sort our patients using four colored tags. Think of these tags like a simple color-code in a video game:

| Tag Color | Meaning & Action | The "Why" |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Red** | **Immediate.** **In disaster triage, a red tag indicates an immediate threat to life requiring prompt intervention.** | This person has a blocked airway or bad bleeding. They will survive *only if* you fix them right this very second. |
| **Yellow** | **Delayed.** **In disaster triage, a yellow tag indicates major injuries that can safely wait up to two hours for treatment.** | They might have a broken bone, but they are not going to get worse in the next 120 minutes. |
| **Green** | **Minimal.** **In disaster triage, a green tag indicates minor injuries for patients who are able to walk without assistance.** | These are people with scrapes. We can just point them to a safe waiting area away from the mess. |
| **Black** | **Expectant.** **In disaster triage, a black tag indicates the patient is deceased or has injuries incompatible with life given available resources.** | It is sad, but in a huge disaster, we cannot do CPR because it takes up too much time and energy away from the people we can easily save. |

> **The Golden Rule of Disasters:** When you have infinite supplies, save every single person. When you have limited supplies, do the math. Save the most people possible.

## Part II: Moving the Masses (Evacuation Physics)

Imagine a fire breaks out on your side of the hospital. You need to get everyone out. But moving a hospital full of sick people is super hard. You can't just yell "Fire!" and expect everyone to run out smoothly.

We have two ways to move people:
1. **Horizontal evacuation:** This **involves moving patients to a safe area on the same floor beyond fire doors.** Hospitals are built with special heavy doors that block fire. If you push a patient through these doors down the hallway, you are in a safe zone. Always move sideways (horizontally) first if you can!
2. **Vertical evacuation:** If the whole floor is in danger, you have to go down. This **involves moving patients down stairwells to a different floor to escape danger.** You can never use elevators during a fire.

### The Order of Operations
Now, who do you move first? You might think you should move the sickest person first. *Stop.* That is a huge mistake. If you try to carry heavy life-support machines down the stairs, you will block the doorway. Then, thirty people who could have easily walked out are trapped behind you.

**Facility evacuation protocols dictate moving people in this exact order:**
1. **Facility evacuation protocols dictate moving ambulatory patients first.** This means you get the people who can walk out of the way! Clear the halls immediately.
2. **Facility evacuation protocols dictate moving non-ambulatory patients requiring minimal assistance second.** These are people in wheelchairs or people who just need someone to hold their hand to walk.
3. **Facility evacuation protocols dictate moving patients requiring extensive life support assistance last.** It sounds tough, but moving these patients takes a super long time. You have to save the biggest group of people first before the exits get blocked.

## Part III: The Ultimate Fortress (Newborn Security)

Let’s change topics. Think about the baby nursery in a hospital. To parents, their new baby is the most precious thing in the world. Because babies are so precious, the hospital turns the nursery into a high-security vault to prevent anyone from stealing a baby. 

If someone tries to take a baby, the hospital sets off an alarm. **A Code Pink is a commonly used hospital emergency code indicating an infant abduction.** The exact second this alarm goes off, the hospital goes into lockdown. **During an infant abduction alert, staff must immediately secure and monitor all facility exits and stairwells.** Nobody is allowed to leave.

But how do we stop this from happening in the first place? We use a mix of cool gadgets and strict rules.

### The Technology Layer
We actually use smart-sensors on the babies! **Infant security systems utilize electronic tags that trigger an alarm if an infant is brought near unit exits.** But what if a bad guy tries to cut the tag off with scissors? The inventors thought of that, too. These **infant security electronic tags automatically lock unit doors if tampered with or removed.** 

### The Human Protocol Layer
Gadgets can break, so the humans have to follow the rules perfectly. 
* **The ID Check:** Even if the person standing there looks exactly like the baby's mom, **facility protocols require matching the newborn identification band with the parent identification band before leaving the infant with the parent.** It's like checking a secret password. If the bands don't match perfectly, you don't hand over the baby. 
* **The Bassinet Rule:** You will never see a nurse carrying a baby in their arms down the hallway. **Safety protocols dictate transporting newborns in rolling bassinets rather than carrying newborns in staff arms.** Why? If a nurse trips over their shoelaces, the baby is safe inside the little rolling bed (the bassinet). It also stops strangers from just walking out holding a baby.
* **The Uniform:** Not just any doctor or nurse is allowed in the baby room. **Staff members authorized to work in newborn units must wear visibly distinct identification badges.** These badges usually have a special color so they stand out. 
* **Parent Education:** Moms and dads are part of our team. **Parents must be educated to only release their newborn to facility staff members wearing the specific neonatal unit identification badge.** If someone walks into their room without that special badge and asks for the baby, the parents know to yell for help.

## Part IV: Locking the Doors (Facility Access and Elopement)

Sometimes, security isn't about keeping bad guys *out*. Sometimes it's about keeping confused patients *in*. 

When a patient wanders away and gets lost, we call it "elopement." It is really scary. Imagine an older patient whose brain isn't working right walking outside into the snow. How do we stop this?

### Identifying the Risk
You can't fix a problem if you don't know it's there. So, **nurses must assess a patient's risk for elopement upon admission using a standardized cognitive assessment tool.** This is basically a simple memory and thinking quiz we give them when they first arrive.

Once we know a patient might wander off, we use our tricks to keep them safe:
1. **Strategic Geography:** **Patients at high risk for elopement must be assigned to rooms located close to the nursing station.** We keep them right where we can see them! We don't put them at the end of a long hallway near the exit door.
2. **Technological Boundaries:** Just like with the babies, we use technology. **Electronic wandering tracking systems trigger perimeter alarms when a high-risk patient approaches an exit.** 
3. **The Ultimate Guard:** Sometimes, gadgets and room choices aren't enough. **Patients with dementia or cognitive impairments require continuous visual monitoring or a one-to-one sitter to prevent elopement.** This means a staff member sits in the room with them 24/7, just to watch them.

### Controlled Access and Tailgating
In super private areas (like the baby room or intensive care), we use **Controlled access units**, which **require all visitors to be individually verified and granted entry by facility staff.** This means the door is locked, and a nurse has to let you in after checking who you are.

But here is a problem: people are too polite! If you use your badge to unlock a door, and someone is walking right behind you holding a cup of coffee, your brain tells you to hold the door open for them. *Do not do this.* This is called "tailgating" (like when a car follows another car too closely). 

**Staff members must intercept unauthorized individuals attempting to follow credentialed personnel through secure doors.** It is your job to stop them, look them in the eye, and say, "I'm sorry, you have to use your own badge or ask the front desk." 

To make it easy to see who belongs there, **facility security policies mandate that all employees wear visible identification badges at all times.** Your badge is your ticket to be in the hospital.

## Part V: When the Unthinkable Happens (Active Threats)

Finally, we have to talk about very bad situations caused by dangerous people. These things rarely happen, but if they do, they are awful. Your brain might panic. You have to remember these strict rules to stay safe.

### Suspicious Packages and Bomb Threats
Imagine you are walking down the hall and see a weird, unmarked box sitting by the trash can with wires on it. **A staff member discovering a suspicious package must secure the immediate area without touching the object.** Do not be a hero. Do not poke it. Just keep people away and call security. 

What if the phone rings, and a bad guy says there is a bomb in the hospital?
1. **A staff member receiving a telephonic bomb threat must keep the caller on the line as long as possible.** Why? So you can gather clues. 
2. **A staff member receiving a telephonic bomb threat must document background noises and specific caller voice characteristics.** Do you hear a dog barking in the background? Does the caller sound nervous or have a funny accent? Write all of this down immediately!

### Active Shooter Protocols
An active shooter in a hospital is the worst-case scenario. Police say you must do three things, exactly in this order:

1. **Evacuate:** **Active shooter protocols prioritize evacuating the area immediately if a safe path is accessible.** Leave your backpack and phone behind. Just run and get out. 
2. **Hide:** If the bad guy is blocking the exit, **active shooter protocols dictate hiding in a secure location and barricading the door if evacuation is impossible.** Turn off the lights, turn your phone completely on silent, and push heavy beds or desks against the door so they can't get in. 
3. **Fight:** **Active shooter protocols consider fighting the attacker as an absolute last resort to protect human life.** If they break through the door and you have absolutely no other choice, you have to fight back using whatever you can find, like a fire extinguisher, to stop them.
