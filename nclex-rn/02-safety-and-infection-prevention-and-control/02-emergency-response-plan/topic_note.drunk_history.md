Listen to me, grab your ginger ale, and lean in close, because normal nursing is like hosting a high-end, painfully polite dinner party for one very delicate VIP. You pamper them. You obsess over their vital signs. You spend an hour folding their metaphorical napkin into a beautiful little swan. But what happens when fifty party-crashers kick down the front doors, the kitchen catches fire, and the power goes out? You don't have time for swans! You are now in the wild, unhinged world of disaster nursing. The whole math of survival changes. Normally, we do everything in our power to save the one person right in front of us, but disaster triage shifts the clinical focus from maximizing individual outcomes to doing the greatest good for the greatest number of people. It’s a ruthless numbers game, honey. It's reality TV elimination night, and you are the host.

To play this game, we use the Simple Triage and Rapid Treatment method, which categorizes disaster victims into four color-coded groups based on injury severity. Think of this method as the ultimate, unforgiving bouncer at an exclusive underground club, sorting the chaos at the door. 

First, you have the absolute VIPs of the disaster: the Reds. A red triage tag indicates an immediate threat to life requiring rapid medical intervention for survival. These are the kitchen grease fires—you handle them right this second, or the whole place burns to the ground. Specifically, a red triage tag is applied to victims with treatable airway obstructions or uncontrolled hemorrhage. You fix the airway, you slap on a tourniquet, you save a life in sixty seconds, and you keep moving! 

Then you have the runner-ups, the Yellows. A yellow triage tag indicates significant injuries that can wait several hours for treatment without an immediate threat to life. They’re hurting, but they aren't dying *right now*. A yellow triage tag is applied to victims with stable abdominal wounds or major bone fractures. They are like a messy breakup text—it’s highly dramatic, it hurts a lot, but honestly, it can sit on read for a few hours while you handle the real emergencies. 

Next up are the extras in our little tragedy, the Greens. A green triage tag indicates minor injuries that do not require immediate medical attention. We literally point them to a corner because a green triage tag is applied to ambulatory victims who are commonly referred to as the walking wounded. If they can walk and complain, they can wait.

Finally, we hit the darkest, hardest part of the night: the Blacks. In a normal world, we run a Code Blue and do chest compressions until we pass out. But in a mass casualty event, a black triage tag indicates injuries that are incompatible with life given the available medical resources. A black triage tag is applied to victims with massive head trauma or victims who are in cardiac arrest upon first responder arrival. If we spend an hour doing CPR on one catastrophic trauma, ten Reds will bleed out behind them. It’s brutal, but we provide comfort and step away. No heroics. 

Now, where is this drama even coming from? We have two flavors of chaos. First, the inside job. An internal disaster is an event occurring within the healthcare facility that threatens client or staff safety. The call is coming from inside the house! Facility fires and severe power outages are examples of internal disasters. 

Then you have the external disaster, which is an event occurring outside the healthcare facility that causes a sudden influx of casualties. Think of a massive, angry mob rolling up to your restaurant patio. Natural disasters and mass transit accidents are examples of external disasters. 

Speaking of outside chaos, what if that external disaster is a massive chemical spill? The victims are going to run to your emergency room doors covered in toxic, glowing goo. If you let them in, your hospital ventilation sucks it right up, and boom—your external disaster just triggered a catastrophic internal one! It's exactly like letting a toxic ex back into your apartment; they will instantly poison the entire vibe. Therefore, victim decontamination during a chemical mass casualty event must occur before those victims enter the main hospital facility. You hose them down in the parking lot. The mess stays outside!

So, an external disaster hits—let's say a hurricane—and you have two hundred patients en route. You need beds. You need surge capacity. It’s time to start kicking people off the island. Who goes? Priority one: during a hospital emergency, clients who are ambulatory and require minimal care are the first recommended for discharge. "Oh, you can walk and just need an oral antibiotic? Pack your bags, sweetie, call an Uber!" Priority two: clients requiring assistance who can be safely managed at home with community support are the second priority for disaster discharge. If home health or their highly capable Aunt Linda can handle them, they are out the door. 

But here is the absolute, unbreakable rule of the hospital mob: unstable clients must not be recommended for discharge during a disaster. Pushing them out into the chaos is a literal death sentence. Likewise, clients requiring continuous skilled medical care must remain in the hospital during a mass casualty event. If they are on a ventilator or a complex ICU drip, congratulations, they are your permanent roommates. 

What if the disaster isn't outside? What if it's an internal fire on your unit? You smell smoke. Do you immediately sprint down the hall and pull the fire alarm? No! Nurses must prioritize rescuing clients in immediate danger before activating the facility fire alarm during a fire event. You don't scream at the chef before you pull the prime rib out of the literal flames. Pull the patient out of the burning room first, then sound the alarm! 

Now we have to evacuate. But wait, you can't just throw people over your shoulder. Nurses must assess client mobility status before making lateral or vertical evacuation decisions. Can they walk, or are they a total dead weight hooked to five machines? Once you know their mobility, you follow the pure physics of escape: hospital evacuation procedures dictate moving clients laterally to a safe area on the same floor before moving clients vertically down stairs. Dragging a massive hospital bed down six flights of stairs is a recipe for broken ankles and absolute disaster; pushing them horizontally through heavy fire doors into the next smoke compartment is a breeze. If you only need to clear part of the floor, hospital staff must relocate unstable clients to the most secure area of the facility if a partial evacuation is required. Keep them away from the danger, but keep them on their life-saving monitors!

You cannot invent this choreography on the fly. You need a script for the madness, which is why nursing staff must know the exact physical or digital location of the facility Emergency Operations Plan. Don't go hunting for the survival binder when the ceiling is caving in! And because you simply cannot read a manual while you are actively on fire, mandatory participation in disaster drills prepares nursing staff to execute emergency protocols automatically. It becomes pure muscle memory, like ducking when a frying pan is thrown in a hostile kitchen. In fact, The Joint Commission requires accredited healthcare facilities to practice their Emergency Operations Plan at least twice a year. They know that without practice, we panic.

When that drill becomes reality, you don't form a collaborative drum circle to discuss your feelings. You become a foot soldier in a mob family. The Hospital Incident Command System provides a standardized hierarchical structure for managing hospital emergencies. It is rigid, it is absolute, and it leaves no room for debate. Nurses must maintain a clear chain of command by reporting to the appointed incident commander during a disaster. You do what the boss says, when they say it. No going rogue.

Finally, the paperwork. You do not have time to write a beautifully detailed nursing narrative while the ER looks like a warzone. Nurses must utilize abbreviated charting methods during a mass casualty incident to maximize time for direct client care. You jot down the vitals, scribble the meds, maybe write it right on their triage tag, and you move to the next bleeding patient. But listen to me carefully—even in the apocalypse, the law applies. Registered nurses must legally practice within their state Nurse Practice Act scope of practice during a declared disaster. Just because there's an earthquake doesn't mean you can suddenly perform open-heart surgery with a butter knife. You stay in your lane, you follow the commander, you triage like a ruthless club bouncer, and you keep the chaos outside! Now finish your drink, because we've got a drill at 0600!
