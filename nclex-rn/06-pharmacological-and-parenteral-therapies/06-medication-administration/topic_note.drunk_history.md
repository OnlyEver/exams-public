And you gotta be paranoid. A nurse must verify the medication label against the medication administration record three times before administration. It’s a three-step interrogation. Some of these drugs are heavy hitters. They don't walk alone. High-alert medications require independent double-verification by a second registered nurse prior to administration. It's the buddy system for mob hits. For example, intravenous insulin is classified as a high-alert medication because it crashes blood sugar like a catastrophic stock market failure, and intravenous heparin is classified as a high-alert medication because it turns your blood into literal water. You do not push those alone!

Now, let's talk about the kitchen disasters: pills and tubes. Imagine an enteric-coated tablet as a delicate soufflé. Enteric-coated tablets must remain intact to prevent premature dissolution in the stomach. If you crush it, the stomach acid ruins the dish before it even gets to the intestines! Same goes for the slow-burn drama. Extended-release capsules must remain intact to prevent an immediate release of a large medication dose. You don't dump all the season's drama into episode one, or the patient's system crashes from the toxicity! 

If your patient has an enteral feeding tube, the plumbing rules change. Medications administered via an enteral feeding tube must be crushed to a fine powder, and those crushed enteral medications must be dissolved in sterile water before tube administration. If you leave it chunky, you clog the pipes and ruin the whole house. P pouring a liquid cocktail? Simple fluid dynamics, my friend! Liquid medications should be poured with the medication label facing the palm of the hand. Why? Because pouring liquid medications with the label facing the palm prevents spilled liquid from obscuring the label. You gotta protect the barcode!

Let's talk angles of attack with needles. Intramuscular injections are administered at a 90-degree angle. Straight down, a total precision air strike. Subcutaneous injections are administered at a 45-degree angle or a 90-degree angle, slipping the drug right into the fat padding. Intradermal injections are administered at a 5-degree to 15-degree angle, just sliding a little microscopic love note right under the top layer of skin. 

Where are you stabbing? For adults, the ventrogluteal site is the preferred anatomical location for intramuscular injections. It's the VIP lounge of muscles—deep, safe, and away from snitching nerves. But babies haven't done their squats yet! The vastus lateralis is the preferred intramuscular injection site for infants; you go right for that thigh meat. When you make that IM hit, you use a special ninja move. The Z-track method prevents medication leakage into subcutaneous tissue during intramuscular injections. You pull the skin taut, stab, inject, and let go—the tissue snaps back, locking the drug in a fleshy vault so it can't climb back up the needle track!

Now, the IV line—that is a direct wiretap to the heart, and it is entirely unforgiving. The nurse must assess an intravenous insertion site for patency and signs of infiltration before administering intravenous medications. Is the line actually in the vein, or are we just flooding the basement with toxic fluid? When you push manually, intravenous push medications must be administered at the specific rate recommended by the manufacturer or pharmacy. Push too fast, and you trigger a system-wide short circuit! 

Sometimes you gotta play DJ with the medication. Medication titration involves adjusting a drug dose based on the patient's physiological response or specific target parameters. But you don't start twisting dials blindly! The nurse must obtain baseline vital signs before initiating a titratable medication infusion. And you absolutely cannot eyeball the drip rate from a bag like a leaky faucet. A continuous intravenous infusion of a titrated medication requires the use of an electronic infusion pump. The classic example from the ICU trenches? Vasoactive intravenous medications are frequently titrated to maintain a specific mean arterial pressure parameter. You are literally driving their blood pressure with a joystick!

Topical and mucosal meds have weird house rules. When administering otic medications to an adult, the nurse must pull the pinna up and back. Stretch out that ear canal! But their cartilage isn't fully grown yet, so when administering otic medications to a child under three years of age, the nurse must pull the pinna down and back. Eyes? Don't bomb the sensitive cornea! Ophthalmic medications must be instilled into the lower conjunctival sac. Once it's in, applying gentle pressure to the nasolacrimal duct prevents systemic absorption of ophthalmic medications. You pinch the bridge of the nose to trap the drug in the eye so it doesn't leak into the bloodstream like a bad rumor! 

Rectal meds? It's an anatomical slip-and-slide. The patient must be placed in the left lateral Sims position for the administration of a rectal suppository, following the natural downward curve of the colon. And, naturally, rectal suppositories must be lubricated with a water-soluble lubricant before insertion. 

Transdermal patches are stickers with venom. Transdermal patches must be applied to clean, hairless areas of intact skin. But for the love of all that is holy, a nurse must remove the old transdermal patch before applying a new transdermal patch! If you don't, you turn them into a sticker book of accidental overdoses! 

Inhalers require perfect timing. A patient must exhale completely before activating a metered-dose inhaler. Empty the lungs so the medicine actually has a parking spot! To make it easier, a spacer device attached to a metered-dose inhaler increases the amount of medication delivered to the lungs, saving them from needing perfect hand-breath coordination. Oh, and if they are huffing steroids? Patients using a corticosteroid inhaler must rinse the mouth with water and spit after administration. Why? Because rinsing the mouth after corticosteroid inhaler use prevents the development of oral candidiasis. Nobody wants a mouth full of fungal thrush snow!

Now, we hit the audit: reading the patient's messy diary. Medication reconciliation is the process of comparing a patient's current medication orders to all of the medications the patient previously took. And it's not just prescriptions! Medication reconciliation includes over-the-counter medications and dietary supplements. That herbal tea they bought at a gas station might be planning a turf war with their heart meds! This audit never stops. Medication reconciliation must occur at every transition of care. Moving into the house? Hospital admission is a transition of care requiring medication reconciliation. Changing bedrooms? Transfer between hospital units is a transition of care requiring medication reconciliation. Getting kicked out? Hospital discharge is a transition of care requiring medication reconciliation. To make this nightmare easier, patients must be instructed to bring a complete list of current medications to all healthcare appointments.

You also have to teach them to survive the wild. The teach-back method is an evidence-based communication tool used to verify a patient's understanding of medication instructions. You basically say, "Explain it back to me in your own words so I know you aren't going to accidentally end yourself." If they are flying solo, a nurse must assess a patient's manual dexterity before teaching the self-administration of subcutaneous insulin injections. Can they even pinch the skin? Furthermore, a nurse must assess a patient's visual acuity before teaching the self-administration of insulin from a vial. Can they see those tiny little microscopic lines? You also have to teach them real estate management. Patients self-administering subcutaneous insulin must be taught to rotate injection sites. Why? Because rotating subcutaneous injection sites prevents lipohypertrophy. You don't want lumpy, spongy scar fat ruining the drug absorption!

Finally, the breakup and hiding the bodies. The absolute golden rule of the paper trail: A nurse must never document the administration of a medication before the patient has actually taken the medication. Pre-charting is perjury, my friend! But what if they say no? Honor the breakup. If a patient refuses a medication, the nurse must document the refusal in the medication administration record. And you can't just keep it a secret! If a patient refuses a medication, the nurse must notify the prescribing healthcare provider.
