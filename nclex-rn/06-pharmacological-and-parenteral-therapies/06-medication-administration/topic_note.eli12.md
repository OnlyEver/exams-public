Welcome, future nurses! Today we are going to explore one of the most important jobs you will ever have: giving medications. 

When you give a medicine, you aren't just handing someone a pill. You are putting a powerful helper into the human body to change how it works. It’s like picking up a special power-up in a video game! If you do it right, the person heals and gets stronger. If you make a mistake, it can be really dangerous. 

To become a pro at this, we aren't just going to memorize boring rules. We are going to learn exactly *why* we do every single step. Let’s dive into the super cool mechanics of keeping patients safe!

## The Foundation of Safety: The "Rights" and the Checks

Before a medicine ever touches a patient, you have to build a safety shield around your routine. We do this by following special "Rights" and doing a series of important checks, kind of like a pre-game checklist in sports.

### The Rights of Administration
In the past, nursing was built on the **traditional five rights of medication administration**: the right patient, right drug, right dose, right route, and right time. But as hospitals got busier, we realized five rules weren't enough. Today, you also follow **additional rights of medication administration**, which include the right documentation, right reason, right response, and the right to refuse. 

### Identity: Who is the Patient?
How do you know you have the "right patient"? You never guess! **The Joint Commission requires two unique patient identifiers before medication administration.** 
*   **Acceptable patient identifiers** include the patient's full name, date of birth, and medical record number (which is like a player's ID tag). 
*   What about looking at the room number? **A patient's room number is an unacceptable patient identifier for medication administration.** Why? Because people move around! You are treating the person, not the room they sleep in.

### The Three Checks
Human beings make mistakes. To stop mistakes before they happen, we double and triple-check our work. **A nurse must verify the medication label against the medication administration record three times before administration:**
1.  **The first medication check** occurs when retrieving the medication from the dispensing system.
2.  **The second medication check** occurs when preparing the medication.
3.  **The third medication check** occurs at the patient's bedside immediately prior to administration.

> **High-Alert Medications: The Buddy System**
> Some medicines are so powerful that the difference between helping and hurting is super tiny. **High-alert medications require independent double-verification by a second registered nurse prior to administration.** This is like playing a co-op video game where both players have to turn a key at the exact same time to unlock a door. Two great examples are **intravenous insulin** and **intravenous heparin**, both of which are classified as high-alert medications because they make huge changes to the blood very quickly.

## The Geometry and Physics of Delivery (Routes of Administration)

Now, let's talk about how the medicine actually gets into the body. The path (or route) you use changes everything about how the medicine is prepared.

### Oral and Enteral Mechanics
If a pill goes into the stomach, it meets a pool of strong stomach acid. Medicine makers design pills to survive this!
*   **Enteric-coated tablets must remain intact to prevent premature dissolution in the stomach.** Think of them like jawbreaker candies with a tough outer shell. If you crush them, you ruin the shield!
*   Also, **extended-release capsules must remain intact to prevent an immediate release of a large medication dose**. If you crush an extended-release pill, it's like popping a water balloon all at once instead of letting it slowly drip out, which could make the person sick.

If the patient eats through a special plastic tube in their stomach (an enteral feeding tube), the rules change. **Medications administered via an enteral feeding tube must be crushed to a fine powder.** Then, those **crushed enteral medications must be dissolved in sterile water before tube administration.** If you don't dissolve it, the powder will clog the tube like mud!

What about pouring liquid medicines? **Liquid medications should be poured with the medication label facing the palm of the hand.** Why? Because **pouring liquid medications with the label facing the palm prevents spilled liquid from obscuring the label.** You don't want sticky cherry syrup to smear the words so you can't read them!

### Parenteral Routes: The Angles of Attack
When using a needle to give a shot, the angle you use depends on how deep the medicine needs to go. It's just like geometry!

| Injection Type | Target Depth | Angle |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Intradermal** | Just under the top layer of skin | **5-degree to 15-degree angle** |
| **Subcutaneous (SubQ)** | In the squishy fat tissue | **45-degree angle or a 90-degree angle** |
| **Intramuscular (IM)** | Deep inside the muscle | **90-degree angle** |

**Site Selection:** Where you give the shot is just as important as how you do it. 
*   For grown-ups, **the ventrogluteal site is the preferred anatomical location for intramuscular injections.** This is a thick muscle by the hip that is super safe.
*   But babies don't have big hip muscles yet! So, **the vastus lateralis is the preferred intramuscular injection site for infants.** This is the big muscle on the front of their thigh.

When giving a shot into the muscle, we use a cool trick. **The Z-track method prevents medication leakage into subcutaneous tissue during intramuscular injections.** By pulling the skin tight, giving the shot, and then letting go, you create a zigzag trap that locks the medicine deep inside the muscle so it can't leak out.

### Intravenous (IV) Administration
The IV route is a direct highway into the blood. It acts super fast!
*   Before giving medicine through an IV, **the nurse must assess an intravenous insertion site for patency and signs of infiltration**. This just means making sure the tube is open and the skin isn't puffy or leaking.
*   When pushing medicine in with a syringe, **intravenous push medications must be administered at the specific rate recommended by the manufacturer or pharmacy.** If you push it too fast, it will shock the body!

**Titration**
Sometimes, a set amount of medicine isn't right for everyone. **Medication titration involves adjusting a drug dose based on the patient's physiological response or specific target parameters.** It’s exactly like adjusting the thermostat in your house until the temperature feels perfectly comfortable!
*   Before adjusting the medicine, **the nurse must obtain baseline vital signs before initiating a titratable medication infusion.** You have to know the starting score to see if you are winning!
*   You can't just guess how much medicine is dripping. **A continuous intravenous infusion of a titrated medication requires the use of an electronic infusion pump.**
*   Here is a great example: **Vasoactive intravenous medications are frequently titrated to maintain a specific mean arterial pressure parameter.** (This means adjusting the drip until the blood pressure is exactly where the doctor wants it).

### Mucosal and Topical Magic
Medicines that go on the skin, eyes, and ears have their own special tricks:

*   **Otic (Ear):** The human ear canal is curved. To straighten it out like a slide:
    *   **When administering otic medications to an adult, the nurse must pull the pinna up and back.** (The pinna is the outer part of the ear).
    *   **When administering otic medications to a child under three years of age, the nurse must pull the pinna down and back.**
*   **Ophthalmic (Eye):** Never drop medicine directly onto the colored part of the eye! **Ophthalmic medications must be instilled into the lower conjunctival sac** (that little pink pouch you see when you pull your lower eyelid down). After the drops are in, **applying gentle pressure to the nasolacrimal duct prevents systemic absorption of ophthalmic medications.** Pressing this little spot by the nose stops the medicine from washing into the rest of the body.
*   **Rectal:** **The patient must be placed in the left lateral Sims position for the administration of a rectal suppository**, which means having them lie on their left side with their right knee bent up. Also, **rectal suppositories must be lubricated with a water-soluble lubricant before insertion** so they slide easily and dissolve comfortably.
*   **Transdermal:** These are medicine patches that look like stickers. **Transdermal patches must be applied to clean, hairless areas of intact skin.** Very importantly, **a nurse must remove the old transdermal patch before applying a new transdermal patch.** If you leave the old one on, the patient gets double the medicine, which is bad!
*   **Inhaled:** When using a puffer (inhaler) for asthma, **a patient must exhale completely before activating a metered-dose inhaler** to make lots of room in their lungs for the medicine. It helps to use a plastic tube attached to the inhaler. **A spacer device attached to a metered-dose inhaler increases the amount of medication delivered to the lungs.** 
    *   *A super important tip:* **Patients using a corticosteroid inhaler must rinse the mouth with water and spit after administration.** Why? Because **rinsing the mouth after corticosteroid inhaler use prevents the development of oral candidiasis** (a funky mouth infection).

## Transitions, Patient Education, and the Big Picture

Giving medicine isn't just about what happens in the hospital room. It connects to everything the patient does at home, too!

### Medication Reconciliation
Imagine trying to fix a Lego spaceship without checking the instructions to see what pieces you already added. That's what it’s like if you forget to do **medication reconciliation**, which **is the process of comparing a patient's current medication orders to all of the medications the patient previously took.**
*   This includes everything! **Medication reconciliation includes over-the-counter medications and dietary supplements** (like daily vitamins).
*   When do we do this? **Medication reconciliation must occur at every transition of care.** Specifically, **hospital admission**, **transfer between hospital units**, and **hospital discharge** are all transitions of care requiring medication reconciliation. 

Let's do a quick step-by-step math check on how many total medications you might need to review:
1. Count the number of prescription medicines the patient takes at home (Let's say 3).
2. Count the number of over-the-counter vitamins they take (Let's say 1).
3. Count the new medicines the hospital doctor ordered (Let's say 2).
4. Add them all together: 3 + 1 + 2 = 6 total medications you must check!

*   To make this math easier, **patients must be instructed to bring a complete list of current medications to all healthcare appointments.**

### Educating the Master of the Vessel
We want patients to be super smart about their own bodies. How do we make sure they understand our teaching? We use a neat trick. **The teach-back method is an evidence-based communication tool used to verify a patient's understanding of medication instructions.** You just ask them to teach it back to you in their own words!

Before teaching a patient to give themselves shots, you have to check if their body can handle the job:
*   **A nurse must assess a patient's manual dexterity before teaching the self-administration of subcutaneous insulin injections.** If their fingers are too stiff to push the plunger, they can't do it safely.
*   Also, **a nurse must assess a patient's visual acuity before teaching the self-administration of insulin from a vial.** If their eyesight is blurry, they won't be able to measure the tiny numbers on the syringe!
*   You must also teach them to move the shot around. **Patients self-administering subcutaneous insulin must be taught to rotate injection sites.** Why? Because **rotating subcutaneous injection sites prevents lipohypertrophy**, which is when the fat gets bumpy, hard, and sore from getting poked in the exact same spot too many times.

## The Final Acts: Documentation, Refusals, and Disposal

The job isn't done until the paperwork is finished and the trash is taken care of safely.

### Documentation and Refusal
Here is a golden rule in nursing: **A nurse must never document the administration of a medication before the patient has actually taken the medication.** That's like telling your teacher you finished reading a book before you even opened it!

But what if a patient says, "No thanks"? Patients are allowed to make their own choices. 
*   **If a patient refuses a medication, the nurse must document the refusal in the medication administration record.**
*   Also, **if a patient refuses a medication, the nurse must notify the prescribing healthcare provider**, so the doctor knows the patient didn't get their medicine.

### Safe Disposal
Finally, what do we do with extra medicine? We don't just toss it in the regular trash. **Safe medication disposal prevents environmental contamination** (keeping medicines out of the water and soil) and **prevents the diversion of controlled substances** (stopping dangerous drugs from getting into the wrong hands).

When you have super strong medicine left over, you have a strict rule to follow. **Wasted controlled substances must be disposed of in a designated receptacle with a second nurse acting as a witness.** 

And what about those medicine skin patches we talked about? Even after they are taken off, they still have strong medicine inside. Because of this, **used transdermal fentanyl patches must be folded in half with the sticky sides together before disposal.** This traps the medicine on the inside so it won't accidentally stick to a pet or a child who might touch the trash!
