# Mastering [Total Parenteral Nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) (TPN)
*An Elite [NCLEX-RN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Council_Licensure_Examination) Study Guide*

Welcome to the [metabolic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metabolism) highway! Let’s think about what we are actually doing when we feed a [patient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patient). Normally, we eat food, and our magnificent [gastrointestinal (GI) tract](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gastrointestinal_tract) breaks it down, filters it, and perfectly meters out the [nutrients](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nutrient) into the [bloodstream](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circulatory_system). But what happens when the gut is broken? What happens when we have to bypass nature's perfectly designed toll booth and dump a massive payload of pure fuel directly into the human [vascular system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circulatory_system)?

That is the magic—and the extreme danger—of **[Total Parenteral Nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) (TPN)**. 

![A prepared prescription bag of total parenteral nutrition containing lipid emulsion, ready for intravenous infusion into the vascular system.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/LipidHPN.jpg)

[Total parenteral nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) provides comprehensive [macronutrients](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macronutrient) and [micronutrients](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Micronutrient) directly into the [venous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vein) bloodstream. It is a brilliant feat of modern [medicine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medicine), because **[total parenteral nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) entirely bypasses the [gastrointestinal tract](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gastrointestinal_tract)**. 

But remember my absolute golden rule of [nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nutrition): *If the gut works, use it!* **[Enteral feeding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enteral_administration) is medically preferred over [total parenteral nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) whenever the [gastrointestinal tract](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gastrointestinal_tract) is functional.** Why? Because using the gut keeps the gut alive. **[Enteral feeding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enteral_administration) preserves [intestinal mucosal integrity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intestinal_mucosal_barrier) better than [total parenteral nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition).** When we don't use the gut, the mucosal barrier [atrophies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atrophy), increasing the risk of [bacterial translocation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacterial_translocation) and [sepsis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sepsis). 

![Physical, biochemical, and immune elements of the intestinal mucosal barrier, which atrophies and loses integrity without the mechanical and chemical stimulation of enteral feeding.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Bischoff_fig_2_adapted_01.png)

So, when do we pull out the big guns and use [TPN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition)? **[Total parenteral nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) is indicated for clients requiring complete [bowel rest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nil_per_os).** You will see this ordered for patients whose guts are either missing, severely inflamed, or requiring massive extra [calories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calorie) that the gut simply cannot process. Specifically, [TPN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) is indicated for clients with **severe [acute pancreatitis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acute_pancreatitis)**, **[short bowel syndrome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Short_bowel_syndrome)**, and **extensive [burn injuries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burn)**.

Let's break down the mechanics, the [chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemistry), and the complications of this incredible therapy.

---

## 1. The Anatomy of the Brew: Chemistry and Handling

[TPN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) is not just a bag of sugar water; it is a highly concentrated, [hypertonic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tonicity) chemical soup tailored exactly to the patient's immediate [metabolic needs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metabolism). Because human chemistry shifts by the hour, **[total parenteral nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) prescriptions are reformulated daily based on the client's morning [laboratory results](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medical_laboratory).** 

### Storage and Inspection
Because this solution is packed with volatile [nutrients](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nutrient), we have strict rules for how we handle it before it ever touches the patient:
*   **Refrigeration:** **[Total parenteral nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) solutions must be [refrigerated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Refrigeration) during storage** to preserve chemical [stability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_stability) and inhibit [bacterial growth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacterial_growth).
*   **The Warm-Up:** You cannot hang it ice cold! **[Total parenteral nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) bags must be removed from the refrigerator 30 to 60 minutes prior to administration.** Why? Because **cold [total parenteral nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) solutions can cause [venous spasm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vasospasm) upon [infusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intravenous_therapy)**, which is terribly painful and restricts [blood flow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hemodynamics).
*   **The Visual Check:** You are the last line of defense. **The [nurse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nurse) must visually inspect [total parenteral nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) bags for [particulate matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particulates) before administration.** 
*   **Lipid Separation:** If the solution contains fats ([lipids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lipid)), **the nurse must inspect [total parenteral nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) [lipid emulsions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lipid_emulsion) for physical separation.** What does this look like? **A separated lipid emulsion exhibits a yellowish oily layer on the fluid surface** (often called "cracking"). If you see this, stop! **[Total parenteral nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) bags exhibiting an oily surface layer must be returned to the [pharmacy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pharmacy) immediately.** Infusing cracked lipids is essentially infusing [fat emboli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fat_embolism) directly into the patient's [heart](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heart).

![A normal 20% lipid emulsion. If physical separation or "cracking" occurs, resulting in a yellowish oily layer on the surface, the bag must be discarded immediately to prevent fat emboli.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/LipidEmulsion.JPG)

> **CRITICAL WARNING:** Because [TPN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) is a high-alert medication precisely tuned to the patient, **two [registered nurses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Registered_nurse) must independently verify the [total parenteral nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) [prescription](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medical_prescription) before initiating the infusion.**

## 2. The Pipeline: Administration Mechanics

You cannot put [TPN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) in a standard [peripheral IV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peripheral_venous_catheter) in the patient's hand. It will destroy the [vessel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_vessel). **[Total parenteral nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) solutions typically contain [dextrose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glucose) concentrations exceeding 10 percent.** This makes the fluid incredibly [hypertonic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tonicity). **The high [osmolarity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osmotic_concentration) of [total parenteral nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) solutions causes chemical [phlebitis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phlebitis) in small [peripheral veins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peripheral_vein).** 

To prevent this, we must deliver the fluid straight into a high-flow, high-volume vessel where it is instantly diluted.
*   **[Total parenteral nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) must be administered through a [central venous catheter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_venous_catheter) (CVC).** 
*   Alternatively, **[total parenteral nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) must be administered through a [peripherally inserted central catheter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peripherally_inserted_central_catheter) (PICC).**
*   *Safety First:* **The nurse must ensure a newly placed central venous catheter has [radiographic (X-ray)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiography) confirmation of tip placement prior to initiating [total parenteral nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition).** Do not hit start until you see the read!

![A chest X-ray confirming the correct placement of a Peripherally Inserted Central Catheter (PICC) tip in the superior vena cava. This radiographic confirmation is mandatory before initiating any TPN infusion.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/PICC_line-correct_position-with_annotation.jpg)

### The VIP Lounge: Dedicated Lines Only
[TPN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) is antisocial. It does not play well with other fluids or [drugs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medication). 
*   **[Total parenteral nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) requires a dedicated [intravenous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intravenous_therapy) [lumen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lumen_%28anatomy%29).** 
*   **[Intravenous push](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intravenous_therapy) medications must never be administered into a dedicated [total parenteral nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) line.**
*   **[Blood products](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_product) must never be [transfused](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_transfusion) through a dedicated [total parenteral nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) line.**

### Control the Flow
Imagine pouring a massive bucket of thick syrup into a garden hose. If you just let gravity take over, disaster strikes. **Administering [total parenteral nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) via [gravity drip](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intravenous_therapy) creates a high risk for [fluid volume overload](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypervolemia)** and a simultaneous **high risk for severe [hyperglycemia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperglycemia)**. 

Therefore, **[total parenteral nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) flow rates must be strictly controlled by an [electronic infusion pump](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infusion_pump).** Never, ever use a gravity roller-clamp to eyeball a [TPN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) rate.

![An electronic infusion pump. TPN flow rates must always be strictly controlled by an infusion pump to prevent severe hyperglycemia or fluid volume overload.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Infusionspumpe.JPG)

### The Filtration Station
To prevent microscopic [precipitates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Precipitation_%28chemistry%29) from entering the patient, we must filter the solution. The type of filter depends entirely on whether [lipids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lipid) ([fat molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fatty_acid)) are in the bag. Lipid molecules are large; if you use the wrong filter, you'll clog the line immediately.

| TPN Composition | Required Inline Filter Size |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Contains [Lipid Emulsions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lipid_emulsion)** | **1.2-[micron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Micrometre) inline filter** |
| **Lacking Lipid Emulsions** | **0.22-micron inline filter** |

To maintain absolute [sterility](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asepsis), **[total parenteral nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) administration tubing must be discarded and replaced every 24 hours**, and similarly, **[total parenteral nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) fluid bags must be discarded and replaced every 24 hours**, even if the bag isn't entirely empty!

## 3. The Four Horsemen of TPN Complications

When you bypass the gut, you take on the responsibility of managing the [side effects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Side_effect). You must anticipate and intercept these four major [complications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complication_%28medicine%29).

### I. The Microbe Buffet (Infection)
[Bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacteria) and [fungi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fungus) absolutely *love* sugar. **High [dextrose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glucose) concentrations in [total parenteral nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) fluids provide an ideal medium for bacterial replication** and an equally **ideal medium for fungal replication.** 

Your defense must be absolute:
*   **[Central venous catheter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_venous_catheter) dressings for [total parenteral nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) require strict [sterile technique](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asepsis) during changes.** 
*   **The nurse must monitor the client's [body temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_body_temperature) frequently to detect [central line-associated bloodstream infections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_line-associated_bloodstream_infection) (CLABSI).** 
*   Locally, **the nurse must assess the central line insertion site for [erythema](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erythema)** and **for [purulent drainage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pus).** 

### II. The Glycemic Rollercoaster (Hyper & Hypoglycemia)
You are dumping pure [glucose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glucose) into the [blood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood). The [pancreas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pancreas) is going to scramble to keep up.
*   **[Blood glucose levels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_sugar_level) must be checked every 4 to 6 hours during [total parenteral nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) therapy.**
*   Don't panic at the first high reading: **[Hyperglycemia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperglycemia) is an expected early complication of [total parenteral nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) therapy.**
*   To manage this, **[regular insulin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_insulin) is frequently added to [total parenteral nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) solutions by the pharmacy.**
*   Additionally, **[subcutaneous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subcutaneous_injection) regular insulin is commonly prescribed on a sliding scale for clients receiving [total parenteral nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition)** to catch any breakthrough spikes.

**The Danger of the Drop:**
If a [TPN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) line gets disconnected or suddenly stops, the pancreas is still pumping out massive amounts of [insulin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Insulin) expecting a huge sugar load. **Abrupt discontinuation of a [total parenteral nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) infusion causes severe [rebound hypoglycemia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reactive_hypoglycemia).** 

*   To prevent this, **[total parenteral nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) infusion rates must be tapered gradually before complete discontinuation.** 
*   If you run out of [TPN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) or the pharmacy is late, you cannot just hang [normal saline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saline_%28medicine%29)! **A 10 percent [dextrose in water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intravenous_sugar_solution) (D10W) infusion must be initiated if a new [total parenteral nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) bag is delayed.**
*   If the patient's sugar crashes, you must spot it early: **The nurse must monitor the client for [diaphoresis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perspiration)** (sweating) and **for [tremors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tremor) as an indicator of [hypoglycemia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypoglycemia).**

### III. Fluid Shifts and Organ Stress
Remember [osmolarity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osmotic_concentration)? Water follows sugar and particles. **Rapid infusion of [hypertonic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tonicity) [total parenteral nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) solutions causes [fluid shifts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluid_compartment) leading to [intravascular fluid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_plasma) [volume overload](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypervolemia).** The fluid is violently pulled from the [tissues](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tissue_%28biology%29) into the [vascular space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_vessel).

![The high osmolarity of TPN creates a hypertonic environment in the bloodstream, driving fluid out of cells and tissues via osmotic flow, which can rapidly result in fluid volume overload.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Blausen_0683_OsmoticFlow_Hypertonic.png)

*   To catch this, **[auscultating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Auscultation) [crackles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crackles) in the [lung bases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Base_of_lung) indicates fluid volume overload during [total parenteral nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) therapy.**
*   **The nurse must obtain the client's [daily weight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_body_weight) at the same time every day during [total parenteral nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) therapy.** Weight is the gold standard for tracking fluid status!
*   **The nurse must maintain strict intake and output records for a client receiving [total parenteral nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition).**

**Organ Monitoring:**
Because we are forcing synthetic [proteins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein_%28nutrient%29) and nutrients into the blood, the [kidneys](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kidney) and [liver](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liver) work in overdrive to clear the waste.
*   **The nurse must monitor [blood urea nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_urea_nitrogen) (BUN) levels** and **[serum creatinine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Creatinine) levels during [total parenteral nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) therapy** to ensure the kidneys are handling the protein load.
*   **Long-term [total parenteral nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) therapy requires routine monitoring of [liver function tests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liver_function_tests) (LFTs).** 
*   Why? Because **[hepatic steatosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fatty_liver_disease) (fatty liver disease) is a recognized complication of long-term [total parenteral nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) therapy.** The liver simply gets overwhelmed processing the constant, unnatural flow of [macronutrients](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macronutrient).

![Micrograph showing hepatic steatosis (fatty liver), a known and serious complication of long-term TPN therapy occurring as the liver becomes overwhelmed processing the continuous, unnatural flow of macronutrients.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Periportal_hepatosteatosis_intermed_mag.jpg)

### IV. The Starvation Shock (Refeeding Syndrome)
This is a high-yield, life-threatening phenomenon! Think of a patient who has been [starving](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Starvation). Their body has adapted to having nothing. If you suddenly flood them with [TPN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition), insulin spikes. That insulin forces glucose into the starved [cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_%28biology%29), but it drags vital [electrolytes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrolyte) in with it, stripping the blood of its circulating supply.

**[Refeeding syndrome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Refeeding_syndrome) occurs when severe [malnutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malnutrition) is treated with rapid nutrient introduction.** This causes **rapid [intracellular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intracellular_fluid) shifts of electrolytes.**

Know these three devastating lab findings:
1.  **Severe [hypophosphatemia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypophosphatemia) is a primary [laboratory finding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medical_laboratory) in refeeding syndrome.** (The cells use all the [phosphate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphate) to make [ATP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate) from the new glucose).
2.  **Severe [hypokalemia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypokalemia) is a life-threatening manifestation of refeeding syndrome.** (Can cause lethal [cardiac dysrhythmias](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arrhythmia)).
3.  **Severe [hypomagnesemia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypomagnesemia) is a classic manifestation of refeeding syndrome.**

> **CLINICAL PEARL:** Refeeding syndrome doesn't happen to the well-fed patient. It happens to the severe [anorexic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anorexia_nervosa), the chronic [alcoholic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alcoholism), or the client who has had zero intake for a week. Start their [TPN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) *slowly* and watch their Phos, K+, and Mg++!

## 4. The Mechanical Nightmare: Air Embolism

When you are changing the tubing on a [central line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_venous_catheter), you open a direct pipeline to the [heart](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heart). The [venous system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venous_system) operates under negative pressure (especially when the patient inhales). **Disconnecting central line tubing during [total parenteral nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) therapy increases the risk of an [air embolism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air_embolism)**, which literally sucks a bubble of air straight into the [right atrium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_atrium).

*   **Sudden [dyspnea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shortness_of_breath) during a [total parenteral nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) tubing change indicates a possible air embolism.**
*   **Sudden [chest pain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chest_pain) during a [total parenteral nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) tubing change indicates a possible air embolism.**

**The Save:** 
If this happens, you must act instantly to trap the air before it reaches the [pulmonary artery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulmonary_artery). 
**A client with a suspected air embolism must be positioned in the left lateral [Trendelenburg position](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trendelenburg_position).**
Why this highly specific contortion? It's pure [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics). **The left lateral Trendelenburg position traps an [air embolus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air_embolism) in the [apex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apex_of_the_heart) of the [right ventricle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_ventricle)**, preventing it from moving forward into the [pulmonary circulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulmonary_circulation) while the body slowly reabsorbs it. 

![A patient positioned in the Trendelenburg position. Placing a client in a left lateral Trendelenburg position is an emergency maneuver to trap a suspected air embolus in the apex of the right ventricle.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Trendelenburg_position.jpg)

## 5. Taking It Home: Patient Education

Many patients survive their [acute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acute_%28medicine%29) crisis but require [TPN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) long-term at home. Education is their lifeline. 

You must empower them with these non-negotiable rules:
1.  **[Infection Control](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infection_control):** **Clients receiving home [total parenteral nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) must be educated to perform meticulous [hand hygiene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hand_washing) before handling equipment.** 
2.  **Storage:** **Clients receiving home [total parenteral nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) must be educated to store nutrition bags in a dedicated [refrigerator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Refrigerator).** Do not put the [sterile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asepsis) [IV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intravenous_therapy) nutrition next to yesterday's leftover chicken.
3.  **Fluid Monitoring:** **Clients receiving home [total parenteral nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) must be educated to report unexpected [weight gain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weight_gain) to the [healthcare provider](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Health_professional).** (Remember, weight gain = [fluid retention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edema) = impending overload).
4.  **Glycemic Awareness:** How will the patient know if their [blood sugar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_sugar_level) is spiking at home? Teach them the classic signs: **Clients must be educated that increased [urination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urination) is an early [symptom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symptom) of [total parenteral nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition)-induced [hyperglycemia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperglycemia)**, and similarly, **clients must be educated that increased [thirst](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thirst) is an early symptom of total parenteral nutrition-induced hyperglycemia.** ([Polyuria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyuria) and [Polydipsia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polydipsia)).

### Final Thoughts
[Total Parenteral Nutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) is not just another [IV fluid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intravenous_therapy). It is artificial [life support](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life_support) for the [metabolic system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metabolism). You must respect the line, control the flow, anticipate the sugar crashes, and guard fiercely against [infection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infection). Master the mechanics, understand the *why* behind the chemistry, and you'll be an incredibly safe, elite [nurse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nurse) for your most critically ill patients. Keep this logical framework in your mind, and you will absolutely dominate this topic on the [NCLEX](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Council_Licensure_Examination)!
