Now, let's talk about the blood sugar rollercoaster. When you dump raw dextrose into the system, the pancreas loses its mind. Blood glucose levels must be checked every 4 to 6 hours during total parenteral nutrition therapy. Don't panic when it spikes on day one; hyperglycemia is an expected early complication of total parenteral nutrition therapy. To manage this drama, regular insulin is frequently added to total parenteral nutrition solutions by the pharmacy, and on the floor, subcutaneous regular insulin is commonly prescribed on a sliding scale for clients receiving total parenteral nutrition. But beware the sudden breakup! If the TPN line gets disconnected or suddenly stops, the pancreas is still aggressively pumping out insulin, expecting a massive sugar payload. Abrupt discontinuation of a total parenteral nutrition infusion causes severe rebound hypoglycemia. To prevent a metabolic crash, total parenteral nutrition infusion rates must be tapered gradually before complete discontinuation. If the current bag runs dry and the pharmacy is dragging their feet on the next one, a 10 percent dextrose in water infusion must be initiated if a new total parenteral nutrition bag is delayed. You have to keep the sugar train moving, and the nurse must monitor the client for diaphoresis, and the nurse must monitor the client for tremors as an indicator of hypoglycemia. 

We also have to watch out for the ultimate kitchen disaster: Refeeding Syndrome. Imagine a starving village that suddenly gets air-dropped a massive feast. Refeeding syndrome occurs when severe malnutrition is treated with rapid nutrient introduction. The starving cells see the insulin and sugar, panic, and violently rip all the electrolytes out of the bloodstream to process the meal. Refeeding syndrome causes rapid intracellular shifts of electrolytes. This leaves the blood completely barren and triggers three lethal lab results: severe hypophosphatemia is a primary laboratory finding in refeeding syndrome, severe hypokalemia is a life-threatening manifestation of refeeding syndrome, and severe hypomagnesemia is a classic manifestation of refeeding syndrome. Start slow, or you'll trigger a fatal arrhythmia!

You also have to watch the plumbing. Remember how dense and hypertonic this fluid is? Rapid infusion of hypertonic total parenteral nutrition solutions causes fluid shifts leading to intravascular fluid volume overload. It pulls water out of the tissues and stuffs it into the veins until they burst. You have to listen closely; auscultating crackles in the lung bases indicates fluid volume overload during total parenteral nutrition therapy. To catch this fluid shift early, the nurse must obtain the client's daily weight at the same time every day during total parenteral nutrition therapy, and the nurse must maintain strict intake and output records for a client receiving total parenteral nutrition. All this synthetic junk is also exhausting for the body's filtration crew. If they are on this stuff for months, long-term total parenteral nutrition therapy requires routine monitoring of liver function tests because the liver gets so overwhelmed trying to process this unnatural sludge that hepatic steatosis is a recognized complication of long-term total parenteral nutrition therapy. 

And let me warn you about the most terrifying moment of your shift: the tubing change. The central venous system is a vacuum. Disconnecting central line tubing during total parenteral nutrition therapy increases the risk of an air embolism. If they take a deep breath while that line is open, they suck a lethal bubble of air straight to their heart. Sudden dyspnea during a total parenteral nutrition tubing change indicates a possible air embolism, and sudden chest pain during a total parenteral nutrition tubing change indicates a possible air embolism. If this happens, you have to fight gravity! A client with a suspected air embolism must be positioned in the left lateral Trendelenburg position. Why? Because the left lateral Trendelenburg position traps an air embolus in the apex of the right ventricle, keeping it away from the pulmonary artery until the body can slowly absorb it.
