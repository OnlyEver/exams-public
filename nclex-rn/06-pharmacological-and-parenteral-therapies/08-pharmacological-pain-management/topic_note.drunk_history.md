Listen to me, Kevin, put the pretzel down and look at me. Managing a patient's pain in the hospital isn't just handing out mints at a hotel front desk. It is exactly like managing a chaotic, high-stakes reality TV show where half the cast is secretly in the mob, the kitchen is on fire, and the bouncers are all corrupt. If you just throw drugs at a crying patient, you're going to accidentally cancel the whole show—and by cancel, I mean kill someone. 

Before we even invite a drug into the building, we have to interrogate the pain like a detective grilling a prime suspect. We conduct a pain assessment, which means we are evaluating the location, the intensity, the quality, the onset, the duration, and exactly what aggravating or alleviating factors make this pain throw a temper tantrum. We need the whole backstory! And human beings are messy, historical creatures. Evaluating a client's prior response to specific analgesics absolutely guides the selection of PRN—"as needed"—pain medications. If a patient tells you that morphine turned them into a violently itchy, vomiting mess on season three of their medical history, you do not cast morphine for season four!

But how do we measure something invisible? If the adult cast member is mentally intact, the numeric rating scale evaluates pain intensity on a scale from 0 to 10. Simple. But you can't ask a four-year-old to quantify their existential agony on a numbered grid; they don't know what a "seven" feels like. So, the Wong-Baker FACES Pain Rating Scale is utilized to assess pain intensity in children over the age of three. It’s a lineup of emotional emoji faces—they just point to the guy who looks exactly as miserable as they feel.

Once we know how bad the drama is, we have to figure out how to get the chemical bouncers into the club. The choice of pain medication route depends entirely on three things: the severity of the pain, the client's cognitive status, and their gastrointestinal function. If the club's front door is jammed—meaning their GI tract is asleep—you can't send a pill through it. But if you do use the front door, remember that the oral route for pain medication administration typically has a slower onset of action compared to the intravenous route. Oral meds have to wait in the digestion line, get frisked by the liver, and take the stairs. Intravenous meds? They get the VIP elevator straight into the bloodstream. But what about tossing the drug through a side window—stabbing it right into the muscle? No! Intramuscular administration of pain medication is avoided due to unpredictable absorption and significant tissue pain. Why beat up a patient who is already crying? 

If the patient just had major surgery, they need constant relief, and we give them the ultimate VIP perk: the patient-controlled analgesia pump. This beautiful piece of machinery allows the client to self-administer predetermined intravenous doses of opioid analgesics by just hitting a button. But Kevin, hear me now, this is a matter of life and death: only the client is authorized to push the button on a patient-controlled analgesia pump. If the patient gets too sedated, they fall asleep, their hand goes limp, and they stop pressing it. It’s a built-in biological safety switch! If their well-meaning, terrified husband starts pushing the button while the patient is sleeping, he will accidentally overdose them. Only the VIP pushes the button!

Now, let's talk about the cast of characters, starting with the non-opioids. We have Acetaminophen. Great guy, cools down fevers, handles mild pain. But he has a strict union contract: the maximum recommended daily dose of acetaminophen for a healthy adult is 4,000 milligrams. Any more than that, and he goes rogue and destroys the liver. In fact, acetaminophen hepatotoxicity risk is increased in clients with pre-existing liver disease, so if the liver is already a dive bar, keep him out! 

Then you have the nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs, the NSAIDs, like ibuprofen. They fight inflammation, but they are incredibly abrasive houseguests. Nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs increase the risk of gastrointestinal bleeding—they literally chew holes in the stomach lining. And because they mess with the plumbing, nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs are contraindicated in clients with severe renal impairment. If the kidneys are failing, NSAIDs will completely shut off the water supply. Oh, and don't forget the godfather of NSAIDs, Aspirin. Aspirin administration is absolutely contraindicated in children with viral infections due to the risk of Reye's syndrome. You give aspirin to a kid with the flu, and it triggers a massive, fatal brain and liver meltdown. Keep the godfather away from the kids!

Sometimes, the pain isn't a bruised muscle; it's the electrical wiring of the house itself sparking and misfiring. That’s nerve pain. Traditional drugs look at that and shrug. So, we call in the repurposed specialists! Adjuvant analgesics such as gabapentin are used to treat neuropathic pain. Gabapentin was supposed to be an anti-seizure guy, but he’s a genius electrician. Similarly, tricyclic antidepressants are utilized as adjuvant medications for the management of chronic neuropathic pain. We take therapists and make them fix the electrical grid!

Now we bring in the heavy mob: the Opioids. These guys go straight into the brain and alter reality. But you have to know who you are dealing with. Opioid-naive clients are at a higher risk for respiratory depression than opioid-tolerant clients. If a patient’s nervous system has never met an opioid before, their brain stem will get so overwhelmed it just forgets to tell the lungs to breathe. Conversely, transdermal fentanyl patches are indicated solely for the management of chronic severe pain in opioid-tolerant clients. Fentanyl is the nuclear option. You slap that patch on a rookie, they won't survive the night. 

Age completely changes the rules of the game, Kevin. Children are not miniature adults; their metabolisms are wild, alien landscapes. Therefore, pediatric dosages for pain medications are primarily calculated based on the child's weight in kilograms. On the other end of the spectrum, older adult clients require lower initial opioid dosages due to decreased renal and hepatic function. Grandma’s biological filtration plants are old and tired. If the liver isn’t breaking the drugs down, decreased hepatic function in older adults prolongs the half-life of many analgesic medications. The drugs just stay in the system, loitering and causing trouble. This is exactly why meperidine is generally avoided in older adults due to the risk of neurotoxicity from normeperidine accumulation. The liver tries to break meperidine down, leaves toxic trash called normeperidine everywhere, and Grandma ends up with tremors and seizures! 

Once you give the drug, you are legally and morally bound to watch the fallout. We are on a strict timer. The effectiveness of intravenous pain medication must be evaluated and documented within 15 to 30 minutes of administration. You gave them the VIP pass, you check on them immediately! But the effectiveness of oral pain medication must be evaluated and documented within 60 minutes of administration, because they had to wait in the digestion line.

When you're monitoring these opioid mobsters, you're watching for the ultimate betrayal: respiratory depression. But here is the biological secret—the lungs don't just quit out of nowhere. The brain gets tired first! Sedation level assessment must precede respiratory rate assessment during opioid administration monitoring. To do this right, the Pasero Opioid-Induced Sedation Scale is used to evaluate the sedation level of clients receiving opioid analgesics. If they are mid-sentence and drifting off to sleep, you are standing on the edge of a cliff. If you ignore that, the breathing crashes. A respiratory rate of fewer than 12 breaths per minute indicates potential opioid-induced respiratory depression in an adult client. 

If they cross that line, you hit the panic button and call the cleaner. Naloxone is the specific pharmacological antagonist used to reverse opioid-induced respiratory depression. Naloxone kicks down the door, violently rips the opioid off the brain's receptors, and saves their life. But there's a catch! Naloxone administration can precipitate immediate withdrawal symptoms in opioid-dependent clients. You rip away the drug they are physically addicted to, and they wake up screaming, sweating, and fighting. Even crazier? Naloxone has a terrible work ethic. The half-life of naloxone is frequently shorter than the half-life of the administered opioid analgesic. Naloxone clocks out and goes home in an hour, but the opioid is still lurking in the bloodstream for four hours! Once Naloxone leaves, the opioid breaks right back into the receptor. Because of this terrifying game of musical chairs, a client may require repeat doses of naloxone due to the return of opioid-induced respiratory depression. (Oh, and just a quick side note for your survival: if the patient isn't dying from opioids, but instead swallowed way too many sedating benzodiazepines, flumazenil is the pharmacological antagonist utilized to reverse the sedative effects of benzodiazepines. Different club, different bouncer.)

Finally, let’s talk about the feds. Schedule II controlled substances have a high potential for abuse and physical or psychological dependence. The government knows these drugs are gold, so we treat them like nuclear launch codes. Controlled substances must be stored in a securely locked and substantially constructed cabinet. You can't just toss them in a drawer. The dispensing of controlled substances requires the utilization of an automated dispensing cabinet or a double-locked medication cart. If you pull a vial of morphine, but you only need half of it, you do not just squirt the rest into the trash while whistling. Two licensed nurses must independently witness and document the wasting of any unused portion of a controlled substance. We watch each other to make sure nobody is stealing the stash. And if you open the vault and the math doesn't math? Discrepancies in controlled substance counts must be reported to nursing management and pharmacy immediately. You don't wait until the end of the shift. You sound the alarms right then and there.

You see, Kevin? It’s not just pills in little plastic cups. It’s anticipating the drama, picking the right bouncer for the right door, protecting the innocent organs, watching the breathing like a hawk, keeping the antidotes locked and loaded, and never taking your eyes off the vault!
