Now, let's talk about the physical plumbing, because physics is a cruel mistress. Venous access gauge size determines the maximum safe flow rate during a blood transfusion. Red blood cells are fat, squishy donuts. If you try to force them through a tiny cocktail straw, what happens? They shred! You will literally puree the donor cells into a bloody smoothie before they even hit the bloodstream! Normal saline (0.9% sodium chloride) is the only intravenous fluid clinically compatible with blood products. It is blood's only friend! If you introduce it to anything else, absolute chaos erupts. Doing that math means the first 15 minutes of a transfusion involves infusing approximately 50 milliliters of blood. It’s a tiny sip, a blind date, a test run. Why? But what if they don't? What if it all goes wrong? You need to recognize the four horsemen of transfusion disasters. 

Horseman number one is the all-out mob war. An acute hemolytic transfusion reaction occurs when client antibodies attack and destroy the transfused donor red blood cells. Billions of cells are exploding in the bloodstream, and the debris is flooding the system. Signs of an acute hemolytic reaction include low back pain—which is actually their kidneys screaming as they choke on the dead cellular debris—tachycardia, hypotension, and hemoglobinuria, which is when their urine turns the color of dark cherry soda from all the spilled blood guts. 

Horseman number two is the hitchhiker drama. A febrile nonhemolytic reaction occurs due to a client immune response against donor white blood cells or platelets that accidentally hitched a ride in the bag. Signs of a febrile nonhemolytic reaction include a sudden temperature increase of 1 degree Celsius, chills, and headache. They aren't destroying the red cells, they're just aggressively bouncing the white cell crashers. 

Horseman number three is the perfume allergy. An allergic transfusion reaction occurs due to client hypersensitivity to plasma proteins present in the donor blood. They are literally allergic to the liquid the cells are swimming in! Signs of a mild allergic transfusion reaction include urticaria—which are hives—pruritus, which is maddening itching, and facial flushing. 

Horseman number four is just terrible plumbing. Transfusion-associated circulatory overload occurs when blood volume is administered faster than the cardiovascular system can accommodate. The pump can't handle the traffic, and the fluid backs up into the lungs. Signs of transfusion-associated circulatory overload include dyspnea, crackles in the lungs, distended neck veins, and hypertension. 

If you see ANY of these signs, you do not pause to think. You hit the emergency brake! The absolute first nursing action upon suspecting any transfusion reaction is to stop the transfusion immediately. Shut it down! But do not just flush the line—that line is full of poison! After stopping a transfusion reaction, the nurse must disconnect the entire blood tubing set at the intravenous catheter hub. Sever the bridge completely! Then, after stopping a transfusion reaction, the nurse must initiate an infusion of normal saline using completely new intravenous tubing to keep the vein open and flush out the patient. Now, because science is amazing, we have VIP upgrades for patients who have had these bad breakups before. Washing red blood cells prior to transfusion reduces the risk of allergic reactions by removing plasma proteins. It’s like rinsing off the toxic ex's perfume so the patient doesn't break out in hives! Consequently, clients with a history of severe allergic transfusion reactions may be prescribed washed red blood cells. To fix the hitchhiker problem, leukocyte reduction of blood products decreases the risk of febrile nonhemolytic transfusion reactions by filtering out the donor's dramatic white blood cells entirely.
