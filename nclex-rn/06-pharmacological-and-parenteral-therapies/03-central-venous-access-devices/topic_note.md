# The Superhighway of the Human Body: Central Venous Access Devices

Welcome! Grab a seat. Today, we are going to talk about plumbing. But not just any plumbing—we are going to talk about the most extraordinary, high-stakes plumbing system in the known universe: the [human circulatory system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circulatory_system). 

Specifically, we’re looking at what happens when the standard [peripheral IV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peripheral_venous_catheter)—that little dirt road of a [vein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vein) in the [hand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hand) or [arm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arm)—just isn’t robust enough for the heavy cargo we need to transport. When a [patient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patient) needs the heavy-duty infrastructure, we bypass the backroads and go straight to the superhighway. We use a **[Central Venous Access Device (CVAD)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_venous_catheter)**.

![A standard peripheral IV is placed in a small superficial vein, making it unsuitable for long-term, highly irritating, or high-volume infusions.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Intravenous_therapy_2007-SEP-13-Singapore.JPG)

Our goal today is to understand not just *what* these devices are, but *why* they behave the way they do, how to maintain them flawlessly, and how to teach your patients to live with them safely. Let’s dive in.

---

## The "Why" and the "Where"

Imagine you need to administer **[Total Parenteral Nutrition (TPN)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition)**. [TPN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition) is essentially a thick, nutrient-dense soup. Because of the extremely high [osmolarity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osmotic_concentration) of this [fluid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluid_replacement), pushing it through a tiny [peripheral vein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superficial_vein) would severely irritate the [vessel walls](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_vessel). We also frequently give **[vesicant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vesicant) [intravenous medications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intravenous_therapy)**—like potent [chemotherapy agents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemotherapy). If a vesicant leaks out of a small vein into surrounding [tissue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tissue_%28biology%29), it causes disastrous [tissue necrosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Necrosis). 

To safely deliver these fluids, we need a massive vessel where the sheer [volume of roaring blood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_volume) instantly dilutes the [medication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medication). That’s why **the internal tip of a central venous access device rests in the lower third of the [superior vena cava](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superior_vena_cava)**. 

But that's not all they do! By tapping directly into this central vessel, **[central venous access devices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_venous_catheter) allow for frequent [venous blood sampling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venipuncture) without repeated [peripheral venipunctures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venipuncture)**. (Just remember: **[central venous access device](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_venous_catheter) [blood sampling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_test) requires discarding the first few [milliliters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Litre) of drawn blood to prevent sample [contamination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contamination)**.) Furthermore, tapping into the superior vena cava means **central venous access devices are utilized to measure [central venous pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_venous_pressure) for [hemodynamic monitoring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hemodynamics)**. 

Ultimately, **the client must understand that the central venous access device provides a reliable route for long-term [intravenous therapy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intravenous_therapy).**

> **CRITICAL RULE:** Because we are flying blind when threading a [catheter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catheter) toward the [heart](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heart), **placement of a newly inserted central venous access device must be verified by a [chest x-ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chest_radiograph) prior to initial use.** You do not push a single drop of fluid until you have photographic proof of where that tip lives!

![A chest radiograph verifying the correct placement of a PICC line, with the catheter tip safely resting in the large-volume superior vena cava.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/PICC_line-correct_position-with_annotation.jpg)

## The Cast of Characters: Types of CVADs

Not all central lines enter the [body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_body) the same way. The journey to the [superior vena cava](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superior_vena_cava) can take a few different routes:

| Type of CVAD | How It Gets There | Key Unique Feature |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **[Peripherally Inserted Central Catheter (PICC)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peripherally_inserted_central_catheter)** | A **central venous access device inserted into a [peripheral vein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superficial_vein)** (usually the [basilic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basilic_vein) or [cephalic vein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cephalic_vein) in the arm), threaded all the way to the heart. | Because the line is in the arm, **[blood pressure cuffs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphygmomanometer) must not be applied to an arm containing a peripherally inserted central catheter**, and **[peripheral venipunctures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venipuncture) must be avoided on an arm containing a [PICC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peripherally_inserted_central_catheter)**. |
| **[Tunneled Central Venous Catheter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_venous_catheter)** | **Tunneled central venous catheters are [surgically](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surgery) placed under the [skin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skin) tissue before entering the [central vein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_venous_catheter).** | The [subcutaneous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subcutaneous_tissue) tunnel creates distance between the outside world and the [bloodstream](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bloodstream), dramatically reducing [infection risk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infection) for long-term use. |
| **[Implanted Vascular Access Port](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Port_%28medical%29)** | **Implanted vascular access ports are surgically placed completely under the skin.** You’ll feel a small, hard bump on the chest. | To use this port, you must puncture the skin. **A non-coring [Huber needle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuohy_needle) is required to access an implanted vascular access port.** Why? **The non-coring Huber needle prevents damage to the self-sealing [septum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Septum) of an implanted vascular access port.** A standard [needle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypodermic_needle) would carve out a tiny chunk of [silicone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicone), ruining the seal! |

![An implanted vascular access port resides entirely under the skin and connects to the central circulation, requiring a specialized non-coring Huber needle for access.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Venous_Access_Port_Catheter.png)

## The Physics of Flushing

Now, let's talk [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics). If you want to be a brilliant [nurse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nurse), you need to understand [pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pressure).

When you flush a [CVAD](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_venous_catheter), **a [syringe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syringe) size of 10 [milliliters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Litre) or larger is used to flush a central venous access device to prevent excessive intraluminal pressure.** Wait, what? Doesn't a bigger syringe mean *more* pressure? 

**NO!** Remember your high school physics: *Pressure = [Force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force) / [Area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area)*. 
Because a smaller syringe has a smaller [plunger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syringe) area, the force your thumb applies is concentrated into a tiny space. Therefore, **smaller syringes generate higher intraluminal pressure than larger syringes when flushing a central venous access device.** If you use a tiny 3 mL syringe, you become a high-pressure [hydraulic pump](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydraulic_pump)! **Excessive pressure from a syringe smaller than 10 milliliters can rupture a central venous access catheter**, leaving pieces of [plastic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plastic) floating in your patient’s bloodstream.

![Pressure is inversely proportional to area. A smaller syringe plunger exerts force over a smaller area, generating dangerously high intraluminal pressure that can easily rupture a catheter line.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Pressure_force_area.svg)

### The Push-Pause Technique
When you flush, you don't just shove the fluid in smoothly. **The push-pause method is used to flush a central venous access device to create fluid [turbulence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turbulence).** By stopping and starting, you create swirling [eddies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eddy_%28fluid_dynamics%29) inside the line. **Fluid turbulence from the push-pause flushing method clears medication residue from the central venous catheter [lumen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lumen_%28anatomy%29)**, ensuring the walls stay clean. **Routine flushing of a central venous access device maintains catheter [patency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patency).**

### What do we flush with?
*   **Valved central venous catheters require only [normal saline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saline_%28medicine%29) for routine flushing.** The mechanical [valve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Valve) keeps [blood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood) from backing up.
*   **Open-ended central venous access devices require a [heparin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heparin) flush solution to prevent [thrombosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thrombosis).** Because the end is wide open to the bloodstream, heparin is needed to stop a [clot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thrombus) from forming at the tip. 
    *   *A word of warning:* **[Heparin-induced thrombocytopenia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heparin-induced_thrombocytopenia) is a potential [systemic complication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Systemic_disease) of using heparin flush solutions in central venous access devices.** Always monitor your patient's [platelet counts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platelet_count)!

### Occlusions: When the Plumbing Backs Up
What if you try to flush, and you can't? Or what if you try to draw blood, and nothing comes back? **The inability to aspirate blood from a central venous access device indicates potential catheter occlusion**, and **resistance met during flushing of a central venous access device indicates a possible internal [occlusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vascular_occlusion).**

Never, *ever* force it. Let me repeat that: **Forcing fluid against resistance in a central venous access device can mechanically rupture the catheter**, or worse, **forcing fluid against resistance in a central venous access device can dislodge a [thrombus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thrombus) into the bloodstream**, sending a clot directly to the [lungs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lung). Instead, specialized **[thrombolytic agents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thrombolytic_drug) are administered into a central venous access device to dissolve a [blood clot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thrombus) within the catheter lumen.**

![When blood stagnates or backs up in a line, a thrombus can form. Forcing a flush against resistance can dislodge this clot directly into the patient's bloodstream.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Thrombosis_formation.gif)

## The Art of the Dressing Change

A central line is a direct, unprotected superhighway for [bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacteria) to travel straight to the [heart](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heart). [Infection control](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infection_control) here isn't just policy; it is life and death.

When performing site care, **[aseptic technique](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aseptic_technique) using a [chlorhexidine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chlorhexidine)-based preparation is required for central venous access device [skin cleansing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiseptic).** To further protect the site, **an [antimicrobial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antimicrobial) patch is often placed around the central venous access device insertion site to reduce [infection risk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infection).**

### The Timetable for Changes
*   **Transparent [semipermeable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semipermeable_membrane) dressings over a central venous access device site are routinely changed every 5 to 7 [days](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Day).**
*   **[Gauze](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauze) dressings over a central venous access device site must be routinely changed every 48 [hours](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hour).** 
*   **Exceptions to the rule:** You don't wait for the calendar if the integrity of the dressing fails. **A central venous access device dressing must be changed immediately if the dressing becomes damp**, **if the dressing becomes loose**, or **if the dressing becomes visibly soiled.**

### The Choreography of the Sterile Field
During a dressing change, everyone in the room has a job:
1.  **The clinician must wear a [mask](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surgical_mask) during a central venous access device dressing change.**
2.  **The client must wear a mask during a central venous access device dressing change.**
3.  **Instructing the client to turn the [head](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Head) away from the insertion site during a central venous access device dressing change prevents site [contamination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contamination)** (nobody wants [respiratory droplets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Respiratory_droplet) on a direct line to the heart!).
4.  **[Sterile gloves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medical_glove) must be worn by the clinician during a central venous access device dressing change.**

### Maintaining the Hardware
The caps on the end of the line are high-touch areas. **Needleless connectors on central venous access devices are typically changed every 72 to 96 hours.** However, **needleless connectors on central venous access devices are changed immediately if blood is present within the connector** (blood is a prime breeding ground for bacteria). 

Whenever you access the line, **the injection cap of a central venous access device must be scrubbed with an [antiseptic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiseptic) for at least 15 [seconds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second) before access.** [Friction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friction) is your friend here! Scrub the hub!

## When Things Go Sideways: Recognizing Complications

You must act like a [detective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Detective) at the [bedside](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bedside_manner). Central lines can cause catastrophic [complications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complication_%28medicine%29), and you need to spot them instantly.

### 1. The Air Embolism
If air gets into the line, it travels to the [right ventricle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_ventricle) and blocks [blood flow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hemodynamics) to the [lungs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lung). This is a profound [emergency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medical_emergency). 
*   *Prevention:* **Placing the client in the [Trendelenburg position](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trendelenburg_position) during central venous catheter insertion increases [venous pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venous_pressure) to prevent [air embolism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air_embolism).** 
*   *During Tubing Changes:* **The client is instructed to perform the [Valsalva maneuver](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Valsalva_maneuver) during tubing changes on a central venous access device.** Why? Because **the Valsalva maneuver increases intrathoracic pressure to prevent air from entering the central venous access device.** Also, **clamping the central venous catheter [extension tubing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tubing_%28material%29) prevents air from entering the [circulatory system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circulatory_system) when the end cap is removed.**
*   *Emergency Response:* If an air embolism occurs, you must rely on [gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity). **A suspected air embolism requires immediately placing the client in the [left lateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Left_lateral_decubitus_position) [Trendelenburg position](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trendelenburg_position).** Why left lateral? **The left lateral Trendelenburg position traps an air embolism in the [apex of the right ventricle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_ventricle), keeping it away from the [pulmonary valve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulmonary_valve) until it can be absorbed or removed!**

![The Trendelenburg position (head lowered) combined with a left lateral tilt uses gravity to trap a life-threatening air embolism in the right ventricle, preventing it from blocking pulmonary blood flow.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Trendelenburg_position.jpg)

> **[Pneumothorax](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pneumothorax) Alert:** The [apex of the lung](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apex_of_lung) sits right under the [collarbone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clavicle). Therefore, **[shortness of breath](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shortness_of_breath) following insertion of a [subclavian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subclavian_vein) central venous catheter indicates a potential pneumothorax**, and **a sudden drop in [oxygen saturation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen_saturation_%28medicine%29) following subclavian central venous catheter insertion suggests a pneumothorax complication.** Check those [lung sounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Respiratory_sounds)!

![A CT scan revealing a right-sided pneumothorax (visible as the dark area on the left of the image). This is a critical complication that can occur if the pleural space is inadvertently punctured during a subclavian central line insertion.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Pneumothorax_CT.jpg)

### 2. Infections (Local vs. Systemic)
You and the patient must watch the insertion site like a hawk. **The central venous access device insertion site must be assessed daily for signs of [redness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erythema)** and **assessed daily for signs of [swelling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edema).**
*   **[Purulent drainage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pus) at the insertion site indicates a localized [infection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infection) of the central venous access device.**
*   If the [bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacteria) hit the bloodstream, it's [systemic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Systemic_disease). **[Fever](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fever) in a client with a central venous access device indicates a potential [central line-associated bloodstream infection (CLABSI)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_line-associated_bloodstream_infection).** Similarly, **[chills](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chills) in a client with a central venous access device indicate a potential central line-associated bloodstream infection.**

### 3. Catheter Migration
Catheters are flexible; sometimes, they take a wrong turn or back out. 
*   **[Neck pain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neck_pain) during [infusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intravenous_therapy) through a peripherally inserted central catheter indicates potential upward catheter migration** into the [jugular vein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jugular_vein). 
*   Likewise, **[ear pain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ear_pain) during infusion through a peripherally inserted central catheter indicates potential upward catheter migration.**
*   If you suspect the line has moved, STOP. **A displaced central venous catheter tip requires immediate notification of the [healthcare provider](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Health_professional) before any fluids are infused.**

## Empowering the Client: Home Care and Education

Many clients go home with these devices. It is your job to turn them into [experts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expert) on their own bodies.

You must sit down with them and explain the ground rules:
*   **Waterproof the line:** **Clients must be taught to completely cover the central venous access device dressing with a [waterproof](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Waterproofing) material during showering.** No baths or swimming!
*   **Anchor the line:** **Clients must be educated to secure central venous access device extension tubing to clothing to prevent accidental dislodgement.** A simple [safety pin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Safety_pin) or [tape](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adhesive_tape) loop saves a trip to the [emergency room](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emergency_department).
*   **When to ring the alarm:** They need to know what abnormal feels like. **Clients must be instructed to report immediate [pain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pain) at the central venous access device insertion site to a healthcare provider.** They also **must be instructed to report [swelling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edema) at the central venous access device insertion site**, and **must be instructed to report a loose central venous access device dressing to a healthcare provider.** 

[Central Venous Access Devices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_venous_catheter) are miracles of modern [medicine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medicine). They provide a safe harbor for the harshest medications and the gentlest continuous therapies. By respecting the [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics), fiercely guarding the [aseptic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asepsis) field, and educating your patients, you keep that vital superhighway open, safe, and flowing perfectly.
