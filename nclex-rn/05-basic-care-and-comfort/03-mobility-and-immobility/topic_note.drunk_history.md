Listen to me, buddy. Sit down, take a sip of that ginger ale, and look at me. The human body is like a massive, highly illegal mob operation. It only survives if everyone is moving, hustling, and constantly fighting gravity to collect the debts. The second you put a patient on bed rest—the second immobility enters the chat—the entire organization goes feral. It’s a reality TV disaster wrapped in a turf war. 

Take the cardiovascular plumbing, for example. The blood in your legs? Those are your low-level enforcers. They need a swift kick to get back up to the big boss, the heart. Normally, walking does that. But immobility reduces skeletal muscle contractions. Without that muscle hustle cracking the whip, you've got a massive management problem, because reduced skeletal muscle contractions decrease venous return to the heart. So what happens? They get lazy. Decreased venous return causes blood pooling in the lower extremities. And when mobsters hang out doing nothing, they form a dangerous gang. This blood pooling in the lower extremities increases the risk of deep vein thrombosis. A DVT! A literal clot mob! And if they get spooked? A deep vein thrombosis can dislodge and travel to the lungs. Boom. The ultimate assassination, because a dislodged deep vein thrombosis traveling to the lungs causes a pulmonary embolism. 

So how do we break up the mob? We use gravity! Elevating the lower extremities above the level of the heart uses gravity to increase venous return. We make it downhill! Or we force them to work. Ankle pump exercises engage the calf muscles to push venous blood upward toward the heart. If they won't pump themselves, we put them in the inflatable mob-busters. Sequential compression devices pump air into sleeves to promote venous return from the lower extremities. By constantly squeezing them, sequential compression devices help prevent deep vein thrombosis. But you can't just leave them in the squeeze box forever! Sequential compression devices must be removed periodically to assess the underlying skin for breakdown. Then you've got the tight pantyhose of justice. Anti-embolic stockings apply external mechanical pressure to the legs to promote venous return. But they are custom suits! Anti-embolic stockings must be measured accurately using the circumference and length of the leg. And listen to me—never, ever roll them down! Rolling the top band of anti-embolic stockings downward creates a localized tourniquet effect. It's a trap! The tourniquet effect from rolled anti-embolic stockings impedes venous blood flow back to the heart. It literally chokes the operation! And if you think they already formed a DVT gang? Hands off! Massaging the calves of an immobile patient can dislodge an existing deep vein thrombosis. Don't touch it! How do you know if you're winning this turf war? The nurse evaluates the response to circulation interventions by checking for the absence of peripheral pitting edema. No swelling means no loitering!

Oh, and the baroreceptors? They are the body’s security cameras in the neck, monitoring blood pressure. Prolonged bed rest reduces the baroreceptor reflex response. They literally fall asleep on the monitors! So when your patient finally stands up, a reduced baroreceptor reflex response causes orthostatic hypotension upon standing. They will drop like a sack of potatoes because orthostatic hypotension is a drop in systolic blood pressure of at least 20 millimeters of mercury when moving from a lying to a standing position. 

Now look at the lungs. The lungs are a high-end bakery with massive bellows. They need to open wide to keep the fires burning. But immobility decreases respiratory chest expansion. You're lying on the bellows! Decreased respiratory chest expansion increases the risk of atelectasis. What's atelectasis? Atelectasis is the collapse of lung alveoli! The little dough balls deflate and stick together into a useless wad! And worse? Decreased respiratory chest expansion causes pooling of respiratory secretions in the lungs. It turns the bakery into a stagnant, nasty swamp, and pooled respiratory secretions increase the risk of hypostatic pneumonia. Gross! To save the bakery, we prop them up. Positioning an immobile patient in a semi-Fowler position facilitates maximum chest cavity expansion. Get off the bellows! Then we give them a plastic party horn. Incentive spirometry promotes sustained deep breathing. That negative pressure from deep breathing via incentive spirometry inflates the alveoli to prevent atelectasis in immobile patients. Pop those balloons open! And water! Adequate oral fluid intake thins respiratory secretions in immobile patients. Why? Because thinner respiratory secretions are easier for an immobile patient to expectorate through coughing. Cough up the swamp water! Getting them up helps everything; early ambulation accelerates the return of normal gastrointestinal and respiratory function. 

Speaking of the gastrointestinal system, when the body stops moving, the internal factories go on a reality-TV-level strike. Immobility decreases gastrointestinal peristalsis. The gut just stops filming, and decreased gastrointestinal peristalsis increases the risk of constipation. Nobody is leaving the house! And the kidneys? Immobility causes urinary stasis in the renal pelvis. It's a stagnant puddle of toxic waste, and urinary stasis increases the risk of urinary tract infections. Then the accountants get involved. The bones! The body says, "Hey, we aren't walking. Why pay for strong bones?" Immobility alters bone metabolism. This betrayal means altered bone metabolism from immobility causes calcium to leach out of the bones. And calcium leaving the bones leads to disuse osteoporosis. The scaffolding is crumbling! Where does that calcium go? Into the blood! Increased serum calcium from bone breakdown increases the risk of renal calculi. Kidney stones! Tiny daggers in the plumbing! Meanwhile, the muscles are dissolving like a messy celebrity divorce. Nitrogen balance becomes negative during immobility due to extensive muscle tissue breakdown. And a negative nitrogen balance delays physiological wound healing. The body can't build anything! To stop the hemorrhage, increased dietary protein intake is required to correct a negative nitrogen balance associated with immobility. Feed them steak!

Now let's talk about the skin. Prolonged physical pressure on tissues reduces local blood flow. It's an eviction notice for oxygen! Reduced local blood flow leads to tissue ischemia. And unrelieved tissue ischemia causes pressure injuries. Dead flesh! We grade this catastrophic risk with a VIP list. The Braden Scale assesses the risk of a patient developing a pressure injury. It's a reality show casting sheet: The Braden Scale evaluates sensory perception, moisture, activity, mobility, nutrition, and friction. And in this twisted game, a lower numerical score on the Braden Scale indicates a higher risk for pressure injuries. You gotta check the bony landmarks where the skeleton tries to stab through the mattress. Skin assessments must include thorough inspection of bony prominences. The prime real estate for destruction? Common bony prominences susceptible to pressure injuries include the sacrum, heels, elbows, and occiput. If you push on a red spot and it stays red like an angry sunburn? Non-blanchable erythema of intact skin indicates a Stage 1 pressure injury. The turf war has begun! To stop the ischemia, repositioning an immobile patient relieves mechanical pressure on bony prominences. Immobile patients lying in a bed must be repositioned at least every two hours. If they are in the VIP chair, immobile patients sitting in a chair must shift body weight at least every fifteen minutes. 

But pressure isn't the only killer. Meet shear, the undercover assassin. Shearing forces occur when the epidermis remains stationary against a surface while the underlying tissues shift. It's like your bedsheets holding your skin hostage while your heavy skeleton slides down the mattress! Shearing forces physically tear blood vessels in the underlying subcutaneous tissue. Because gravity is a jerk, elevating the head of the bed greater than 30 degrees increases the risk of a shearing injury on the sacrum. Keep them flatter or they'll tear! And if they have a broken neck? A cervical collar must maintain the neck in a neutral anatomical position to prevent further spinal cord injury. But that tight plastic collar is a death trap for the skin underneath, so the nurse must regularly assess the skin directly under a cervical collar for signs of pressure injuries.

Let’s talk joints. If you don't move them, they lock up in a bitter, permanent divorce. Frequent range of motion exercises prevent joint contractures. What's a contracture? Joint contractures result from the permanent shortening of muscle fibers and connective tissue. The body superglues the hinge shut! To stop it, you force the reunion. Active range of motion exercises require the patient to independently move a joint through the full extent of movement. Because they do the work, active range of motion exercises maintain existing muscle strength. If they are completely paralyzed? Passive range of motion exercises require the nurse to manually move the joints of the patient. This is just life support for the joint, because passive range of motion exercises prevent contractures without maintaining muscle strength. If you don't use props, the joints will warp! Look at the ankles. Foot drop is a permanent joint contracture of the ankle. It literally fixes the foot in a state of plantar flexion, like they are permanently stepping on a gas pedal! To fix it, a footboard prevents foot drop by maintaining the foot in continuous dorsiflexion. The hips? They love to flop outward. Trochanter rolls prevent external rotation of the hips in a supine patient. We roll up a towel to keep the femurs pointing up! And if you leave them sitting in a chair too long? Prolonged hip flexion in a seated position increases the risk of hip flexion contractures. They will literally be stuck in a sitting shape forever! If they just had a hip replaced? An abduction pillow placed between the legs prevents hip adduction. You spread those legs wide, because preventing hip adduction is necessary to prevent joint dislocation after a total hip arthroplasty. Pop! And the hands! Without intervention, the fingers curl into a furious, useless claw. Hand rolls maintain the thumb in slight adduction and in opposition to the fingers. Hand rolls prevent flexion contractures of the fingers!

Finally, let's talk about the heavy artillery. Casts and traction. Orthopedic casts immobilize a specific body part to facilitate bone healing. But it's a concrete prison! If the tissue swells, there's nowhere to go! An excessively tight orthopedic cast impairs arterial circulation to the distal extremity. It chokes off the blood! And an excessively tight orthopedic cast compresses peripheral nerves in the affected extremity. If that pressure builds up too high inside the muscle envelope? Boom! Compartment syndrome is a surgical emergency caused by increased pressure within a confined anatomical muscle space. And what causes it? Tight orthopedic casts and restrictive dressings can cause compartment syndrome. How do you spot this ticking time bomb? Pain! Severe pain unrelieved by opioid medication is an early clinical sign of compartment syndrome. If the morphine doesn't touch it, the muscle is dying! Nerves scream first, so paresthesia is an early clinical sign of peripheral nerve compression from an orthopedic device. Tingling! You must interrogate the limb! The nurse evaluates nerve function distal to an orthopedic cast by assessing tactile sensation and motor movement. Check the blood supply! Pallor distal to an orthopedic device indicates decreased arterial blood flow. Ghost white! Worse? Cyanosis distal to an orthopedic device indicates severe localized tissue hypoxia. Blue means death! You must check the plumbing: the nurse evaluates circulation distal to an orthopedic device by assessing the strength of peripheral pulses. And the fingertips! The nurse evaluates circulation distal to an orthopedic cast by assessing capillary refill time. If it takes too long? A capillary refill time greater than three seconds indicates impaired tissue perfusion.

If a cast isn't enough, we string them up like a medieval rack! Skin traction applies a pulling force directly to the skin using tape or a specialized boot. But if the bones are totally shattered? Skeletal traction applies a pulling force directly to the bone using surgically implanted pins or wires. Literal metal through the bone! For this physics experiment to work, the physical weights in a therapeutic traction system must hang freely without touching the floor. If they hit the ground, the magic is gone, because weights resting on the floor in a traction system completely eliminate the intended pulling force on the patient. And listen to me—this is a cardinal sin—a nurse must never remove the weights from a skeletal traction system! Never! Because there is metal sticking out of their leg, the nurse must assess the pin insertion sites of skeletal traction for signs of localized infection. It's a highway for bacteria! Initially, clear serous fluid drainage from a skeletal traction pin site is a normal expected finding in the first 72 hours. Just weeping. But if it turns into gross mustard? Purulent yellow drainage from a skeletal traction pin site indicates an active infection. And lastly, the robot leg! A continuous passive motion machine repeatedly moves a target joint through a prescribed range of motion. It's a relentless robotic physical therapist! It's there because a continuous passive motion machine prevents joint stiffness after joint replacement surgery. But the robot doesn't have eyes! The nurse must ensure the anatomical joints of the patient align correctly with the mechanical joints of the continuous passive motion machine. If the hinges don't match, you're just breaking their leg all over again! Keep the mob moving, buddy, or the whole system burns to the ground.
