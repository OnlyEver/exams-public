## 1. The Gauges: Evaluating Nutritional and Hydration Status

If you want to know how much fuel is in a car, you check the dashboard gauges. In nursing, we check the body's numbers and look for clues.

### The Macroscopic View: Weight and Skin
First, we look at the big picture: the person's size. We use a math score called the body mass index (BMI). 
*   **A body mass index between 18.5 and 24.9 is considered normal weight.** 
*   **A body mass index below 18.5 indicates underweight status.**

But here is the trick: a number by itself doesn't tell us if things are changing too fast. A person might have a normal score today, but they are losing points rapidly! That is why **unintentional weight loss greater than 5 percent in one month indicates severe nutritional risk.** It means the body is so hungry, it's starting to use up its own backup energy.

Next, we look at water levels. You don't need fancy tools; just look at the skin and the bathroom visits!
*   **Decreased skin turgor with tenting indicates fluid volume deficit.** If you pinch the skin on the back of your hand and it stays up like a little tent, the body's cells are super thirsty!
*   **Dark concentrated urine indicates poor hydration status.** When you don't drink enough water during sports, your kidneys hold onto every drop they can, making your bathroom trips look really dark.
*   We can measure this exactly. **Normal urine specific gravity ranges from 1.005 to 1.030.** If the number is higher than 1.030, the body is crying out for a drink!

### The Microscopic View: The Protein Biomarkers
Now let's look at the blood. We need to measure protein, which is like the Lego bricks the body uses to build muscles.
*   **Normal serum albumin levels range from 3.5 to 5.0 g per dL.** Albumin is a protein that stays in the blood a long time. It tells us how the person was eating a few weeks ago.
*   But what if we want to know what's happening right this very minute? **Serum prealbumin levels indicate short-term changes in nutritional status due to a short half-life** (it only lasts a couple of days!). **Normal serum prealbumin levels range from 15 to 36 mg per dL.** If this number drops, it means the person isn't getting enough protein right now.

## 2. The Gateway: Swallowing and Dysphagia

Swallowing food is like a perfectly timed video game combo. Muscles and nerves have to work together perfectly. But what happens when the combo fails? We call this dysphagia (trouble swallowing).

Imagine trying to swallow and your muscles just forget what to do, so the food just sits in your mouth. **Pocketing food in the cheeks indicates impaired swallowing ability.** As nurses, we see this happening, but we don't guess why it's broken. **A speech-language pathologist evaluates the exact mechanism of dysphagia.** They are the experts who figure out which muscle or nerve is struggling.

When someone can't swallow safely, regular watery drinks are dangerous because they splash too fast and can accidentally go into the lungs instead of the stomach! To fix this, **patients with dysphagia require thickened liquids to prevent aspiration.** We make their drinks thicker, almost like a milkshake, so they go down the right pipe nice and slow.

## 3. Dietary Textures: The Mechanics of Eating

Sometimes a person's stomach works just fine, but they don't have the teeth or jaw strength to chew. We have to make the food easier to handle.

| Diet Type | The Rule | What It Includes |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Clear Liquid** | If you can hold it up and see light through it, it counts! | **A clear liquid diet includes water**, **plain tea**, **black coffee**, **clear broths**, and **plain gelatin** (like Jell-O!). |
| **Full Liquid** | Drinks that are thicker and you can't see through. | **A full liquid diet includes milk**, **yogurt**, and **pureed soups**. |
| **Pureed** | The blender does the chewing for you. | **A pureed diet provides food blended to a smooth consistency for patients with severe chewing difficulties.** (Think of it like baby food). |
| **Mechanical Soft** | Very soft food that is super easy to mash up. | **A mechanical soft diet includes easily chewed foods for patients with missing teeth.** |

## 4. Therapeutic Diets: Fixing the Chemistry

When the body's organs get sick, the chemistry goes crazy. We can use food as medicine by taking away things the sick organs can't handle anymore.

### The Failing Filter (Renal Failure)
Your kidneys are like coffee filters that clean your blood. When they break, they can't filter out salts or waste. 
*   To help out, **a renal diet restricts dietary sodium**, **restricts dietary potassium**, and **restricts dietary phosphorus**. 
*   Also, **end-stage renal disease requires a low-protein diet to decrease blood urea nitrogen accumulation.** (When you eat protein, it leaves behind a trash called blood urea nitrogen. A broken kidney filter can't clean up that trash!).

### The Struggling Pump (Cardiac Disease)
To protect the heart (the body's pump) and the blood vessels (the pipes):
*   **A cardiac diet restricts dietary sodium**, **restricts dietary saturated fats**, and **restricts dietary cholesterol.** This keeps the pipes clean and stops the body from swelling up like a water balloon.

### The Glucose Rollercoaster (Diabetes)
Having diabetes means your body struggles to handle sugar. It's not about never eating sugar again; it's about keeping the energy smooth.
*   **A diabetic diet focuses on consistent carbohydrate intake throughout the day.** You want a steady, even flow of fuel, not a crazy rollercoaster of highs and lows!

### The Autoimmune Intestine (Celiac Disease)
For some people, a protein found in certain plants acts like a poison to their tummy. 
*   **Patients with celiac disease must maintain a lifelong gluten-free diet.** 
*   What does that actually mean? **A gluten-free diet strictly eliminates wheat**, **strictly eliminates barley**, and **strictly eliminates rye.** No regular pizza crusts or sandwich bread for life!

### Bowel Management (Fiber vs. Rest)
*   Need help going to the bathroom? **A high-fiber diet prevents constipation by increasing stool bulk.** It makes everything bulky so it moves easily!
*   Is your tummy sick and needs a break? **A low-residue diet restricts raw fruits**, **restricts raw vegetables**, and **restricts whole grains.** This gives your intestines less work to do.

## 5. Cultural and Religious Dietary Laws

You can't take care of a person without respecting what they believe. A person's diet is tied to their family, culture, and religion. It's like respecting the house rules when you play a board game at a friend's house. You must know these rules:

*   **Judaism:** **Kosher dietary laws prohibit combining meat and dairy in the same meal.** (No cheeseburgers!). Also, **Kosher dietary laws prohibit the consumption of pork**, and **prohibit the consumption of shellfish** (like shrimp or crab).
*   **Islam:** **Halal dietary laws prohibit the consumption of pork**, and **prohibit the consumption of alcohol.**
*   **Hinduism:** Because of a deep respect for all animals, **many practicing Hindus follow a vegetarian diet.**
*   **Mormonism (Latter-Day Saints):** **The Mormon religion restricts the consumption of alcohol**, **restricts the consumption of coffee**, and **restricts the consumption of tea.**
*   **Seventh-Day Adventists:** **Seventh-Day Adventists typically follow a lacto-ovo vegetarian diet** (they will eat dairy and eggs, but no meat). Also, **Seventh-Day Adventists strictly avoid pork**, and **strictly avoid alcohol.**

## 6. Enteral Nutrition: The Tube Feedings

What if a patient's tummy works great, but they are asleep in a coma and can't eat? We can slide a tiny, soft tube down their nose straight into their stomach! It's super cool, but we have to follow strict rules to keep them safe.

### Step 1: Verification (Where are we?)
You just slid a tube down a patient's nose without looking. Is it in their stomach, or did it accidentally go into their lungs? You cannot guess! 
> **A chest radiograph provides the only definitive verification of initial enteral tube placement.** (A radiograph is just a fancy word for an X-ray picture).

Once we know it's in the right spot, how do we check it the next day? We pull back a little stomach juice and check how sour (acidic) it is. **Gastric aspirate pH verification helps confirm ongoing enteral feeding tube placement.** The stomach is super acidic, so **gastric aspirate pH is typically between 1.0 and 4.0 in a fasting patient.**

### Step 2: Administration and Gravity
Liquid always flows downhill. If a patient is lying flat on their back, the liquid food could slide up their throat and into their lungs!
*   **The head of the bed must remain elevated at least 30 degrees during continuous enteral feeding.** (Propped up like they are watching TV).
*   If you give them a quick meal (a bolus), **the head of the bed must remain elevated for 30 to 60 minutes after intermittent enteral feeding.**

### Step 3: Tube Maintenance and Medication
Tubes get sticky and clogged easily. To keep them clean like a freshly rinsed straw:
*   **Enteral feeding tubes must be flushed with water before medication administration.**
*   **Enteral feeding tubes must be flushed with water after medication administration.**

### Step 4: Measuring the Backlog (Gastric Residuals)
Imagine pouring water into a funnel. If you pour faster than it drains, the funnel overflows! In a patient, we have to make sure the stomach is actually emptying into the intestines. **Gastric residual volumes should be checked every 4 to 6 hours during continuous enteral feeding.** 

If you pull back a ton of leftover food, the stomach is backed up. **High gastric residual volumes indicate delayed gastric emptying.** What do we do? 
*   **Enteral feedings are typically withheld if the gastric residual volume exceeds 500 milliliters.** (Stop pouring!).
*   *Super Important Rule:* Don't throw those leftovers in the sink! **Aspirated gastric residual volume must be returned to the patient stomach to prevent electrolyte imbalance.** The body worked hard to put special salts and juices into that liquid; throwing it away is like throwing away their health points!

### Step 5: Infection Control
Bacteria love liquid food just as much as we do. We have to keep things super clean!
*   **Enteral feeding bags and tubing must be replaced every 24 hours to prevent bacterial contamination.** 
*   Just like milk at home, **liquid enteral feeding formulas must be stored in the refrigerator after opening.**

## 7. The Balance Sheet: Intake and Output (I&O)

Finally, nurses have to be the money-managers of the human body. Think of water like an allowance. We have to track every drop that goes in and every drop that comes out!

### The Math (Conversions)
We have to turn normal kitchen measurements into scientific "milliliters" (mL). Here are the facts you must know:
*   **One fluid ounce equals 30 milliliters.**
*   **One cup equals 8 fluid ounces.**
*   **One cup equals 240 milliliters.**

Let's do the step-by-step math to prove why one cup is 240 milliliters:
Step 1: Write down how many ounces are in one cup (8 fluid ounces).
Step 2: Write down how many milliliters are in one single ounce (30 milliliters).
Step 3: Multiply the ounces by the milliliters (8 x 30 = 240).
Step 4: Your answer is 240 milliliters!

*The Ice Rule:* When a cup of solid ice melts, it shrinks down because it was full of air! So, **ice chips are recorded as half of their total volume when calculating liquid intake.** 
Let's do the step-by-step math for ice:
Step 1: Find out how much ice the patient ate (Let's say a cup measuring 100 milliliters of ice).
Step 2: Remember the rule that ice counts as exactly half.
Step 3: Divide the starting number by 2 (100 / 2 = 50).
Step 4: The patient's actual liquid intake is 50 milliliters!

### The Inflow (Intake)
Everything that is liquid at room temperature going into the body counts. 
*   **Fluid intake calculations must include all liquids consumed orally** (drinking through the mouth).
*   **Fluid intake calculations must include all liquids administered enterally** (tube feedings and water flushes).
*   **Fluid intake calculations must include all fluids administered intravenously** (IV bags going into the veins).

### The Outflow (Output)
Where does it all go? We have to measure the mess!
*   **Fluid output calculations must include urine volume** (pee).
*   **Fluid output calculations must include liquid stool volume** (diarrhea).
*   **Fluid output calculations must include emesis volume** (throw up).
*   **Fluid output calculations must include drainage tube output** (fluid pulled out of wounds).

The most important number of all is the urine output. It proves the kidney filters have enough water pressure to stay alive!
> **Minimum normal urine output for an adult is 30 milliliters per hour.** 

If the patient pees less than 30 milliliters in an hour, warning alarms should go off in your head! The kidneys are thirsty, and it's time to help!
