Listen to me, put the peanuts down and look me in the eye, because you just passed your boards and you think you are just a nurse now. You are not. You are a mob boss running a Michelin-star kitchen that is actively being filmed for a reality TV show, and the moment you step onto that hospital floor, you have to orchestrate chaos without losing your license. 

You cannot cook every dish yourself. You have to use your crew. But you have to know the difference between handing out stations and handing over your soul. 

Let's get the absolute basics straight. Every morning, you have a huddle, right? You say, "Tony, you take rooms one through four, Maria, you take five through eight." That is an assignment. Assignment is simply the routine distribution of client care responsibilities among staff members for a specific work period. It’s just dealing the cards. 

But delegation? Delegation is a blood oath. Delegation is the process of transferring the authority to perform a specific task to another team member. You are looking your sous-chef in the eye and saying, "I have the power to chop this pufferfish, but I am giving you the authority to do it for me." But here is the terrifying, keep-you-up-at-night reality: the registered nurse retains ultimate accountability for the outcome of any delegated nursing task. If Tony chops the pufferfish wrong and poisons the VIP at table four, the food critic doesn't write a bad review about Tony. They write it about YOU. Your license is on the chopping block!

Because your neck is on the line, you cannot just fire and forget. You must engage in supervision. Supervision involves providing guidance to a nursing team member during the performance of a delegated task. You have to keep an eye on them while they whisk the sauce! And it doesn't stop there. Supervision requires the registered nurse to evaluate the outcome of a task after completion by a team member. You have to taste the sauce! The registered nurse must evaluate the correct completion of any delegated task to make sure it actually did what it was supposed to do. 

To keep from poisoning the guests and losing your restaurant, you live and die by the Five Rights of Delegation. It's the survival algorithm. The Five Rights of Delegation include Right Task, Right Circumstances, Right Person, Right Direction, and Right Supervision. 

First, Right Task ensures the delegated activity falls within the legal scope of practice for the delegatee. You do not ask the dishwasher to flambé a dessert, it's illegal for their role! Right Task also requires the delegated activity to involve minimal risk of harm to the client. Keep it safe. 

Second, Right Circumstances requires the client's medical condition to be stable before a task is delegated. If the kitchen is literally on fire, you don't delegate, you grab the extinguisher! For example, a registered nurse must assess a new client returning from a surgical procedure before delegating care. That fresh post-op patient is a total mystery box, an unproven recipe! You have to establish their baseline first. 

Third, Right Person requires matching the complexity of client care to the demonstrated competency of the delegatee. It doesn't matter if an assistant claims they know how to julienne carrots; the registered nurse must directly verify the competency of a delegatee before assigning a specialized task. Show me you can do it first, buddy. 

Fourth, Right Direction requires the registered nurse to provide clear instructions regarding the task. You don't say "go do stuff." Right Direction requires the registered nurse to clearly communicate the expected timeline for task completion. "I need these vitals in exactly ten minutes, do you understand me?" 

Fifth, Right Supervision requires the registered nurse to monitor the performance of the delegated task. And if they mess it up, Right Supervision requires the registered nurse to provide corrective feedback upon task completion. 

Now, listen to my voice: there is sacred ground in this kitchen. The brain work. The overarching strategy. The independent clinical judgment. A registered nurse cannot delegate tasks requiring independent clinical judgment. Ever! A registered nurse cannot delegate initial nursing assessments to a licensed practical nurse, and a registered nurse cannot delegate initial nursing assessments to an unlicensed assistive person. I don't care how busy you are! You take the first bite of the dish. A registered nurse cannot delegate the creation of nursing diagnoses to any other team member, nor can a registered nurse delegate the establishment of a nursing care plan, nor the evaluation of client care goals. You write the menu! You decide if the menu was a success! 

And teaching? A registered nurse cannot delegate initial client education. You have to explain the allergy warnings to the table first. Also, a registered nurse cannot assign the care of physiologically unstable clients to a licensed practical nurse. If the patient's vitals are crashing like a reality star's marriage, that is your patient now. Finally, a registered nurse must personally perform triage activities in an emergency setting. When a ten-car pileup rolls through the doors, you are the one sorting the chaos because that takes maximum clinical brainpower.

So, who does what? Let's talk about your Sous-Chef, the Licensed Practical Nurse, the LPN. They are skilled, they are licensed, but they operate in the realm of the known. A licensed practical nurse can care for clients with predictable health outcomes. They follow the recipe perfectly. A licensed practical nurse can administer oral medications to stable clients. A licensed practical nurse can perform sterile dressing changes, and a licensed practical nurse can insert urinary catheters. If you already taught the patient about their diabetes, a licensed practical nurse can reinforce client education previously provided by a registered nurse. If you already did the first assessment, a licensed practical nurse can collect specific physiological data for an established client. A licensed practical nurse can even administer enteral tube feedings. 

But what can't the Sous-Chef do? The high-risk magic. A licensed practical nurse cannot administer intravenous push medications. Slamming pure medication straight into a vein is RN territory. And a licensed practical nurse cannot initiate blood product transfusions. Blood is the most expensive vintage wine in the cellar, and only the Executive Chef pops the cork!

Then you have your prep cooks, the Unlicensed Assistive Personnel, or UAPs. These guys keep the restaurant breathing, but before you hand them a job, the registered nurse must consider the potential for client harm before assigning a task to an assistive personnel member. Unlicensed assistive personnel can perform routine activities of daily living for stable clients. Unlicensed assistive personnel can assist stable clients with bathing, and unlicensed assistive personnel can assist stable clients with routine ambulation. They can walk 'em and wash 'em! Unlicensed assistive personnel can measure vital signs on stable clients, unlicensed assistive personnel can record client fluid intake and output, and unlicensed assistive personnel can perform routine client positioning in bed. Fluff the pillows, measure the water pitcher!

But stay out of the danger zone! Unlicensed assistive personnel cannot administer routine medications. Unlicensed assistive personnel cannot perform sterile medical procedures—no sterile fields for the busboy! And here is a massive trap: while they can feed a normal patient, unlicensed assistive personnel cannot feed clients with diagnosed swallowing precautions. If the patient has dysphagia and might choke on their mashed potatoes, feeding them becomes a high-stakes hazard that requires clinical observation.

All of this operates under the law. The state Nurse Practice Act dictates the legal parameters of nursing delegation for a specific geographic jurisdiction. The state law is the ultimate mob boss. Now, your specific hospital might be extra paranoid. A healthcare organization's internal policies can restrict delegation limits beyond the state Nurse Practice Act. If the state says LPNs can draw blood, but your hospital says no, you follow the hospital. However, a healthcare organization's internal policies cannot expand delegation limits beyond the state Nurse Practice Act. The hospital cannot just decide to let the janitor do open-heart surgery!

And what if you hand a task to a UAP, and they look panicked and say they don't know how to do it? Let them back out! A delegatee can legally refuse a delegated task if the delegatee lacks the necessary training to perform the task safely. That is a good thing! It saves your license! And if you are walking down the hall and you see a team member doing something crazy, like about to drop a patient or break a sterile field, do not wait for the performance review! A registered nurse must intervene immediately if a delegated task is being performed unsafely by a team member. You jump in, stop the bleeding, and fix the soup!

You are the boss now. Delegate the chopping, delegate the stirring, but you own the restaurant. Now drink your water, your shift starts in ten minutes!
