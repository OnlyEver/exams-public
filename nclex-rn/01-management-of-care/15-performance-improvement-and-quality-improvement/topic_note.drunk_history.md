Listen to me, slide your drink over and pay attention, because if you want to survive the absolute reality-TV kitchen nightmare that is modern nursing, you have to understand how the restaurant is run! You’ve been looking at the hospital like you’re just a line cook tossing salads, but the National Council of State Boards of Nursing includes performance improvement under the Management of Care section of the NCLEX-RN test plan because they expect you to think like a Michelin-star executive chef! If the system is broken, it doesn't matter how good your knife skills are; the whole kitchen goes down in flames. 

Back in the day, hospitals were run like a mafia-owned diner. It was all about pointing fingers. That old mindset is called Quality Assurance. Quality assurance is an older methodology that is historically retrospective and focuses on discovering individual errors. When the risotto burned, the mob boss didn't care that the stove was broken; he just wanted to know who to whack! Quality assurance focuses on evaluating care to ensure organizational compliance with established minimum standards. Did you wear your hairnet? Did you clock in on time? Did you meet the absolute bare minimum so the health department doesn't board up the doors? A perfect example of this old-school policing is peer review, which is a quality assurance process where registered nurses evaluate the clinical performance of other registered nurses. It’s literally the line cooks watching each other chop onions to make sure nobody is slacking on the minimum safety standards. 

But you can’t run a global hospital empire like a mob diner anymore! We had to bring in the reality-TV restaurant fixers. Enter the new era. Quality improvement is a continuous, systematic process used to measurably improve healthcare services and patient outcomes. Instead of screaming at a nurse for a mistake after the fact, quality improvement focuses on proactively optimizing systems and clinical processes! It’s about replacing the faulty stove before it catches fire. Hand in hand with that is performance improvement, which is the continuous study and adaptation of healthcare functions to increase the probability of desired clinical outcomes. We study the kitchen workflow every single night, tweaking the recipe just a little bit, until the food comes out hot and perfect ninety-nine percent of the time!

Now, how do we define a perfect dining experience? The Institute of Medicine defines six domains of healthcare quality as safe, effective, patient-centered, timely, efficient, and equitable. If your hospital process doesn't hit those six domains, your restaurant is a failure and the critics will destroy you! To hit those six marks, healthcare steals secrets from the most ruthless corporate tycoons on Earth. Have you ever noticed that a bartender keeps the ice right next to the shaker so they don't have to walk across the room? That is Lean! The Lean methodology in healthcare focuses on eliminating waste and streamlining processes to improve efficiency. Stop wasting time walking across the unit for a flush! And when we want absolute perfection, we use Six Sigma. The Six Sigma methodology in healthcare focuses on reducing variation and eliminating defects in clinical processes. It means every single central line dressing is changed the exact same flawless way, every single time, so there are zero defects—zero infections!

But say you want to change a recipe. You don't just throw a ghost pepper into the soup pot on a Friday night and hope the entire dining room doesn't choke! You use a scientific method. The Plan-Do-Study-Act cycle is a four-step model used for testing quality improvement changes in healthcare settings. First, the Plan phase of the Plan-Do-Study-Act cycle involves identifying a clinical problem and developing a strategy for change. "The soup is always cold; we plan to buy a heat lamp." Second, you test it quietly! The Do phase of the Plan-Do-Study-Act cycle involves implementing a proposed change on a small scale. You put just one heat lamp on the appetizer station for a week. Third, the Study phase of the Plan-Do-Study-Act cycle involves analyzing the data collected during the implementation phase. Did the appetizers actually stay hot? Finally, the Act phase of the Plan-Do-Study-Act cycle involves adopting the change permanently or modifying the strategy based on test results. If it worked, you buy heat lamps for the entire kitchen!

To know if any of this is working, the health inspectors are going to show up with their clipboards to run audits. They look at three different things. First, a structure audit evaluates the physical environment and material resources available for providing client care. This is them checking if you actually have enough pans, functioning ovens, and working IV pumps. Next, a process audit evaluates the actual interventions performed by healthcare providers during client care delivery. This is the inspector standing over your shoulder watching to see if you actually washed your hands before touching the raw chicken or flushing the port! Finally, an outcome audit evaluates the final results of client care activities. The proof is in the pudding, literally! An outcome audit measures changes in the client's health status related to the provided healthcare services. Did the customer leave a five-star review and a healed wound, or did they get food poisoning? 

We also run audits based on time! Think about reading a scathing Yelp review the day after a customer leaves—that is a retrospective audit, which evaluates client care after the client has been discharged from the healthcare facility. If the executive chef is standing at the expo window tasting the sauce while it is actively boiling on the stove, that is a concurrent audit, which evaluates client care while the client is actively receiving treatment in the healthcare facility. And if we are looking at the calendar realizing Valentine's Day is next month and ordering extra lobster, that is a prospective audit, which evaluates potential future client care needs and required resources. 

But how do we know if a ten-minute wait time for a steak is actually good? We have to spy on our rivals! Benchmarking is the process of comparing a healthcare facility's performance metrics against best-performing organizations. Sometimes we just spy on ourselves; internal benchmarking compares performance data between different departments within the same healthcare organization. Does the maternity ward answer call bells faster than the emergency room? Other times, we grab binoculars and stare across the street! External benchmarking compares an organization's performance data with data from national databases or other external facilities. Does our hospital have fewer falls than the national average? 

Here is the most critical part of the whole kitchen survival guide. If you drop a steak on the floor, you need to be able to tell the executive chef without getting a meat cleaver thrown at your head! Blame-free organizational cultures encourage healthcare workers to report errors and near misses without fear of punitive action. Why? Because we are obsessed with near misses! A near miss is an unplanned event that did not reach the patient. You almost served a dish with peanut oil to a highly allergic customer, but you caught it at the window! No harm was done, but a near miss is an unplanned event that had the potential to cause patient harm. If you don't tell anyone because you're scared of getting fired, the next cook might actually serve it! That is why quality improvement initiatives utilize incident reports as a primary source of data for identifying systemic risks. The incident report reveals that the peanut oil and the olive oil bottles look exactly identical, so we can change the labels before a disaster strikes!

Because let me tell you, when a disaster strikes, the whole world stops. A sentinel event is an unexpected occurrence involving death or serious physical or psychological injury to a client. The allergic customer eats the peanut oil and ends up in anaphylactic shock. When that nightmare happens, the supreme judges step in. The Joint Commission requires healthcare organizations to conduct a root cause analysis following any sentinel event. They mandate it! And root cause analysis is a systematic process used for identifying the underlying systemic reasons for a critical incident or error. We do not do this to fire the poor waiter; healthcare organizations use root cause analysis to redesign systems and prevent future occurrences of adverse events. We ask "why" over and over until we find the cracked foundation of the kitchen!

When the investigators do this, they use crazy detective boards. A fishbone diagram is a visual tool used in root cause analysis to categorize potential causes of a systemic problem. It literally looks like the skeleton of a fish, where the "head" is the dead customer, and the "bones" branch out into categories like faulty equipment, bad policies, and environmental hazards. They also use Pareto charts, which are bar graphs used in quality improvement to identify the most significant factors contributing to a problem. It follows the 80/20 rule, showing you that 80% of your ruined dishes are caused by just 20% of your broken ovens, so you know exactly which appliance to throw out first! If they want to see how things are going week to week, run charts display performance data over time to help quality improvement teams identify ongoing trends and patterns, like a line graph showing wait times getting longer every single Friday. And if a process is just hopelessly confusing, flowcharts provide a visual step-by-step map of a clinical process to help teams identify workflow inefficiencies. It traces the exact path of a food ticket from the host stand to the kitchen so you can see exactly where the waiter keeps getting lost!

So, what is your job in all this madness? You are the boots on the ground! Registered nurses participate in quality improvement by identifying recurring clinical problems on the nursing unit. You are the one screaming that the walk-in freezer is broken! Then, registered nurses participate in quality improvement by collecting objective data regarding client outcomes. You are the one keeping a tally of exactly how many IV pumps failed on your shift! And when it’s time to rewrite the recipe, we don't just guess. Evidence-based practice integrates the best current research evidence with clinical expertise and client preferences. We combine Gordon Ramsay's proven techniques with what the locals actually want to eat! Because of that, quality improvement teams use evidence-based practice guidelines to establish new standard operating procedures. You literally write the new official cookbook for the hospital! And finally, how do you prove your new recipe saved the restaurant? Evaluating the impact of a quality improvement measure requires comparing post-implementation data against pre-implementation baseline data. If we had fifty patient falls a month before we bought the new bed alarms, and zero falls a month after, congratulations my friend—you just saved the hospital!
