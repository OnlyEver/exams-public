Imagine you are playing a multiplayer video game. You're the team healer, and the moment the match starts, four of your teammates need your help at the exact same time. One teammate just took massive damage, another's screen is glitching so they are confused, a third has a really bad headache, and a fourth has a tiny scratch on their arm. Who do you heal first? Who can wait?

This isn't just about managing your time. This is the amazing, life-saving skill of **prioritization**. In nursing, **prioritization determines the specific order in which nursing interventions (helping actions) are provided to multiple clients.** It is how we use the science of the human body to make a step-by-step game plan to keep people alive.

Before we look at the rules, let's look at a math example of why we can't just share our time equally. Imagine a nurse has 1 hour to check on 4 patients:

Step 1. Find your total time in minutes: 1 hour = 60 minutes.
Step 2. Count the number of patients: 4 patients.
Step 3. Divide the total minutes by the total patients: 60 / 4.
Step 4. Solve the equation: 60 / 4 = 15 minutes per patient.

If we did that, every patient would get exactly 15 minutes. But healthcare doesn't work that way! We don't just guess or help whoever yells the loudest. Instead, **the nurse evaluates the pathophysiology (how the disease works) of multiple clients concurrently (at the same time) to identify the individual at greatest risk of imminent harm (immediate danger).** 

Let’s pull back the curtain and look at the actual *rules of the game*. 

## The Prime Directive: Maslow and the ABCs

If you want to understand who needs help first, you have to understand what a cell needs to survive. The human body is like a car engine. It needs fuel (food), it needs oxygen (air), and it needs a delivery system (blood flow).

A famous rule called **Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs requires the nurse to address a client's physiological needs (body needs) before addressing psychological needs (mind and feelings).** A patient feeling super worried is having a hard time, yes, but if the patient in the next room is suffocating, biology says we must help the suffocating person first. 

Even within those body needs, there is an order. Because your cells will die in minutes without air but can survive for days without food, **the nurse must address physiological needs associated with oxygenation (breathing) before addressing physiological needs associated with nutrition (eating).**

We use a super specific tool for this called the **Airway, Breathing, and Circulation (ABC) framework**. Why? Because **the Airway, Breathing, and Circulation framework prioritizes oxygenation and perfusion (getting blood to the body) above all other physiological needs.** 

*   **Airway:** The pipes in your throat must be open. Therefore, **an open and patent (clear) airway represents the absolute highest physiological priority in emergency care.** If air can't get in, nothing else matters.
*   **Breathing:** The lungs must be doing their job to swap gases. 
*   **Circulation:** The heart and blood must carry that oxygen everywhere else.

What about a patient screaming because they have a broken arm? It might sound harsh, but it's a profound rule of biology: **severe pain represents a high nursing priority *only* after airway, breathing, and circulatory issues have been completely resolved.** Pain is a distress alarm, but a blocked airway is an immediate game-over. Fix the airway, then fix the pain. 

## The Rules of Pathophysiological Combat

When you have multiple patients, you have to compare them. How do we sort them? We use a few brilliant, simple rules. 

### 1. Acute vs. Chronic
The human body is amazing at getting used to long-term (chronic) problems. Over time, it learns to live with a weaker heart or bad lungs. But a sudden, fast (acute) event? The body gets surprised and goes into shock. Therefore, **the nurse must prioritize clients with acute pathophysiological conditions over clients with chronic pathophysiological conditions.** 

How do we spot an acute event? Look for new stuff! **A newly reported symptom indicates an acute physiological change requiring immediate nurse evaluation.** If a patient who has had asthma for ten years is breathing normally for them, that's chronic. But if they complain of a *brand new* sharp chest pain, that is an acute change we must check right away. 

### 2. Unstable vs. Stable
This is like comparing a wobbly bicycle to a bicycle with training wheels. **The nurse must prioritize clients with unstable vital signs (like wild heart rates or dropping blood pressures) over clients with stable vital signs.** 

By the same logic, **clients requiring continuous oxygen therapy demand higher prioritization than clients breathing room air.** The person needing an oxygen tank is fundamentally closer to being unstable; their survival is being artificially supported, like a phone plugged into the wall at 1% battery.

### 3. Systemic vs. Localized
A localized issue affects just one small spot (like a cut on your finger). A systemic issue affects your entire whole body. Because a whole-body problem is much more dangerous, **systemic physiological manifestations take priority over localized physiological manifestations.** 

> **The Sepsis Principle:** Think of an infection. A regular skin infection stays in one place. But **sepsis represents a dangerous systemic inflammatory response to an infection**, meaning the infection has made the whole body go haywire. The blood pressure drops and organs fail. Because of this, **the nurse must prioritize a client experiencing sepsis over a client experiencing a localized skin infection.**

### 4. Unexpected vs. Expected & Actual vs. Potential
If a player in a sport sprains their ankle, we *expect* their ankle to hurt. If that same player suddenly can't breathe, that is *unexpected*. **Unexpected clinical findings demand higher prioritization than expected clinical findings for a specific disease process.**

Likewise, deal with the fire currently burning before you worry about the fire that *might* start. **Actual life-threatening problems take priority over potential health risks.** The risk that a patient might fall out of bed is a serious potential problem, but active bleeding from a cut is an actual problem happening right this second. 

## The Ticking Clocks: High-Risk Scenarios

Certain situations carry a huge risk of going wrong really fast. You must always keep a close eye on these patients.

### The Post-Operative Client
Surgery is rough on the body. Medicines that put you to sleep and the stress of the operation totally change how the body works. For this reason, **clients returning from surgical procedures require immediate prioritization due to the high risk of post-operative complications**, like breathing trouble or heavy bleeding.

### Active Hemorrhage
Think of your blood vessels as a closed loop of water pipes. What happens if there's a massive leak? **Active hemorrhage (heavy bleeding) causes a rapid decrease in intravascular fluid volume (the amount of blood in the pipes).** If there's no fluid in the pipes, the heart has nothing to pump. **The nurse prioritizes active hemorrhage immediately because rapid intravascular volume loss leads to fatal hypovolemic shock** (a deadly crash when the body runs out of fluid). 

### The Brain's Cry for Help
Never ignore a suddenly confused patient. The brain eats more oxygen than almost any other organ. When oxygen drops, the brain glitches. **A sudden change in a client's mental status requires immediate assessment for underlying hypoxia (low oxygen) or severe neurological injury (brain damage).** Don't just assume they are tired; assume their brain is starving for air!

## The Scientific Method in Action: Assess, Evaluate, Revise

Nursing isn't just doing chores; it is an active science experiment. 

Before you try to fix a problem, you must gather your clues. **The nursing process dictates that the nurse must perform a physical assessment (looking and listening) before implementing any intervention (taking action).** You can't fix someone's breathing if you haven't looked in their mouth to see if there's a piece of food stuck!

Once you act, you have to see if it worked. **The evaluation of an initial nursing intervention directly dictates the priority of subsequent nursing actions.** If you give oxygen and the patient turns pink again, your next priority is finding out *why* they needed it. If you give oxygen and they are still turning blue, your next action is immediately calling for an emergency doctor. 

The game board changes constantly. 
*   **The nurse must comprehensively revise the plan of care when a client experiences a sudden physiological deterioration (gets worse quickly).** What was priority #4 suddenly becomes priority #1.
*   Clues change your plan. **The nurse reprioritizes the delivery of client care every time new laboratory results indicate critical pathophysiological changes** (like a blood test showing a dangerously low amount of blood cells).
*   Even teamwork changes on the fly. **Delegation of client care tasks must be adjusted continuously based on the current acuity level (how sick they are) of each assigned client.** If one patient gets sicker, you have to shift your team's chores around.

## Mass Casualty: When the Rules Bend (Disaster Triage)

There is one major exception to everything we just talked about: Disaster Triage. 

Normally, we put all our energy into the absolute sickest person. But what happens if a building collapses and you have 50 injured people and only 3 nurses? The goal shifts from *saving the single sickest person* to *saving the most people possible with the supplies we have*.

To do this fast, **disaster triage systems use color-coded tags to categorize clients based on injury severity (how hurt they are) and survival probability (chances of living).** 

| Triage Color | Category | What it Means & What You Do |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **RED** | **Immediate** | **The red disaster triage tag designates an immediate threat to life requiring immediate medical intervention.** These are people who *can* survive if we act right now. |
| **YELLOW** | **Delayed** | **The yellow disaster triage tag designates major injuries that can wait a brief period for medical care.** They are seriously hurt (like a broken leg), but won't die in the next hour. |
| **GREEN** | **Minimal** | **The green disaster triage tag designates minor injuries that do not require immediate medical attention.** These are the walking wounded with small scrapes. |
| **BLACK** | **Expectant** | **The black disaster triage tag designates a client who is deceased or possesses injuries completely incompatible with life.** In a giant disaster, we sadly cannot spend 45 minutes doing CPR on one person while ten others are waiting for life-saving help. |

## Putting It All Together

Prioritization is not magic. It is just applying biology and logic. 

When you look at a list of patients, just ask yourself:
1. *Who is losing their Airway, Breathing, or Circulation right now?*
2. *Who has an acute (new), unstable, or systemic (whole-body) issue?*
3. *Who has a sudden, unexpected change in their brain or a brand new symptom?*
4. *Who will suffer permanent harm if I wait 10 minutes to see them?*

Check the patient first. Trust the science. Understand *why* the body is acting the way it is, and knowing "who to see first" becomes as easy as beating level one of your favorite game.
