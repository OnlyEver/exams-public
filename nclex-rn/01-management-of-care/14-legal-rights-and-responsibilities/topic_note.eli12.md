# The NCLEX-RN Survival Guide: Legal Rights and Responsibilities

Hello there! Welcome. Grab a seat. Today, we are going to talk about something incredibly fascinating. When you think about nursing, you might just think about science, bandages, and saving lives. And that’s true! But there is an invisible shield around every single patient you will ever help. 

That shield is **the law**. 

You see, in a hospital, it doesn't just matter *that* you do the right thing; it matters *how* you do it, *who* is allowed to do it, and *why*. Laws in nursing are like the rules of a really important board game. They aren’t just boring rules meant to annoy you. They are built to keep people safe when they are feeling their worst. 

Let’s break down the rules of your new job. We’re going to figure out how to play the game, how to keep things fair, and what happens when things go wrong.

---

## Part 1: The Boundaries of Your Sandbox

Imagine your job is like playing in a sandbox. Inside this sandbox are all the fun tools, skills, and medicines you are legally allowed to use. But who built this sandbox? 

The **state Nurse Practice Act** determines the legal boundaries of nursing practice within a specific state jurisdiction. That means it’s the official rulebook for exactly what you are allowed to do in your state. 

Now, who are the referees watching the sandbox to make sure you follow the rules? The **State Boards of Nursing**. They have all the power over your career. State Boards of Nursing possess the legal authority to issue nursing licenses. But if you break the rules, State Boards of Nursing possess the legal authority to suspend nursing licenses. If things are really bad, State Boards of Nursing possess the legal authority to revoke nursing licenses completely, meaning you can never play the game again. 

Because your license is super important, you must protect it. That means a registered nurse must refuse to perform tasks falling outside the legally defined scope of nursing practice. If a doctor asks you to do a surgery that isn't in your rulebook, your answer is a firm, professional "No." 

### Staffing, Floating, and Abandonment
What happens if the hospital is short on helpers and asks you to work on a completely different floor? 
Floating nurses must only accept patient assignments aligning with previous training and competence. It’s like being asked to play a sport you’ve never practiced; if you don't know how to play, it isn't safe!

But be careful *how* you refuse. Once you agree to help a group of patients, they are your responsibility. Abandonment occurs when a nurse accepts a patient assignment and subsequently leaves the unit without transferring care to another qualified professional. This is exactly like pausing a multiplayer video game and walking away from the TV without giving your controller to a teammate. You can't just leave!

## Part 2: Torts — When Things Go Wrong

What is a tort? It sounds like a yummy fruit dessert, but in the law, a tort means doing something wrong that hurts someone else. Torts come in two flavors: on purpose and by accident.

### Intentional Torts
These are actions where you *meant* to do the thing that broke the rules. 

| Tort | Definition | Real-World Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Assault** | Assault is an intentional tort defined as threatening to touch a client without client consent. | Saying to a patient, "If you don't eat your broccoli, I'm going to pinch you!" |
| **Battery** | Battery is an intentional tort defined as actual physical contact with a client without client consent. | Actually pinching them, or giving them a shot when they yelled "No!" |
| **False Imprisonment** | False imprisonment is an intentional tort involving the unlawful restraint of a client against the wishes of the client. | Basically, trapping someone. For example, using physical restraints on a competent client wishing to leave the hospital constitutes false imprisonment. |
| **Defamation of Character** | Defamation of character is an intentional tort involving false communication damaging to a person's reputation. | Spreading totally fake, mean rumors about someone so they lose their friends. |

> **A Quick Note on Defamation:**
> Defamation splits into two categories based on how it is shared. 
> * Slander is an intentional tort defined as spoken defamation of character. (Remember: **S**lander = **S**poken out loud). 
> * Libel is an intentional tort defined as written defamation of character. (Remember: **L**ibel = **L**etters or written words).

### Unintentional Torts: Negligence and Malpractice
Sometimes, you don't mean to do harm, but you make a mistake. 

Negligence is an unintentional tort involving the failure to act as a reasonably prudent person would under similar circumstances. Like carelessly leaving a slippery banana peel on the floor.

But **malpractice** is a bigger deal. Malpractice is a specific type of professional negligence occurring when standard care is not met and results in client harm. 

To actually prove malpractice in court, a plaintiff must establish duty, breach of duty, foreseeability of harm, causation, and injury. 

Let's look at how lawyers "add up" malpractice using a simple math formula. You start with 0 points, and if you get to 5 points, it equals malpractice:

Step 1: 0 + Duty (You were assigned to help the patient) = 1 point.
Step 2: 1 + Breach of Duty (You broke the standard rules) = 2 points.
Step 3: 2 + Foreseeability of Harm (You should have guessed it was dangerous) = 3 points.
Step 4: 3 + Causation (Your exact mistake caused the accident) = 4 points.
Step 5: 4 + Injury (The patient actually got hurt physically or lost money) = 5 points.

If the final score is 5 points, it is Malpractice! If it is missing even one step, it does not count.

## Part 3: The Sacred Right to Choose

Every adult gets to be the boss of their own body. That means a competent adult client possesses the legal right to refuse any medical treatment, even if refusing means they won't get better. It is always their choice. 

### Informed Consent
Before a doctor does a big procedure, they need permission. But who asks for it? 

The healthcare provider performing a procedure is legally responsible for obtaining informed consent from the client. It is *their* job to explain the trade-offs, just like explaining a Pokémon card trade. 

So what does the nurse do? The role of the nurse during informed consent is to act as a witness to the client signing the document. You are just watching, like a referee! But as you watch, you have to check three things:
1. The nurse must verify that a client giving informed consent is voluntarily signing the document. (No one is bullying them into it).
2. The nurse must verify that a client giving informed consent possesses the mental capacity to understand the procedure. (They aren't confused).
3. The nurse must advocate for the client by ensuring the healthcare provider answers all client questions before an informed consent document is signed. (If they hold the pen and look confused, you must stop everything and get the doctor to answer their questions).

**Watch out:** A client under the influence of mind-altering medications is legally considered incompetent to sign an informed consent document. If they had sleepy medicine, they cannot sign!
*Exception to the adult rule:* Emancipated minors possess the legal right to provide medical consent without guardian approval. (These are teenagers that a judge has officially declared to be adults).

### Against Medical Advice (AMA)
What if a patient is mad and wants to quit the game early, leaving the hospital while they are still sick? 
1. The nurse cannot legally forcefully detain a competent client attempting to leave a facility against medical advice. (You can't lock them in; remember, that's false imprisonment!)
2. The nurse must inform a client of the potential medical risks associated with leaving a healthcare facility against medical advice. (You have to warn them why quitting early is a bad idea).
3. The nurse must notify the healthcare provider if a client wishes to leave the facility against medical advice. (Always tell the team captain!). 

### Advance Directives
What happens when a patient gets so sick they lose their ability to speak for themselves? We rely on Advance Directives. These are like instruction manuals written ahead of time.
* A living will is a type of advance directive specifying client wishes regarding end-of-life medical care (like saying, "Please don't put me on a breathing machine").
* A durable power of attorney for healthcare designates a surrogate decision-maker for a client becoming legally incapacitated. (This is picking a trusted backup player to make choices for you). 

Because these instruction manuals are so important, the Patient Self-Determination Act requires healthcare facilities to ask clients about the existence of an advance directive upon admission. The hospital *has* to ask you about it right when you walk in the door.

## Part 4: The Legal Mandate to Speak Up

As a nurse, you are a superhero who has to protect people who can't protect themselves. Because of this, the rules say you *must* report certain things, no matter what. 

**Mandatory Reporting:**
* **Abuse:** Federal and state laws mandate healthcare professionals to report suspected child abuse to designated authorities. Also, healthcare professionals are legally required to report suspected elder abuse to appropriate state agencies. Notice the word *suspected*. You don't need absolute proof; if you think someone is hurting a kid or a grandparent, you must tell the authorities!
* **Violence:** Healthcare providers must report injuries caused by a deadly weapon to local law enforcement agencies. Plus, healthcare providers must report injuries resulting from suspected criminal acts to local law enforcement agencies. (You must tell the police).
* **Public Health:** To keep the whole neighborhood safe from germs, state health departments require the reporting of specific communicable diseases such as tuberculosis and syphilis. 

**Internal Reporting:**
What if your coworker is acting super dizzy or silly because they are taking drugs on the job? 
A nurse has a legal duty to report a coworker suspected of substance impairment directly to nursing management. You have to tell the boss right away. A nurse must not confront an impaired coworker directly regarding suspected substance abuse. Don't try to handle it yourself; just go get the boss!

## Part 5: Safe Daily Practice and Delegation

You will be super busy during your shift, so you might ask helpers (called unlicensed assistive personnel) to do simple tasks. But you are still the boss of your patients! 

* A nurse cannot legally delegate initial nursing assessments to unlicensed assistive personnel. (You have to be the first one to check the patient).
* A nurse cannot legally delegate nursing judgment to unlicensed assistive personnel. (Helpers can't make the big brain decisions).
* A nurse cannot legally delegate patient education to unlicensed assistive personnel. (You have to be the teacher).

And here is the biggest rule: A nurse remains legally accountable for the outcome of any task delegated to unlicensed assistive personnel. If you ask a helper to walk a patient and the patient falls down, *you* are still responsible because you gave them the task.

### Communication and Medications
When a doctor calls you on the phone to order a medicine in an emergency, telephone prescriptions must be immediately written down and read back to the prescribing provider to ensure legal accuracy. (Write it, then say it out loud to make sure you heard right). 

When dealing with very strong, locked-up medicines, the rules are super strict. If you have to throw some away, a nurse must ensure a second licensed nurse witnesses the wasting of any controlled substance. (Have a buddy watch you throw it away). Then, a nurse must sign the controlled substance log alongside the witnessing nurse after wasting a medication. (Both of you sign the paper to promise it was thrown out properly). 

If you ever have to use safety belts to keep a patient from hurting themselves, a nurse must document the exact times and reasons for applying physical restraints to comply with legal standards. You have to write down everything perfectly!

## Part 6: Mishaps, Protections, and Federal Laws

We are all humans, and sometimes mistakes happen. Suppose you give the wrong medicine by accident. What do you do?

You fill out an "oops" paper called an incident report. An incident report is an internal facility document used to record unexpected events causing or potentially causing harm to a client. 

**WARNING:** This paper is a secret note just for the hospital to learn how to fix its mistakes. Do NOT mention it to anyone outside! 
* An incident report must not be referenced within the official medical record of the client.
* An incident report must not be filed within the official medical record of the client.

Instead, the nurse must document objective facts regarding a medication error directly in the medical record of the client. Just write the plain, simple facts of what happened in their chart without making excuses.

### Federal Laws You Must Know
There are three giant rules for the whole country that you must follow:
1. The Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act establishes federal standards to protect client medical records and personal health information. (This keeps patient secrets totally safe!).
2. The Americans with Disabilities Act prohibits discrimination against individuals with disabilities in all areas of public life. (This makes sure everyone gets a fair chance to play and get help).
3. The Emergency Medical Treatment and Active Labor Act requires Medicare-participating hospitals to provide emergency medical screening to all clients regardless of financial status. (Even if someone has zero dollars in their piggy bank, the hospital must check them out!). This law also means the Emergency Medical Treatment and Active Labor Act requires hospitals to stabilize a client before transferring the client to another facility. (You can't send them to another hospital while they are still having a giant emergency).

### The Good Samaritan Laws
Let’s end on a happy note. Imagine you are at the grocery store on your day off, and someone faints. You run over to do CPR to save them, but you accidentally crack their rib. Can they sue you for making a mistake? 

Usually, no! Good Samaritan laws protect nurses from civil liability when providing voluntary emergency care outside of a clinical setting. 

However, there are two exceptions where you wouldn't be protected: Good Samaritan protections do not apply if a nurse commits gross negligence during an emergency. (Like doing something totally wild and unsafe on purpose). Furthermore, Good Samaritan protections do not apply if a nurse acts outside the legal scope of nursing practice during an emergency. (Like trying to do brain surgery in the parking lot when you are only trained to do CPR!). Stick to what you know!

They protect the patient from getting hurt, and they protect *you* from getting in trouble. Know your rulebook, tell the truth, always stand up for your patients, and speak out when the rules tell you to.
