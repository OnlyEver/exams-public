# The [NCLEX-RN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Council_Licensure_Examination) Survival Guide: Legal Rights and Responsibilities

Hello there! Welcome. Grab a seat. Today, we are going to talk about something incredibly fascinating. When most people think about [nursing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nursing), they think about [biology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology), [chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemistry), [anatomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anatomy), and saving lives with swift interventions. And that’s true! But there is an invisible, powerful forcefield surrounding every single patient you will ever touch. 

That forcefield is **the [law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law)**. 

You see, in [healthcare](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Health_care), it doesn't just matter *that* you do the right thing; it matters *how* you do it, *who* is allowed to do it, and *why*. Laws in nursing aren’t just arbitrary rules designed to bore you in a classroom. They are the mechanisms human beings invented to protect the sacred [dignity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dignity), [autonomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autonomy), and safety of people when they are at their most vulnerable. 

Let’s break down the legal landscape of your nursing practice. We’re going to figure out the rules of the game, exactly how you play it, and what happens when things go wrong.

---

## Part 1: The Boundaries of Your Sandbox

Imagine a sandbox. Inside this sandbox are all the skills, assessments, and [medications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medication) you are legally allowed to administer. Who built the sandbox? 

The **state Nurse Practice Act**. This crucial piece of legislation determines the legal boundaries of nursing practice within a specific state [jurisdiction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jurisdiction). It tells you exactly what a nurse is—and isn't—allowed to do. 

Now, who are the referees watching the sandbox? The **[State Boards of Nursing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Board_of_nursing)**. They possess the legal authority to be the ultimate gatekeepers of your career. They have the power to **issue** [nursing licenses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nursing_license), but if you break the rules, they also possess the legal authority to **suspend** or even completely **revoke** your license. 

Because your license is on the line, you must fiercely protect it. That means **a [registered nurse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Registered_nurse) must refuse to perform tasks falling outside the legally defined [scope of nursing practice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scope_of_practice).** If a doctor asks you to do a [surgical incision](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surgical_incision) and it's not in your state's Nurse Practice Act, your answer is a firm, professional "No." 

![Diagram outlining various surgical incisions across the human torso. Making a surgical incision is an advanced medical intervention that strictly falls outside a registered nurse's scope of practice.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Incisions_of_the_torso.svg)

### Staffing, Floating, and Abandonment
What happens when the [hospital](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hospital) is short-staffed and asks you to float to a different unit? 
**Floating nurses must only accept patient assignments aligning with previous training and competence.** If you are a [postpartum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postpartum_period) nurse being floated to the [ICU](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intensive_care_unit) and assigned a patient on a [ventilator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ventilator) you don't know how to use, accepting that assignment is unsafe and illegal. 

![A standard hospital mechanical ventilator setup. Floating nurses must legally decline assignments—such as managing a ventilated patient—if they lack the specialized training required to safely meet the standard of care.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Ventilators.jpg)

But be careful *how* you refuse. Once you accept an assignment and receive a report, those patients are legally yours. **[Abandonment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patient_abandonment)** occurs when a nurse accepts a patient assignment and subsequently leaves the unit without transferring care to another qualified professional. If you need to step away, you must hand off your patients properly!

## Part 2: Torts — When Things Go Wrong

What is a [tort](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tort)? It sounds like a fancy French pastry, but in law, a tort is a [civil wrong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civil_wrong) committed against a person or their property. They come in two flavors: [intentional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intentional_tort) and unintentional.

### Intentional Torts
These are actions where you *meant* to do the action that caused the infringement on the patient's rights. 

| Tort | Definition | Real-World Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **[Assault](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Assault_%28tort%29)** | An [intentional tort](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intentional_tort) defined as threatening to touch a client without client [consent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consent_%28law%29). | Saying to a patient, "If you don't swallow this [pill](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tablet_%28pharmacy%29), I'm going to force it down your throat." |
| **[Battery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battery_%28tort%29)** | An intentional tort defined as actual physical contact with a client without client consent. | Actually shoving the pill down the patient's throat, or starting an [IV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intravenous_therapy) after they said no. |
| **[False Imprisonment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/False_imprisonment)** | The unlawful restraint of a client against the wishes of the client. | **Using [physical restraints](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medical_restraint) on a competent client wishing to leave the hospital constitutes false imprisonment.** |
| **[Defamation of Character](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Defamation)** | False communication damaging to a person's reputation. | Gossiping falsely that a patient has an [STD](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sexually_transmitted_infection), causing them to lose their job. |

> **A Quick Note on Defamation:**
> Defamation splits into two categories based on how it is delivered. 
> * **[Slander](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Defamation)** is spoken defamation of character. (Remember: **S**lander = **S**poken). 
> * **[Libel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Defamation)** is written defamation of character. (Remember: **L**ibel = **L**iterature).

### Unintentional Torts: Negligence and Malpractice
Sometimes, you don't mean to do harm, but you mess up. 

**[Negligence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Negligence)** is an unintentional tort involving the failure to act as a reasonably prudent person would under similar circumstances. If you spill water on the floor, don't clean it up, and a visitor slips, that's negligence.

But **[malpractice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medical_malpractice)** is special. It is a specific type of professional negligence occurring when [standard care](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_of_care) is not met and results in client harm. 

To actually prove malpractice in a court of law, a [plaintiff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plaintiff) can't just say, "The nurse made a mistake!" They must establish a rigorous chain reaction of five specific elements:

> **The Malpractice Formula:**
> **[Duty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duty_of_care)** (You were assigned to the patient) + 
> **Breach of Duty** (You failed the [standard of care](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_of_care)) + 
> **[Foreseeability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foreseeability) of Harm** (You should have known it was dangerous) + 
> **[Causation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Causation_%28law%29)** (Your specific mistake caused the issue) + 
> **[Injury](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Personal_injury)** (The patient suffered actual physical or financial harm). 

If any one of these five elements is missing, it is not legally considered malpractice.

## Part 3: The Sacred Right to Choose

Every [competent adult](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Competence_%28law%29) has [autonomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autonomy). That means **a competent adult client possesses the legal right to refuse any medical treatment**, even if refusing that treatment will result in their death. 

![A standard medical refusal of treatment form. A core tenet of healthcare law is patient autonomy, guaranteeing competent adult clients the legal right to reject prescribed medications or life-saving interventions.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Refusal_of_treatment_form.jpg)

### Informed Consent
Before we cut someone open or do a major procedure, we need their permission. But who gets it? 

**The healthcare provider performing a procedure is legally responsible for obtaining [informed consent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Informed_consent) from the client.** It is *their* job to explain the risks, benefits, and alternatives. 

So what does the nurse do? **The role of the nurse during informed consent is to act as a witness to the client signing the document.** But it's not a passive role! As you watch them sign, you are assessing the situation. You must:
1. Verify that a client giving informed consent is **voluntarily** signing the document (no [coercion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coercion)).
2. Verify that the client possesses the **[mental capacity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mental_capacity)** to understand the procedure. 
3. **[Advocate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patient_advocacy)** for the client by ensuring the healthcare provider answers all client questions *before* the document is signed. If the patient holds the pen and asks, "Wait, will this paralyze me?", you must stop everything and call the provider back to answer.

**Watch out:** A client under the influence of [mind-altering medications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Psychoactive_drug) (like [opioids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Opioid) or [sedatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sedative)) is **legally considered incompetent** to sign an informed consent document. 
*Exception to the adult rule:* **[Emancipated minors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emancipation_of_minors)** possess the legal right to provide medical consent without guardian approval.

### Against Medical Advice (AMA)
What if a competent patient decides they hate the hospital and want to walk out with an IV in their arm? 
1. **The nurse cannot legally forcefully detain a competent client attempting to leave a facility [against medical advice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Against_medical_advice).** (Remember, that's false imprisonment!)
2. **The nurse must inform a client of the potential medical risks** associated with leaving.
3. **The nurse must notify the healthcare provider** that the client wishes to leave. 

### Advance Directives
What happens when a patient *loses* their competency? We rely on [Advance Directives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Advance_healthcare_directive). 
* A **[living will](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Advance_healthcare_directive)** is a type of advance directive specifying client wishes regarding end-of-life medical care (e.g., "Do not put me on a ventilator").
* A **[durable power of attorney for healthcare](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_of_attorney)** designates a specific [surrogate decision-maker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surrogate_decision-maker) for a client if they become legally incapacitated. 

Because these documents are so vital, the [federal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federal_government_of_the_United_States) **[Patient Self-Determination Act (PSDA)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patient_Self-Determination_Act)** requires healthcare facilities to ask clients about the existence of an advance directive upon admission.

## Part 4: The Legal Mandate to Speak Up

As a nurse, you are the eyes and ears of the state. You see things behind closed doors that nobody else sees. Because of this, the law compels you to report certain things, no matter what. 

**Mandatory Reporting to the State/Authorities:**
* **Abuse:** Federal and state laws mandate healthcare professionals to report **suspected [child abuse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Child_abuse)** to designated authorities, and you are legally required to report **suspected [elder abuse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elder_abuse)** to appropriate state agencies. Notice the word *suspected*. You do not need proof; you just need a reasonable suspicion. 
* **Violence:** Healthcare providers must report injuries caused by a **[deadly weapon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deadly_weapon)** (like [gunshot wounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gunshot_wound)) and injuries resulting from **suspected [criminal acts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crime)** to local [law enforcement agencies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law_enforcement_agency).
* **Public Health:** State health departments require the reporting of specific **[communicable diseases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infectious_disease) such as [tuberculosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuberculosis) and [syphilis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syphilis)**. This is how [epidemiology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epidemiology) works; we track outbreaks to protect the [herd](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herd_immunity).

![Infant radiograph displaying multiple rib fractures. Recognizing and reporting suspicious clinical presentations is a strict legal mandate designed to intervene in cases of suspected child abuse.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Fractured_ribs.jpg)

**Internal Reporting:**
What if your coworker is slurring their words and pocketing patient [narcotics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Narcotic)? 
**A nurse has a legal duty to report a coworker suspected of [substance impairment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Substance_abuse) directly to nursing management.** You **must not confront an impaired coworker directly** regarding suspected [substance abuse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Substance_abuse). Why? Because they might flee, destroy evidence, or become violent. You go up the [chain of command](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chain_of_command), immediately.

## Part 5: Safe Daily Practice and Delegation

You will be spinning a lot of plates on a nursing shift. You have to delegate. But you cannot delegate your brain. 

When working with [Unlicensed Assistive Personnel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unlicensed_assistive_personnel) (UAP, like nursing assistants):
* A nurse cannot legally delegate **[initial nursing assessments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nursing_assessment)** to unlicensed assistive personnel.
* A nurse cannot legally delegate **nursing judgment** to unlicensed assistive personnel.
* A nurse cannot legally delegate **[patient education](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patient_education)** to unlicensed assistive personnel.

And here is the kicker: **A nurse remains legally accountable for the outcome of any task delegated to unlicensed assistive personnel.** If you ask a UAP to [ambulate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Walking) a patient and they drop them, *you* are still responsible for ensuring that task was delegated safely and followed up on.

### Communication and Medications
When a doctor calls you on the phone to order a medication in an emergency, it's a game of telephone that can turn deadly. Therefore, **[telephone prescriptions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medical_prescription) must be immediately written down and read back to the prescribing provider to ensure legal accuracy.** 

When it comes to narcotics, the law is airtight. If you draw up 2mg of [morphine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morphine) but only need 1mg, you must waste the rest. **A nurse must ensure a second licensed nurse witnesses the wasting of any [controlled substance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Controlled_substance)**, and both of you must **sign the controlled substance log alongside the witnessing nurse** after wasting it. 

If you have to use physical restraints (as a last resort, for safety), **a nurse must document the exact times and reasons for applying [physical restraints](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medical_restraint) to comply with legal standards.**

## Part 6: Mishaps, Protections, and Federal Laws

We are humans. Mistakes happen. Suppose a [medication error](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medication_error) occurs. What do you do?

You write an **[incident report](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Incident_report)**. This is an internal facility document used to record unexpected events causing or potentially causing harm to a client. 

**WARNING:** This is a major trap for new nurses. Do NOT mention the incident report in the chart! 
* An incident report **must not be referenced** within the official medical record of the client.
* An incident report **must not be filed** within the official medical record of the client.

Why? Because the incident report is for the hospital's [risk management](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Risk_management) team to fix systemic errors. If you put it in the chart, it becomes discoverable by lawyers to sue the hospital. Instead, **the nurse must document objective facts regarding a medication error directly in the official [medical record](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medical_record) of the client.** (e.g., "0900: Administered 50mg [Metoprolol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metoprolol). Patient's heart rate dropped to 40. Physician notified.")

### Federal Laws You Must Know
Beyond the PSDA we discussed earlier, three other massive federal laws govern your practice:
1. **The [Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Health_Insurance_Portability_and_Accountability_Act)** establishes federal standards to protect client medical records and personal health information. No snooping on neighbors' charts!
2. **The [Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Americans_with_Disabilities_Act_of_1990)** prohibits discrimination against individuals with disabilities in all areas of public life, guaranteeing equal access to care.
3. **The [Emergency Medical Treatment and Active Labor Act (EMTALA)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emergency_Medical_Treatment_and_Active_Labor_Act)** is the "anti-dumping" law. It requires [Medicare](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medicare_%28United_States%29)-participating hospitals to provide [emergency medical screening](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medical_screening) to all clients **regardless of financial status**, and requires hospitals to **stabilize a client before transferring the client to another facility.**

![A pie chart breaking down common HIPAA violations. Understanding how personal health information is most frequently compromised helps nurses protect patient privacy and avoid severe federal penalties.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Hipaa_Violations_by_Type_-_Pie_Chart.png)

### The Good Samaritan Laws
Let’s end on a high note. Imagine you're walking down the street, off duty, and someone collapses. You rush over to do [CPR](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardiopulmonary_resuscitation). You crack a rib. Can they sue you? 

Generally, no. **[Good Samaritan laws](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Good_Samaritan_law) protect nurses from [civil liability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legal_liability) when providing voluntary emergency care outside of a clinical setting.** 

However, there are caveats. Good Samaritan protections **do not apply if a nurse commits [gross negligence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gross_negligence)** during an emergency (like trying to do an emergency [tracheostomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tracheotomy) with a dirty pen when you aren't trained for it). Furthermore, the protections **do not apply if a nurse acts outside the legal scope of nursing practice** during an emergency. Stick to [basic life support](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basic_life_support) and what you know!

![Medical diagram demonstrating the placement of a tracheostomy tube. Performing invasive, advanced procedures outside your authorized nursing scope during a public emergency constitutes gross negligence and instantly voids Good Samaritan legal protections.](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Redirect/file/Tracheostomy_NIH.jpg)

### Final Thoughts
Legal rights and responsibilities in nursing are not restrictions; they are the guardrails of a safe society. They protect the patient from harm, and they protect *you* from [liability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legal_liability). Know your scope, document the objective truth, advocate fiercely for your patients' right to choose, and speak up when the law demands it. 

Now, take a deep breath. You've got this. Study these principles, master the sandbox, and go be brilliant!
